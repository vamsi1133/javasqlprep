11 — 1D Dynamic Programming

You never invent a DP solution. You write the honest recursion, notice you're recomputing subproblems, and cache. That's the whole topic.

Why interviewers ask this

DP is the topic candidates fear, and interviewers know it — which is exactly why it appears. At the enterprise tier (Optum, JPMC, Goldman Sachs and their peers) they are not asking you to invent a novel recurrence under time pressure; they are asking whether you can take a problem with choices and turn it into a recursion, then notice the recursion is doing the same work over and over, then kill that waste. The problems they actually ask are the ones on this page: Climbing Stairs, House Robber, Coin Change, Word Break, Kadane's, Longest Increasing Subsequence. All of them are 1D — one index of state, one array of answers. The exotic stuff (bitmask DP, digit DP, DP on trees) is not coming. What is coming is a 45-minute slot where you must produce a correct recurrence, say what dp[i] means in one English sentence, and get to O(n) time and O(1) space without flailing.

Here is the reframe that makes this tractable: you have been writing top-down DP in React for years without calling it that. useMemo(() => expensiveThing(a, b), [a, b]) is memoization — a cache keyed by its inputs, so the same inputs never recompute. A Map keyed by a request URL is memoization. React's reconciler bailing out of a subtree because props are referentially equal is memoization. DP is the same move applied to a recursion where the "inputs" are just an index, plus one extra step (inverting the recursion into a loop) that buys you the O(1)-space version interviewers love. There is no magic here and there is nothing to memorize. There is a ladder, and every problem on this page is climbed with the same four rungs.

1. Recognizing the pattern

DP problems announce themselves. These phrasings, almost verbatim, are the name tag:

  • "How many ways…" — count the distinct ways to reach a goal. Climbing Stairs, Decode Ways, Coin Change II. Counting problems where the count explodes combinatorially but the states don't.
  • "Minimum / maximum cost to reach X" — Min Cost Climbing Stairs, Coin Change, Jump Game II. An optimization over a sequence of choices.
  • "Can you make / reach / build X?" — Word Break, Partition Equal Subset Sum. A boolean reachability question over a set of choices.
  • "Longest / largest … subsequence / substring / subarray" — LIS, Maximum Subarray, Longest Common Subsequence. The word subsequence especially: you're choosing, for each element, whether to include it.
  • You wrote a recursion and it's exponential. This is the real trigger, and it's the one that generalizes. If your honest brute force branches (solve(i-1) + solve(i-2), or a loop of recursive calls), and the arguments to those calls come from a small set, you are recomputing. Cache.

The two formal conditions, in plain language:

  • Optimal substructure — "the best answer for the whole thing is built out of the best answers to smaller versions of the same thing." If you know the best way to rob houses 0..i-1 and the best way to rob 0..i-2, you can compute the best way to rob 0..i without re-examining how those smaller answers were achieved. The sub-answer is a number, not a story. When this fails — when you'd need to know which houses were robbed, not just how much — your state is too small and you need another dimension. (That failure is what tutorial 13 is about.)
  • Overlapping subproblems — "the same smaller question gets asked many times." fib(30) calls fib(28) from two different places. In a tree of 2ⁿ calls there are only n distinct arguments, so the tree is almost entirely duplicate work. This is the condition that makes caching pay. Without it, caching is just wasted memory — that's why backtracking (tutorial 09) has no memo: every branch there explores a genuinely distinct partial solution.
The recognition sentence to say out loud, before writing a line: "There's a choice at each step, the answer for step i only depends on a couple of earlier steps, and the naive recursion would recompute those — so this is a 1D DP. Let me define dp[i] as … and I'll start from the brute-force recursion so we can both see where the memo goes." That sentence buys you the whole first five minutes and signals a candidate who derives instead of recalls. Interviewers at JPMC and Goldman are explicitly listening for "state definition" and "recurrence" as named artifacts.
Greedy vs. DP is the fork in the road. Greedy takes the locally best choice and never reconsiders; DP considers all choices at each step and keeps the best. Greedy is faster when it works — and it catastrophically doesn't work on Coin Change (§5), which is exactly why that problem is asked so often. Tutorial 12 covers how to prove greedy is safe; when you can't prove it, use DP.

2. The ladder

Every DP solution on this page — and in tutorial 13, and in the DP question you get in your real loop — is produced by climbing the same four rungs. Do not skip rungs. Do not try to write rung 4 first; nobody can, including the people who look like they can.

RungWhat you writeCostWhy you write it
1. Brute-force recursionThe honest "try every choice" functionExponentialIt encodes the recurrence, which is the actual intellectual content. Everything else is bookkeeping.
2. Memoize (top-down)Same function + a cache keyed by the argumentsO(states × work)Three lines. Turns exponential into linear without changing a single line of logic. This is useMemo.
3. Tabulate (bottom-up)Invert it: a dp array filled with a loopSame time, O(n) spaceKills recursion (no stack overflow at n = 10⁵) and makes the fill order explicit.
4. Collapse the tableKeep only the cells the recurrence touchesO(1) spaceIf dp[i] only reads dp[i-1] and dp[i-2], the other n−2 cells are dead weight — replace the array with two variables.

In the interview you narrate all four rungs and you code rung 3 or 4. Saying "the brute force is 2ⁿ because each step branches two ways; but there are only n distinct subproblems, so I'll memoize — and since the recurrence only reaches back two cells, I can drop the array entirely and use two variables" is a complete senior answer before you type anything. Now watch it happen on the smallest possible problem.

Climbing Stairs (LC #70) EASY ★

You're climbing a staircase with n steps. Each move, you may climb either 1 step or 2 steps. How many distinct ordered ways are there to reach the top? Two ways are different if the sequence of moves differs — 1+2 and 2+1 are two different ways, not one.

Example 1: Input: n = 2 → Output: 2 — the ways are [1,1] and [2].
Example 2: Input: n = 3 → Output: 3 — [1,1,1], [1,2], [2,1]. Note [1,2] and [2,1] count separately.
Example 3: Input: n = 5 → Output: 8 — you will not enumerate these by hand, and that's the point: the count grows like Fibonacci while the state stays a single integer.
Constraints: 1 ≤ n ≤ 45. (That ceiling of 45 is a tell — it's exactly where a 2ⁿ brute force stops finishing.)

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Don't think about the whole staircase; think about the last move only. To be standing on step n, your final move was either a 1-step (so you were on n-1) or a 2-step (so you were on n-2). Those two scenarios are mutually exclusive and together they're exhaustive — there is no third way to arrive. So the number of ways to reach n is the number of ways to reach n-1 plus the number of ways to reach n-2. That single sentence is the recurrence, and you got it by asking "what was the last thing that happened?" — which is the question that produces the recurrence in every DP problem on this page. The base cases: there's exactly 1 way to be at step 0 (do nothing — the empty sequence of moves is a way), and 1 way to reach step 1 (one 1-step). ways(n) = ways(n-1) + ways(n-2) with ways(0)=ways(1)=1 is Fibonacci, shifted by one. Say that out loud in the interview — "this is Fibonacci in disguise" — because it proves you see the structure and not just the code.

Rung 1 — the honest brute force. Write this first, always. It is a direct transcription of the sentence above:

// Rung 1: exponential. Each call branches into two calls.
var climbStairs = function(n) {
  if (n < 0) return 0;   // overshot the top — not a valid way
  if (n === 0) return 1;  // arrived exactly: the empty move-sequence counts as ONE way
  return climbStairs(n - 1) + climbStairs(n - 2);
};

This is correct and unusably slow. climbStairs(45) makes on the order of 2⁴⁵ calls. Why? Draw two levels of the call tree for n = 5: f(5) calls f(4) and f(3); f(4) calls f(3) and f(2). f(3) is now being computed twice, from scratch, and its entire subtree with it. That duplication compounds at every level. But notice what the arguments are: only ever 5, 4, 3, 2, 1, 0. There are exactly n+1 distinct subproblems and we are solving them 2ⁿ times. That observation — not cleverness — is what produces the DP.

Rung 2 — memoize (top-down). Identical logic. A cache keyed by the argument. Three lines:

// Rung 2: top-down DP = the same recursion + a cache. O(n) time, O(n) space
// (O(n) of that space is the recursion stack, not just the map).
var climbStairs = function(n, memo = new Map()) {
  if (n < 0) return 0;
  if (n === 0) return 1;
  if (memo.has(n)) return memo.get(n);          // cache HIT: never recompute a subproblem

  const result = climbStairs(n - 1, memo) + climbStairs(n - 2, memo);
  memo.set(n, result);                            // cache WRITE, before returning
  return result;
};

Every distinct n is now computed exactly once; after that it's a Map lookup. n subproblems × O(1) work each = O(n). You just took an algorithm from 2⁴⁵ operations to 45 by adding a cache — and you did it without changing the logic. That's the entire promise of DP.

This is useMemo. useMemo(() => compute(n), [n]) means "compute is a pure function of n; if n hasn't changed, reuse the cached result." The memo Map above is the same contract with a bigger cache. If you can explain why useMemo's dependency array must list every input the callback reads, you already understand the one rule of memoization that matters: the cache key must be everything the function's result depends on. Get that wrong in React and you get a stale render; get it wrong in DP and you get a wrong answer. Same bug.

Rung 3 — tabulate (bottom-up). The recursion computes f(5) by descending to f(0) and returning back up. So just… go up in the first place. Same recurrence, no stack:

// Rung 3: bottom-up DP. O(n) time, O(n) space, no recursion.
var climbStairs = function(n) {
  // STATE: dp[i] = the number of distinct ways to reach step i.
  const dp = new Array(n + 1).fill(0);

  dp[0] = 1;                 // BASE: one way to be at the ground (do nothing)
  dp[1] = 1;                 // BASE: one way to reach step 1 (safe: n >= 1 per constraints)

  // ORDER: ascending, because dp[i] reads dp[i-1] and dp[i-2] — they must already be final.
  for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) {
    dp[i] = dp[i - 1] + dp[i - 2];   // RECURRENCE: last move was a 1-step or a 2-step
  }

  return dp[n];             // ANSWER: ways to reach the top
};

Compare it to rung 2 line for line. memo.get(i) became dp[i]. The recursive descent became an ascending loop. The base cases moved from if-guards at the top of the function to initialized cells before the loop. Nothing was invented. The table is the memo, turned inside out.

Rung 4 — collapse the table. Look hard at the loop body: it reads dp[i-1] and dp[i-2] and nothing else. Once you're at i = 10, cells 0 through 7 will never be read again. You are storing 45 numbers to use 2 of them. Throw the array away:

// Rung 4: O(n) time, O(1) space. THIS is what you submit.
var climbStairs = function(n) {
  let twoBack = 1;   // dp[i-2]  (starts as dp[0])
  let oneBack = 1;   // dp[i-1]  (starts as dp[1])

  for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) {
    const current = oneBack + twoBack;  // same recurrence, same order
    twoBack = oneBack;                   // slide the window forward one step
    oneBack = current;
  }

  return oneBack;   // after the loop, oneBack holds dp[n]  (and for n=1, the loop never ran — still correct)
};
Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space

The rung-4 code above is the submission. Restated with the interview narration baked into the comments:

var climbStairs = function(n) {
  // dp[i] = ways to reach step i. dp[i] = dp[i-1] + dp[i-2]  (Fibonacci).
  // Only two cells are ever live, so keep two variables instead of an array.
  let twoBack = 1, oneBack = 1;
  for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) {
    [twoBack, oneBack] = [oneBack, oneBack + twoBack];  // destructuring = simultaneous update, no temp
  }
  return oneBack;
};

Trace on n = 5: start twoBack=1 (dp[0]), oneBack=1 (dp[1]). i=2 → (1, 2) so dp[2]=2. i=3 → (2, 3) dp[3]=3. i=4 → (3, 5) dp[4]=5. i=5 → (5, 8) dp[5]=8. Return 8 ✓ — matches Example 3, and it's the Fibonacci sequence 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8 exactly as predicted.

Complexity: O(n) time — one pass, O(1) work per step. O(1) space — two numbers, regardless of n. Contrast with rung 1's O(2ⁿ) time and rung 2's O(n) space (the memo plus an n-deep call stack, which would actually blow up for n = 10⁵ — a real reason to prefer bottom-up when n is large).

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Now you can climb 1, 2, or 3 steps." — dp[i] = dp[i-1] + dp[i-2] + dp[i-3]. Three variables. The recurrence is a sum over the allowed step sizes; generalize to an array steps and it's an inner loop. Show you see it's the same problem.
  • "Now some steps are broken and can't be landed on." — dp[i] = 0 for broken i instead of the sum. Reachability falls out for free.
  • "n = 10¹⁸." — the honest answer: matrix exponentiation / fast doubling gives O(log n). Say the name, note it's out of scope for this loop, move on. Knowing the escalation exists is the signal; implementing it is not.
  • "Why is dp[0] = 1 and not 0?" — because it's a count of ways, and there is exactly one way to do nothing (the empty sequence). If you set it to 0, every count downstream collapses to 0. This is the single most common base-case error in counting DPs, and interviewers ask it deliberately.
Do not start at rung 3. Candidates who try to write the dp array directly, from memory, get the base cases wrong and then cannot debug them, because they never wrote down what dp[i] means. The recursion is where the meaning lives. Ninety seconds spent on rung 1 saves you ten minutes of staring at an off-by-one. Write the recursion, then mechanically convert it.

Min Cost Climbing Stairs (LC #746) EASY

You're given an integer array cost, where cost[i] is the price you pay to step off step i. Once you've paid, you may move either 1 or 2 steps up. You may start on step 0 or step 1 (starting is free — you only pay when you leave a step). Return the minimum total cost to reach the top of the floor, which is the position just past the last element — index cost.length.

Example 1: Input: cost = [10, 15, 20] → Output: 15 — start at index 1, pay 15, take a 2-step, and you're past the end. Paying 10 at index 0 then 15 at index 1 would cost 25.
Example 2: Input: cost = [1, 100, 1, 1, 1, 100, 1, 1, 100, 1] → Output: 6 — start at index 0 and hop over every 100: pay at indices 0, 2, 4, 6, 7, 9 → six 1s. Greedily always taking the cheaper next step gets trapped by the 100s; you need to look ahead, which is what DP does for you.
Constraints: 2 ≤ cost.length ≤ 1000; 0 ≤ cost[i] ≤ 999.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Same staircase, but now you're minimizing instead of counting — so the + in the recurrence becomes a Math.min, and that is genuinely the entire difference. Climb the ladder. Rung 1: the honest recursion asks "what's the cheapest way to reach position i?" and answers with the last move: you either came from i-1 (paying cost[i-1] to leave it) or from i-2 (paying cost[i-2]). So minCost(i) = min(minCost(i-1) + cost[i-1], minCost(i-2) + cost[i-2]). Base cases: minCost(0) = minCost(1) = 0 — starting on either of the first two steps is free. Rung 2: memoize on i. Rung 3: fill dp[0..n] ascending. Rung 4: the recurrence reaches back exactly two cells → two variables. The critical detail candidates fumble: the answer is dp[n], not dp[n-1]. "The top" is past the last step. The cost array has n entries but there are n+1 positions you can stand on. Read the state definition back to yourself and the off-by-one dies.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var minCostClimbingStairs = function(cost) {
  const n = cost.length;

  // STATE: dp[i] = min cost to ARRIVE AT position i (not including leaving it).
  // Positions run 0..n; position n is "the top", one past the last step.
  // BASE: arriving at 0 or 1 is free — you're allowed to start there.
  let twoBack = 0;   // dp[i-2]
  let oneBack = 0;   // dp[i-1]

  for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) {
    // RECURRENCE: to stand on i, my last move came from i-1 or i-2,
    // and I paid that step's cost to leave it.
    const current = Math.min(
      oneBack + cost[i - 1],   // came from i-1, paid cost[i-1]
      twoBack + cost[i - 2]    // came from i-2, paid cost[i-2]
    );
    twoBack = oneBack;
    oneBack = current;
  }

  return oneBack;   // dp[n] — the TOP, past the last step. Not dp[n-1].
};

Trace on [10, 15, 20]: n=3. Start twoBack=0 (dp[0]), oneBack=0 (dp[1]). i=2: min(0+cost[1], 0+cost[0]) = min(15, 10) = 10 → dp[2]=10; slide → (0, 10). i=3: min(10+cost[2], 0+cost[1]) = min(10+20, 0+15) = 15 → dp[3]=15. Return 15 ✓. Note dp[2]=10 was the cheaper way to reach index 2, but the winning path skipped index 2 entirely — that's exactly the lookahead greedy can't do.

Complexity: O(n) time, one pass. O(1) space — two accumulators. The rung-3 array version is O(n) space and equally accepted; the collapse is free, so take it.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Which steps did you actually take?" — reconstruct by storing the array (rung 3, not rung 4) and walking backwards from dp[n], at each cell checking which of the two predecessors produced it. Path reconstruction always costs you the space optimization — that's the trade, and naming it out loud is senior signal.
  • "Why can't I just always take the cheaper of the next two steps?" — Example 2 is the counterexample; greedy gets trapped paying a 100. Have it ready.
  • "Steps of size 1, 2, or 3?" — the min takes three arguments, or becomes a loop over allowed jumps. Same skeleton.

3. The five questions to ask every DP problem

This is the checklist. Run it out loud, in order, before you write code — under pressure it's the difference between a derivation and a panic. Every problem in the rest of this tutorial is solved by answering these five, and nothing else.

  1. What's the state? Finish this sentence in plain English: "dp[i] is the ______ for the first i items / ending at index i." If you can't finish it, you don't have a solution yet — stop and keep thinking, do not start typing. Two shapes cover almost everything on this page: "the best answer considering the prefix 0..i" (House Robber, Coin Change, Word Break) and "the best answer for a subsequence/subarray that ends exactly at i" (LIS, Kadane's). Those two are different and choosing the wrong one is the most expensive mistake in DP. The tell: if the answer at the end is dp[n], you have a prefix state; if the answer is Math.max(...dp), you have an ends-at state.
  2. What's the recurrence? Ask: "what was the last choice made?" Enumerate every possible last move, and for each one, express the answer in terms of a smaller state. Then combine them with the operator the problem asks for: + to count ways, Math.min/Math.max to optimize, || to test reachability. The set of last moves is usually two ("take it or skip it") or a loop (one per coin, per word, per earlier index).
  3. What are the base cases? The smallest inputs, where the recurrence has nothing to recurse into. For counting DPs, the empty case is almost always 1, not 0 — there's exactly one way to do nothing (dp[0] = 1 in Climbing Stairs, Decode Ways, Coin Change II). For minimization it's usually 0 or Infinity; for reachability, true. Get these wrong and every cell downstream is garbage — and the bug looks like a recurrence bug, which is why people lose twenty minutes to it.
  4. What order do I fill? Whatever order guarantees that when you compute dp[i], everything it reads is already final. For 1D DP that's nearly always ascending i. (Top-down memoization sidesteps this question entirely — the call stack figures the order out for you. That's a legitimate reason to submit the memoized version when the fill order is non-obvious, and it's a fine thing to say out loud.)
  5. What's the answer — and it's not always dp[n]. If your state is "considering the whole prefix," the answer is the last cell. If your state is "ending exactly at i," the best solution can end anywhere, so the answer is Math.max(...dp) — this is the #1 wrong answer in LIS and Maximum Subarray. Ask the question explicitly every single time. It costs three seconds and it saves the problem.
Answer all five aloud before coding and you will be graded as if you'd already solved it, because you have. The code is then a transcription. Candidates who fail DP rounds almost never fail on syntax — they fail because they started typing a dp array before they could say what a cell meant, and then spent the round fighting their own indices.
1D vs 2D. If one index isn't enough to describe your position in the problem — if you need "index i in string A and index j in string B," or "item i with capacity c remaining" — you need a 2D table. That's tutorial 13. Everything here needs exactly one index, which is what makes the space collapse to O(1) possible.

4. Take it or skip it

Here's the archetype of the entire topic. At each element you face a binary choice — take it or skip it — and taking it constrains what you may do next. The recurrence is always dp[i] = best(skip, take), where skip defers to dp[i-1] and take adds the current value to whatever state is legal after taking. Learn this shape and you have House Robber, Knapsack, Best Time to Buy and Sell Stock with Cooldown, and Delete and Earn.

House Robber (LC #198) MEDIUM ★

You're a burglar planning to rob houses along a street. Each house i holds nums[i] in cash. The one constraint: adjacent houses have connected security systems, so if you rob two houses next to each other, the alarm goes off. Given the array of house values, return the maximum amount you can rob without ever robbing two adjacent houses.

Example 1: Input: nums = [1, 2, 3, 1] → Output: 4 — rob house 0 (1) and house 2 (3). Robbing house 1 (2) and house 3 (1) only gets 3.
Example 2: Input: nums = [2, 7, 9, 3, 1] → Output: 12 — rob houses 0, 2, 4 → 2 + 9 + 1 = 12. Note the greedy "grab the biggest house first" instinct takes 9, then is forbidden from 7 and 3, and ends at 9 + 2 + 1 = 12 here by luck — but see the next example.
Example 3: Input: nums = [2, 1, 1, 2] → Output: 4 — rob houses 0 and 3. Here "biggest first" is genuinely ambiguous and the alternating-houses heuristic ([0,2] → 3, or [1,3] → 3) is wrong. You must actually consider both choices at every house.
Constraints: 1 ≤ nums.length ≤ 100; 0 ≤ nums[i] ≤ 400.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Brute force: for each house, either rob it or don't → 2ⁿ subsets, filter out the ones with adjacent picks, take the max. Too slow, and it's exponential for a reason we can exploit. Stand at house i and ask the last-choice question: either I rob house i, or I don't.

  • Skip house i — then my total is exactly the best I could do through house i-1, with no extra constraint: dp[i-1].
  • Rob house i — then I collect nums[i], but house i-1 is now off limits, so the rest of my haul is the best I could do through house i-2: nums[i] + dp[i-2].

Those two cases are exhaustive, so dp[i] = Math.max(dp[i-1], nums[i] + dp[i-2]). And here's the subtlety that makes this a medium and not an easy: "rob i" pairs with dp[i-2], not dp[i-1] — and it's dp[i-2], the best-through-house-i-2, not "the best that ends at house i-2." Because dp is a prefix maximum, dp[i-2] already includes the option of not robbing house i-2 either. You do not need a "did I rob the previous house?" flag; the recurrence's structure enforces the constraint for you. Base cases: dp[0] = nums[0], dp[1] = max(nums[0], nums[1]). Then rung 4: the recurrence touches two cells → two variables.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var rob = function(nums) {
  // STATE: dp[i] = max loot achievable considering houses 0..i (whether or not i is robbed).
  // RECURRENCE: dp[i] = max( dp[i-1],            // skip house i
  //                          nums[i] + dp[i-2] ) // rob house i, so i-1 is off limits
  // Only two cells are ever live → collapse to two variables (rung 4).
  let twoBack = 0;   // dp[i-2]; conceptually dp[-2] = 0 (no houses, no loot)
  let oneBack = 0;   // dp[i-1]; conceptually dp[-1] = 0

  for (const money of nums) {
    const current = Math.max(oneBack, money + twoBack);
    twoBack = oneBack;
    oneBack = current;
  }

  // Starting both at 0 makes the base cases fall out automatically:
  // house 0 computes max(0, nums[0] + 0) = nums[0]. No special-casing, no empty-array crash.
  return oneBack;
};

Trace on [2, 7, 9, 3, 1]:

  • money=2 → max(0, 2+0)=2 → (twoBack, oneBack) = (0, 2)
  • money=7 → max(2, 7+0)=7 → (2, 7)
  • money=9 → max(7, 9+2)=11 → (7, 11)  ← robbed 0 and 2
  • money=3 → max(11, 3+7)=11 → (11, 11)  ← skipping house 3 wins
  • money=1 → max(11, 1+11)=12 → (11, 12)

Return 12 ✓ — houses 0, 2, 4.

Complexity: O(n) time, single pass, O(1) work per house. O(1) space. The brute-force subset enumeration was O(2ⁿ · n); memoizing on i collapses it because there are only n distinct subproblems, each asking the same question: "best loot from house i onward/through house i."

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Which houses did you rob?" — keep the full dp array and walk backwards: at cell i, if dp[i] !== dp[i-1] then house i was robbed, so record it and jump to i-2; otherwise jump to i-1. Costs you O(n) space.
  • "The houses are in a circle." — that's House Robber II, next. Do not improvise; reduce.
  • "No two robberies within k houses of each other." — dp[i] = max(dp[i-1], nums[i] + dp[i-k-1]). Same shape, longer reach-back — so the space collapse now needs k+1 variables (or, honestly, just keep the array).
  • "What if values can be negative?" — then "rob nothing" must be an option; seed with 0 and the Math.max already handles it. The code above is already correct.
The tempting wrong recurrence is dp[i] = max(dp[i-1], dp[i-2]) + nums[i] — which forces you to rob every house. The nums[i] must sit inside the "take" branch only, because the whole point is that skipping is allowed. If your answer is always too big, this is why. Second trap: trying to track a boolean "did I rob the last house" alongside i. It works, but it's a 2D state where a 1D one suffices — the prefix-max definition of dp[i] already absorbs that information.

House Robber II (LC #213) MEDIUM ★

Same burglar, same alarm rule — but now the houses are arranged in a circle. The last house is adjacent to the first. So robbing house 0 means house n-1 is off limits, and vice versa. All other rules are identical: no two adjacent houses, maximize the loot.

Example 1: Input: nums = [2, 3, 2] → Output: 3 — you cannot rob houses 0 and 2 (2 + 2 = 4) because in a circle they're neighbours. The best is the lone house 1.
Example 2: Input: nums = [1, 2, 3, 1] → Output: 4 — rob houses 0 and 2. They're not adjacent even in the circle (n = 4), so the circular constraint doesn't bite here and the answer matches House Robber I.
Example 3: Input: nums = [1] → Output: 1 — the degenerate case. A single house is adjacent to itself in a circle, which is nonsense; you must special-case it or your slicing produces an empty array. Interviewers will feed you [1].
Constraints: 1 ≤ nums.length ≤ 100; 0 ≤ nums[i] ≤ 1000.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Resist the urge to invent a new circular recurrence with a "did I take the first house?" flag threaded through the loop. It's doable and it's a mess. Reduce the new problem to the one you already solved — that instinct is worth more in the interview than any recurrence on this page. The circular constraint is a single fact: houses 0 and n−1 cannot both be robbed. That means any valid plan falls into one of two buckets: it doesn't rob house n−1, or it doesn't rob house 0. (Plans that rob neither land in both buckets — that's fine, we're taking a max, so double-counting a candidate is harmless. This is the one line that makes the argument airtight, and it's worth saying.) Every valid plan is in at least one bucket, and within a bucket the circle is cut — it's a plain line of houses. So: run the linear House Robber on nums[0..n-2], run it again on nums[1..n-1], and take the larger. Two calls to code you already wrote. The only landmine is n === 1, where both slices are empty and you'd return 0 instead of nums[0] — guard it.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var rob = function(nums) {
  const n = nums.length;
  if (n === 1) return nums[0];   // a single house has no "circle" — both slices below would be empty

  // The two buckets: skip the LAST house, or skip the FIRST house.
  // Either way the circle is cut open and it's plain House Robber I.
  return Math.max(
    robLine(nums, 0, n - 2),   // houses 0..n-2  (house n-1 excluded → house 0 is safe to rob)
    robLine(nums, 1, n - 1)    // houses 1..n-1  (house 0 excluded → house n-1 is safe to rob)
  );
};

// House Robber I, but over an inclusive index range instead of the whole array —
// taking indices avoids allocating slices, which matters when the interviewer asks about space.
function robLine(nums, lo, hi) {
  let twoBack = 0, oneBack = 0;
  for (let i = lo; i <= hi; i++) {
    const current = Math.max(oneBack, nums[i] + twoBack);
    twoBack = oneBack;
    oneBack = current;
  }
  return oneBack;   // empty range (lo > hi) correctly returns 0
}

Trace on [2, 3, 2]: n=3. robLine(nums, 0, 1) over [2, 3] → max(2, 3) = 3. robLine(nums, 1, 2) over [3, 2] → max(3, 2) = 3. Math.max(3, 3) = 3 ✓. Neither slice can ever contain both house 0 and house 2, so the illegal 4 is unreachable by construction — the reduction, not a check, is what enforces the rule.

Complexity: O(n) time — two linear passes is still O(n); constants don't change the class, and say that explicitly rather than apologizing for it. O(1) space — robLine uses two accumulators and index bounds instead of slice(), so no array is copied.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Why is it correct to take the max of two overlapping cases?" — because the buckets are exhaustive (every legal plan omits house 0 or house n−1, since it can't rob both) and each bucket's optimum is achievable. Overlap is harmless under max. This is the question that separates people who understood the reduction from people who pattern-matched it.
  • "Now the houses form a binary tree." — that's House Robber III (LC 337): the same take-it-or-skip-it recurrence, but the DP runs on a post-order traversal and each node returns a pair [robbedThisNode, skippedThisNode]. Say the shape; you don't need to code it here.
  • "What if n === 2?" — the slices are [0,0] and [1,1], giving max(nums[0], nums[1]) ✓. Walk through it aloud; it's free credibility.
The transferable lesson here isn't circular arrays — it's reduction. When a problem is "X, but with one extra global constraint," the strongest move is to enumerate the small number of cases that constraint creates and solve each with the algorithm you already have. Say it: "Rather than write a new recurrence, I'll fix the constraint by case-splitting and reuse the linear solution twice." That is precisely how a working engineer handles a changed requirement, and interviewers hear it as such.

5. Unbounded choices

In House Robber the last choice was binary. Here it's a loop: at each state you try every coin, or every dictionary word, and take the best. The recurrence gets an inner for, the complexity picks up a factor, and the shape is otherwise identical. "Unbounded" means an item can be reused any number of times — which, perversely, makes the code simpler, because there's no "remaining supply" dimension to track.

Coin Change (LC #322) MEDIUM ★

You're given an array of distinct coin denominations coins and an integer amount. You have an unlimited supply of each denomination. Return the fewest number of coins that sum exactly to amount. If no combination of coins reaches the amount, return -1. An amount of 0 requires 0 coins.

Example 1: Input: coins = [1, 2, 5], amount = 11 → Output: 3 — 5 + 5 + 1. (Here greedy happens to work, which is exactly how it lulls you.)
Example 2: Input: coins = [1, 3, 4], amount = 6 → Output: 2 — 3 + 3. Greedy fails here. "Take the biggest coin that fits" grabs 4, leaving 2, which needs 1 + 1 → three coins total. The optimal answer skips the biggest coin entirely. Memorize this counterexample; it's the one you recite when the interviewer asks "why not just be greedy?"
Example 3: Input: coins = [2], amount = 3 → Output: -1 — parity makes it impossible. The "unreachable" case is a real branch, not a formality.
Constraints: 1 ≤ coins.length ≤ 12; 1 ≤ coins[i] ≤ 2³¹−1; 0 ≤ amount ≤ 10⁴.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

The greedy instinct is wrong, and it is wrong on purpose. With US/euro coin systems (1, 5, 10, 25) greedy does produce the optimum — those systems are deliberately designed ("canonical") so that cashiers can be greedy. LeetCode hands you arbitrary denominations, where that property evaporates. coins = [1, 3, 4], amount = 6: greedy says 4 + 1 + 1 = 3 coins; optimal is 3 + 3 = 2 coins. Taking the largest coin destroyed the answer, because it left a remainder that no large coin fits. Say this counterexample out loud before you write DP code — it proves you rejected greedy for a reason rather than never considering it.

Thinking process. Climb the ladder. Rung 1: the honest recursion asks "what's the fewest coins for amount?" and answers with the last choice — which coin did I place last? It was one of the coins in the list. If the last coin was c, then I needed fewest(amount - c) coins before it, plus this one. I don't know which c is right, so try them all and take the minimum: fewest(a) = 1 + min over c of fewest(a - c), for every c <= a. Base: fewest(0) = 0 (zero coins make zero). Unreachable: fewest(a) = Infinity when nothing works — using Infinity rather than -1 as the sentinel is the key coding trick, because Math.min and +1 both propagate it harmlessly, and you convert it to -1 only at the very end. Rung 2: that recursion re-derives fewest(6) from many different paths → memoize on a; there are only amount+1 distinct states. Rung 3: invert it — fill dp[0], dp[1], … dp[amount] ascending, since dp[a] only reads strictly smaller amounts, so they're always ready. Rung 4 does not apply: dp[a] reads dp[a - c] for coins that can be as large as amount, so the whole table is live. Recognizing that a problem can't collapse to O(1) is just as important as collapsing the ones that can — don't force it.

Solution (JS) — O(amount × coins) time · O(amount) space 
var coinChange = function(coins, amount) {
  // STATE: dp[a] = fewest coins summing exactly to a. Infinity = unreachable.
  const dp = new Array(amount + 1).fill(Infinity);

  dp[0] = 0;   // BASE: it takes zero coins to make zero. Everything is built on this cell.

  // ORDER: ascending amounts — dp[a] only reads SMALLER amounts, so they're already final.
  for (let a = 1; a <= amount; a++) {
    // RECURRENCE: try every coin as the LAST coin placed, keep the best.
    for (const coin of coins) {
      if (coin > a) continue;              // this coin overshoots — not a legal last coin
      if (dp[a - coin] === Infinity) continue;  // the remainder is itself unreachable; skip
      dp[a] = Math.min(dp[a], dp[a - coin] + 1);  // +1 for the coin we just placed
    }
  }

  // ANSWER: dp[amount], with Infinity translated to the -1 the problem demands.
  return dp[amount] === Infinity ? -1 : dp[amount];
};

Trace on coins = [1, 3, 4], amount = 6 (the greedy counterexample — watch DP find the answer greedy can't):

  • dp[0] = 0
  • dp[1]: only coin 1 fits → dp[0] + 1 = 1
  • dp[2]: coin 1 → dp[1] + 1 = 2
  • dp[3]: coin 1 → dp[2]+1 = 3; coin 3 → dp[0]+1 = 1 → min = 1
  • dp[4]: coin 1 → dp[3]+1 = 2; coin 3 → dp[1]+1 = 2; coin 4 → dp[0]+1 = 1 → 1
  • dp[5]: coin 1 → dp[4]+1 = 2; coin 3 → dp[2]+1 = 3; coin 4 → dp[1]+1 = 2 → 2
  • dp[6]: coin 1 → dp[5]+1 = 3; coin 3 → dp[3]+1 = 2; coin 4 → dp[2]+1 = 3 → 2

Return 2 ✓ (3 + 3). The DP considered "last coin = 4" and rejected it, because it also considered "last coin = 3" and that path was cheaper. Greedy never got to compare.

Complexity: O(amount × coins.length) time — the outer loop runs amount times and the inner loop runs once per denomination. With the given constraints that's 10⁴ × 12 = 120,000 operations, trivial. O(amount) space for the table. Note the table size depends on the numeric value of the input, not the input's length — that makes this pseudo-polynomial, and naming that term correctly is a genuine senior flex if the interviewer probes complexity. (Knapsack has the same property.)

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Return the actual coins, not just the count." — keep a parallel from[a] = coin array recording which coin won at each cell, then walk backwards from amount, subtracting.
  • "Count the number of combinations that make the amount" — that's Coin Change II (LC 518), and it is not a one-line change: to count each combination once (not each permutation), the coin loop must be the outer loop and the amount loop the inner one. Loop order is the whole problem. Covered in tutorial 13.
  • "Why not greedy?" — [1, 3, 4], amount 6. Have it loaded.
  • "Solve it with BFS." — legitimate and worth mentioning: treat amounts as graph nodes with an edge a → a + coin; the fewest coins is the shortest path from 0 to amount, so BFS finds it level by level. Same complexity, different framing — and it shows you see that "minimum number of steps" DP and BFS shortest-path are the same animal (tutorial 10).

Word Break (LC #139) MEDIUM ★

Given a string s and a dictionary of strings wordDict, return true if s can be segmented into a space-separated sequence of one or more dictionary words. The same dictionary word may be reused as many times as you like, and you don't have to use every word in the dictionary. You are not asked to produce the segmentation — only whether one exists.

Example 1: Input: s = "leetcode", wordDict = ["leet", "code"] → Output: true — "leet code".
Example 2: Input: s = "applepenapple", wordDict = ["apple", "pen"] → Output: true — "apple pen apple"; note "apple" is reused.
Example 3: Input: s = "catsandog", wordDict = ["cats", "dog", "sand", "and", "cat"] → Output: false — and this one is the whole lesson. A greedy left-to-right match grabs "cats", then "and", then is stranded at "og". But the correct failure requires also trying "cat" → "sand" → "og", which also fails. Only after exploring both prefix choices may you answer false. Greedy would have to backtrack; DP just remembers.
Constraints: 1 ≤ s.length ≤ 300; 1 ≤ wordDict.length ≤ 1000; 1 ≤ wordDict[i].length ≤ 20; all dictionary words are unique.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. The brute force is backtracking: try every dictionary word as the prefix of s; if one matches, recurse on the remaining suffix. That's exponential — and crucially, it revisits the same suffix from many different prefix decompositions. In Example 3, the suffix "og" is reached via "cats"+"and" and via "cat"+"sand", and both times you re-derive that it's unsegmentable. Overlapping subproblems, verbatim. So: the state is a position in the string — and that's the leap worth practicing, because "the state is an index into s" is not obvious the first time. Define dp[i] = true if the prefix s[0..i) — the first i characters — can be segmented. Then ask the last-choice question: what was the final word? If the last word ends at i and starts at some j, then two things must hold: the prefix s[0..j) is itself segmentable (dp[j]), and the chunk s[j..i) is a dictionary word. Try every split point j < i: dp[i] = OR over j of ( dp[j] && wordSet.has(s.slice(j, i)) ). Base case: dp[0] = true — the empty prefix is trivially segmentable (zero words), and everything else is built off that single true. Answer: dp[s.length]. Put the dictionary in a Set so the membership test is O(1) rather than a scan.

Solution (JS) — O(n² · L) time · O(n + dict) space 
var wordBreak = function(s, wordDict) {
  const words = new Set(wordDict);         // O(1) membership instead of scanning the list
  const n = s.length;

  // STATE: dp[i] = can the FIRST i characters, s[0..i), be segmented?
  // Length n+1 because i ranges over 0..n (a prefix length, not an index).
  const dp = new Array(n + 1).fill(false);

  dp[0] = true;   // BASE: the empty prefix is segmentable (into zero words). The seed for everything.

  // ORDER: ascending prefix length — dp[i] reads dp[j] for j < i, already final.
  for (let i = 1; i <= n; i++) {
    // RECURRENCE: try every possible START j of the LAST word ending at i.
    for (let j = 0; j < i; j++) {
      // The prefix before the last word must itself be segmentable,
      // AND the last chunk must be a real word.
      if (dp[j] && words.has(s.slice(j, i))) {
        dp[i] = true;
        break;   // one valid split is enough — this is an OR, so stop early
      }
    }
  }

  return dp[n];   // ANSWER: is the whole string segmentable?
};

Trace on s = "leetcode", wordDict = ["leet", "code"]: dp[0] = true. For i = 1..3, no j gives both dp[j] and a dictionary chunk ("l", "le", "lee" aren't words) → all false. At i = 4: j = 0 → dp[0] = true and s.slice(0,4) = "leet" ∈ dict → dp[4] = true. i = 5..7: the only true earlier cells are dp[0] and dp[4], and neither "leetc"/"c", "leetco"/"co", "leetcod"/"cod" is a word → false. At i = 8: j = 4 → dp[4] = true and s.slice(4,8) = "code" ∈ dict → dp[8] = true. Return true ✓.

Complexity: O(n²) iterations of the double loop, each doing an O(L) slice plus an O(L) string hash for the Set lookup, where L is the chunk length — so O(n² · L) time, commonly written O(n³) for the worst case where chunks are O(n). Space: O(n) for the table plus O(total dictionary characters) for the Set. The optimization worth naming: a chunk can never be longer than the longest dictionary word (max 20 here), so bound the inner loop to j >= i - maxWordLen and the time drops to O(n · maxWordLen · L). Interviewers like that you noticed the inner loop is doing provably pointless work.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Return all possible segmentations." — that's Word Break II (LC 140). Now DP alone is insufficient: the number of answers can be exponential, so you backtrack, using this dp array as a pruning oracle ("don't explore this suffix, we've proven it's dead"). The combination of DP-for-feasibility plus backtracking-for-enumeration is a very strong thing to articulate.
  • "Solve it top-down." — canBreak(i) memoized on the start index; equally accepted, and it's a cleaner mirror of the backtracking brute force you started from. Show you can do either.
  • "The dictionary is huge and words share prefixes." — a Trie replaces the Set, and you walk the Trie character by character from j, which removes the slice allocations and abandons impossible chunks early. Tutorial 14.
  • "Why not just greedily match the longest word at each position?" — Example 3, and also s = "aaaaab", dict = ["aaaa", "aaa", "b"]: longest-first takes "aaaa" and strands "ab"; the right split is "aaa" + "aa"… have a counterexample ready, always.
The dp[0] = true pattern is universal. In Climbing Stairs it was dp[0] = 1 ("one way to do nothing"); here it's dp[0] = true ("the empty string is segmentable"); in Coin Change it's dp[0] = 0 ("zero coins make zero"). All three are the same idea — the empty solution is a valid solution — and all three are the cell every other cell ultimately depends on. When a DP returns 0/false for everything, check this cell first.

6. Subsequences

Both problems here use the other state shape from §3, question 1: dp[i] = the best answer for a subsequence/subarray that ends exactly at index i. That definition is forced on you, and understanding why is the real content. Its consequence: the answer is Math.max(...dp), not dp[n-1], because the winning run can end anywhere.

Longest Increasing Subsequence (LC #300) MEDIUM ★

Given an integer array nums, return the length of the longest strictly increasing subsequence. A subsequence is formed by deleting zero or more elements without changing the order of the remaining ones — it does not have to be contiguous. Strictly increasing means each element is greater than the one before it; equal values cannot both be used.

Example 1: Input: nums = [10, 9, 2, 5, 3, 7, 101, 18] → Output: 4 — the subsequence [2, 3, 7, 101] (or [2, 3, 7, 18]; ties are fine, you return the length).
Example 2: Input: nums = [0, 1, 0, 3, 2, 3] → Output: 4 — [0, 1, 2, 3]. Note the picked elements are at indices 0, 1, 4, 5 — non-contiguous, and the second 0 is skipped. Contiguity is not required and assuming it is a fatal misread.
Example 3: Input: nums = [7, 7, 7, 7] → Output: 1 — strictly increasing, so duplicates can't chain. The answer is a single element.
Constraints: 1 ≤ nums.length ≤ 2500; −10⁴ ≤ nums[i] ≤ 10⁴. Follow-up on LeetCode itself: can you do it in O(n log n)?

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Brute force: enumerate all 2ⁿ subsequences, keep the increasing ones, take the longest. Hopeless. Now try the state you'd reflexively reach for — dp[i] = "the LIS length using the first i elements" — and watch it break: to extend a subsequence you need to know what its last value was, and that prefix-max state has thrown that away. This failure is the whole lesson. So change the state to carry the missing fact: dp[i] = the length of the longest increasing subsequence that ends exactly at index i. Now the last value is pinned — it's nums[i] — and the recurrence writes itself. To end at i, the previous element of my subsequence was some earlier index j with nums[j] < nums[i] (so the chain stays increasing), and the best I could do is whatever that chain was worth plus one: dp[i] = 1 + max over valid j of dp[j], defaulting to 1 (the element alone). Every dp[i] starts at 1 because a single element is always an increasing subsequence of length 1 — that handles Example 3 for free. And the answer is Math.max(...dp), not dp[n-1]: the longest chain need not end at the last element. In Example 1 it ends at index 6, not 7. This is the mistake to not make.

Solution (JS) — O(n²) time · O(n) space (plus the O(n log n) follow-up)

The O(n²) DP — this is the expected answer, and submitting it confidently beats fumbling the clever one:

var lengthOfLIS = function(nums) {
  const n = nums.length;

  // STATE: dp[i] = length of the longest increasing subsequence ENDING EXACTLY at index i.
  // BASE: every element alone is a valid subsequence of length 1.
  const dp = new Array(n).fill(1);

  let best = 1;   // n >= 1 per constraints, so the answer is at least 1

  for (let i = 1; i < n; i++) {
    // RECURRENCE: which earlier element could come right before nums[i] in the chain?
    for (let j = 0; j < i; j++) {
      if (nums[j] < nums[i]) {              // STRICTLY less — '<=' would allow duplicates through
        dp[i] = Math.max(dp[i], dp[j] + 1);   // extend j's best chain with nums[i]
      }
    }
    best = Math.max(best, dp[i]);           // ANSWER: the chain can end ANYWHERE, so track a running max
  }

  return best;   // NOT dp[n-1] — that's only the best chain ending at the LAST element
};

Trace on [10, 9, 2, 5, 3, 7, 101, 18]:

  • dp[0] = 1 (10)
  • dp[1] = 1 (9 — nothing before it is smaller)
  • dp[2] = 1 (2 — nothing smaller before it)
  • dp[3] = 2 (5 — extends 2)
  • dp[4] = 2 (3 — extends 2)
  • dp[5] = 3 (7 — extends 5 or 3, both give dp=2 → 3)
  • dp[6] = 4 (101 — extends the chain ending at 7)
  • dp[7] = 4 (18 — extends the chain ending at 7; it can't follow 101)

dp = [1,1,1,2,2,3,4,4], max = 4 ✓. Notice dp[7] = 4 too — a tie — but if the array had ended …, 101, 18, 1 then dp[n-1] would be 1 and returning it would be catastrophically wrong. Always take the max.

Complexity: O(n²) time — for each i you scan every earlier j. O(n) space. With n ≤ 2500, that's ~3 million operations: comfortably fast. O(n²) is the expected answer at this tier — get it working, state the complexity, and then offer the improvement.

The O(n log n) follow-up (patience sorting). Offer it verbally; code it only if asked, because it's easy to botch and it is not what you're being graded on:

var lengthOfLIS = function(nums) {
  // tails[k] = the SMALLEST possible tail value of an increasing subsequence of length k+1.
  // Key insight: tails is always sorted ascending, so we can BINARY SEARCH it.
  // It is NOT itself a valid subsequence — only its LENGTH is meaningful.
  const tails = [];

  for (const x of nums) {
    // lowerBound: first index whose value is >= x  (the boundary template from tutorial 05)
    let lo = 0, hi = tails.length;
    while (lo < hi) {
      const mid = lo + ((hi - lo) >> 1);
      if (tails[mid] >= x) hi = mid;
      else lo = mid + 1;
    }

    if (lo === tails.length) tails.push(x);   // x beats every tail → it EXTENDS the longest chain
    else tails[lo] = x;                        // x is a better (smaller) tail for a chain of length lo+1
  }

  return tails.length;   // the length of the longest chain we ever built
};

Why it works, in one sentence you can actually say: "Keeping the smallest possible tail for each achievable length never hurts — a smaller tail can be extended by strictly more future elements — so greedily overwriting the first tail ≥ x keeps every length achievable while making future extensions as easy as possible." The binary search is exactly the lowerBound boundary template from tutorial 05 — same four lines, different predicate. O(n log n) time, O(n) space.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Print the actual subsequence." — with the O(n²) DP, store a parent[i] pointer to the j that won, then walk back from the argmax. (Reconstructing from the tails array is possible but fiddly — it needs a separate index-tracking array — and that's a fine thing to say out loud as a reason to prefer the O(n²) version when reconstruction is required.)
  • "Non-decreasing instead of strictly increasing." — change nums[j] < nums[i] to <= in the DP; in the binary-search version, switch lowerBound to upperBound (> instead of >=). One character, and knowing exactly which one is the flex.
  • "Number of longest increasing subsequences" (LC 673) — carry a parallel count[i] alongside dp[i]. Same loop, two arrays.
  • "Russian Doll Envelopes" (LC 354) — sort by width ascending, height descending on ties, then run LIS on the heights. It's this problem wearing a hat, and the descending tiebreak is the whole trick.
Two lethal misreads. (1) Subsequence ≠ subarray. Subsequence allows gaps; subarray must be contiguous. LIS is a subsequence problem; Maximum Subarray (next) is not, and the recurrences differ entirely because of it. (2) dp[i] here means "ending at i", so the answer is Math.max(...dp). Returning dp[n-1] passes the given examples by coincidence and fails the moment the array ends on a small number.

Maximum Subarray / Kadane's (LC #53) MEDIUM ★

Given an integer array nums, find the contiguous subarray (containing at least one number) with the largest sum, and return that sum. The array may contain negative numbers — that's the entire difficulty; with all-positive numbers the answer is trivially the whole array.

Example 1: Input: nums = [-2, 1, -3, 4, -1, 2, 1, -5, 4] → Output: 6 — the subarray [4, -1, 2, 1]. Note it includes a negative number: the −1 is worth carrying because the run around it is strong enough.
Example 2: Input: nums = [-3, -1, -2] → Output: -1 — every number is negative, so the best you can do is the single least-bad element. This is the case that breaks the "just reset to 0 when the sum goes negative" folk version of Kadane's, which would return 0 — an empty subarray, which the problem forbids.
Example 3: Input: nums = [5, 4, -1, 7, 8] → Output: 23 — the whole array; the −1 never makes the running sum worse than starting fresh.
Constraints: 1 ≤ nums.length ≤ 10⁵; −10⁴ ≤ nums[i] ≤ 10⁴. Follow-up in the problem itself: solve it with divide and conquer too.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Brute force: every (start, end) pair, sum it → O(n³), or O(n²) with a running sum. The DP framing that kills it is exactly the LIS framing — and this is why these two problems live in the same section. Define dp[i] = the largest sum of a subarray that ends exactly at index i. Why "ends exactly at"? Because a subarray must be contiguous, so the only question at index i is: does the best run ending here extend the best run ending at i-1, or does it start fresh at i? Those are the only two options — there is no third — which gives dp[i] = Math.max(nums[i], nums[i] + dp[i-1]). Read it out loud: "either I'm a brand-new subarray starting here, or I'm the previous best run with me appended." Note the corollary: you extend the previous run whenever dp[i-1] > 0 and abandon it whenever dp[i-1] <= 0, because a negative-sum prefix can only drag you down — that's the real content of Kadane's, and it falls out of the recurrence rather than being an incantation. The answer, once more, is Math.max(...dp), since the best subarray can end anywhere.

Now the punchline. dp[i] reads only dp[i-1]. Apply rung 4: replace the whole table with one variable. What you're left with is two lines of code that people call "Kadane's algorithm" and teach as a magic trick — and it is nothing but this DP with the table collapsed. You did not need to know Kadane's name. You derived it. That retroactively explains what rung 4 has been doing on every problem in this tutorial: the famous O(1)-space "clever" solutions are all just DP tables that got collapsed, and the cleverness was in the recurrence all along.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var maxSubArray = function(nums) {
  // STATE:      dp[i] = max sum of a subarray ENDING EXACTLY at i.
  // RECURRENCE: dp[i] = max(nums[i], nums[i] + dp[i-1])
  //             -> "start fresh at i" vs "extend the best run ending at i-1"
  // RUNG 4:     dp[i] only reads dp[i-1], so one variable replaces the whole table.
  let endingHere = nums[0];   // dp[0]: a subarray ending at 0 can only BE nums[0]
  let best = nums[0];         // ANSWER: max over ALL dp[i] — seed with dp[0], NOT with 0

  for (let i = 1; i < nums.length; i++) {
    endingHere = Math.max(nums[i], nums[i] + endingHere);
    best = Math.max(best, endingHere);   // the winning run can end anywhere — check every i
  }

  return best;
};

Trace on [-2, 1, -3, 4, -1, 2, 1, -5, 4]:

  • i=0: endingHere = -2, best = -2
  • i=1 (1): max(1, 1 + (-2)) = 1 → start fresh; best = 1
  • i=2 (−3): max(-3, -3 + 1) = -2 → extend; best = 1
  • i=3 (4): max(4, 4 + (-2)) = 4 → start fresh (the negative prefix was dead weight); best = 4
  • i=4 (−1): max(-1, -1 + 4) = 3 → extend; best = 4
  • i=5 (2): max(2, 2 + 3) = 5 → extend; best = 5
  • i=6 (1): max(1, 1 + 5) = 6 → extend; best = 6
  • i=7 (−5): max(-5, -5 + 6) = 1 → extend; best = 6
  • i=8 (4): max(4, 4 + 1) = 5 → extend; best = 6

Return 6 ✓ — the run [4, -1, 2, 1], exactly the i=3..6 stretch.

Complexity: O(n) time, one pass. O(1) space — one accumulator and one max. This is why the constraint says n ≤ 10⁵: an O(n²) prefix-sum brute force would be 10¹⁰ operations and time out.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Return the indices of the subarray, not just the sum." — track a start that resets to i whenever you choose "start fresh," and commit [start, i] to the answer whenever endingHere beats best. Very commonly asked as the immediate follow-up; practice it.
  • "Do it with divide and conquer." — split in half; the max subarray is entirely left, entirely right, or crosses the midpoint (compute the best suffix of the left plus the best prefix of the right in O(n)). T(n) = 2T(n/2) + O(n) = O(n log n) — strictly worse than Kadane's, but LeetCode explicitly asks for it, so know the shape.
  • "Maximum product subarray" (LC 152) — the sequel, and it's genuinely harder: a big negative times a negative becomes a big positive, so you must carry both the running max and the running min. Same one-pass shape, two accumulators.
  • "Circular maximum subarray" (LC 918) — the House Robber II move: the answer is either the plain Kadane max, or totalSum − (minimum subarray sum). Watch the all-negative edge case, where the second formula yields an empty array.
If you take one sentence from this page into your loop, take this one: "Kadane's algorithm is just this DP with the table collapsed to a single variable." Interviewers hear that and immediately reclassify you from "memorized the trick" to "understands the derivation." The same sentence is true of Climbing Stairs vs. Fibonacci, and of House Robber's two accumulators. Every famous O(1)-space trick on this page is a DP table that got collapsed.

7. Strings & palindromes

Decode Ways (LC #91) MEDIUM

A message of letters A–Z was encoded to digits using the mapping 'A' → "1", 'B' → "2", …, 'Z' → "26", and then the spaces were thrown away. Given the resulting digit string s, return the number of ways to decode it. The catch is that the grouping is ambiguous: "11106" can be split as 1 11 06 (invalid — 06 is not a legal code) or 111 06 (also invalid) or 1 1 10 6 (valid) or 11 10 6 (valid). A code may never have a leading zero: "06" is not 'F', it is nothing at all.

Example 1: Input: s = "12" → Output: 2 — "1 2" (AB) or "12" (L).
Example 2: Input: s = "226" → Output: 3 — 2 2 6 (BBF), 22 6 (VF), 2 26 (BZ).
Example 3: Input: s = "06" → Output: 0 — a leading zero kills it. There is no valid decoding at all, and returning 1 here (or 2) is the classic failure.
Constraints: 1 ≤ s.length ≤ 100; s contains only digits and may contain leading zeros.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Structurally this is Climbing Stairs — at each position the last "move" consumed either one digit or two digits, so dp[i] = dp[i-1] + dp[i-2], where dp[i] = the number of ways to decode the first i characters. But each of those two terms is conditional on the digits being legal, and the '0' rules are where every candidate bleeds. Enumerate them once, carefully, and never think about them again:

  • The one-digit move (take s[i-1] alone) is legal iff s[i-1] !== '0'. There is no letter for 0. Codes run 1–26; zero is not among them.
  • The two-digit move (take s[i-2..i)) is legal iff that two-digit number is between 10 and 26 inclusive. The lower bound of 10 is what rejects "06" — it's numerically 6, which is ≤ 26, but the leading zero makes it an invalid code. Checking only num <= 26 is the single most common bug in this problem.
  • A '0' can only survive as the second digit of a 10 or a 20. Everything else about zeros follows from the two rules above. "30" → 0 ways (3 can't take a 0 as one digit, and 30 > 26). "100" → 0 ways (the trailing 0 has no legal parse). "0" → 0 ways.
  • If both moves are illegal, dp[i] = 0, and that zero correctly poisons everything downstream — an unparseable prefix means no parse of the whole string can exist. You don't need an early return; the arithmetic handles it.

Base cases: dp[0] = 1 (one way to decode the empty string — the same "the empty solution is a solution" seed as always), and dp[1] = s[0] === '0' ? 0 : 1. Then rung 4: only two cells are ever live → two variables.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var numDecodings = function(s) {
  const n = s.length;
  if (s[0] === '0') return 0;   // a leading zero can never start a valid code — bail immediately

  // STATE: dp[i] = number of ways to decode the first i characters, s[0..i).
  // Collapsed to two variables because dp[i] reads only dp[i-1] and dp[i-2].
  let twoBack = 1;   // dp[0] = 1: exactly ONE way to decode the empty string (do nothing)
  let oneBack = 1;   // dp[1] = 1: s[0] is non-zero (guarded above), so one single-digit decode

  for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) {
    let current = 0;

    // MOVE A — take one digit, s[i-1]. Legal iff it isn't '0'.
    if (s[i - 1] !== '0') {
      current += oneBack;
    }

    // MOVE B — take two digits, s[i-2..i). Legal iff 10 <= value <= 26.
    // The >= 10 check is what rejects "06": leading zeros are not valid codes.
    const twoDigit = Number(s.slice(i - 2, i));
    if (twoDigit >= 10 && twoDigit <= 26) {
      current += twoBack;
    }

    // If BOTH moves were illegal, current stays 0 — and that 0 correctly
    // propagates forward: an unparseable prefix has no valid extensions.
    twoBack = oneBack;
    oneBack = current;
  }

  return oneBack;   // dp[n]
};

Trace on "226": twoBack = dp[0] = 1, oneBack = dp[1] = 1 ("2" → B).
i=2: one-digit s[1] = '2' ≠ '0' → current += dp[1] = 1. Two-digit "22" is in [10,26] → current += dp[0] = 1. So dp[2] = 2 ("2 2", "22"). Slide → (1, 2).
i=3: one-digit s[2] = '6' ≠ '0' → current += dp[2] = 2. Two-digit "26" is in [10,26] → current += dp[1] = 1. dp[3] = 3 ✓ — BBF, VF, BZ.

Trace on "100" (the zero-trap): guard passes ('1'). dp[0]=1, dp[1]=1.
i=2: s[1] = '0' → move A illegal. "10" ∈ [10,26] → move B legal, current = dp[0] = 1. dp[2] = 1.
i=3: s[2] = '0' → move A illegal. "00" = 0, below 10 → move B illegal. current = 0. Return 0 ✓ — correct, and it required no special case. The two rules did all the work.

Complexity: O(n) time, one pass with O(1) work per character. O(1) space. (The slice of a fixed 2 characters is O(1); if you want zero allocation, compute (s.charCodeAt(i-2) - 48) * 10 + (s.charCodeAt(i-1) - 48) instead.)

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "What if the string contains '*' as a wildcard digit 1–9?" — that's Decode Ways II (LC 639), and it's a HARD: each branch multiplies by the number of digits the wildcard could be (9 ways for a lone *, 15 for "**"…), with a modulus. Same recurrence, brutal case analysis. Name it and move on.
  • "Enumerate the decodings, don't count them." — that's backtracking (tutorial 09); the count can be exponential, so no DP can enumerate in polynomial time.
  • "Walk me through "06", "10", "30", "27"." — 0, 1, 0, 1. They will probe the zero cases. Rehearse them; this problem is graded almost entirely on edge-case discipline rather than on the recurrence.
Three specific ways this problem kills people. (1) Checking twoDigit <= 26 without >= 10 — "06" then counts as a valid 'F'. (2) Forgetting the leading-zero guard on s[0], so "0" returns 1. (3) Setting dp[0] = 0 instead of 1, which zeroes out every count in the table. Each one is a single character, and each one fails a hidden test.

Longest Palindromic Substring (LC #5) MEDIUM ★

Given a string s, return the longest substring of s that is a palindrome — reads the same forwards and backwards. Substring means contiguous. If several palindromes tie for longest, any one of them is accepted.

Example 1: Input: s = "babad" → Output: "bab" — "aba" is equally valid; both are length 3.
Example 2: Input: s = "cbbd" → Output: "bb" — an even-length palindrome, with no single centre character. Any approach that only considers single-character centres misses this entirely, which is why the even case is the one to get right.
Example 3: Input: s = "ac" → Output: "a" — no palindrome longer than one character exists, and a single character always qualifies.
Constraints: 1 ≤ s.length ≤ 1000; s consists of digits and English letters.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. The brute force checks all O(n²) substrings and verifies each in O(n) → O(n³). The DP fix is real: define isPal[i][j] = "is s[i..j] a palindrome?", with the recurrence isPal[i][j] = (s[i] === s[j]) && isPal[i+1][j-1], filled by increasing substring length. That's O(n²) time and O(n²) space, and it's a perfectly good answer. But it is not the best answer, and you should say so.

Flip the question around. Instead of asking "is this substring a palindrome?" for every substring, ask: "where could a palindrome be centred?" Every palindrome grows outward symmetrically from a centre. There are exactly 2n − 1 possible centres — n single characters (odd-length palindromes like "aba") and n − 1 gaps between adjacent characters (even-length palindromes like "abba"). From each centre, walk two pointers outward while the characters match. That's expand around centre: O(n²) time (2n−1 centres × up to O(n) expansion) and O(1) space — it beats the DP on space and it's shorter to write.

Expand-around-centre is not dynamic programming, and knowing when not to reach for DP is itself senior signal. Here is the sentence: "The DP is O(n²) time and O(n²) space; expanding around each of the 2n−1 centres is the same O(n²) time but O(1) space, so I'll do that instead. There's also Manacher's algorithm at O(n), but it's rarely worth the implementation risk in an interview." That single paragraph — presenting the DP, rejecting it with a reason, and naming the theoretical optimum without pretending you'd write it — is a stronger answer than a flawless DP table. Candidates who reach for DP reflexively because the problem looks like a DP problem are pattern-matching, not thinking, and the difference is exactly what a 7-YOE loop is grading.
Solution (JS) — O(n²) time · O(1) space (expand around centre) 
var longestPalindrome = function(s) {
  if (s.length < 2) return s;   // "" and single chars are trivially palindromes

  let start = 0, maxLen = 1;   // best found so far: s.slice(start, start + maxLen)

  // Expand outward from a centre while the two ends match.
  // Called with (i, i) for odd-length centres and (i, i+1) for even-length ones.
  const expand = (left, right) => {
    while (left >= 0 && right < s.length && s[left] === s[right]) {
      left--;
      right++;
    }
    // The loop exits one step PAST the palindrome, so the real bounds are
    // [left + 1, right - 1] and the length is (right - 1) - (left + 1) + 1 = right - left - 1.
    const len = right - left - 1;
    if (len > maxLen) {
      maxLen = len;
      start = left + 1;
    }
  };

  for (let i = 0; i < s.length; i++) {
    expand(i, i);       // ODD centre:  "aba" — centred ON character i
    expand(i, i + 1);   // EVEN centre: "abba" — centred BETWEEN i and i+1
  }

  return s.slice(start, start + maxLen);
};

Trace on "cbbd": i=0: expand(0,0) → "c", len 1 (no improvement over maxLen 1). expand(0,1) → 'c' ≠ 'b', loop never runs, len = 1 − 0 − 1 = 0.
i=1: expand(1,1) → "b", len 1. expand(1,2) → s[1]='b' === s[2]='b' ✓ → left=0, right=3; s[0]='c' ≠ s[3]='d' → stop. len = 3 − 0 − 1 = 2 > 1 → maxLen=2, start = 0 + 1 = 1.
i=2, i=3: no expansion beats length 2.
Return s.slice(1, 3) = "bb" ✓ — the even-length case, caught by the expand(i, i+1) call that a naive solution omits.

Complexity: O(n²) time — 2n−1 centres, each expanding at most O(n) steps (a string of all identical characters, "aaaa…", is the worst case and every centre expands fully). O(1) extra space — just the two pointers and the two result variables; the output slice isn't counted as working space. Compare: the isPal[i][j] DP is the same O(n²) time but needs an n×n boolean table — 10⁶ cells at n = 1000, for no speed benefit.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Can you do better than O(n²)?" — yes: Manacher's algorithm is O(n). It works by reusing the expansion radii already computed inside a known palindrome, mirroring them across the centre. Naming it and explaining the mirror idea in one sentence is expected; implementing it is not, and at the enterprise tier nobody will make you.
  • "Return the count of palindromic substrings instead." — LC 647, immediately below. Same expansion, different bookkeeping — and interviewers love asking these back to back precisely because the second one is free if you understood the first.
  • "Longest palindromic subsequence" (LC 516) — completely different problem, and that one genuinely is DP: it's Longest Common Subsequence between s and its reverse. The word "substring" vs "subsequence" changes the algorithm entirely. Tutorial 13.

Palindromic Substrings (LC #647) MEDIUM ★

Given a string s, return the number of palindromic substrings in it. Substrings are counted by position, not by content: two substrings that are identical as strings but occupy different index ranges count as two. Every single character is itself a palindrome, so the answer is always at least s.length.

Example 1: Input: s = "abc" → Output: 3 — "a", "b", "c". No multi-character palindromes exist.
Example 2: Input: s = "aaa" → Output: 6 — "a" ×3 (at indices 0, 1, 2), "aa" ×2 (at 0–1 and 1–2), and "aaa" ×1. The two "aa"s are the same string but different substrings — that's the counting rule people get wrong.
Constraints: 1 ≤ s.length ≤ 1000; s consists of lowercase English letters.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. This is LC 5 with the bookkeeping changed, and recognizing that instantly is the point of putting the two problems side by side. Same 2n−1 centres, same outward walk — but instead of tracking the longest expansion, you count every successful expansion step. Here's the key observation that makes it a one-liner: every time the two pointers match and you take another step outward, you have discovered exactly one new palindromic substring. A centre that expands k times contributes k palindromes, and every palindromic substring in s has exactly one centre — so summing over centres counts each one exactly once, with no double-counting and no set needed. So: increment a counter inside the expansion loop, and you're done.

Solution (JS) — O(n²) time · O(1) space 
var countSubstrings = function(s) {
  let count = 0;

  // Each successful match-and-step-outward IS one distinct palindromic substring.
  // Every palindrome has exactly one centre, so no substring is counted twice.
  const expand = (left, right) => {
    while (left >= 0 && right < s.length && s[left] === s[right]) {
      count++;     // s[left..right] is a palindrome — that's the whole algorithm
      left--;
      right++;
    }
  };

  for (let i = 0; i < s.length; i++) {
    expand(i, i);       // odd-length centres  ("a", "aba", …)
    expand(i, i + 1);   // even-length centres ("aa", "abba", …)
  }

  return count;
};

Trace on "aaa":

  • expand(0,0): "a" ✓ (count 1); then left=−1 → stop.
  • expand(0,1): "aa" ✓ (count 2); then left=−1 → stop.
  • expand(1,1): "a" ✓ (3); expand to s[0..2] = "aaa" ✓ (4); then left=−1 → stop.
  • expand(1,2): "aa" ✓ (5) — the other "aa", at indices 1–2; then left=0, right=3 → right out of bounds → stop.
  • expand(2,2): "a" ✓ (6); then right=3 → stop.
  • expand(2,3): right = 3 is out of bounds → 0.

Return 6 ✓. Every one of the 6 substrings was found from exactly one centre.

Complexity: O(n²) time — 2n−1 centres × O(n) worst-case expansion, hit exactly by "aaaa…" where every centre expands to the string's edge. O(1) space. The isPal[i][j] DP alternative is again O(n²) time but O(n²) space — same trade, same verdict.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Count only distinct palindromic substrings." — a genuinely different problem: add a Set of the matched strings (easy, O(n³) worst case on string hashing), or reach for Eertree / suffix automaton (nobody will make you). Notice that the moment "distinct" appears, the count-by-centre argument collapses — this is a good example of a one-word change that invalidates the whole approach.
  • "Partition the string into palindromic pieces" — Palindrome Partitioning (LC 131) is backtracking (tutorial 09), typically with a precomputed isPal table for pruning. This is where the O(n²) DP table earns its keep, so mention it: the table you rejected for LC 5 is the right tool there.
  • "Why doesn't every palindrome get counted twice?" — because a palindrome's centre is unique (determined by its parity and its midpoint), so each is found from exactly one of the 2n−1 centres. Be ready to say it; it's the correctness argument, and it's the only real thinking in the problem.
Both palindrome problems share a single expand(left, right) helper called twice per index. If you internalize "there are 2n − 1 centres: n characters and n − 1 gaps", you can write either problem from scratch in under three minutes. That fluency — recognizing that two LeetCode numbers are one technique — is worth more than a third DP recurrence.

Interview Q&A

"What is dynamic programming, in one sentence?"

"Solving a problem by breaking it into smaller subproblems, solving each one exactly once, and reusing the results — which works when the problem has optimal substructure and the subproblems overlap." Then, without being asked, add the operational version: "In practice it means I write the brute-force recursion, notice it's recomputing the same arguments, and cache them." The second sentence is what proves you can actually do it; the first is what proves you can talk about it. Give both.

"Top-down memoization vs. bottom-up tabulation — which should I use?"

They compute the same thing; the trade-offs are real but small.

  • Top-down is easier to derive — it's your brute-force recursion plus a Map, so you can't get the recurrence wrong in translation. It only computes the states it actually needs (a real win when the state space is sparse). Costs: recursion-stack space, and a genuine stack-overflow risk in JS at depth ~10⁴–10⁵.
  • Bottom-up has no stack, is usually a constant factor faster, and — critically — it's the only form that lets you do rung 4 and collapse the table to O(1) space. Cost: you must reason explicitly about fill order.

The answer that scores: "I'll derive it top-down because that's where the recurrence is obvious, then convert to bottom-up so I can drop the space to O(1)." That's the ladder, and it's the honest workflow.

"How do you know a problem is DP and not greedy?"

Greedy works only when the locally optimal choice is provably globally optimal — when you never have to reconsider. DP is what you use when you can't make that promise, because it considers all choices and keeps the best. The practical test: try to construct a counterexample to the greedy rule. If you find one in thirty seconds, it's DP. Coin Change with [1, 3, 4] and amount 6 is the canonical case — greedy takes the 4 and needs 3 coins; the optimum is 3 + 3 = 2 coins. Contrast with Jump Game (tutorial 12), where greedy provably is optimal. If a greedy heuristic and a DP both seem plausible and you can't break the greedy one, say that honestly and implement the DP — correct-and-slower beats fast-and-wrong every single time.

"Why is the brute-force recursion exponential, exactly?"

Because the recursion tree branches, and the same node reappears in many branches. fib(n) makes two calls, each of which makes two calls: ~2ⁿ nodes. But the distinct arguments are only 0..n — n+1 of them. So the tree is (2ⁿ ÷ n) redundant on average. Memoization prunes every repeat node to a Map lookup, and the runtime collapses to (number of distinct states) × (work per state). That formula is the one to internalize, because it's also how you compute the complexity of any DP: Coin Change has amount states × coins.length work each = O(amount × coins). Say the formula out loud and the complexity answer is automatic.

"When can you NOT collapse the DP table to O(1) space?"

When the recurrence reads cells that aren't a bounded distance behind the current one. Climbing Stairs, House Robber, Kadane's, Decode Ways all read only dp[i-1] and dp[i-2] → two variables suffice. Coin Change reads dp[a - coin] for arbitrary coins, so any cell could be needed → the whole table stays live. LIS (O(n²) version) reads every earlier cell → whole table. Word Break reads every earlier cell (bounded only by the longest dictionary word) → whole table. Also: if you're asked to reconstruct the actual solution, not just its value, you need the table regardless of the recurrence, because reconstruction walks it backwards. Naming exactly why a problem can't be optimized is as strong a signal as optimizing one that can.

"How do you pick the state? I always get this wrong."

The state must contain everything you need to know to make the next decision, and nothing else. Test it: pretend I hand you dp[i] as a bare number and delete the array. Can you still compute dp[i+1]? If yes, your state is sufficient. If you find yourself wanting to ask "…but what was the last element I picked?" — as you do in LIS — then your state is missing that fact, and you must either bake it in (LIS: redefine dp[i] as "ending exactly at i", which pins the last element to nums[i]) or add a dimension (tutorial 13). Conversely, if you're tracking something the recurrence never reads, your state is too big and you're doing extra work. The two 1D shapes — "best over the prefix 0..i" and "best ending exactly at i" — cover every problem on this page. When one fails, try the other before reaching for 2D.

"Isn't memoization just caching? What makes it 'dynamic programming'?"

Yes — and that's the point, not a gotcha. Memoization is caching applied to a pure function of its arguments, which is precisely the contract useMemo asks you to honour with its dependency array. What makes it DP is which function you're caching: a recursion over subproblems that overlap. If the subproblems don't overlap (plain backtracking, N-Queens), caching buys you nothing and costs memory. If they do, caching converts exponential to polynomial. So DP = "the problem has a recursive structure with overlapping subproblems" + "so I cache." Nothing more mystical is going on, and saying so out loud tends to relax both you and the interviewer.

"Your DP returns the wrong answer. How do you debug it in an interview?"

Debug in this order — it's almost always the same three culprits, and going in order looks methodical:

  1. Print the table for the smallest failing input and read it against your English state definition. If dp[2] doesn't equal what "the number of ways to reach step 2" should be, the bug is above index 2.
  2. Check the base cases. Overwhelmingly the culprit. dp[0] = 1 vs dp[0] = 0 in a counting DP will silently return 0 for everything, and it looks exactly like a broken recurrence.
  3. Check what you return. dp[n] vs dp[n-1] vs Math.max(...dp) — the third of these is the one people forget in LIS and Kadane's, and it passes the sample tests by luck often enough to be dangerous.

Only after those three do you suspect the recurrence itself. Narrating this order is itself a strong signal — it's how you'd debug production code, and interviewers notice.

"What's the difference between a subarray, a substring, and a subsequence?"

A subarray and a substring are contiguous (subarray for numbers, substring for characters — same idea). A subsequence keeps relative order but allows gaps. This one word changes everything: Maximum Subarray (contiguous) is Kadane's in O(n); Longest Increasing Subsequence (gaps allowed) is O(n²) or O(n log n) and a completely different recurrence. Longest Palindromic Substring is expand-around-centre; Longest Palindromic Subsequence is a 2D LCS DP. Read the problem statement twice and circle the word — misreading it means you solve the wrong problem perfectly, which scores zero.

"Which of these actually get asked at enterprise-tier companies?"

Climbing Stairs and House Robber are the two most common DP questions across the entire tier, precisely because they're derivable in 20 minutes and clearly separate candidates who can build a recurrence from those who can't. Coin Change and Maximum Subarray are extremely common (Kadane's shows up in fintech loops constantly — "best contiguous run of P&L" is literally the business problem). Word Break and LIS appear in the harder rounds. Decode Ways is a classic screen question at banks because the edge cases are unforgiving and they want to see whether you enumerate them or get bitten by them. What almost never appears at this tier: bitmask DP, digit DP, DP on trees beyond House Robber III, and anything requiring a non-obvious state you'd have to have seen before. If you can climb the ladder on the ten problems on this page, you're covered.

Re-solve drills

DP does not stick from reading. It sticks from re-deriving. The failure mode is recognizing a problem you've seen, recalling the final code, and typing it — which feels like knowing and collapses the moment the problem is phrased differently. So the drill has a rule attached: on every re-solve, you must write the brute-force recursion first, out loud, even when you already know the answer.
