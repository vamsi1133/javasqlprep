12 — Greedy & Intervals

Take the locally best move and never look back — plus the sort-then-sweep template that owns every calendar, booking and trade-window problem.

Why interviewers ask this

Greedy is the one topic where the code is not what's being graded. Jump Game is five lines. Gas Station is eight. Any candidate can memorize them. What the interviewer is actually watching is whether you can justify that the locally-best move is globally safe — because the classic failure mode at 7 YOE isn't "couldn't write the loop," it's "confidently wrote a greedy that's wrong." You saw it last tutorial: Coin Change with coins [1, 3, 4] and amount 6. Take the biggest coin first (4) and you need 4+1+1 = 3 coins; the optimum is 3+3 = 2. Same instinct, same code shape, wrong answer. Greedy and DP look identical from the outside; only the proof tells them apart. So this page spends its most important section not on code but on the exchange argument and on an honest 60-second decision procedure for deciding, live, whether to bet on greedy.

Intervals are the other half, and for your target tier they are arguably the highest-value section in the whole course. Banks, healthcare and large product companies (Optum, JPMC, Goldman Sachs and their peers) run on intervals: meeting-room and clinician-shift scheduling, trade windows and market sessions, claim-coverage date ranges, rate-limit buckets, maintenance windows, booking calendars. Merge Intervals and Meeting Rooms II show up in these loops constantly, often lightly disguised as a domain problem ("given these policy coverage periods, find the gaps"). The good news: it is one template — sort, then sweep, holding a single piece of state — and once you own the two sort keys (by start to merge, by end to select the most non-overlapping items) you can derive the entire family at the whiteboard. The sweep-line variant of Meeting Rooms II generalizes to a dozen more problems, which is why it gets top billing here.

1. Recognizing the pattern

Suspect greedy when the problem statement has these signals:

  • "Minimum number of X" / "maximum number of Y" with no need to reconstruct which choices you made. You return a count or a boolean, not the set. That freedom is what lets you throw away state.
  • "Can you reach / is it possible?" — feasibility questions (Jump Game) usually collapse to tracking one running quantity, because you don't need the path, only whether one exists.
  • There is an obvious local rule that a human scheduler would use without thinking: "always take the one that finishes soonest," "always jump as far as you can," "always start over after the point that broke you."
  • The array/inputs have a natural order, or become useful the moment you sort them. Greedy almost always runs a single pass over a sensibly-ordered sequence.
  • An obvious DP exists but is too slow — Jump Game II has a clean O(n²) DP; the interviewer asking for O(n) is telling you a greedy exists.

Reach for sort-then-sweep intervals when you see:

  • Pairs of numbers that are start/end, from/to, arrive/depart, open/close, begin/finish. Even dressed up: [checkIn, checkOut], [tradeOpen, tradeClose], [effectiveDate, expiryDate].
  • The words "overlap," "merge," "conflict," "gap," "coverage," "concurrent," "at the same time."
  • "Minimum number of rooms / servers / machines / platforms" — that's maximum concurrency, which is sweep line.
  • "Maximum number of non-conflicting meetings" or "minimum removals to make it conflict-free" — those are the same problem, and they want sort by end time.
The recognition sentences to say out loud. For greedy: "There's an obvious local rule here. Before I commit, let me spend a minute trying to break it — if I can't find a counterexample, I'll go greedy and state the assumption I'm making, and I'll mention the DP fallback in case you want the proof." For intervals: "Anything with start/end pairs, my first line is a sort. Sorting by start lets me merge with a single 'does this one begin before the last one ended?' check." Naming the sort key and why before you write a line is the single highest-scoring 10 seconds on these problems.
The bug that has cost real candidates real offers: arr.sort() with no comparator sorts lexicographically, as strings. [10, 9].sort() returns [10, 9] — because "10" < "9". Every single problem in the interval half of this page starts with a sort, so make this muscle memory: intervals.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]). Never type a bare .sort() on numbers, ever, and never on the sub-arrays either. Type the comparator first, then think.

2. Greedy: how do you know it's safe?

This is the most important section on the page. A greedy algorithm makes an irrevocable choice at each step and never reconsiders it. DP, by contrast, keeps every option alive and lets the best one win at the end. Greedy is strictly faster and strictly more dangerous: the moment the locally-best choice can cut you off from the global optimum, greedy silently returns the wrong answer — no crash, no infinite loop, just a plausible-looking number.

2a. The exchange argument, in plain language

The proof technique interviewers want to hear is the exchange argument. You don't have to write it formally. You have to be able to say it. Here's the whole idea:

"Take any optimal solution. Suppose its first choice differs from mine. I'll show I can swap my greedy choice in for theirs and end up with a solution that is still valid and no worse. Repeat that swap step by step and the optimal solution transforms into mine — so mine is optimal too."

Concretely, for activity selection (Non-overlapping Intervals, LC 435), my greedy choice is the meeting that ends earliest. Take any optimal schedule. Its first meeting M ends at some time e(M), and my greedy pick G ends at e(G) ≤ e(M) — because I chose the earliest-ending one that's available. Now swap: replace M with G in the optimal schedule. Is it still valid? Yes — every other meeting in that schedule started after e(M), and e(G) ≤ e(M), so they all still start after G ends. Is it worse? No — it has exactly the same number of meetings. So there is an optimal solution that starts with my choice. Apply the same argument to what remains, and greedy is optimal all the way down. That paragraph, spoken out loud, is a complete proof and it will be scored as one.

The mental hook: the greedy choice must be safe (never destroys an optimal solution) and the problem must have optimal substructure (after you commit to the choice, the leftover problem is the same kind of problem, so induction closes the loop). Those two words — "safe" and "optimal substructure" — are the vocabulary. Use them.

2b. Why greedy fails on Coin Change — the contrast that makes it click

In tutorial 11 you solved Coin Change with DP. Try the exchange argument on the greedy "always take the largest coin ≤ remaining" with coins [1, 3, 4], amount 6:

  • Greedy picks 4. Remainder 2 → 1 + 1. Total: 3 coins.
  • Optimal is 3 + 3 = 2 coins.
  • Now try to run the exchange: take the optimal solution {3, 3} and try to swap my greedy 4 in for one of its 3s. You can't — after taking 4 you have 2 left, and no single coin makes 2. The swap strictly damages the solution. The greedy choice is not safe.

That's the whole difference. On US coin denominations (1, 5, 10, 25) the greedy is provably safe — which is exactly why it's such an effective trap: it works on every example the candidate tries, and then dies on [1, 3, 4]. Greedy failing is not exotic; it's the default. Optimality is the special case.

2c. The honest decision procedure (use this live, in the room)

You will not have time to construct a formal proof mid-interview. Do this instead:

  1. Spend 60 seconds trying to break your greedy rule. Actively hunt for a counterexample — build tiny adversarial inputs where the locally-best move costs you later. Two coins that don't compose. A jump that lands you one short. This is exactly what the interviewer is doing in their head while you talk.
  2. If you find one, greedy is dead. Say so, out loud, and pivot to DP. Finding the counterexample yourself is a positive signal, not a failure — it's the single most senior thing you can do on this topic.
  3. If you can't break it in 60 seconds and the local rule is obvious, go greedy — but SAY you're assuming it. "I'm going to go greedy here: always take the interval that ends earliest. My informal justification is the exchange argument — swapping in the earliest-finishing choice never invalidates the rest of the schedule and never reduces the count. If you'd like a bulletproof answer instead, there's an O(n²) DP fallback and I'm happy to write that."
  4. Sanity-check against the constraints. If n ≤ 2000 and they're happy with O(n²), a correct DP beats a possibly-wrong greedy. If n = 10⁵ and they want O(n) or O(n log n), they have told you a greedy (or a sort-based sweep) exists. Constraints are a hint, not decoration.
The point of step 3 is that you are trading a risk explicitly instead of hiding it. A 2-YOE candidate writes the greedy and hopes. A 7-YOE candidate writes the greedy, names the unproven assumption, and offers the fallback in the same breath. Interviewers at Goldman/JPMC-tier loops are grading exactly that calibration — the whole reason greedy problems are on their lists is that they separate "confidently correct" from "confidently wrong," and those look the same until you ask for the justification.

3. Greedy problems

Jump Game (LC #55) MEDIUM ★

You're given an integer array nums. You start at index 0. Each element nums[i] is the maximum jump length from that position — from index i you may land on any index from i+1 up to i + nums[i], inclusive (you may also jump less far, or not at all). Return true if you can reach the last index, false otherwise.

Example 1: Input: nums = [2,3,1,1,4] → Output: true — jump 1 step to index 1, then 3 steps to the last index.
Example 2: Input: nums = [3,2,1,0,4] → Output: false — every route lands on index 3, which holds a 0; you're stuck there and index 4 is unreachable.
Example 3: Input: nums = [0] → Output: true — you're already on the last index. The zero never matters. This edge case breaks naive "if you hit a 0 return false" solutions.
Constraints: 1 ≤ nums.length ≤ 10⁴; 0 ≤ nums[i] ≤ 10⁵.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. The brute force is a DFS/backtracking search over every jump length from every index — exponential, O(2ⁿ) in the worst case. Memoize it and you get an O(n²) DP: canReach[i] is true if any j in i+1 … i+nums[i] can reach the end. That passes, but it's doing far more work than the question needs. Here's the collapse: the question is only "is the last index reachable?" — not which route gets there. And reachability here has a beautiful property: if you can reach index i, you can reach every index before it (you can always jump shorter). So the entire set of reachable positions is just a prefix [0 … reach] — one number describes it completely. Sweep left to right maintaining reach = the furthest index reachable so far. At index i: if i > reach, there is a hole you cannot cross — return false immediately. Otherwise i is reachable, so it extends your horizon: reach = max(reach, i + nums[i]). Survive to the end and the answer is true. The greedy choice — "always extend the horizon as far as possible" — is trivially safe here, because a larger reach can never eliminate a position a smaller reach could have hit. That's the exchange argument in one line, and it's the sentence to say.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var canJump = function(nums) {
  let reach = 0;   // furthest index reachable using indices seen so far

  for (let i = 0; i < nums.length; i++) {
    // If the furthest we can get is BEFORE i, index i is unreachable —
    // and so is everything after it. There is a gap we cannot cross.
    if (i > reach) return false;

    // i IS reachable, so every landing spot it offers is reachable too.
    // Extending the horizon can never hurt: a bigger reach is a superset.
    reach = Math.max(reach, i + nums[i]);

    // Optional early exit — nice to mention, not required for correctness.
    if (reach >= nums.length - 1) return true;
  }

  return true;  // n === 1: loop never fails, we're already at the end
};

Trace on [2,3,1,1,4]: i=0, reach 0 → not a gap; reach = max(0, 0+2) = 2. i=1 ≤ 2; reach = max(2, 1+3) = 4 ≥ 4 → early exit true. ✓
On [3,2,1,0,4]: i=0 → reach 3. i=1 → max(3, 3) = 3. i=2 → max(3, 3) = 3. i=3 → max(3, 3+0) = 3. i=4: 4 > 3 → false. ✓ Notice the algorithm never special-cased the zero; the zero simply failed to extend the horizon.

Complexity: O(n) time — a single pass, O(1) work per index. O(1) space — one integer. Compare with the O(n²) DP it replaces: the insight that "reachable positions form a prefix" is what collapses an n-wide table into a single number.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Prove the greedy." The exchange argument: any route that reaches index i can also reach every index below i (jump shorter), so the reachable set is always a prefix. Maximizing the prefix end never removes a reachable index — the greedy choice is safe.
  • "Solve it backwards." Track goal = n-1; scan right to left; if i + nums[i] >= goal, set goal = i. Return goal === 0. Same O(n), equally valid — worth naming to show flexibility.
  • "Now return the minimum number of jumps." That's Jump Game II — next problem, and the reason this one is asked first.
The tempting wrong solutions here are both zero-flavored. (1) "Return false if the array contains a 0" — wrong: [2,0,1] has a zero and is reachable, you just hop over it. (2) "Return false when you land on a 0" — wrong: you're never forced to land anywhere, and [0] is true. Zeros aren't the concept; the gap between i and reach is the concept. Reason about the horizon, not about the values.

Jump Game II (LC #45) MEDIUM ★

Same array, same jump rule — but now you're guaranteed the last index is reachable, and the question is the minimum number of jumps needed to get there from index 0.

Example 1: Input: nums = [2,3,1,1,4] → Output: 2 — jump to index 1 (1 step), then jump 3 steps to the last index. Note that jumping the full 2 from index 0 lands you on index 2, which only reaches index 3 — so the greedy "jump as far as possible each time" reading is a trap; you still need 2 jumps but for a different reason. Read on.
Example 2: Input: nums = [2,3,0,1,4] → Output: 2.
Example 3: Input: nums = [1] → Output: 0 — you're already there, no jumps.
Constraints: 1 ≤ nums.length ≤ 10⁴; 0 ≤ nums[i] ≤ 1000; the answer is guaranteed to exist.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Start with the honest DP: dp[i] = min jumps to reach i, relax every reachable target from each index — O(n²), correct, and a perfectly good first answer to state out loud. Now the reframe that makes it O(n), and it is the framing you should carry into the room: this is a shortest-path problem on an unweighted graph, so it's really BFS — and the array structure lets you do the BFS without a queue. Think in levels. Level 0 is {index 0}. Level 1 is every index you can reach in one jump — a contiguous window [1 … nums[0]]. Level 2 is every index reachable from anywhere in level 1 — which, because reachability is a prefix, is again a contiguous window ending at max(i + nums[i]) over all i in level 1. Each BFS level is an interval of indices, so instead of a queue you just track the current level's right boundary (currentEnd) and the furthest index any member of the current level can reach (farthest). Sweep i left to right, keep updating farthest; the moment i hits currentEnd, you've exhausted this level — increment the jump count and let currentEnd = farthest, which opens the next level. The answer is the number of level boundaries you crossed. "Each jump is a BFS level, and because the reachable set is always contiguous I can represent a whole level with one boundary index instead of a queue" — say that, and you've earned the problem.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var jump = function(nums) {
  let jumps = 0;        // BFS levels completed
  let currentEnd = 0;   // last index of the CURRENT level (the boundary we must cross)
  let farthest = 0;     // furthest index reachable from ANY member of the current level

  // Stop at length-2: if we ever step ON the last index we've already counted the jump
  // that got us there. Looping to length-1 would over-count by one.
  for (let i = 0; i < nums.length - 1; i++) {
    farthest = Math.max(farthest, i + nums[i]);   // widen the NEXT level's reach

    if (i === currentEnd) {
      // We've walked every index in this level. To go further we MUST jump.
      jumps++;
      currentEnd = farthest;                      // the next level spans up to `farthest`

      // Optional: the next level already covers the goal — done.
      if (currentEnd >= nums.length - 1) break;
    }
  }

  return jumps;
};

Trace on [2,3,1,1,4]:
i=0: farthest = max(0, 0+2) = 2. i === currentEnd (0) → jumps = 1, currentEnd = 2. (Level 1 = indices 1–2.)
i=1: farthest = max(2, 1+3) = 4.
i=2: farthest = max(4, 2+1) = 4. i === currentEnd (2) → jumps = 2, currentEnd = 4 ≥ 4 → break.
Answer 2 ✓. Notice the algorithm never decides which index to jump to — it only asks "how far can this whole level see?" That's the BFS insight doing the work.

Complexity: O(n) time — one pass, O(1) per index (this is a BFS that visits each node once, with the queue replaced by two integers). O(1) space, versus O(n) for the queue an explicit BFS would need.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Why is greedily jumping to the farthest index each time wrong?" Because "farthest single landing spot" ≠ "farthest next horizon." From index 0 in [2,3,1,1,4], jumping the full 2 lands on index 2, whose reach is only 3. Jumping 1 lands on index 1, whose reach is 4. The correct greedy maximizes reach of the next level (i + nums[i]), not the distance of this jump. Candidates get this wrong constantly — say the distinction before you code.
  • "Reconstruct the actual jump sequence." Record, at each level boundary, the index i that produced farthest. Same O(n), plus O(#jumps) space.
  • "What if the answer isn't guaranteed to exist?" Merge in Jump Game I's check: if i > farthest you're stuck → return −1.
The connection to tutorial 10 is real and worth naming out loud: an unweighted shortest path is always BFS. Jump Game II is BFS on the graph whose edges are i → i+1 … i+nums[i]. What makes it O(n) rather than O(n²) is that you never materialize the edges — the levels happen to be contiguous index ranges, so two integers replace the entire queue. "It's a BFS in disguise" is the sentence that converts a memorized trick into a derivation.

Gas Station (LC #134) MEDIUM ★

There are n gas stations arranged in a circle. Station i holds gas[i] units of fuel, and driving from station i to the next station i+1 costs cost[i] units. You have a car with an unlimited tank, starting empty. Return the index of the station from which you can complete one full circuit clockwise; if it's impossible, return -1. The problem guarantees the answer is unique if it exists.

Example 1: Input: gas = [1,2,3,4,5], cost = [3,4,5,1,2] → Output: 3. Start at station 3 with 4 fuel; drive to 4 (cost 1) → tank 3; pick up 5 → 8, drive to 0 (cost 2) → 6; pick up 1 → 7, drive to 1 (cost 3) → 4; pick up 2 → 6, drive to 2 (cost 4) → 2; pick up 3 → 5, drive to 3 (cost 5) → 0. Made it, tank never went negative.
Example 2: Input: gas = [2,3,4], cost = [3,4,3] → Output: -1. Total gas 9 < total cost 10 — the loop is not completable from anywhere, regardless of start.
Constraints: n == gas.length == cost.length; 1 ≤ n ≤ 10⁵; 0 ≤ gas[i], cost[i] ≤ 10⁴.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process — this proof is the interview. The brute force is obvious: try each of the n starting stations, simulate n steps → O(n²). The O(n) solution is six lines, and if you just write those six lines without the argument, a good interviewer will assume you memorized it. The argument has exactly two parts, and you should state them as two parts.

Part 1 — feasibility: if sum(gas) ≥ sum(cost), a valid start exists. Define diff[i] = gas[i] - cost[i]: the net fuel change from leaving station i. Completing the circle from start s means every prefix sum of diff, read circularly starting at s, stays ≥ 0. Now pick the station where the running total of diff (starting anywhere, say at 0) reaches its minimum, and start at the station right after it. Every partial sum measured from that point is (running total) − (the global minimum) ≥ 0 by definition of minimum. So a valid start exists whenever the total is non-negative. The converse is trivial: if sum(diff) < 0 you burn more fuel than exists, so no start works. Therefore total ≥ 0 ⟺ an answer exists — and one if at the top of the function decides the -1 case completely, before any simulation.

Part 2 — the restart rule: if you start at s and run dry between station k and k+1, then no station in s … k can be the answer, so restart at k+1. Why? Suppose some m strictly between s and k worked. Because you reached m from s with a tank that was ≥ 0 the whole way, your tank at m when coming from s was ≥ 0 — i.e. at least as much fuel as a fresh start at m would have (which is 0 extra). Starting at m therefore gives you no more fuel at every subsequent station than the run from s did. And the run from s died before reaching k+1. So the run from m, carrying less-or-equal fuel at every point, dies too. Contradiction. Every candidate in s … k is eliminated by the single failure — that's why one linear sweep suffices instead of n restarts. This is an exchange/dominance argument, and articulating it is the entire point of the problem.

Put the parts together: sweep once. Keep total (never reset — it answers Part 1) and tank (reset to 0 whenever it goes negative — Part 2). Whenever you reset, the next station becomes the new candidate start. At the end, if total >= 0 the surviving candidate is the answer, because Part 1 guarantees some answer exists and Part 2 has eliminated every station before the candidate.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space 
var canCompleteCircuit = function(gas, cost) {
  let total = 0;   // sum of ALL diffs — decides IF an answer exists (Part 1)
  let tank  = 0;   // fuel since the current candidate start — decides WHERE (Part 2)
  let start = 0;   // current candidate starting station

  for (let i = 0; i < gas.length; i++) {
    const diff = gas[i] - cost[i];   // net fuel from leaving station i
    total += diff;
    tank  += diff;

    if (tank < 0) {
      // We ran dry leaving station i having started at `start`.
      // PROOF: no station in [start .. i] can work — a run beginning at any of them
      // arrives everywhere with LESS-OR-EQUAL fuel than this run did, and this run died.
      // So skip them all in one move: the next viable candidate is i + 1.
      start = i + 1;
      tank = 0;                     // fresh empty tank at the new candidate
    }
  }

  // total >= 0 guarantees SOME start works; the sweep has eliminated everything
  // before `start`; and the problem promises uniqueness. So `start` is it.
  return total >= 0 ? start : -1;
};

Trace on gas = [1,2,3,4,5], cost = [3,4,5,1,2]: diffs = [-2, -2, -2, 3, 3].
i=0: total −2, tank −2 < 0 → start = 1, tank = 0.
i=1: total −4, tank −2 < 0 → start = 2, tank = 0.
i=2: total −6, tank −2 < 0 → start = 3, tank = 0.
i=3: total −3, tank 3.
i=4: total 0, tank 6.
total 0 ≥ 0 → return 3 ✓. Note how the answer emerges without ever simulating the wrap-around — the two-part argument replaced the circular simulation entirely.

Complexity: O(n) time — one pass, no modular arithmetic, no restart loop. O(1) space — three integers. The O(n²) simulation is what you're beating, and the reason you can is Part 2: each failure eliminates a whole block of candidates instead of one.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Why don't you need to simulate the wrap-around from start back to start?" Because Part 1 already proved an answer exists (total ≥ 0) and Part 2 proved no earlier station can be it. Existence + elimination = the survivor is correct. If the problem did not guarantee uniqueness/existence you'd verify with one extra O(n) pass — cheap, and worth offering.
  • "What if the answer weren't unique?" The algorithm still returns a valid start (the first one after the global-minimum prefix). It just isn't the only one.
  • "Show me the prefix-minimum formulation." Answer: build the running prefix sums of diff, find the index of the minimum, start at (minIndex + 1) % n. It's a different-looking O(n) that is provably the same algorithm — mentioning it demonstrates you understand the invariant, not just the code.
If you get Gas Station in a JPMC/Goldman-style loop and you write the six lines correctly but in silence, you'll be graded as "probably memorized it." The output they want is the two-part argument. Lead with it: "Two claims. One: total gas minus total cost ≥ 0 is both necessary and sufficient for an answer to exist. Two: if I break down between s and k, no station in that whole range can be a valid start — so I skip them all in one step. Together those give me a single O(n) pass." Then write the code. That ordering is the difference between a hire and a lean-hire.

Partition Labels (LC #763) MEDIUM

Given a string s of lowercase letters, split it into as many parts as possible so that each letter appears in at most one part. (Equivalently: no letter may straddle a boundary.) Return a list of the sizes of those parts, in order. The parts must concatenate back into s.

Example 1: Input: s = "ababcbacadefegdehijhklij" → Output: [9,7,8] — the split is "ababcbaca" | "defegde" | "hijhklij". A split like "ababcbaca" | "defegde" | "hijhkl" | "ij" is invalid because i and j would appear in two parts.
Example 2: Input: s = "eccbbbbdec" → Output: [10] — e appears at index 0 and index 8, and c at 1 and 9, so nothing can be cut; the whole string is one part.
Constraints: 1 ≤ s.length ≤ 500; s is lowercase English letters only.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Reframe it and the answer falls out: each distinct letter is an interval — from its first occurrence to its last. A valid part must be a union of intervals that doesn't cut any of them. So this is secretly Merge Intervals over 26 intervals, and "as many parts as possible" means "make each merged block as small as it can be." Concretely: precompute last[c], the final index of every character (one O(n) pass — 26 entries, so it's O(1) space). Then sweep left to right holding a single number, end = the furthest last-occurrence of anything seen in the current part. At each index i, extend end = max(end, last[s[i]]) — because taking that character into the part obligates you to run at least to its last occurrence. The moment i === end, every character in the current part has had its final appearance, so cutting here is safe and — because you cut at the earliest legal point — optimal. The greedy is safe by the standard exchange argument: cutting at the first legal boundary can never reduce the number of parts, since any later cut point is still available afterwards.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(1) space (26-entry map) 
var partitionLabels = function(s) {
  // Pass 1: last index of every character. Overwriting as we go leaves the LAST one.
  const last = {};
  for (let i = 0; i < s.length; i++) last[s[i]] = i;

  const res = [];
  let start = 0;   // first index of the current part
  let end   = 0;   // furthest index this part is OBLIGATED to reach

  // Pass 2: sweep. This is exactly Jump Game's "furthest reach" idea on characters.
  for (let i = 0; i < s.length; i++) {
    // Including s[i] forces the part to extend to s[i]'s final appearance.
    end = Math.max(end, last[s[i]]);

    if (i === end) {
      // Every char in [start..i] has had its last occurrence — a cut here is legal,
      // and it's the EARLIEST legal cut, so it maximizes the number of parts.
      res.push(i - start + 1);

  }

Hand of Straights (LC #846) MEDIUM

Alice has a hand of cards, given as an integer array hand, and an integer groupSize. She wants to rearrange the cards into groups such that every group has exactly groupSize cards and consists of groupSize consecutive integers (e.g. [3,4,5]). Every card must be used. Return true if this is possible, false otherwise. (This is the same problem as LC 1296, "Divide Array in Sets of K Consecutive Numbers.")

Example 1: Input: hand = [1,2,3,6,2,3,4,7,8], groupSize = 3 → Output: true — groups [1,2,3], [2,3,4], [6,7,8].
Example 2: Input: hand = [1,2,3,4,5], groupSize = 4 → Output: false — 5 isn't divisible by 4, so some card would be left over. Check divisibility first; it's a free early exit.
Example 3: Input: hand = [8,10,12], groupSize = 3 → Output: false — the values exist but aren't consecutive.
Constraints: 1 ≤ hand.length ≤ 10⁴; 0 ≤ hand[i] ≤ 10⁹; 1 ≤ groupSize ≤ hand.length.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. The brute-force instinct — try every way to partition the cards — is combinatorial and hopeless. The greedy insight is a forcing argument, which is the strongest kind: the smallest remaining card has no choice. Whatever group it lands in, it must be that group's minimum (nothing smaller exists to sit below it), so its group is completely determined: it is exactly [min, min+1, …, min+groupSize−1]. There is no decision to make — so there's nothing to be greedy about, and nothing that can go wrong. Remove those cards, repeat. If any required card is missing or has count 0, the answer is false. That "forced move" framing is the cleanest possible safety proof: the exchange argument is vacuous because there was only ever one option.

Implementation: a count map (Map of value → count), plus the sorted distinct keys so you can walk smallest-first. For each key in ascending order, if its count c is still > 0, then c groups must start here — so decrement c from each of key … key+groupSize−1, failing if any of them has fewer than c left. Batching by c instead of peeling one group at a time is what keeps this fast.

Solution (JS) — O(n log n + n·groupSize) time · O(n) space 
var isNStraightHand = function(hand, groupSize) {
  // Free early exit: leftovers make it impossible no matter how you group.
  if (hand.length % groupSize !== 0) return false;

  // value -> how many copies remain unassigned
  const count = new Map();
  for (const card of hand) count.set(card, (count.get(card) ?? 0) + 1);

  // Distinct values, ascending. NOTE the comparator — a bare .sort() would sort
  // these numbers as STRINGS and silently produce garbage.
  const keys = [...count.keys()].sort((a, b) => a - b);

  for (const start of keys) {
    const c = count.get(start);
    if (c === 0) continue;          // already absorbed into earlier groups

    // FORCED MOVE: `start` is the smallest card left, so it can only ever be the
    // MINIMUM of its group. All c copies must each open their own group.
    for (let v = start; v < start + groupSize; v++) {
      const have = count.get(v) ?? 0;
      if (have < c) return false;   // not enough copies of v to feed all c groups
      count.set(v, have - c);          // consume c copies of v (batch, not one at a time)
    }
  }

  return true;
};

  • "Can you avoid the sort?" Yes — with a min-heap of distinct values (tutorial 08) you pop the smallest each time; same asymptotics, but the heap version generalizes if cards stream in.
  • "Why is starting from the smallest card obviously correct?" Because it's forced, not chosen: the minimum remaining card cannot be anything except the minimum of its own group. When a greedy move is the only legal move, correctness is free — this is the easiest greedy proof you'll ever give, so say it in exactly those words.
  • "groupSize = 1?" Always true. A one-liner check that shows you thought about degenerate inputs.

4. The interval template

Every interval problem starts the same way: sort, then sweep left to right holding one piece of state (usually "the last interval I committed to"). Unsorted intervals force you to compare every pair — O(n²). Sorted intervals mean the only thing that can possibly overlap the current one is the one immediately before it in your output, which is why the whole family is O(n log n) with an O(n) sweep and O(1) working state.

4a. Sort by START — the merge template

// The archetype: merge every set of overlapping intervals.
function merge(intervals) {
  // ALWAYS the comparator. A bare .sort() compares "10" vs "9" as strings.
  intervals.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]);

  const res = [intervals[0]];

  for (let i = 1; i < intervals.length; i++) {
    const curr = intervals[i];
    const last = res[res.length - 1];   // the only interval that can overlap curr

    // ===== THE ONE-LINE MERGE CONDITION =====
    if (curr[0] <= last[1]) {         // curr.start <= last.end  ⇒  they touch/overlap
      last[1] = Math.max(last[1], curr[1]);  // absorb: extend the end, never the start
    } else {
      res.push(curr);                       // disjoint: start a new output block
    }
  }

  return res;
}

Line by line:

  • Sorting by start is what makes "last" sufficient. After sorting, curr.start is ≥ every start you've already processed. So if curr doesn't overlap the most recent output block, it can't overlap any earlier one either (they end even earlier — well, they started earlier and were already merged, so the last block's end is the frontier). One comparison replaces the O(n) scan. Say this: "after sorting by start, the only candidate for overlap is the last merged interval."
  • curr[0] <= last[1] is the entire overlap test. Two intervals overlap iff one starts before the other ends. Because sorting guarantees last[0] <= curr[0], the general two-sided test a.start <= b.end && b.start <= a.end collapses to one side. Memorize the collapsed form and know where it came from.
  • Math.max on the end, not plain assignment. [1,10] followed by [2,3]: the second is fully contained. Overwriting the end with curr[1] would shrink the block to [1,3] and silently corrupt the answer. This is the #1 bug in Merge Intervals.
  • Touching vs overlapping. <= merges [1,4] and [4,5] into [1,5]. < keeps them separate. The right choice depends on whether the endpoints are inclusive — and that is a clarifying question you should ask out loud. Meeting rooms are half-open (a meeting ending at 10 doesn't conflict with one starting at 10, so use strict <); merge-intervals ranges are inclusive (use <=).

4b. Sort by END — the activity-selection template

When the question is "how many non-conflicting intervals can I keep?" (equivalently: "what's the minimum I must remove?"), sorting by start is wrong and sorting by end is right. Here's the argument, and it's the exchange argument again: among the intervals still available, the one that finishes earliest leaves the maximum amount of the timeline free for everything after it. No other choice can dominate it — any other pick ends later (or the same), so it can only block more, never fewer, future intervals. Swap it in and the solution is never worse. Safe choice + optimal substructure = greedy is optimal.

The counterexample that kills start-time sorting — have this ready, it's the standard follow-up:

intervals = [ [1, 10], [2, 3], [4, 5], [6, 7] ]

// Greedy by START: take [1,10] first (earliest start). It swallows the whole day,
// so [2,3], [4,5], [6,7] all conflict and must be dropped.  →  KEEPS 1

// Greedy by END:   sorted → [2,3], [4,5], [6,7], [1,10].
//   take [2,3] (ends at 3) → take [4,5] → take [6,7] → [1,10] conflicts, drop.
//                                                        →  KEEPS 3   ✓ optimal

One long greedy-by-start interval destroys three short ones. Duration doesn't matter either — sorting by shortest-first is also wrong: [[1,4], [3,5], [4,7]] → shortest is [3,5], taking it blocks both others and keeps 1, while end-sorting keeps [1,4] and [4,7] (2, with half-open times). Finish time is the only key that works. This is the textbook activity-selection theorem and it comes up in real scheduling systems constantly.

5. Interval problems

Insert Interval (LC #57) MEDIUM ★

You're given an array intervals of non-overlapping intervals [start, end], already sorted by start, plus one newInterval. Insert it so the result is still sorted and still non-overlapping — merging with any intervals it touches. Return the resulting array. (You may assume the input is well-formed and you don't need to modify it in place.)

Example 1: Input: intervals = [[1,3],[6,9]], newInterval = [2,5] → Output: [[1,5],[6,9]] — [2,5] overlaps [1,3], so they fuse into [1,5]; [6,9] is untouched.
Example 2: Input: intervals = [[1,2],[3,5],[6,7],[8,10],[12,16]], newInterval = [4,8] → Output: [[1,2],[3,10],[12,16]] — the new interval swallows three existing ones ([3,5], [6,7], [8,10]) and the merged block runs from min(4,3)=3 to max(8,10)=10.
Example 3: Input: intervals = [], newInterval = [5,7] → Output: [[5,7]] — the empty case must not crash.
Constraints: 0 ≤ intervals.length ≤ 10⁴; intervals is sorted by start and pairwise non-overlapping; 0 ≤ start ≤ end ≤ 10⁵.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. The lazy answer — push newInterval onto the array, re-sort, run Merge Intervals — is correct and O(n log n). Say it, then beat it: the input is already sorted, and throwing that away to re-sort is exactly the kind of wasted structure interviewers watch for. You can do it in a single O(n) pass with three phases, and naming the three phases before you code is the whole performance:

  • Before: every interval that ends strictly before newInterval starts (interval[1] < newInterval[0]) — copy it out untouched.
  • Overlapping: every interval that starts before newInterval ends (interval[0] <= newInterval[1]) — don't emit it; instead absorb it into a growing new interval: start = min(starts), end = max(ends). When this phase finishes, push the grown interval once.
  • After: everything left — copy it out untouched.

The subtlety candidates trip on: the grown interval is pushed after the overlap loop, not inside it — you don't know its final extent until the last overlapper is absorbed. And the two conditions are exact mirror images of the merge condition from the template, applied to the two ends.

Solution (JS) — O(n) time · O(n) space (output) 
var insert = function(intervals, newInterval) {
  const res = [];
  let [newStart, newEnd] = newInterval;
  let i = 0;
  const n = intervals.length;

  // PHASE 1 — strictly before: ends before the new one begins. Emit as-is.
  while (i < n && intervals[i][1] < newStart) {
    res.push(intervals[i]);
    i++;
  }

  // PHASE 2 — overlapping: starts at or before the new one ends. Absorb, don't emit.
  // Every absorbed interval widens the new one, which may make it swallow the NEXT one too.
  while (i < n && intervals[i][0] <= newEnd) {
    newStart = Math.min(newStart, intervals[i][0]);
    newEnd   = Math.max(newEnd,   intervals[i][1]);   // max, not assign — containment!
    i++;
  }
  res.push([newStart, newEnd]);   // push ONCE, after it has finished growing

  // PHASE 3 — strictly after: everything remaining is disjoint and already sorted.
  while (i < n) {
    res.push(intervals[i]);
    i++;
  }

  return res;
};

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Can you find phase 1's boundary faster?" Yes — binary search (tutorial 05) for the first interval with end >= newStart. It doesn't change the O(n) worst case (phase 2 can still touch everything, and you still copy phase 3), but on a huge sorted list with a tiny insert it's a real win, and mentioning it shows you know when a sorted array is a searchable one.
  • "Do it in place." Doable with splice, but splice is O(n) per call — say that out loud and stick with the output array unless they insist.
  • "Now handle a stream of inserts." Repeated inserts want a balanced BST / interval tree keyed by start (or, in JS, keeping a sorted array + binary search). That's the design-flavored escalation — booking systems live here.

Merge Intervals (LC #56) MEDIUM ★

Given an array of intervals [start, end] in arbitrary order, merge all overlapping intervals and return an array of the non-overlapping intervals that cover exactly the same total range. Intervals that merely touch at an endpoint ([1,4] and [4,5]) count as overlapping here and must be merged.

Example 1: Input: [[1,3],[2,6],[8,10],[15,18]] → Output: [[1,6],[8,10],[15,18]] — [1,3] and [2,6] overlap (2 ≤ 3) so they become [1,6].
Example 2: Input: [[1,4],[4,5]] → Output: [[1,5]] — touching counts as overlapping (this is the <= vs < decision; ask about it).
Example 3: Input: [[1,10],[2,3],[4,5]] → Output: [[1,10]] — the containment case. Any solution that assigns last.end = curr.end instead of taking the max returns [[1,5]] and is wrong.
Constraints: 1 ≤ intervals.length ≤ 10⁴; 0 ≤ start ≤ end ≤ 10⁴.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Unsorted, you'd have to compare every interval against every other, and worse, a merge can create a new overlap with something you already passed — so you'd loop until nothing changes, O(n²) or worse. Sorting by start removes that entire class of problem. Once sorted, the intervals arrive in the order they open, so a new interval can only ever overlap the block you're currently building — never an older, already-closed one, because an older block closed only when an interval arrived that started after it ended, and every subsequent interval starts even later. That's the invariant; state it, then the code is four lines. Walk the sorted list holding the last block in the output. If curr.start <= last.end, they overlap: extend last.end to max(last.end, curr.end). Otherwise, the current block is closed forever — push curr as the new block. This is the archetype of the entire section; everything else on this page is a variation on it.

Solution (JS) — O(n log n) time · O(n) space 
var merge = function(intervals) {
  if (intervals.length <= 1) return intervals;

  // THE line. Bare .sort() would compare [10,…] and [9,…] as strings → wrong order.
  intervals.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]);

  const res = [intervals[0].slice()];   // copy: we mutate ends, don't clobber the input

  for (let i = 1; i < intervals.length; i++) {
    const [start, end] = intervals[i];
    const last = res[res.length - 1];    // invariant: the ONLY block that can still overlap

    if (start <= last[1]) {
      // Overlap (or touch). Absorb. Math.max handles the CONTAINED case [1,10] + [2,3]:
      // a plain `last[1] = end` would shrink the block from 10 to 3 — the classic bug.
      last[1] = Math.max(last[1], end);
    } else {
      // Disjoint. Because starts only increase, `last` can never be revisited — it's final.
      res.push([start, end]);
    }
  }

  return res;
};

  • "Return the gaps instead" (free time between blocks). Merge first, then emit [res[i][1], res[i+1][0]] for consecutive blocks. This is Employee Free Time (LC 759) and it's a favorite dressed-up version in scheduling/calendar domains.
  • "Total covered length?" Sum end - start over the merged blocks. Naively summing the raw intervals double-counts overlaps — that's the trap, and it's the actual bug behind a lot of real billing/coverage reports.
  • "Intervals arrive as a stream — you can't sort." Now you need a sorted structure keyed by start (interval tree / balanced BST / SortedList). That's LC 715 Range Module territory. Naming the escalation is sufficient at this tier.
  • "Why is the sort safe if two intervals have the same start?" Order between them doesn't matter — whichever comes first, the Math.max absorbs the other. Worth a sentence; interviewers do probe tie behavior.

(2) last[1] = end instead of Math.max(last[1], end) — silently destroys containment, and it passes Example 1, so you won't catch it without testing [[1,10],[2,3]]. Non-overlapping Intervals (LC #435) MEDIUM ★

Given an array of intervals, return the minimum number you must remove so that the rest are pairwise non-overlapping. Intervals that only touch at an endpoint ([1,2] and [2,3]) are not considered overlapping here — endpoints are exclusive.

Example 1: Input: [[1,2],[2,3],[3,4],[1,3]] → Output: 1 — remove [1,3] and the remaining three are compatible.
Example 2: Input: [[1,2],[1,2],[1,2]] → Output: 2 — keep one copy, remove the other two.
Example 3: Input: [[1,100],[11,22],[1,11],[2,12]] → Output: 2 — the big [1,100] is exactly the interval a start-time greedy would keep, and keeping it forces you to throw away three. End-time greedy keeps [1,11] and [11,22] → remove 2.
Constraints: 1 ≤ intervals.length ≤ 10⁵; −5·10⁴ ≤ start < end ≤ 5·10⁴.

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. First, flip the question — this reframe is half the problem: minimizing removals is the same as maximizing keeps. "Keep the largest set of mutually compatible intervals" is the classic activity-selection problem, and the answer is intervals.length − kept.

Now: which greedy? Three candidate rules, and only one survives.

  • Earliest start first — WRONG. [[1,10],[2,3],[4,5],[6,7]]: it takes [1,10], which blocks all three short ones. Keeps 1; optimal is 3. Example 3 above is the same trap in LeetCode's own test data. This is the trap.
  • Shortest duration first — ALSO WRONG. [[1,4],[3,5],[4,7]]: shortest is [3,5] (length 2), and taking it conflicts with both neighbours → keeps 1. Optimal keeps [1,4] and [4,7] → 2. So "least disruptive-looking" is not a valid heuristic either.
  • Earliest END first — CORRECT. Sort by end time. Sweep, keeping a running lastEnd. If curr.start >= lastEnd, it's compatible — keep it and advance lastEnd. Otherwise it conflicts with something you already kept, so remove it (count++) and — crucially — leave lastEnd alone, because you kept an interval that ends no later.

Why earliest-finish is safe (the exchange argument, in the form to say out loud): among all intervals still available, the one that ends earliest leaves the largest possible remaining timeline. Take any optimal solution; its first-finishing interval M ends at e(M), and my greedy pick G has e(G) ≤ e(M). Swap G in for M: every other interval in the optimal set began after e(M) ≥ e(G), so they're all still compatible with G. Same count, still valid. So an optimal solution exists that contains my greedy choice — and by induction on the remainder, greedy is optimal. That's it. Ending sooner can never hurt you; starting sooner can.

Solution (JS) — O(n log n) time · O(1) space 
var eraseOverlapIntervals = function(intervals) {
  if (intervals.length <= 1) return 0;

  // SORT BY END, not start. Earliest-finishing interval leaves the most room
  // for everything after it — the only greedy that's provably optimal here.
  intervals.sort((a, b) => a[1] - b[1]);

  let removed = 0;
  let lastEnd = intervals[0][1];   // we always keep the first (it finishes soonest of all)

  for (let i = 1; i < intervals.length; i++) {
    const [start, end] = intervals[i];

    if (start >= lastEnd) {
      // Compatible with everything kept so far (endpoints exclusive → >= not >).
      lastEnd = end;          // KEEP it; it becomes the new frontier
    } else {
      // Conflicts. Drop THIS one, not the kept one: because we sorted by end,
      // the interval we already kept finishes no later — it dominates. So lastEnd
      // deliberately does NOT move. That non-update is the whole algorithm.
      removed++;
    }
  }

  return removed;
};

Meeting Rooms (LC #252) EASY

Given an array of meeting time intervals [start, end], determine whether a single person could attend all of them — i.e. return true iff no two meetings overlap. A meeting that ends at 10 and one that starts at 10 do not conflict (endpoints are half-open: you can walk out of one and into the next).

Example 1: Input: [[0,30],[5,10],[15,20]] → Output: false — the [0,30] meeting collides with both of the others.
Example 2: Input: [[7,10],[2,4]] → Output: true — they're disjoint once you sort them.
Example 3: Input: [[1,5],[5,9]] → Output: true — touching endpoints are fine. Using <= here instead of < returns false and is the only real bug available in this problem.
Constraints: 0 ≤ intervals.length ≤ 10⁴; 0 ≤ start < end ≤ 10⁶. (LeetCode Premium; the free equivalent is the boolean half of LC 253.)

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. Brute force compares all pairs: O(n²). Sort by start and the pairwise check collapses — after sorting, if any conflict exists, it must exist between two adjacent meetings. Why? If meeting i overlaps meeting j with j > i+1, then meeting i also overlaps i+1 (which starts no later than j and therefore also starts before i ends). So checking neighbors is sufficient — that's the proof, and it's a 15-second one worth giving because it's the same "sorting makes the local check global" idea that powers Merge Intervals. Then it's one pass: if (intervals[i][0] < intervals[i-1][1]) return false. This problem is really a warm-up whose only purpose is to set up Meeting Rooms II — treat it as ten seconds of thinking and a clean answer, then expect the follow-up immediately.

Solution (JS) — O(n log n) time · O(1) space 
var canAttendMeetings = function(intervals) {
  intervals.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]);   // by start — comparator, always

  for (let i = 1; i < intervals.length; i++) {
    // After sorting, ANY conflict shows up between neighbours — so neighbours suffice.
    // STRICT < : a meeting starting exactly when the previous ends is fine (half-open).
    if (intervals[i][0] < intervals[i - 1][1]) return false;
  }

  return true;   // 0 or 1 meetings: loop never runs → true, correct by default
};

Trace on [[0,30],[5,10],[15,20]]: sorted → [[0,30],[5,10],[15,20]]. i=1: 5 < 30 → conflict → false ✓.
On [[1,5],[5,9]]: i=1: 5 < 5 is false → no conflict → true ✓ (the strict < earning its keep).

Complexity: O(n log n) time (sort), O(n) sweep. O(1) auxiliary space.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Are endpoints inclusive?" Ask this before writing the comparison, not after. It's the only decision in the problem and interviewers seed the ambiguity deliberately.
  • "How many rooms would you need instead?" That's Meeting Rooms II — next, and it's the real question. This one is the setup.
  • "Which pair conflicts?" Return the pair instead of a boolean; same loop, one extra line.

Meeting Rooms II (LC #253) MEDIUM ★

Given an array of meeting time intervals [start, end], return the minimum number of conference rooms required to hold all of them. Two meetings can share a room only if they don't overlap in time; as before, a meeting ending at 10 and one starting at 10 can share a room.

Example 1: Input: [[0,30],[5,10],[15,20]] → Output: 2 — [0,30] occupies a room the whole time; [5,10] needs a second room; [15,20] can reuse that second room because [5,10] has ended.
Example 2: Input: [[7,10],[2,4]] → Output: 1 — disjoint, one room does it.
Example 3: Input: [[1,5],[2,6],[3,7],[4,8]] → Output: 4 — all four are live at time 4.5, so you genuinely need four rooms. This is the case that proves the answer is maximum concurrency, not a count of "conflicts."
Constraints: 1 ≤ intervals.length ≤ 10⁴; 0 ≤ start < end ≤ 10⁶. (LeetCode Premium; the free twin is LC 1094 Car Pooling and LC 2402 Meeting Rooms III.)

Solve on LeetCode ↗

Thinking process. First, reframe the question until it becomes trivially answerable, because the reframe is the insight: the number of rooms you need is the maximum number of meetings that are simultaneously in progress at any instant. Not the number of overlapping pairs, not the number of conflicts — the peak concurrency. Once you say that sentence, both solutions write themselves, and you should say it before you pick one.

Solution A — the min-heap of end times. Sort meetings by start. Keep a min-heap holding the end times of the meetings currently occupying rooms. For each new meeting: if the earliest-finishing occupied room (the heap's root) has already ended by the time this meeting starts, that room is free — pop it and reuse it. Then push this meeting's end time. The heap's size at any moment is the number of rooms in use, so the answer is the maximum size the heap ever reaches (equivalently, its final size, since you only ever pop when you immediately push). Why check only the earliest-ending room? Because if the room that frees up soonest is still busy, every other room is busy too — the heap root dominates. That's tutorial 08's MinHeap doing exactly the job it exists for.

Solution B — the sweep line (learn this one properly). Forget intervals; think about events on a timeline. A meeting [s, e] is two events: at time s, concurrency goes +1; at time e, concurrency goes −1. Dump all 2n events into a list, sort them chronologically, walk through them keeping a running counter, and track the maximum the counter ever reaches. That maximum is your answer. The interval structure is gone entirely — you've turned a 2D scheduling problem into a 1D running sum. The tie rule matters: when a start and an end land on the same timestamp, process the end first (the −1 before the +1), because a meeting ending at 10 frees its room for the one starting at 10. Get that backwards and you over-count rooms by one on touching meetings.

Both are O(n log n). Solution B is the one to invest in: it generalizes to any "what's the peak value of some quantity over time" question — car pooling capacity, peak concurrent users, maximum population alive in a given year, server load, bandwidth. Section 6 unpacks that.

Solution A (JS) — min-heap · O(n log n) time · O(n) space 
// A minimal MinHeap — the same one from tutorial 08. In an interview you can
// say "assume the MinHeap from before" and go straight to minMeetingRooms.
class MinHeap {
  constructor() { this.h = []; }
  size()  { return this.h.length; }
  peek()  { return this.h[0]; }

  push(v) {
    this.h.push(v);
    let i = this.h.length - 1;
    while (i > 0) {                       // sift up
      const p = (i - 1) >> 1;
      if (this.h[p] <= this.h[i]) break;
      [this.h[p], this.h[i]] = [this.h[i], this.h[p]];
      i = p;
    }
  }

  pop() {
    const top = this.h[0];
    const last = this.h.pop();
    if (this.h.length) {
      this.h[0] = last;
      let i = 0;                          // sift down
      while (true) {
        const l = 2 * i + 1, r = 2 * i + 2;
        let small = i;
        if (l < this.h.length && this.h[l] < this.h[small]) small = l;
        if (r < this.h.length && this.h[r] < this.h[small]) small = r;
        if (small === i) break;
        [this.h[i], this.h[small]] = [this.h[small], this.h[i]];
        i = small;
      }
    }
    return top;
  }
}

var minMeetingRooms = function(intervals) {
  if (intervals.length === 0) return 0;

  intervals.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]);   // process meetings in the order they begin

  const rooms = new MinHeap();   // holds END TIMES of currently-occupied rooms
  rooms.push(intervals[0][1]);

  for (let i = 1; i < intervals.length; i++) {
    const [start, end] = intervals[i];

    // The room that frees up SOONEST is the heap root. If even that one is still
    // busy, every room is busy. If it's free, reuse it — pop it off.
    // <= : a room whose meeting ends exactly at `start` is available.
    if (rooms.peek() <= start) rooms.pop();

    rooms.push(end);   // this meeting now occupies a room (reused or brand new)
  }

  // We popped at most one per push, so the heap's final size IS the peak size.
  return rooms.size();
};

Trace on [[0,30],[5,10],[15,20]]: sorted already. heap = [30] (1 room).
[5,10]: root 30 > 5 → no room free → push 10. heap = [10, 30] (2 rooms).
[15,20]: root 10 ≤ 15 → that room is free → pop 10; push 20. heap = [20, 30] (2 rooms).
Answer 2 ✓. On [[1,5],[2,6],[3,7],[4,8]]: no root ever ends before the next start, so the heap only grows → size 4 ✓.

Complexity: O(n log n) — the sort is O(n log n), and the sweep does n pushes and ≤ n pops on a heap of size ≤ n, each O(log n). O(n) space for the heap (all meetings could be concurrent).

Why the final size equals the peak size: each iteration pops at most once and pushes exactly once, so the heap never shrinks. Its size is monotonically non-decreasing, and therefore its final size is its maximum. Nice detail to state — it saves you from tracking a separate max variable, and noticing it out loud is a small, cheap senior signal.

Solution B (JS) — sweep line / chronological events · O(n log n) time · O(n) space 
var minMeetingRooms = function(intervals) {
  // Step 1: shatter each interval into two timeline EVENTS.
  //   at `start`: +1 concurrent meeting     at `end`: -1 concurrent meeting
  const events = [];
  for (const [start, end] of intervals) {
    events.push([start, 1]);
    events.push([end, -1]);
  }

  // Step 2: sort chronologically. TIE RULE: at the same timestamp, process the
  // -1 (an ending) BEFORE the +1 (a start) — a meeting ending at 10 frees the room
  // for one starting at 10. Sorting by delta ascending puts -1 first. Flip this and
  // you over-count rooms on back-to-back meetings.
  events.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0] || a[1] - b[1]);

  // Step 3: sweep. The running sum IS the number of meetings live right now.
  let concurrent = 0;
  let maxConcurrent = 0;

  for (const [time, delta] of events) {
    concurrent += delta;
    maxConcurrent = Math.max(maxConcurrent, concurrent);   // peak = rooms needed
  }

  return maxConcurrent;
};

Trace on [[0,30],[5,10],[15,20]]:
events → [0,+1] [30,−1] [5,+1] [10,−1] [15,+1] [20,−1]; sorted → [0,+1] [5,+1] [10,−1] [15,+1] [20,−1] [30,−1].
Sweep: 1 → 2 (max = 2) → 1 → 2 → 1 → 0. Answer 2 ✓.
Tie rule in action, on [[1,5],[5,9]]: events sorted → [1,+1] [5,−1] [5,+1] [9,−1] (the −1 sorts before the +1 at time 5). Sweep: 1 → 0 → 1 → 0 → max = 1 ✓ — one room, correctly reused. If the tie broke the other way you'd see 1 → 2 → … and report 2 rooms. That single || a[1] - b[1] is the whole bug surface of this solution.

Complexity: O(n log n) — 2n events, dominated by their sort; the sweep is O(n) with two integers of state. O(n) space for the event list.

Why this framing is worth more than the heap one: the heap answers "minimum rooms." The sweep answers "what is the maximum value of a quantity that changes by known deltas at known times" — which is a strictly more general question. Swap ±1 for ±numPassengers and it's Car Pooling. Sort by year and it's "which year had the most people alive." Keep a running sum over prices and it's a load/bandwidth profile. Learning the sweep buys you a dozen problems; learning the heap solution buys you one.

Follow-ups you should expect:

  • "Which meetings are in the peak room-count window?" Track the timestamp when concurrent hits its max; a second pass collects every interval covering that instant.
  • "Now there's a capacity limit — return false if you'd exceed C rooms." Same sweep, return false the moment concurrent > C. That's literally LC 1094 Car Pooling.
  • "Assign each meeting an actual room number." The heap version generalizes better here: store [endTime, roomId] in the heap and reuse the popped room's id. This is LC 2402 Meeting Rooms III, and it's the fintech-flavored escalation (which trading desk / which clinician gets the slot).
  • "Times are Date objects, not integers." Map to epoch millis first; the algorithm is unchanged. Mention it — real calendar systems hand you timestamps, and interviewers in healthcare/booking domains do ask.
The tie-breaking rule in the sweep is where this solution dies. If you write events.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]) and leave the tie to the engine, JS's sort is stable — so equal timestamps keep insertion order, which is whatever order the intervals happened to arrive in. Your answer becomes input-order-dependent: correct on some inputs, off by one on others. Always break the tie explicitly with || a[1] - b[1] (ends before starts). And note this is the opposite convention if the problem says endpoints are inclusive — in that case a meeting ending at 10 does conflict with one starting at 10, so starts must be processed first. Ask which convention applies, then encode it deliberately.

6. Sweep line

The sweep line deserves its own name because it is a reusable frame, not a trick for one problem. The move: stop thinking about objects (intervals, bookings, people) and start thinking about events on a timeline. Anything with a beginning and an end contributes two events — one that increases some running quantity, one that decreases it. Sort the events by time, walk them once, and maintain a running aggregate. You have converted a 2D problem (objects × time) into a 1D scan. 

// The sweep-line template. Learn this shape; the deltas and the aggregate are the knobs.
function sweep(items) {
  const events = [];
  for (const [start, end, weight = 1] of items) {
    events.push([start, +weight]);   // something begins
    events.push([end,   -weight]);   // something ends
  }

  // Chronological. Ties: decide DELIBERATELY whether an ending frees the resource
  // for something starting at the same instant (half-open → -1 first, as here).
  events.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0] || a[1] - b[1]);

  let running = 0, best = 0;
  for (const [time, delta] of events) {
    running += delta;                // the aggregate at this instant
    best = Math.max(best, running);  // or: check a cap, record the time, emit a profile…
  }
  return best;
}

Interview Q&A

How do you decide, during an interview, whether greedy is safe?

Four steps, said out loud. (1) Name the local rule you're tempted by. (2) Spend ~60 seconds actively trying to build a counterexample — small adversarial inputs where taking the locally-best move costs you later. (3) If you find one, greedy is dead; pivot to DP and say why. (4) If you can't break it, go greedy but state the assumption: "I'm assuming the earliest-finishing choice is safe — informally, by an exchange argument, because swapping it into any optimal solution never invalidates the rest. If you want a provably-correct answer instead, there's an O(n²) DP."

The thing being graded is not whether you guessed right; it's whether you knew you were guessing. Silent confidence on a wrong greedy is the worst outcome available on this topic.

Explain the exchange argument to someone who's never heard the term.

"Take any optimal solution. Show that I can replace its first choice with my greedy choice without making the solution invalid or worse. If that swap always works, then there's an optimal solution that starts with my choice — and by repeating the argument on what's left, an optimal solution that agrees with every choice I make. So mine is optimal."

The two ingredients: the greedy choice must be safe (the swap never breaks anything) and the problem must have optimal substructure (after committing, the rest is a smaller instance of the same problem). Concrete instance: for activity selection, my choice ends earliest, so every other activity in the optimal set — which started after its first activity ended — still fits after mine. Same count, still valid. Done.

Why does greedy work for Jump Game but fail for Coin Change?

In Jump Game the greedy choice is monotone: extending your reach can only add reachable positions, never remove one. A bigger reach is a strict superset of a smaller reach, so the greedy move can't destroy an option — it's safe by construction.

In Coin Change, taking the biggest coin removes options: coins [1,3,4], amount 6. Greedy takes 4, leaving 2, which needs two 1s → 3 coins. The optimum is 3+3 = 2 coins. Taking the 4 made 3+3 unreachable. The choice is not safe, so you need DP, which keeps every option alive. Same code shape, completely different correctness story — and that's exactly why interviewers pair these two problems.

Merge Intervals sorts by start; Non-overlapping Intervals sorts by end. Why the difference?

They're asking different questions. Merging/detecting overlap is about combining everything in timeline order, so you want them in the order they open — sort by start, and then the only interval that can overlap the current one is the block you're building.

Selecting the largest compatible subset is about rejecting things, and you want to reject as little as possible. The interval that finishes earliest leaves the most timeline free for everything after it, so it's the safe pick. Sorting by start picks up [1,10] first and blocks [2,3], [4,5], [6,7] — keeping 1 where the optimum is 3. One sentence to remember: ending sooner never hurts; starting sooner can.

Meeting Rooms II: heap or sweep line — which do you present, and why?

Present the reframe first — "minimum rooms = maximum number of meetings concurrently in progress" — then offer both and let them choose. If they don't care, code the sweep: it's shorter, needs no custom heap (JS has no built-in priority queue, so the heap answer costs you 25 lines of boilerplate or an awkward "assume a MinHeap"), and it generalizes to Car Pooling, capacity checks, and load profiles.

Choose the heap when the follow-up needs identity, not just a count — "which room did each meeting get?" (LC 2402). The heap physically models rooms, so you can store [endTime, roomId] and reuse the popped room's id. Knowing when each solution is the better tool is the answer they're actually fishing for.

What's the tie-breaking rule in a sweep line, and why does it matter?

When a start event and an end event share a timestamp, you must decide which is processed first. For half-open intervals (a meeting ending at 10 frees the room for one starting at 10 — the usual calendar convention) process the end first: events.sort((a,b) => a[0]-b[0] || a[1]-b[1]), which puts −1 before +1. For closed intervals (endpoints conflict) process the start first.

Get it wrong and you over- or under-count by one exactly on back-to-back intervals — which is both the most common real-world case and the test case the interviewer has ready. Leaving the tie to the engine's stable sort is worse than wrong: it makes the answer depend on input order.

What does [10, 9].sort() return in JavaScript, and why should you care?

[10, 9] — unchanged. Array.prototype.sort with no comparator coerces elements to strings and sorts lexicographically, and "10" < "9" because '1' < '9'. This is the single most expensive default in the language for interview purposes: every problem in the interval half of this page begins with a sort, and a silently-mis-sorted array produces a plausible-looking wrong answer with no error.

Never type a bare .sort(). Type intervals.sort((a, b) => a[0] - b[0]) — comparator first, then think about the rest of the problem. Same for keys.sort((a, b) => a - b) in Hand of Straights. Real candidates have lost real offers to this.

Gas Station: why is one linear pass enough — why don't you restart the simulation at each candidate?

Because a single failure eliminates a whole block of candidates, not just one. If you start at s and run dry between k and k+1, then no station in s … k works: a run beginning at any m in that range arrives at every subsequent station with less-or-equal fuel than your run from s did (your tank was ≥ 0 when you passed m, so you were carrying at least as much as a fresh start would), and your run died. So the run from m dies too. Skip the whole block; the next candidate is k+1.

Separately, sum(gas) ≥ sum(cost) is necessary and sufficient for an answer to exist, so that one check settles the -1 case entirely. Existence + elimination means the survivor of the sweep must be the answer — no verification pass needed.

Jump Game II: why isn't "jump as far as you can each time" the right greedy?

Because the thing you want to maximize is not the length of this jump but the reach of the next level. In [2,3,1,1,4], jumping the full 2 from index 0 lands on index 2, whose reach is only 3. Jumping just 1 lands on index 1, whose reach is 4 — the whole array. The correct greedy scans every index in the current BFS level and takes the maximum of i + nums[i]; the jump distance itself is irrelevant.

Framing it as BFS is what makes this obvious rather than lucky: each jump is a level, each level is a contiguous range of indices, and you're just computing where the next level's frontier lands. Say "it's a BFS in disguise, and because levels are contiguous I can replace the queue with two integers."

When does an interval problem stop being greedy and become DP?

The moment intervals get weights. "Keep the most intervals" is greedy (earliest finish time). "Keep the intervals with the highest total value" is Weighted Interval Scheduling, and greedy is provably wrong — a single high-value long interval can beat many cheap short ones, and no local rule sees that. The DP: sort by end, binary-search for p(i) = the last interval compatible with i, then dp[i] = max(dp[i-1], value[i] + dp[p(i)]) — O(n log n).

Being able to name the exact feature that breaks the greedy (weights) rather than just saying "greedy might not work" is what senior sounds like. It's also the natural bridge into tutorial 13.
