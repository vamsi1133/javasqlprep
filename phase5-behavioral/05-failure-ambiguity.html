05 — Failure, Mistakes & Ambiguity

Two families of question that reward the thing juniors hide: a real failure owned cleanly, and a blank spec you turned into a plan.

Why this matters in the interview

This is the page where candidates lie, and interviewers know it. Ask a room of engineers for their biggest failure and most reach for a fake one — "I care too much," "I took on too much work" — because admitting a real mistake feels like handing the interviewer a reason to reject you. It's the exact opposite. The failure question is not testing whether you fail; everyone senior has broken production, missed a date, shipped the wrong thing. It's testing whether you can look at your own mistake without flinching, own it without deflecting onto a teammate or "the process," and — the part almost everyone drops — tell the interviewer what you changed afterwards. A failure with no learning is just a confession. A failure with a concrete "and here's the guardrail I added so it can't happen the same way again" is a story about judgment, which is exactly what a 7-YOE engineer is graded on.

The ambiguity questions — "a time the requirements were vague," "you had to make a call without enough information," "no one told you what to build" — are the same skill pointed forward instead of backward. At 3 YOE you are handed tickets; at 7 YOE you are handed problems, and the interviewer wants evidence that a blank page doesn't freeze you. The signal they're hunting is bias for action under uncertainty: do you wait for someone to write the spec, or do you write down your assumptions, cut a v1, get a fast signal, and adjust? These two families — failure looking back, ambiguity looking forward — are the ones enterprise/fintech panels (Optum, JPMC, Goldman) and Amazon's Bar Raiser probe hardest, because they are where trust is actually decided. This page splits them cleanly, gives you the trap for each, and works three STAR stories end to end that you can drop your real projects into.

Part 1 — Failure & mistakes

The questions in this family

They all look different and they are all the same question. Recognise the family and you never get surprised:

  • "Tell me about your biggest failure / your biggest professional mistake."
  • "Tell me about a time you missed a deadline / didn't deliver on time."
  • "Describe a time you made a mistake that affected others / caused a production incident."
  • "Tell me about a decision you got wrong / a project that failed."
  • "When did you receive difficult feedback?" (the failure-adjacent one — real overlap with page 04).
  • "What's something you'd do differently if you could redo it?"

What the interviewer is actually testing

Not the size of the disaster. Three things, in this order: self-awareness (can you name a genuine mistake, unprompted, without it being dragged out of you?), ownership (do you say "I" and stop, or do you narrate your way to "and that's why it was really the PM's fault"?), and growth (did the failure change how you work, concretely and permanently?). The senior tell is the third one delivered without being asked. A mid-level candidate tells the story and stops at the result. A senior candidate lands the result and then, unprompted, says "and here's the guardrail I put in so this class of thing can't recur" — because to a senior engineer a failure isn't an event, it's a gap in a system, and you fix systems.

Four ways people torch this question, all common: (1) The humblebrag / fake failure. "I work too hard," "I'm a perfectionist," "I cared too much about quality." The interviewer has heard it 500 times; it reads as evasion and burns your credibility for the rest of the loop. (2) The disqualifying failure. The other ditch — do not confess something that makes you unhireable: a security breach you hid, missing a deadline because you didn't show up, an ethics lapse, blowing a relationship past repair. Pick a real failure of judgment or execution, not of integrity or basic reliability. (3) Blaming others. "The requirements were bad, QA missed it, the PM changed scope." Even when partly true, every sentence you spend on someone else's fault is a sentence you didn't spend owning yours — and the interviewer is counting. (4) Skipping the learning. You tell the story, land the fix, and stop. No "what changed." This is the single most common miss, and it converts a strong story into a neutral one.

Picking a failure that's real but not disqualifying

The sweet spot is a mistake that was consequential enough to be believable and squarely about engineering judgment — something the interviewer can imagine themselves doing. Run a candidate story through this filter before you use it:

A good failure story is…Avoid a story that is…
A judgment or execution mistake (shipped a regression, over-engineered, mis-scoped, trusted a bad assumption).An integrity or reliability failure (hid a bug, no-showed, cut a security corner). Unrecoverable.
Real, with real consequences you can quantify (broke checkout for X% of users; slipped a quarter's date).A fake / trivial "failure" (worked too hard, too detail-oriented). Reads as dodging.
Owned by you — your decision, your code, your call.Owned by someone else, where your "failure" is really their fault. No ownership signal.
Resolved — you fixed the immediate problem and changed something so it can't recur.Still open, or "resolved" by leaving the company / the problem quietly persisting.
Recent-ish and senior-appropriate (a lead/owner-level mistake, not a first-week-intern mistake).From so long ago it implies you've made no interesting mistakes since. Or so junior it undersells your level.
Counter-intuitive but true: a slightly bigger owned failure often scores better than a tiny one, because the ownership and the recovery have more room to show. "I broke checkout" told with clean ownership and a real postmortem beats "I once had a typo in a log line." Don't reach for the smallest safe thing — reach for the biggest thing you fully owned and fixed. The safety comes from the ownership and the fix, not from the mistake being minor.

Worked story A — the production regression you owned

Use this when the question is about a mistake that affected others, a production incident, or "your biggest failure." It's the strongest failure archetype for a frontend-heavy engineer because the mistake, the blast radius, and the fix are all yours. Round metrics are placeholders — replace with your real ones.

STARStory A — "I shipped a design-system change that broke checkout"
Situation I owned our shared design system — the component library ~[40] product teams built on. I shipped what I thought was a safe, backwards-compatible refactor of the shared form-validation hook, mainly to cut bundle size. It went out in a minor version bump, so teams picked it up automatically.
Task My job was to evolve that library without breaking the teams downstream — the whole promise of a design system is that upgrades are safe. I had unit tests on the component and they passed.
Action The refactor changed one edge case: an empty string now coerced to "valid" on a specific code path the checkout team relied on to block blank card fields. My tests didn't cover that path. It reached production and the payment form started accepting empty submissions for a slice of users — checkout conversion dropped ~[8%] before anyone connected it to my release. When the funnel alert fired, I didn't wait to be sure it was me — I checked the release timeline, saw my change correlated, and said in the incident channel "this is likely mine, rolling back now." I reverted within ~[15] minutes, confirmed conversion recovered, then owned the postmortem myself rather than letting it be assigned to me.
Result Conversion was back to baseline the same afternoon; the customer impact was bounded to that window. The postmortem was blameless but I was explicit that the root cause was my change and my test gap — I didn't hide behind "the tests passed." (This is the setup; the what-changed close below is the part that makes it land.)
The close that makes Story A senior — say it as one movement straight after the result: "What I changed matters more than the incident. Three things: I added contract tests on the design system so a behavioural change to a shared hook fails CI instead of reaching a consumer; I moved anything touching a critical flow like checkout behind a canary release so a regression hits 1% of traffic, not 100%; and I stopped treating 'minor version, tests pass' as a proxy for 'safe' — for shared infra, safe means I've proven the contract, not that my own tests are green. We haven't shipped a silent design-system regression to a critical flow since." Notice the shape: you don't just fix the bug, you fix the class of bug, and you name the mental model that let it through. That's the move a 3-YOE candidate almost never makes.

Worked story B — the deadline you missed by over-engineering

Use this for "a time you missed a deadline" or "a decision you got wrong." It's a judgment failure, not a reliability one — you didn't slack, you over-invested — which is a safe and senior-flavoured thing to admit.

STARStory B — "I missed a deadline because I over-engineered the abstraction"
Situation I was tech lead on migrating ~[40] screens off our legacy component set onto the new design system, committed for one quarter. Leadership was counting on the date because a rebrand depended on it.
Task Deliver the migration on time. The concrete need was narrow: one brand, one theme, get the screens moved.
Action I talked myself into building a fully generic theming layer that could support any number of future brands — a config-driven system nobody had actually asked for. I spent ~[3] weeks on an abstraction for a requirement that didn't exist yet. Around the two-thirds mark it was obvious the real migration was behind, and I had to make the call: I killed my own abstraction, kept the one concrete theme, and re-pointed the team at moving screens.
Result We still slipped — shipped ~[2] weeks late, which I owned to leadership directly rather than letting it surface in standup. The migration landed and worked. The theming layer I'd built was deleted. The failure was entirely mine: I optimised for an imagined future over the committed present.
Story B's what-changed close: "What changed is how I treat abstraction and dates. I now ship the concrete version first and extract the abstraction only on the third real use — 'rule of three' — instead of predicting the future. And I timebox any speculative design work to a fixed spike; if it's not clearly paying off by the end of the box, it's cut. I'd rather refactor to a generalisation I've earned than pay weeks up front for one I've imagined. Since then I've hit every committed date I've owned." The senior signal here is naming the specific engineering discipline (rule of three, timeboxed spikes) that replaced the bad instinct — a rule, not a resolution to "be more careful."
A subtle failure mode inside these stories: burying the mistake so deep it stops sounding like a failure. If you spend 90 seconds on how heroically you fixed it and five words on what you did wrong, the interviewer reads it as "couldn't produce a real failure" — the same score as the humblebrag. Lead with the mistake plainly ("I broke checkout"; "I missed the date because I over-built"), spend real words owning it, then pivot to the recovery and the change. The ratio should feel roughly a third owning, a third fixing, a third what-changed.

Part 2 — Ambiguity & no clear spec

Different family, opposite direction. Failure looks backward at a mistake; ambiguity looks forward at a blank page. Keep them separate in your head — reaching for a failure story when you're asked about ambiguity (or vice versa) is a common misfire under pressure.

The questions in this family

  • "Tell me about a time you had to work with vague or incomplete requirements."
  • "Describe a project where no one told you exactly what to build."
  • "A time you had to make a decision without all the information you wanted."
  • "How do you handle a situation where the goal keeps changing / the scope is unclear?"
  • "Tell me about the most ambiguous project you've owned."

What the interviewer is actually testing

Comfort with ambiguity and bias for action. Seniority is largely defined by how much uncertainty you can absorb before you stall — a junior needs a ticket, a senior turns a one-sentence problem into a plan. The interviewer wants to see a specific loop: you don't freeze, and you don't guess blindly either. You make the ambiguity explicit — write down what you're assuming, name the decisions that have to be made, pick the smallest thing that produces a real signal, ship it, and let reality correct you. They're checking whether a blank page reads to you as paralysing or as permission.

Two opposite traps, and interviewers watch for both. The passive one — waiting for a spec. "The requirements weren't clear, so I asked the PM to clarify and waited." That's a mid-level answer: it outsources the ambiguity back up the chain and shows no ownership of the problem. Asking questions is fine; stopping at questions is the tell. The reckless one — building in the dark. Charging off and coding for two months on your own guess, then discovering you built the wrong thing. That's not bias for action, it's bias for motion — you skipped the cheap step of validating the assumption before the expensive step of building on it. The senior answer threads them: act fast, but make the first act a cheap probe (a doc, a prototype, a walking skeleton) that de-risks the guess before you commit real time.

Worked story C — the greenfield feature you had to scope yourself

Use this for any ambiguity or "vague requirements" prompt. The situation is deliberately under-specified because that's the point of the story. Swap in your own greenfield project and real numbers.

STARStory C — "The brief was one sentence: 'merchants need an analytics dashboard'"
Situation A PM came to me with a single line: "our merchants need an analytics dashboard." No spec, no designs, no defined metrics, no scope — just a felt need and a quarter to do something about it. It was greenfield and it was mine to figure out.
Task Turn that one sentence into something real that merchants would actually use, without burning the quarter building the wrong dashboard. The hard part wasn't the React — it was deciding what to build.
Action Instead of either waiting for a spec or guessing, I made the ambiguity explicit on paper. I wrote a one-page scoping doc: my assumptions ("merchants mostly want to answer three questions: how much did I sell, what sold, and is it up or down"), an explicit v1 cut (those three questions, last 30 days, one currency), and an out-of-scope list as long as the in-scope one (custom date ranges, exports, real-time — all "later"). I got 30 minutes each with [3] actual merchants and my PM to pressure-test the assumptions before writing feature code, then built a walking skeleton — one real metric wired end to end, backend query to chart — in ~[1] week and put it in front of them.
Result The skeleton immediately corrected me — merchants cared far more about "what sold" than the revenue headline I'd assumed was primary, so I re-ordered the build around that. Shipped v1 in ~[6] weeks, well inside the quarter, and it was right because the ambiguity got resolved by cheap signal early instead of an expensive miss late. The out-of-scope list became the actual roadmap.
The reusable technique inside Story C is worth naming on its own because you can apply it live in a system-design round too: when the spec is blank, write the assumptions down and make them the artifact. A one-page doc with "here's what I'm assuming, here's the v1 cut, here's what I'm explicitly not doing" turns an unownable ambiguity into a reviewable proposal. It converts silence into a fast yes/no, and — crucially — it makes your assumptions falsifiable, so a wrong one gets caught in a 30-minute review instead of after a month of building.
The ambiguity close, forward-looking rather than "what changed": "My rule with a vague brief is that the most expensive thing I can do is build confidently in the wrong direction, so I front-load the cheapest possible way to be wrong — write the assumptions down, cut the smallest end-to-end slice, and get it in front of a real user in days, not weeks. I'd rather ship a walking skeleton that proves the direction than a polished v1 that proves I misunderstood. Ambiguity isn't a blocker to me; it's the part of the job that's actually mine." That last line is the senior positioning in a sentence — owning ambiguity as your job rather than treating it as someone else's failure to spec.

The follow-up drill both families share

Whichever story you tell, the interviewer's next question is almost always the same: "What would you do differently?" — or its twins, "what did you learn?" and "how would you handle it now?" This is not a trap; it's the interviewer handing you the highest-value part of the answer and checking whether you have it ready. Most candidates fumble here because they already spent their learning in the main answer, or never had one. Two rules make it clean:

  • Have the answer pre-loaded — but don't spend it early. If you delivered the what-changed close well in the main story, you've partly answered this already. So go one level deeper here: give the second change, or the broader principle. For Story A: "beyond the contract tests, I now treat any change to shared infra as a change to a public API — I version and communicate it like one." That shows the learning generalised beyond the single incident.
  • "Differently" means a different decision, not a different outcome. "I'd have not broken checkout" is not an answer — of course you'd rather not have. Name the specific earlier decision you'd change: "I'd have written the contract test before the refactor, not after the incident taught me to." The interviewer wants the fork in the road you'd take differently, with hindsight, as a concrete engineering choice.
The wrong answer to "what would you do differently?" is "honestly, nothing — it worked out." Even if the recovery was flawless, that response says you extracted no learning, which collapses the whole story back to a neutral. There is always an earlier decision you'd change — the one that created the mistake or the ambiguity in the first place. Name it. And the equally-wrong opposite: a grovelling list of everything wrong with you. One sharp, specific "I'd do X instead of Y, here's why" beats both.

Interview Q&A

1. "Tell me about your biggest failure." How do I even start?

Start with the mistake stated plainly, in one sentence, before any context: "My biggest failure was shipping a design-system change that broke checkout for a slice of our users." Naming it up front signals you can face it. Then STAR it: the situation, that it was your decision, the blast radius, the recovery — and close on what you changed so the class of bug can't recur. Do not open with a wind-up ("well, it's hard to pick one…") and do not reach for a fake failure. The opening sentence is where the score is largely decided.

2. Isn't admitting a real failure just giving them a reason to reject me?

No — refusing to is the reason they reject you. The question presupposes you've failed; everyone at 7 YOE has. What's being scored is self-awareness, ownership and growth, none of which a fake failure can demonstrate. A clean, owned, learned-from failure reads as seniority. The candidates who get dinged are the ones whose "failure" is a humblebrag (evasive) or genuinely disqualifying (integrity, reliability). Stay in the middle — a real mistake of judgment or execution — and the honesty is an asset, not a liability.

3. What actually counts as a "disqualifying" failure I should never pick?

Anything about integrity or basic reliability rather than judgment. Don't confess: hiding a bug or a breach, cutting a security corner knowingly, missing a deadline because you didn't show up or didn't care, an ethics lapse, or torching a relationship past repair. Those aren't "I made a bad call" — they're "I can't be trusted with the system," which is exactly what the panel is protecting against. Safe territory: a technical misjudgment, an over-engineered solution, a wrong estimate, a bad assumption you built on. Consequential is fine; untrustworthy is not.

4. The interviewer keeps asking "and what else went wrong?" — are they trying to break me?

No, they're testing whether your ownership is real or a rehearsed veneer. The move is to stay in ownership and stay specific — give another honest layer ("I also assumed my unit tests covered the behaviour when they only covered the happy path") rather than getting defensive or pivoting to someone else's fault. Depth of self-awareness under a little pressure is a strong signal; brittleness or blame-shifting is a weak one. It's the same instinct a Bar Raiser probes for. Answer the deeper layer calmly and it works in your favour.

5. How do I talk about a production incident without sounding incompetent?

Frame it as incident response, not incident cause — the panel knows outages happen; they're watching how you behave in one. Emphasise the timeline: you detected/were alerted, you didn't wait to be certain it was you before flagging it, you mitigated fast (rollback), you ran a blameless postmortem, and you closed the loop with a systemic fix. Owning the cause plainly and demonstrating a calm, fast, structured response reads as competent, not incompetent. In fintech especially, "I broke it and here's the disciplined way I handled it" is a strong, on-target answer.

6. What's the difference between a failure story and a conflict story? They feel similar.

Different signal, so don't swap them. A failure story (this page) is about your mistake — the axis is ownership and growth. A conflict story (page 04) is about a disagreement with a person — the axis is influence, respect and resolution. If you tell a conflict story when asked for a failure, you'll sound like you're dodging by making it about someone else. Some material overlaps ("hard feedback I received" touches both), but pick the framing that matches the question's axis: for "failure," keep the spotlight on your own decision.

7. "Tell me about a time the requirements were vague." What are they really after?

Bias for action under uncertainty, plus judgment about how you act. They want to see you neither froze (waiting for a spec) nor charged off blind (building for months on a guess). The winning shape: make the ambiguity explicit (write down assumptions), cut the smallest end-to-end slice, get a fast real-world signal, adjust. If your story ends with "so I asked my manager to clarify and then built what they said," that's mid-level — the ownership of resolving the ambiguity stayed with them. Show that you turned the blank page into a plan.

8. How do I show "bias for action" without sounding reckless?

Make your first action a cheap, reversible probe rather than an irreversible commitment. Bias for action isn't "I skipped the thinking and started coding" — it's "I found the fastest way to learn, which was a one-week walking skeleton in front of real users, before committing the quarter." Pair speed with reversibility and you get action without recklessness. Say the trade-off out loud: "the most expensive mistake was building confidently in the wrong direction, so I front-loaded the cheapest way to be wrong." That sentence is the whole distinction.

9. "What would you do differently?" — I froze on this last time.

Have it pre-loaded and give a different decision, not a different outcome. Not "I'd not have broken it" (empty) — instead "I'd have written the contract test before the refactor, and treated the shared hook as a public API from the start." If you already gave your what-changed close in the main story, go one level deeper here: the broader principle, or the second guardrail. Never answer "nothing, it worked out" — that reads as zero learning and collapses the story to neutral. There's always an earlier fork you'd take differently.

10. Should I use the same story for "failure" and for "ambiguity"?

Generally no — they test opposite skills, so keep at least one dedicated story for each. A failure story centres a mistake you made and fixed; an ambiguity story centres a blank spec you turned into a plan. Trying to stretch one to cover both usually dilutes the signal for both. That said, a single rich project can yield a failure angle and an ambiguity angle if you open the two tellings with different sentences — which is exactly the re-aiming skill the story bank (page 02) is built to give you.

11. How long should a failure answer be, and how much on the recovery vs the mistake?

Aim for the standard ~2-minute STAR shape. Rough thirds: a third owning the mistake plainly, a third on the fix/recovery, a third on what changed permanently. The most common imbalance is spending 90% on the heroic recovery and 10 seconds on the mistake — which reads as "couldn't produce a real failure." Lead with the mistake in a plain sentence, give it real airtime, then earn the recovery and the learning. The what-changed close is non-negotiable; if you're running long, cut the middle, never the close.

12. What if my honest answer is that I've never really failed at anything big?

Then you haven't looked hard enough, and saying "I've never failed" is itself a red flag — it reads as either low self-awareness or not having operated at a level where failure is possible. Every 7-YOE engineer has: a regression that shipped, an estimate that was badly wrong, an abstraction that didn't pay off, a project that got cancelled after you argued to keep it, a hire or a design call you'd redo. Mine page 02's story-bank process for the mistakes, not just the wins. A senior engineer with no failures on tap is a contradiction the interviewer will notice.

What to remember

Failure & mistakes

  • Never fake it, never disqualify yourself. No humblebrags ("I work too hard"); no integrity/reliability confessions (hid a bug, no-showed). Pick a real mistake of judgment or execution that you fully owned and fixed.
  • The three signals: self-awareness, ownership, growth. Name the mistake plainly up front, say "I" not "we," and spend zero words blaming others — every one of those sentences costs you the ownership score.
  • The what-changed close is the part everyone skips and the part that scores. Don't just fix the bug, fix the class of bug and name the guardrail (contract tests, canary, rule of three). A failure with no learning is a confession; a failure with a permanent change is a judgment story.
  • Ratio ≈ a third owning · a third fixing · a third what-changed. Don't bury the mistake under the heroic recovery — that reads the same as having no real failure.
  • Two anchor shapes: a production regression you owned (Dive Deep / Earn Trust) and a deadline you missed by over-engineering (Learn & Be Curious / Deliver Results).

Ambiguity & no spec

  • The signal is bias for action under uncertainty. A blank page should read as permission, not paralysis. Seniority = how much ambiguity you absorb before you stall.
  • Thread the two traps: don't wait for a spec (passive, no ownership) and don't build blind for months (motion, not action). Make the first act a cheap probe.
  • The reusable move: write the assumptions down and make them the artifact. A one-page doc — assumptions · v1 cut · explicit out-of-scope — turns unownable ambiguity into a reviewable, falsifiable proposal, then ship a walking skeleton for fast real signal.
  • The line: "the most expensive thing I can do is build confidently in the wrong direction, so I front-load the cheapest way to be wrong." Maps to Bias for Action + Learn & Be Curious + Ownership.

Both families

  • The follow-up is always "what would you do differently?" Pre-load it; give a different decision, not a different outcome; never say "nothing, it worked out."
  • Keep failure and ambiguity stories separate — opposite skills (backward-looking ownership vs forward-looking action). One rich project can yield both angles via a different opening sentence.
  • These are the two families the Bar Raiser and fintech panels probe hardest, because they're where trust is decided. Own the mistake, own the ambiguity — both are your job, not someone else's failure.
The through-line of the whole page: a senior engineer treats a failure as a gap in a system and an ambiguity as a problem to scope — both are owned, neither is deflected. The junior version of each answer pushes the responsibility outward ("the tests passed," "the requirements were unclear"). The senior version pulls it in ("my test gap; my job to turn the blank page into a plan") and then shows the concrete thing that changed as a result. If you can say what you changed after the failure, and what you wrote down to tame the ambiguity, you have already out-answered most of the room.
