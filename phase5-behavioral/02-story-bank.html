02 — Build Your Story Bank

The one page that pays for the whole phase. Do the work here on paper, or wing the round.

Why this matters in the interview

The candidates who bomb the behavioral round almost never bomb it for lack of experience. They bomb it because they walk in and try to compose an answer live — searching seven years of memory, in real time, for "a time you disagreed with a manager," while the interviewer watches them stall. Seven years is too much to search under pressure. The fix is not a better memory or a calmer nerve; it's preparation that happens before the room. You pre-select eight to ten of your strongest stories, write each one once in STAR shape, learn what themes each can serve, and walk in with a small, deep, rehearsed set instead of an infinite, shallow, unindexed one. Every strong candidate you've ever been impressed by in a debrief did exactly this. It reads as "senior and composed." It is actually just homework.

The counter-intuitive part — and the thing that makes this page high-leverage rather than a chore — is that you do not need a story per question. The behavioral round has maybe fifteen recurring question themes, but they overlap massively: one good project usually contains leadership and conflict and a measurable result and a moment of ambiguity, all at once. A well-chosen story is a Swiss Army knife you re-aim by changing which part you emphasise. So the real task isn't "generate forty answers"; it's "pick ten rich projects and learn to angle each one four different ways." This page builds that: the selection criteria, the matrix that proves the coverage, ten worked templates in your material, how to recover metrics you never formally tracked, the index-card format you revise from the night before, and how to rehearse it aloud without turning into a person reciting a script.

1 — A few stories cover the whole round

Start with the arithmetic, because it's what makes the whole approach work. The behavioral question bank an interviewer draws from is large in surface — "tell me about a time you led," "…you disagreed with someone," "…you failed," "…you had no clear requirements," "…you influenced without authority," "…you delivered impact," "…you got tough feedback" — but it is small in substance. Every one of those is a probe for a signal, and a single meaty project throws off several signals at once. The design-system rollout you led is simultaneously a leadership story, an influence-without-authority story, a cross-team-conflict story, and an impact story. You don't need four projects for those four questions. You need one project and the skill to walk into it from four doors.

That reframing changes what "prepared" means. Un-prepared is trying to recall a fresh incident for each new question. Prepared is: the interviewer says "disagreed with a peer," and you think "API-contract fight — angle it on the conflict and the resolution"; two questions later they say "influenced without authority," and you think "same underlying skill, different project — the TypeScript adoption, angle it on winning people over." You're not retrieving; you're routing. And routing is fast and calm, which is exactly the affect the round is grading.

This is the shape, not your script. Every model story on this page is a template in the correct structure, built on plausible senior-frontend-pivoting-to-full-stack situations — drop your real project into the skeleton and keep the structure. The metrics are round placeholders (40%, 2.1s, ~15 teams) flagged for you to replace with your real numbers. Never claim a number, a project, or an outcome that wasn't yours. A fabricated story dies on the first follow-up question, and the follow-up is where senior candidates are actually separated from juniors — you cannot improvise depth about something that didn't happen.

One more consequence worth internalising: a bank of ten deep stories is more robust than a bank of forty shallow ones, because the round is adversarial in a specific way. Good interviewers drill. They ask "why did you do it that way and not the obvious other way," "what did the person who disagreed with you actually say," "what would you do differently." Depth survives drilling; breadth doesn't. Ten stories you can each defend for five minutes beats forty you can each recite for ninety seconds and then fold on.

The mental model to carry in: "I'm not preparing answers, I'm preparing a small set of projects I know cold, and I re-aim the same project at whatever they ask." Say that to yourself before you walk in and you'll stop trying to pattern-match a unique story to every question — which is the single biggest source of the mid-round freeze.

2 — Picking the right stories

Not every project makes a good story, and the mistake is to reach for the ones you're proudest of technically. The cleanest architecture you ever built is often a terrible behavioral story, because nothing went wrong, no one disagreed with you, and there was no judgment call — it's a competence story, and competence is assumed at 7 YOE. The behavioral round grades ownership, judgment, influence and impact, not effort or elegance. A story earns its place in your bank when it contains tension and a decision you made. Screen every candidate project against three things.

IngredientWhy it mattersThe test question to ask the project
Conflict or tension Almost half the round is conflict-shaped (disagreed with, pushed back, difficult stakeholder, hard feedback). A frictionless project can't answer any of them. Tension is where judgment becomes visible. Was there a disagreement, a hard trade-off, a person I had to win over, or a moment it nearly went wrong?
Ambiguity or a decision Seniority is graded on what you do without a spec. A story where you were handed exact instructions and followed them shows nothing. A story where you chose the direction shows everything. Did I decide the approach, or was it decided for me? Was the requirement vague when I started?
Measurable impact "Leading with impact" (page 01) needs a number to lead with. A result you can quantify — latency, revenue, adoption, time saved, defects avoided — is the difference between "I did a thing" and "I moved the business." What changed because of this, and can I put a number on it — even a reconstructed one (section 5)?

The strongest stories have all three; a story with two of three is still a keeper; a story with one is a filler you use only if you're cornered. Run your last seven years through that filter and you'll find the ten. Bias toward the recent (last 2–3 years reads as current) and toward projects you drove rather than rode along on — "we" stories don't survive the "what did you specifically do?" follow-up that every interviewer asks.

The two failure patterns in story selection, before delivery even starts. First, the humblebrag failure: picking "my biggest weakness is I care too much / I worked too hard" dressed as a failure story. Interviewers have heard it a thousand times and score it as evasion. A real failure has a real cost. Second, the disqualifying failure: a story where you were negligent, dishonest, or the outcome was catastrophic and unrecovered. The target is a failure that was genuine, was yours, hurt a bit, and that you visibly learned from and fixed — not one that makes the interviewer worry about hiring you. Section 4 and page 05 show you where that line sits.
A filter to say to yourself while choosing: "Does this story show a decision I made under pressure, or just work I completed?" Keep the decision stories, cut the completion stories. At 3 YOE you're forgiven for a bank full of "I built X." At 7 YOE the interviewer is listening for "I decided X, over Y, because Z, and here's what it cost and what it returned." That's the altitude the whole bank should sit at.

3 — The story-bank matrix

This is the artefact. Ten stories down the side, the eight recurring question themes across the top, a tick where a story can serve a theme, and a ★ where that theme is the story's primary angle — the one you lead with if the question lands squarely on it. Build your own version of this table with your real projects; it is the single most useful page you will produce in this whole phase, and it's what proves the claim from section 1: ten well-chosen stories blanket the entire round, and most themes are covered three or four times over, so you're never stuck if the interviewer already "used up" your best one.

2. Core Web Vitals perf push ✓✓✓★

10. Missed-deadline / regression I owned ✓★✓★

Read the columns, not the rows, and the point jumps out. Every theme has at least three stories under it. Conflict: stories 1, 5, 7, 9. Failure: 3, 4, 10. Ambiguity: 2, 3, 4, 8, 10. Handling feedback: 5, 6, 10. So when the interviewer opens with "tell me about a conflict" and you spend your API-contract story, and then twenty minutes later asks "tell me about a time you disagreed with a decision," you still have the PM-scope story and the TypeScript story in reserve — angled differently so it doesn't feel like the same beat twice. That redundancy is the entire reason to build the matrix rather than a flat list.

Use the matrix to find your gaps, not just your coverage. Scan the columns: if "Failure" only has one weak entry, that's the theme you're one unlucky question away from fumbling — go find a second real failure now, on paper, calmly, instead of manufacturing one live in the room. The senior move is walking in knowing your thinnest column and having deliberately shored it up, because the interviewer will absolutely find it if you haven't.
Keep the ★ discipline. A story can serve five themes, but it has one or two it tells best — where the tension in the story is genuinely about that signal. Leading with a ★ theme means the story's natural climax is the answer to the question. Leading with a mere ✓ theme means you're bending the story, and bent stories feel bent. Fire ★ first; fall back to ✓ only when your ★ story for that theme is already spent.

4 — Your ten starter stories

Here is the matrix filled in — ten stories in the correct STAR shape, built from the situations a senior frontend-pivoting-to-full-stack engineer actually lives. They are compressed here to their skeletons; the archetype pages (03–06) blow the relevant ones up into full two-minute answers. Your job on this page is to replace each skeleton with your real project — same structure, your situation, your numbers.

Read these as templates, never as claims. The metrics (40%, 2.1s → 0.9s, ~15 teams, p95) are round placeholders marking where a number goes — swap in yours, and if you don't have a formal number, section 5 shows you how to reconstruct an honest one. If a template doesn't map to anything you did, drop it and use one of your own that hits the same theme. The structure is the product; the specifics must be true.

1 — The design-system rollout  ★ Leadership/Ownership · Influence · Impact · Teamwork · Conflict

S/T: ~15 product teams each shipped their own buttons, modals, and date-pickers; the UI was inconsistent, accessibility was failing audits, and every team re-solved the same problems. No one owned it, and it wasn't on any roadmap. A: I proposed and built a shared component library, but the hard part wasn't the code — it was adoption across teams that didn't report to me. I ran a design-dev working group, migrated two pilot teams myself to prove the value, wrote migration codemods so switching cost hours not weeks, and handled the teams that resisted (see story 5's cousin) by co-owning their first migration rather than mandating it. R: ~15 teams on the system within [X] months, [~40%] less UI code duplicated, accessibility audit pass rate from [~60% to ~95%], and new-feature UI build time roughly halved. Angle it on: ownership (you created the mandate), influence (no authority over those teams), or impact (the numbers) depending on the ask.

2 — The Core Web Vitals performance push  ★ Impact · Ownership · Ambiguity · Influence

S/T: The main app's Largest Contentful Paint was [~4.2s] on mobile, we were failing Core Web Vitals, and there was circumstantial evidence it was costing conversions — but no one had proven it or owned fixing it. A: I made it my problem. Profiled the real bottlenecks (render-blocking bundles, an unoptimised image pipeline, a third-party tag), rather than guessing; got buy-in for a two-sprint budget by tying the fix to a revenue estimate; code-split the bundle, moved to responsive/lazy images, deferred the tag, and added a CWV budget to CI so it couldn't regress. R: LCP [2.1s → 0.9s], we passed CWV, and the A/B test showed [~X%] conversion lift on mobile — the number that turned it from a "tech-debt nice-to-have" into a funded win. Angle it on: impact (lead with the conversion number), ambiguity (the slowness had no obvious cause), or influence (you sold the budget).

3 — The risky framework migration  ★ Ambiguity · Ownership · Impact · Failure(minor) · Influence

S/T: A large legacy front end (old Angular / a home-grown framework) was slowing every team down and couldn't hire against; leadership wanted it modernised but there was no safe way to stop the world and rewrite. A: I argued against the big-bang rewrite everyone wanted and for the strangler-fig approach — run new (React) alongside old behind a routing shim, migrate route by route, ship continuously. I built the interop layer, migrated the highest-traffic flow first to de-risk, and set a measurable exit criterion so "are we done" wasn't a vibe. R: [~70%] of traffic on the new stack in [X] months with zero big-bang outage; the honest wrinkle to include — I under-estimated the shared-auth interop and lost [~two weeks], which is where the failure/learning angle comes from. Angle it on: ambiguity (no safe path existed), ownership (you set the strategy), or as a controlled failure (the interop miss + what you changed).

4 — The production incident  ★ Failure · Ownership · Ambiguity · Impact · Teamwork

S/T: A deploy I was involved in took checkout down / spiked error rates [~X%] during peak; revenue was actively bleeding and the cause wasn't obvious. A: I took incident lead even though it was partly my change — called it, rolled back first and diagnosed second (stop the bleeding before you satisfy your curiosity), coordinated with backend and on-call in one channel, and once stable ran the blameless post-mortem. R: Mitigated in [~12 min], root cause was [a missing null guard / a cache stampede on a cold key], and — the part that matters — I drove the systemic fixes: a canary deploy, an alert on the specific metric, and a contract test that would have caught it. Angle it on: failure (own the change), ownership (you led the response), or ambiguity (debugging live under pressure). The follow-up here is always "what did you change so it can't recur" — have that ready; it's the whole point.

5 — The scope disagreement with a PM  ★ Conflict · Influence · Impact · Handling-feedback · Ownership

S/T: A PM wanted to ship a feature by a hard date by cutting error handling, empty states, and accessibility to "fast-follow later." I believed it would ship a visibly broken experience and the fast-follow would never come. A: I didn't dig in emotionally or go over their head. I disagreed with data — pulled support-ticket rates from the last time we shipped "we'll fix it later," and re-scoped so the most-used path was fully finished and the long-tail states were the honest cut. I made it a trade-off conversation, not a fight. R: We shipped on the date with the core path solid; the deferred work was a two-item list we actually did, and support tickets came in [~X%] below the previous launch. Angle it on: conflict (disagreed and reached a better answer), backbone/influence (data not opinion), or handling-feedback (if the story flips to a time they pushed back on you and were partly right).

6 — Mentoring a struggling junior  ★ Leadership/Ownership · ★ Handling-feedback · Teamwork · Influence · Impact

S/T: A junior on the team was stalling — PRs bouncing repeatedly, missing estimates, visibly losing confidence — and it was starting to affect the team's throughput and their standing. A: I took it on without being asked to. Rather than just fixing their code, I paired weekly, gave specific, kind, actionable feedback instead of vague "be more careful," broke their work into smaller reviewable chunks, and gave them a contained but real piece to own so they could rebuild confidence. I also fed the pattern back to how we onboarded. R: Within [~a quarter] they were shipping independently and later mentored someone themselves; our PR-cycle time on their work dropped from [~X to Y]. Angle it on: leadership (grew a person, not just code), giving feedback (the how of hard feedback delivered well), or teamwork. This is your best pure people story — most engineers' banks are all systems and no humans, and this one fixes that gap.

7 — The API-contract fight with backend  ★ Conflict · Teamwork · Influence · Ownership

S/T: The backend team wanted to ship an API that mirrored their database tables — chatty, requiring the client to make [~6] calls and stitch data together for one screen — and were treating the shape as their call alone. It would have made the front end slow and brittle. A: Instead of complaining in standup, I came with a concrete alternative: I wrote the client's ideal response as a document, showed the round-trip and rendering cost of their version versus a screen-shaped one, and proposed a BFF / aggregation layer as the compromise so neither side owned the other's model. I framed it as "how do we make the whole product fast," not "your API is wrong." R: We agreed on the aggregated contract; the screen went from [~6 calls to 1] and time-to-interactive dropped by [~X]. Just as important, we set up contract tests so the fight didn't recur every sprint. Angle it on: conflict (cross-functional), influence without authority, or teamwork (found the win-win). Especially strong for your pivot narrative — it shows you think about the backend, not just consume it.

8 — The ambiguous greenfield feature  ★ Ambiguity · Ownership · Impact · Leadership · Teamwork

S/T: Leadership asked for "a dashboard / a self-serve X" with a one-line brief, no spec, no designs, and stakeholders who each wanted something different. The ambiguity was the whole difficulty. A: I didn't wait for clarity — I created it. Interviewed the stakeholders to extract the actual jobs-to-be-done, wrote a one-page problem definition and got it signed off so we were building the same thing, shipped a deliberately thin vertical slice in [~2 weeks] to turn opinions into feedback on something real, then iterated. I made the roadmap visible so scope creep was a conversation, not a surprise. R: Shipped a focused v1 that [~X users] adopted / that hit [metric], instead of the sprawling everything-machine the initial requests would have produced. Angle it on: ambiguity (you manufactured clarity), ownership (bias for action without a spec), or leadership (aligned stakeholders). This is your headline Amazon "Bias for Action / Dive Deep" story.

9 — Championing TypeScript / a testing culture  ★ Influence · Ownership · Conflict/Backbone · Impact

S/T: The codebase was plain JS with thin test coverage; bugs that types or tests would have caught were shipping regularly, but the team was skeptical of the migration cost and no one wanted to be the one to push it. A: I built the case instead of just advocating — categorised a month of production bugs and showed [~X%] were type- or test-preventable, converted one high-churn module to TypeScript with tests as a proof, measured the drop in its bug rate, and made adoption incremental (allow-JS, strict mode ratcheted up file by file) so no one faced a scary rewrite. I held the line when momentum flagged. R: Team-wide adoption over [~X months], preventable-bug rate down [~Y%], and — the softer win — code review shifted from "does this work" to design. Angle it on: influence (changed how a team works with no authority to mandate it), backbone (persisted against skepticism with data), or ownership (you saw a systemic problem and fixed the system).

10 — The missed deadline / regression I owned  ★ Failure · ★ Handling-feedback · Ambiguity · Ownership

S/T: I committed to a delivery date and missed it / shipped a regression that a stakeholder caught before I did — a real, non-catastrophic miss that was genuinely on me (under-scoped the edge cases, skipped a test path under time pressure). A: I owned it immediately and without deflection — flagged the slip early rather than hiding it and hoping, took the feedback without getting defensive, did the honest root-cause (it was my estimate and my process, not "the requirements changed"), and changed the process: broke estimates down finer, added the missing test class, and started surfacing risk earlier in the sprint. R: Recovered the delivery with a re-planned date I then hit, and the process change meant my estimates were materially more reliable afterward. Angle it on: failure (a real one, owned and fixed), handling feedback (took a hard message well and acted on it), or ambiguity. Keep this one genuinely humble — the signal it sends is self-awareness, and self-awareness dies the instant the "failure" turns out to be a brag.

When an interviewer says "tell me about another time…" and you're reaching for a second story on the same theme, say the frame out loud: "A different example — this one was less about X and more about Y." It signals that you have depth in reserve, that you're choosing deliberately rather than scraping the barrel, and it stops two stories about "conflict" from blurring into the same beat. Ten stories, cross-indexed, is what lets you do that all round without ever repeating yourself.

5 — Reconstructing metrics you never tracked

The most common objection to "quantify everything" is honest: "I didn't measure that. We didn't have a dashboard for it. I don't have the number." That is fine, and it does not excuse you from having a number — because the interviewer isn't auditing your analytics, they're checking whether you think in terms of impact. A reasonable, clearly-estimated number beats "it was a lot faster" every time. You almost always have more data than you think; you just have to reconstruct it. Here's how to get an honest number for a result you never formally tracked.

You don't have…Reconstruct it from…The number becomes
A latency / performance metric Re-measure the old version if it still exists in git history; or the Lighthouse/CWV report; or "it went from users complaining to no complaints"; or a before/after you can re-run locally. "LCP dropped from roughly 4s to under 1s" — round, hedged, credible.
An adoption / usage number Team count, repo count, PRs migrated, Slack channel members, "every team in the org except one." "~15 of our ~17 product teams adopted it."
A revenue / conversion figure What the business tracked even if you didn't; the A/B test if there was one; or size it: "on our traffic, a 1% conversion move is roughly $X, and the test showed a lift." "A low-single-digit-percent conversion lift, which at our volume was meaningful enough to fund the work."
A time-saved / productivity number Estimate the unit and multiply: "each new form used to take ~2 days of UI plumbing; after the design system, ~half a day; across ~30 forms a year that's ~45 engineer-days." "Roughly halved new-UI build time — on the order of tens of engineer-days a year."
A quality / bug number Count from the issue tracker; bug-bash results; support-ticket volume before vs after; "we went from a hotfix most weeks to roughly one a quarter." "Preventable production bugs dropped by well over half."

The reconstruction method is always the same three steps: find the unit (a form, a page load, a ticket, a team), estimate the per-unit change honestly, and multiply by the volume. Say your assumptions out loud — "assuming roughly thirty new forms a year" — because a visible, reasonable estimate reads as more credible than a suspiciously precise figure, not less. Interviewers trust "about 40%, based on the duplication we removed" over "40.3%," because the first shows you know where the number came from.

Do not invent precision you don't have, and do not round up into a lie. "We cut load time by 80%" when it was more like 40% is the kind of claim that unravels when the interviewer asks "how did you measure that?" and you have no method. The honest move is the hedge — "roughly," "on the order of," "a low-single-digit percent" — paired with the reasoning for the estimate. A defensible approximation is a strength; a precise fabrication is a landmine, because the follow-up question is specifically designed to find it.
The framing that turns a missing metric into a strength: "We didn't instrument it at the time, so let me reconstruct it — each form was about two days of UI work and we had roughly thirty a year, so halving that saved on the order of thirty engineer-days annually." Estimating impact live, with stated assumptions, demonstrates exactly the business-sense-plus-rigour the round is grading — arguably better than reciting a number off a dashboard, because it shows the thinking, not just the result.

6 — The one-page cheat sheet

You do not revise from ten full essays the night before an interview — you'd re-memorise them into a stiff recital, which is the failure mode section 7 is about. You revise from index cards: one per story, each small enough to fit on a physical 3×5 card or one screen. The card holds only the load-bearing hooks — enough to trigger the story you already know, not to read it out. The whole bank fits on a single page, which is exactly what you skim in the ten minutes before the call. Here's the format for one card. 

┌─ STORY 5 · SCOPE DISAGREEMENT WITH PM ──────────────────────────┐
│ Themes:  ★Conflict · Influence · Handling-feedback · Impact      │
│                                                                  │
│ S/T:  PM wanted to cut error-handling + a11y to hit a date       │
│ A:    disagreed w/ DATA (old support tickets) → re-scoped so     │
│       core path finished, long-tail = the honest cut            │
│ R:    shipped on date · tickets ~X% below last launch           │
│                                                                  │
│ Metric to lead with:  "~X% fewer support tickets"                │
│ Follow-up ready:      "what if the PM had refused?" → escalate   │
│                        as a shared trade-off, not a veto        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Six lines, and every one earns its place. Themes (with the ★) is your routing index — you scan it to pick which card answers the question just asked. S / A / R is one line each: not the sentences you'll say, just the beats, so you reconstruct the full answer live and it comes out fresh instead of canned. Metric to lead with is the impact you open on (page 01's "lead with the result"). Follow-up ready is the one drill question you know is coming — "what would you do differently," "what if it hadn't worked" — pre-answered so it can't ambush you. That last line is what separates a bank that survives an interviewer who digs from one that folds on the second question.

Lay ten of these on one page — a compact table works just as well as literal cards — and you have your entire behavioral prep on a surface you can review in the time it takes the interviewer to join the call. A workable table layout for the whole bank:

#Story (trigger)★ Primary theme(s)Lead metricThe drill answer
1Design-system rolloutOwnership · Influence~15 teams · ~40% less dup"how'd you handle resisters?" → co-owned their migration
4Prod incidentFailure · Ownershipmitigated ~12 min"what stops recurrence?" → canary + alert + contract test
7API-contract fightConflict · Influence6 calls → 1"what if backend said no?" → BFF as neutral ground

Build the card after you've written the story out in full once — never instead of it. The full write-up is where you discover the story's actual shape, find the number, and decide the ★ theme; the card is the compression of work you've already done. A card you make without having written the long version first is just a prompt for a story you don't actually know yet, and it'll show.

7 — Rehearsing out loud without a script

There is a specific trap at the end of all this preparation, and it's worth naming because the well-prepared candidate is the one most likely to fall into it: over-rehearsing into a memorised script. A word-for-word answer sounds word-for-word — flat, over-smooth, eyes-up-and-to-the-left as you retrieve the next sentence — and it collapses the instant the interviewer interrupts with a follow-up, because a recital has no random access. You've memorised a paragraph, not learned a story, so a question that lands in the middle of it derails you completely. The goal is the opposite: know the story so well from the beats that you can tell it fresh, differently, every time, and answer a question from any point in it.

The mechanism that prevents scripting is simple: rehearse from the bullet card, never from a written-out answer. If your prep artefact is full sentences, you will memorise the sentences. If it's the six-line card, there are no sentences to memorise — you're forced to generate the words live each time, which is exactly what you'll do in the room. Same content, different phrasing, every rep. That's what "known cold but not scripted" feels like.

The drill, in order of value:

  • Say it out loud, standing up, timed to two minutes. Not in your head — out loud. The single biggest gap in all of behavioral prep is that a story feels crisp when you think it and turns into rambling the first time you say it. Your mouth is slower and messier than your mind; only rehearsal out loud closes that gap. Two minutes is the target length (page 01) — a story that runs four minutes on the first telling needs tightening, and you only learn that by hearing it.
  • Record one telling and watch it back. It's uncomfortable and it's worth more than three more silent read-throughs. You'll catch the filler ("so basically… kind of… you know"), the place you buried the result at the end instead of leading with it, and the ninety-second Situation that should have been fifteen seconds. You cannot hear these while you're producing them; you can only see them on playback.
  • Have someone throw the follow-ups. Get a friend to ask "what would you do differently," "what did the other person actually say," "why not the other approach" mid-story. This is the rep that builds random access — the thing a memorised script fundamentally lacks — and it's the single best predictor of surviving a Bar-Raiser-style interviewer who drills.
  • Practise re-aiming, not just reciting. Take one story and deliver it three ways — once for "conflict," once for "influence," once for "impact" — changing which beat you dwell on and what you open with. This is the skill from section 1 made physical, and it's what lets the ten-story bank cover forty questions.
The tell that you've over-rehearsed: you get irritated by a follow-up because it knocked you off your track. A well-learned story welcomes the interruption — "good question, so what actually happened there was…" — because you know the material, not a paragraph. A scripted one treats the follow-up as an obstacle to getting back to the recital. Interviewers read that difference instantly, and the scripted version reads as junior no matter how polished the words are. If a follow-up ever makes you feel derailed, you've memorised where you should have learned.
The standard to rehearse to: you should be able to tell any story in your bank in two minutes, lead with the metric, and take a hard follow-up from the middle of it without losing your place — and it should sound slightly different every time you tell it. That "slightly different every time" is the proof you've learned the story rather than a script, and it's the single most reliable signal that separates the candidate who did this homework from the one who tried to wing seven years of memory live in the room.

Interview Q&A

These are the meta-questions about your prep — some you'll be asked directly, some you should be able to answer for yourself as a check that the bank is actually ready.

1. How many stories do I actually need?

Eight to ten strong ones, and no more. Fewer than eight and you'll get "tell me about another time" and run dry, or repeat a story and signal a shallow bank. More than ten and you can't keep them rehearsed to the depth that survives follow-ups — you trade the depth that actually scores for a breadth that doesn't.

The number that matters isn't the count, it's the coverage: build the matrix and check every theme column has at least two or three entries. Ten stories that blanket all eight themes with redundancy beats fifteen that pile up on "leadership" and leave "failure" with one weak entry.

2. What if the interviewer asks about something none of my ten stories covers?

First, it's rarer than you fear — the ten themes in the matrix genuinely cover the vast majority of what's asked. But when it happens, don't force a bad fit and don't freeze. Buy a beat honestly — "let me think of a good example of that" — and then reach for the story whose underlying skill is closest, and angle it. "A time you had to learn something fast" isn't in your matrix by name, but the migration story or the pivot-to-full-stack narrative both contain it.

The skill you built in section 1 — routing by signal rather than retrieving by keyword — is exactly what saves you here. You're never really out of stories; you're one re-aim away from one that fits.

3. Can I reuse the same story for two different questions in one interview?

Reusing the same project is fine and often unavoidable — a rich project legitimately contains both a conflict and an impact. But announce the re-aim so it doesn't feel like you only have one story: "I touched on this project earlier for the conflict piece — here it's more about the influence it took to get everyone on board."

Telling the same angle twice is the thing to avoid, because it reads as an empty bank. That's precisely why the matrix has redundancy: when your ★ conflict story is spent, you fire the ✓ conflict story from a different project rather than re-running the first one.

4. My best projects are technical wins with no conflict. Are they useless as stories?

Not useless, but weaker than you think, and you should know why. A frictionless technical win is a competence story, and competence is assumed at 7 YOE — it shows the interviewer nothing they're grading. The fix is usually to find the tension that was actually there and you're glossing over: the trade-off you chose, the person who wanted a different approach, the ambiguity before you had a plan. Almost every "clean" project had friction; you've just smoothed it out of the retelling.

If a project genuinely had no tension, no decision, and no ambiguity, it's a filler — keep it for "tell me about a project you're proud of" and don't count it toward your ten.

5. I don't have hard metrics for most of my work. What do I do?

Reconstruct them (section 5): find the unit, estimate the per-unit change honestly, multiply by volume, and state your assumptions out loud. "We didn't track it, but each form was ~2 days and we had ~30 a year, so halving it saved on the order of 30 engineer-days" is a strong answer — arguably stronger than reading a number off a dashboard, because it shows the business reasoning.

What you must not do is either give up ("it was faster, I don't have a number") or fabricate precision ("we cut it 81%"). A hedged, reasoned estimate is credible; a suspiciously exact figure invites the "how did you measure that?" question that a fabrication can't survive.

6. Isn't preparing stories in advance "gaming" the interview?

No — it's the expected behaviour, and interviewers assume you've done it. The behavioral round tests whether you can communicate real experience with structure and self-awareness, not whether you can retrieve it cold under stress. Nobody thinks worse of a candidate for being prepared; they think worse of one who rambles because they weren't.

The line you must not cross is between preparing your real stories and fabricating stories that didn't happen. Prepared-and-true is the whole game. Rehearsed-and-false dies on the first follow-up, because you can't improvise depth about something you didn't live.

7. How recent do my stories need to be?

Bias hard toward the last two to three years — recent work reads as "this is who I am now," and a senior story from six years ago quietly raises the question of what you've done since. One older story is fine if it's genuinely your best example of a specific theme (a formative failure, say), but the bulk of the bank should be current.

For your specific pivot narrative, lean on the stories that show the full-stack direction — the API-contract fight, anything touching the backend — because they double as evidence for the positioning, not just the theme.

8. Should I write my stories out word-for-word first, or just make the cards?

Write each one out in full once — that's where you discover the real shape, find the metric, and decide the primary theme. Then compress to the card and rehearse only from the card. The full write-up is a thinking tool, not a script to memorise; the moment you rehearse from full sentences, you memorise sentences, and memorised sentences are the scripted-recital failure from section 7.

So: long-form once to build it, card to revise and rehearse from. Never skip the long-form (the card alone is a prompt for a story you don't yet know), and never rehearse from the long-form (that's how you script yourself).

9. How do I keep two "conflict" stories from sounding like the same answer?

Choose them so the conflict is structurally different, and signal the difference when you switch. The PM-scope story is a conflict over priorities with someone in another function; the API-contract story is a conflict over a technical decision with a peer team; the TypeScript story is conflict as persistent persuasion against skepticism. Different shapes of the same skill.

Then flag it out loud: "a different kind of disagreement — this one was less about priorities and more about a technical call." That one clause tells the interviewer you have range, not one conflict story you're stretching.

10. The interviewer keeps drilling into one story. Is that bad?

It's the opposite of bad — it usually means the story is landing and they're probing for depth to justify the score. Sustained drilling is a buying signal. What you need is to have the depth: the "what would you do differently," the "what did the other person say," the "why not the obvious alternative." That's exactly why the card has a follow-up-ready line and why you practise with someone throwing follow-ups.

The failure here isn't being drilled — it's being drilled and running out of substance by the third question, which is what happens with a memorised paragraph instead of a lived story. Depth survives the drill; a script doesn't.

11. How is this different at a FAANG values loop versus an enterprise/fintech interview?

The bank is identical; only the framing re-aims (page 07 does this in full). For Amazon you'll tag each story to the Leadership Principles it demonstrates and lean into data and "what did you do." For enterprise/fintech (Optum, JPMC, Goldman) the same stories re-angle toward ownership under regulation, incident response, working across siloed teams, and a risk-and-compliance mindset — your production-incident and migration stories carry more weight there.

The point of building one well-indexed bank is exactly this reusability: you don't prep two sets of stories, you prep one set and learn to point it at whichever rubric is in the room.

12. How long does building the bank actually take, and when do I do it?

Budget real time — the schedule on the course home gives this three to four days for a reason, and it's the highest-return time in the whole phase. Roughly: half a day to brainstorm candidate projects and run them through the three-ingredient filter; a day or two to write each of the ten out in full and reconstruct the metrics; a few hours to build the matrix, spot the thin columns, and make the cards; then the rest is rehearsal, which continues right up to the interviews.

Do it early in your prep, not the night before — the archetype pages (03–06) assume the bank exists so you can practise mapping your real stories to each question, and the rehearsal has to be spread over days, out loud, to actually stick.

What to remember

  • A few stories cover the whole round. The behavioral bank is large in surface, small in substance — one rich project answers four different questions. You're not preparing forty answers; you're preparing eight to ten deep projects and re-aiming each one. Prepared means routing by signal, not retrieving by keyword.
  • Pick for tension, not elegance. A story earns its place only if it has conflict or a decision, ambiguity, and measurable impact. Your cleanest technical win is often a bad story — competence is assumed at 7 YOE; the round grades ownership, judgment, influence, and impact.
  • The matrix is the artefact. Ten stories × eight themes, ★ for primary, ✓ for serviceable. Read the columns: every theme should have three-plus entries, so you're never stuck when your best story is spent. Use it to find your thinnest column and shore it up before the room does it for you.
  • The ten templates are shapes, not scripts. Drop your real project and real numbers into each skeleton; the metrics on this page are placeholders. Never claim a story that isn't yours — fabrication dies on the first follow-up, which is where senior candidates are actually separated.
  • Reconstruct missing metrics; don't skip them. Find the unit, estimate the per-unit change, multiply by volume, state your assumptions out loud. A hedged, reasoned estimate ("roughly 40%, based on the duplication we removed") beats both "it was faster" and a fabricated "40.3%."
  • Revise from index cards, not essays. Six lines per story — themes, one-line S/A/R, lead metric, follow-up-ready. Write the full version once to build the story; rehearse only from the card so you never memorise sentences.
  • Rehearse out loud, timed, recorded — and never scripted. The gap between the story in your head and the story out of your mouth is the entire round. Know it cold from the beats so you can tell it fresh every time and take a hard follow-up from the middle without losing your place. If an interruption ever derails you, you've memorised where you should have learned.
The one sentence that captures this whole page: you are not preparing answers, you are building a small, deep, cross-indexed set of true stories and learning to point any one of them at whatever you're asked. Do the work here — on paper, out loud, over several days — and the archetype pages that follow become "which story do I fire" instead of "what do I even say." Skip it, and no amount of framing on pages 03–08 will save an empty bank.
