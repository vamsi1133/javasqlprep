06 — "Tell Me About Yourself" & Why-Company

The first ninety seconds and the last five minutes of every loop. They frame everything in between — waste them and every strong story afterward is fighting uphill.

Why this matters in the interview

Everything else in this phase is about the questions the interviewer asks. This page is about the two moments they don't ask you to prove anything — the open ("so, tell me about yourself") and the close ("what questions do you have for me?") — and those two moments carry more weight than their airtime suggests. The opener sets the frame: it decides which of your stories the interviewer is primed to hear, whether they read you as a senior engineer who owns outcomes or a strong coder who does tasks, and — for you specifically — whether your pivot from frontend expert to full-stack reads as deliberate range or as papering over a gap. The close is the one part of the interview you fully control, and the questions you ask are graded as hard as the answers you give: they are the clearest signal of your level that exists, because a senior engineer and a junior engineer are curious about visibly different things.

There's a specific tightrope on this page that the rest of your prep doesn't have. Your positioning is a senior 7-YOE engineer, frontend-expert, with strong current Java/Spring backend — and the honest version of that is a genuinely compelling story, but only if you tell it precisely. Over-claim ("I'm a seven-year full-stack engineer") and a single follow-up about the JVM memory model or Spring's transaction propagation exposes you, and now you're the candidate who inflates. Under-sell ("I'm mostly a frontend person, still learning the backend") and you've handed them a reason to down-level you before you've written a line of code. The whole game on this page is saying the true thing in the confident register: deep frontend, deliberately extended into backend, currently shipping Java/Spring in production, pivoting to full-stack on purpose. This tutorial gives you the exact shape of that pitch — as a template to fill with your real material, never a script to recite.

Part 1 — "Tell me about yourself"

The questions in this family — all the same prompt wearing different clothes:

  • "Tell me about yourself." / "Walk me through your background."
  • "Give me the two-minute version of your career."
  • "So, what brings you here?" / "Why don't you start by introducing yourself?"
  • "Walk me through your resume."

1.1 What the interviewer is actually testing

Not your history — they have your resume. This question tests judgment about relevance: out of seven years, what do you choose to foreground, and does that choice match the role you're interviewing for? A strong opener is a thesis statement — "here is who I am as an engineer, and here is why that's exactly what this job needs" — delivered in about ninety seconds. It also tests composure and signposting: can you be concise and structured about the one topic you know best, or do you ramble? Interviewers form a level estimate in the first two minutes and then spend the rest of the loop confirming or revising it. This is where you set the anchor. For your pivot specifically, it's also the moment you either frame the frontend-to-full-stack move as intentional range or let it look like uncertainty — and you don't get to re-frame it later.

1.2 The structure: present → past → why-here

Do not go chronological from your first job. Chronological order buries your strongest, most recent work under years of throat-clearing and forces the interviewer to wait for the point. Use the recruiter-and-hiring-manager standard instead — three beats, present first:

1.3 A fully worked script — your positioning

Here is the pitch written out for your exact situation, with the honesty tightrope walked deliberately. Read how it claims deep frontend + current, real backend without ever implying seven years of Java. The [bracketed] parts and round numbers are placeholders — swap in your real company, product, stack, and true metrics.

[Present] "I'm a senior engineer with about seven years of experience, and I've spent most of that becoming a real specialist on the frontend — React, design systems, web performance — at [current company], where I currently own [product area] end to end. Over the last [~18 months] I've deliberately pushed into the backend: I've been building and shipping the services behind my own features in Java and Spring Boot, against Postgres, so I'm now delivering full vertical slices rather than handing an API contract over a wall."

[Past] "The work I'm proudest of on the way here is the frontend platform side. I led a design system that [N] product teams build on, which cut our UI delivery time by roughly [40%] and killed a whole class of accessibility and inconsistency bugs. Before that I ran a performance push that took [a key flow] from a [4-second] load down to under [1.5 seconds], which moved [conversion / engagement metric]. And I've been the person who mentors the newer engineers and reviews the hard PRs — so a lot of my impact is through other people, not just my own commits."

[Why here] "What I want next is to be genuinely full-stack, not frontend-with-a-side-of-API — to own a feature from the database schema to the pixel. That's why [this company / this role] stood out: [one specific, true reason — see Part 2]. My frontend depth is a differentiator here rather than something I'm leaving behind, and I get to keep growing the backend muscle on a team that takes it seriously."

This is the shape, not your script. Drop your real company, product, stack and true numbers into this skeleton and keep the structure — present-first, three beats, ~90 seconds. Never deliver a version that isn't true of you. In particular, keep the Java/Spring claim exactly as calibrated: current, real, shipping in production — not "seven years of Java." If your backend tenure is nine months, say nine months with confidence; owned-in-production for nine months beats vaguely-claimed-for-years the instant they probe.

1.4 The follow-up drill

The opener always invites a follow-up, and for your profile it's predictable: "You said you've moved into the backend recently — tell me more about that," or bluntly, "How much Java have you actually written?" Have the honest, confident answer ready and rehearsed, because this is the exact spot where under-claiming or over-claiming both cost you. Name the real scope: "On [project] I built the Spring Boot service end to end — REST controllers, the JPA/Postgres data layer, the transaction boundaries, and the tests. Where I'm still deepening is [the JVM performance-tuning / concurrency-at-scale] side, and that's exactly the kind of thing I'm looking to do more of." Volunteering the edge of your knowledge reads as senior self-awareness; getting caught at it reads as inflation.

The two ways this opener dies. The resume recital: "So I graduated in [year], then I joined [company] as a junior, then after two years I moved to…" — chronological, flat, no thesis, and you've spent forty seconds before saying anything that matters. The apology: hedging the pivot — "I'm mostly a frontend developer, I've done a bit of backend, I'm still pretty new to Java…" — which invites the interviewer to down-level you on the spot. You are not apologizing for a gap; you are describing a deliberate expansion of range. Same facts, opposite frame. Pick the frame that's both true and confident.
Say it like this: "I'm a frontend specialist who has deliberately grown into full-stack — I currently ship the Java/Spring services behind my own features, so I own the whole vertical slice now." The senior move is the word deliberately and the phrase own the whole vertical slice: they reframe the pivot as a strategic choice you're driving, not a transition happening to you. A mid-level candidate lists what they've done; a senior one narrates why they've steered their own growth the way they have.

Maps to: every rubric's "communication" and "level/scope" dimensions; Amazon's Are Right, A Lot and Learn & Be Curious (owning your own growth); and the fintech read of "can this person articulate their value and their limits honestly."

Part 2 — Why this company / role / now

The questions in this family:

  • "Why do you want to work here?" / "Why [company]?"
  • "Why this role / this team specifically?"
  • "Why are you looking to leave your current job?" / "Why now?"
  • "What do you know about what we do?"

2.1 What the interviewer is actually testing

Two things, and they matter differently at different companies. First, genuineness of interest — have you done any homework, or are you spraying resumes and they're one of forty? A specific, researched answer predicts that you'll actually accept the offer and stay, which is the thing the hiring manager is quietly worried about. Second, self-knowledge — do you know what you want your next few years to look like, and is this role actually it? "Why now" probes whether you're running toward something (growth, ownership, full-stack range) or merely away from something (a bad manager, boredom, comp). Running-toward is a strong signal; running-away, said carelessly, is a red flag. At FAANG this is a lighter-touch question; at enterprise/fintech (Optum, JPMC, Goldman) it carries real weight, because they invest heavily in onboarding and are genuinely screening for people who'll commit to the domain.

2.2 How to research so your answer isn't generic

"Generic" means an answer that would be equally true of any company of that size — "you're a leader in your space, you have great engineers, and I love the mission." That's not an answer; it's a compliment, and it signals you did zero preparation. A non-generic answer names something only true of them. Where to find it, in ascending order of how much it impresses:

  • Their engineering blog / tech talks. The highest-yield source by far. Read two or three recent posts and you can reference an actual technical decision they made — their migration off [X], their approach to [design systems / observability / their data platform]. This is gold for you: an eng-blog post about their frontend architecture or design system lets you connect your specialty directly to their work.
  • The product itself. Use it. Have opinions. "I signed up and the onboarding flow does [specific thing] really well — that's the kind of frontend craft I want to work on" is worth ten generic mission statements.
  • The job description, read closely. It lists the actual stack and the actual problems. If it says "React + Spring Boot microservices," your full-stack pivot is a bullseye — say so explicitly.
  • Recent news / earnings / launches. A new product line, a funding round, an expansion into [area]. Shows you follow them as a company, not just as a logo.
  • The people. Glance at the team's public talks or the interviewer's own posts (not creepily — publicly). "I saw your team gave a talk on [topic]" is a strong, human opener.
Budget 30–45 minutes of research per company, and write down the two or three specifics you find on the same one-page cheat-sheet as your story bank. You need exactly one genuinely specific hook for "why here," not an essay. For fintech/enterprise loops, add one sentence on the domain — why healthcare data, or payments infrastructure, or capital markets is a space you want to build in — because "why this industry" is a real part of their screen in a way it usually isn't at consumer tech.

2.3 A worked answer — the shape

A strong "why here" braids three threads: something specific about them, something specific about you, and the fit between the two. For your pivot, the fit thread is your superpower — few candidates offer "deep frontend and growing backend" as a matched pair.

"Two things, really. One is the work itself — I read your engineering post on [the design-system / performance / platform thing], and that's exactly the kind of problem I've spent years on; it's rare to find a team that treats frontend architecture as a first-class engineering discipline rather than an afterthought. The other is the shape of the role: it's genuinely full-stack on a React-and-Spring stack, which is precisely the direction I've been deliberately moving. I get to bring real frontend depth on day one and keep growing the backend side on a team that takes it seriously. That combination — my strength being useful now, and a clear path to grow — is what I'm optimizing for at this stage of my career."

Notice what it does not do: it doesn't recite their mission statement, it doesn't flatter, and it doesn't lead with comp or with leaving your current job. "Why now" is answered implicitly — you're moving toward full-stack range — so you rarely need to badmouth your current employer at all. If pushed directly on why you're leaving, keep it forward-looking and brief: "I've gotten what I came for on the frontend platform side, and I want a role where full-stack ownership is the expectation, not something I carve out on the margins."

The generic-pitch trap: an answer built entirely from words on their careers page — "innovative," "impact," "world-class team," "mission-driven." It's not wrong, it's empty, and every candidate says it, so it scores zero and can score negative (it signals you didn't prepare). The tell that you've fallen into it: your answer would survive find-and-replacing the company name with any competitor. If it would, it's not an answer yet. Also in this trap: leading with what they can do for you (comp, brand, remote policy) before anything about the work — true or not, said first, it reads as transactional.
Say it like this: "I read your team's post on [X] — that's the kind of problem I want to be working on, and the role is full-stack on exactly the stack I've been moving toward." The senior signal isn't enthusiasm; it's specificity plus self-direction. You've named a real technical detail (proof of research) and connected it to a deliberate career thesis (proof of judgment). Junior "why here" answers are about the company's prestige; senior ones are about a considered fit between concrete work and a concrete plan.

Maps to: genuine interest / retention risk (every company); Amazon Learn & Be Curious and Ownership (a considered career thesis); and, for fintech/enterprise, commitment to the domain — the thing they most want to de-risk before investing in your onboarding.

Part 3 — Questions to ask them

"What questions do you have for me?" is not a formality and it is not the interview ending — it's a graded part of the interview, and often the tie-breaker between two candidates who scored the same on the technical bar. It tests three things at once: whether you were engaged and listening, what you actually care about (which reveals your level far more honestly than any answer you rehearsed), and whether you're evaluating them back — because senior engineers with options do, and it shows. Always have questions ready. Running out, or asking nothing, is one of the most reliable negative signals there is.

3.1 What makes a question read as senior

Junior questions are about the surface: perks, the tech stack list, "what's a typical day." Senior questions are about how the machine actually runs: how decisions get made, how failure is handled, where the team is under strain, what "good" looks like in six months. You already know these things matter because you've lived their consequences for seven years — ask about the things a person who has been burned by their absence would ask about. Ask the interviewer questions matched to their role, too: ask an engineer about the codebase and on-call; ask a manager about team direction and growth; ask a skip-level or the hiring manager about strategy and how success is measured.

3.2 The bank — grouped, pick 2–3 per interviewer

Keep a running list of 8–10 questions on your cheat-sheet and cross off any that get answered during the interview — which happens constantly, so you need spares. The best questions are often the ones that arise from the conversation: "you mentioned you're migrating [X] — what's driving that?" A follow-up on something they said proves you were actually listening, which no pre-written question can match. Aim to ask 2–3 per interviewer; you don't need to burn the whole list.
Two traps here. "No, I think you've covered everything" — the single most common way to leave points on the table. Even if they genuinely covered a lot, ask something; silence reads as disengagement or as having already checked out. The softball / self-serving question — "how many vacation days do I get?", "is it remote?", "when do reviews happen?" — logistics that are real but that you ask a recruiter, not the engineer evaluating your level. Asking your hiring-panel engineer about perks tells them your ceiling of curiosity is your own comfort, and that's a level signal in the wrong direction. Save logistics for the recruiter call; spend interviewer airtime on the work.
The single strongest question in the bank, said plainly: "Walk me through your path from a merged PR to serving real traffic — and what happens when something breaks in production." It reads as senior because only someone who has owned systems in production thinks to ask it — you're probing deploy velocity, test culture, observability, and incident maturity in one breath, and their answer tells you exactly how much friction and how much chaos you'd be signing up for. You're not asking to look smart; you're asking because you've been burned by the absence of a good answer, and that lived-in quality is unmistakable.

Part 4 — "What's your biggest weakness?"

The questions in this family: "What's your biggest weakness?" · "What would your last manager say you need to work on?" · "Where do you struggle?" · "What kind of feedback do you get consistently?"

4.1 What it's actually testing

Self-awareness and honesty, full stop. The interviewer is not trying to find a reason to reject you — they know everyone has weaknesses, and a candidate who claims not to is the actual red flag. What they're checking is whether you can name a real one, without spin, and show you're actively working on it. A genuine weakness plus a concrete corrective action reads as maturity; a disguised strength reads as evasion; a disqualifying weakness read as unchanged reads as risk. The bar is simply: real, owned, and being addressed.

The humblebrag is the trap everyone falls into and every interviewer has heard a thousand times: "I'm a perfectionist," "I work too hard," "I care too much." It is transparent, it wastes the question, and it quietly signals you're either not self-aware or not being straight — the two exact traits the question screens for. The opposite failure is just as bad: naming something disqualifying for the role ("I miss deadlines," "I don't take feedback well," "I struggle to collaborate") with no evidence you've changed. Pick a weakness that is real, is not load-bearing for the job, and comes attached to a concrete fix already in motion.

4.2 The formula

Three beats: a real weakness → the concrete thing you're doing about it → the evidence it's working. The third beat is what separates a strong answer from a confession. Anyone can name a flaw; a senior engineer treats their own weakness the way they'd treat a system problem — instrument it, act, measure the result.

"Honestly, my instinct to go deep on frontend polish has sometimes cost me at the margins on time. Early on I'd over-invest in getting an interaction or an edge case perfect when 'good and shipped' was what the moment needed. What I do now is set an explicit time box and check the trade-off against the actual priority before I go deep — and I've gotten more deliberate about asking the PM 'is this worth another day?' rather than deciding unilaterally. It's made me faster without losing the craft, and it's something my last manager specifically called out as improved in my last review."

"A real one for me right now is depth on the backend performance side — I own Java/Spring services in production, but I'm still building the instinct for JVM-level tuning and high-concurrency edge cases that comes from years on the backend. I'm closing it deliberately: I've been [reading Effective Java / pairing with a senior backend engineer on our services / profiling our hot paths], and it's the exact kind of growth this role would accelerate."

That second answer does something clever for your positioning: it turns the honesty tightrope into an asset. By volunteering that your backend depth is newer, in the controlled setting of the weakness question, you (a) look self-aware, (b) inoculate yourself against the "how much Java, really?" gotcha, and (c) frame the gap as a growth trajectory this job supports. Use the weakness question to say the true thing about your pivot on your own terms, before an interviewer digs for it on theirs. Just keep it a genuine weakness, not a disguised brag.
Say it like this: "Here's a real one, here's the specific thing I changed, and here's how I know it's working." The senior signal is the third clause — evidence. A mid-level answer stops at "I'm aware of it and trying to improve"; a senior one closes the loop with a measurable change and, ideally, external confirmation ("my manager noted it improved"). You're demonstrating that you run the same feedback-and-iteration loop on yourself that you run on your code.

Maps to: Amazon Learn & Be Curious and Earn Trust (candor and self-critique); and every rubric's "coachability / self-awareness" line — the one that quietly predicts whether you'll grow on the team.

Part 5 — Gaps & job-hops, framed honestly

The questions in this family: "I see a gap here between [dates] — what happened?" · "You were at [company] for only a year — why so short?" · "You've changed jobs a few times — talk me through that." · "Why did you leave [X]?"

5.1 What it's actually testing

Stability and judgment — and, honestly, they're often just filling in a picture, not accusing you of anything. The interviewer wants to know whether you're a flight risk, whether there's a pattern of running from difficulty, and whether you can talk about your own career decisions like an adult without deflecting or getting defensive. The answer that scores is honest, brief, and forward-looking. The one that hurts you is defensive, over-explained, or dripping with resentment toward a former employer — because how you talk about your last team is the loudest available signal for how you'll talk about this one.

5.2 How to frame each case

SituationThe frame that works
An employment gap (layoff, health, caregiving, deliberate break, upskilling) State it plainly and without shame, then pivot to what you did with it or what you're ready for now. "I was part of a [20%] layoff — a business decision, not a performance one — and I used the time to [go deep on Spring Boot / ship a side project / recover]. I'm now looking for [X]." A gap owned confidently is a non-event; a gap you're visibly embarrassed about invites suspicion that isn't there.
One short stint among longer ones Give the honest, non-bitter reason and let your other tenures do the talking. "The role was mis-scoped versus what I was hired for — it turned out to be [X] rather than [Y] — so I made a deliberate move rather than coast. As you can see, my other roles have been multi-year." One short stint next to a stable record needs one clean sentence, not a defense.
A pattern of job-hops (several short stints) Get ahead of it and reframe around a through-line. "Earlier in my career I moved to chase learning and range fast, and I don't regret it — it's why I'm strong across [frontend depth + now backend]. What I want now is the opposite: somewhere to go deep and compound over several years, which is exactly why [this role]'s long-term ownership appeals to me." Name the pattern, own the reason, then signal that you're now optimizing for staying.
"Why are you leaving your current job?" Forward-looking, never a grievance. "I've gotten what I came for on the frontend side and I want full-stack ownership to be the default, not something I carve out." Pulls toward the new role instead of pushing away from the old one.
Two failure modes, opposite in shape. Over-explaining: a two-minute defensive monologue about a six-month gap tells the interviewer it's a wound, and now they're poking it — brevity signals it's genuinely fine. Badmouthing: "my manager was incompetent," "the company was a mess," "the code was garbage." Even when it's all true, it makes you look like someone who'll trash-talk this team next, and it's the single fastest way to turn a neutral question into a negative mark. You can name a real problem ("the role was mis-scoped," "priorities kept whipsawing") without assigning villains. Describe the situation; don't prosecute a person.
Say it like this: "That was a deliberate decision, here's the honest reason, and here's what I'm optimizing for now." The senior move is treating your own job history as a set of choices you made and own, not things that happened to you — even the layoff, which you didn't choose, you own the response to. Ownership of your narrative is the same signal the whole loop is grading; a career story told with agency and without blame is a person who takes responsibility, delivered in miniature.

Maps to: Amazon Ownership and Earn Trust; retention risk (every company); and the fintech read of "is this person stable, straight, and low-drama" — which they weight heavily.

Part 6 — Red flags to avoid

The opener and closer are also where a strong candidate can quietly torpedo an otherwise good loop. None of these are about your engineering ability; all of them are about signal, and all are avoidable. Scan this list the night before.

Red flagWhy it sinks youDo instead
Badmouthing a past employer, manager, or teammate Predicts you'll do the same to them. The most reliable negative signal in the whole loop. Describe the situation neutrally; stay forward-looking; assign no villains.
No questions at the end Reads as disengaged or already checked out; leaves free points on the table. Always have 2–3 ready per interviewer, plus spares (Part 3).
Over-claiming the pivot ("7-year full-stack engineer") One JVM or Spring-internals follow-up exposes it, and now you're the candidate who inflates. Deep frontend + current, real, shipping backend. Calibrated and confident (Part 1).
Leading with comp, perks, or remote policy Reads as transactional before you've shown interest in the work. Lead with the work; save logistics for the recruiter.
A generic "why here" that survives a name-swap Signals zero preparation; scores zero or negative. One specific, researched hook only true of them (Part 2).
Rambling — no structure, no time discipline Undercuts the senior read; the interviewer stops listening and starts waiting. Present → past → why-here, ~90 seconds, rehearsed out loud.
Arrogance / not listening / dismissing their stack "Why are you still on [X]?" said with a sneer reads as someone hard to work with. Curiosity over judgment; ask why, don't pronounce.
The through-line of the whole page: the open and the close are the two moments the interviewer is reading you as a colleague, not a candidate solving a problem. Everything that reads well here — a calibrated, confident self-summary; a specific reason you're there; questions that reveal you've owned real systems; honesty about your weaknesses and your history without spin or blame — is the same underlying signal: this is a senior person with good judgment who takes responsibility and would be low-drama to work with. You can't fake that in the middle of the loop if you didn't set it at the edges.

Interview Q&A

"Tell me about yourself."

Present → past → why-here, ~90 seconds, present first. "I'm a senior engineer, ~7 years, who specialized deep on the frontend — React, design systems, performance — and has deliberately grown into full-stack over the last [18 months], currently shipping the Java/Spring services behind my own features." Then two or three quantified signature achievements (design system, a performance turnaround, mentoring), then one true, specific reason this role fits. Don't go chronological, don't apologize for the pivot, don't recite your resume. The subtext you're setting: senior, owns outcomes, full-stack by design.

"How much Java have you actually written? / You said backend is recent — say more."

The honest, confident scope. "On [project] I built the Spring Boot service end to end — controllers, the JPA/Postgres layer, transaction boundaries, tests. Where I'm still deepening is [JVM tuning / high-concurrency edge cases], which is exactly the growth I'm looking for." Name the real scope and volunteer the edge of your knowledge. Never inflate to "seven years of Java" — that's the one claim a single follow-up dismantles. Owned-in-production for a real-but-short time, said with confidence, beats a vague long claim every time.

"Why do you want to work here?"

Braid three threads: something specific about them (an eng-blog post, a product detail, the exact stack in the JD), something about you (your full-stack thesis), and the fit. "I read your post on [X] — that's the kind of problem I've spent years on — and the role is genuinely full-stack on a React-and-Spring stack, which is the direction I'm deliberately moving." The failure mode is anything that would survive find-and-replacing their name with a competitor's. Research 30–45 minutes; you need one real hook, not an essay.

"Why are you looking to leave your current role?"

Forward-looking, never a grievance. "I've gotten what I came for on the frontend platform side, and I want full-stack ownership to be the expectation rather than something I carve out on the margins." Pull toward the new role, don't push away from the old one. If there's a real problem (mis-scoped role, whipsawing priorities), name the situation without prosecuting a person. Badmouthing is the fastest way to turn a neutral question into a negative mark.

"What's your biggest weakness?"

Real weakness → concrete fix in motion → evidence it's working. Avoid the humblebrag ("perfectionist") and avoid anything disqualifying for the role. A strong option for you: backend depth is newer — "I own Java/Spring in production but I'm still building the JVM-tuning and high-concurrency instinct that comes from years on the backend; I'm closing it by [pairing with a senior backend engineer / profiling our hot paths]." It's honest, it inoculates the "how much Java?" probe, and it frames the gap as a trajectory this job accelerates. Close with the evidence clause — that's the senior half.

"I see you were only at [company] for a year — why?"

One clean, non-bitter sentence, then let your other tenures carry it. "The role was mis-scoped versus what I was hired for, so I made a deliberate move rather than coast — my other roles have been multi-year." Brevity signals it's genuinely fine; over-explaining signals a wound. Treat it as a choice you made and own, not something that happened to you.

"There's a gap in your history here — what happened?"

State it plainly, no shame, then pivot to what you did with it or what you're ready for now. "I was part of a layoff — a business decision, not performance — and I used the time to go deep on [Spring Boot / a side project]. Now I'm looking for [X]." A gap owned confidently is a non-event. Embarrassment invites suspicion that wasn't there to begin with.

"What questions do you have for me?"

Always have some — "no, you covered it" leaves points on the table. Match them to the interviewer's role, and favor questions only a person who's owned systems would ask. The strongest single one: "Walk me through your path from a merged PR to serving real traffic — and what happens when something breaks in production." That probes deploy velocity, test culture, and incident maturity at once. Keep logistics (comp, remote, PTO) for the recruiter, not the evaluating engineer.

"You've moved around a few times — are you going to stay?"

Reframe the pattern around a through-line, then signal you're now optimizing for depth. "Earlier on I moved to chase range fast — it's why I'm strong across frontend and now backend. What I want now is the opposite: somewhere to compound over several years, which is exactly why this role's long-term ownership appeals." Name the pattern, own the reason, close on staying. Defensiveness reads worse than the hops themselves.

"What do you know about us?"

This is the research question in disguise, and it's pass/fail on preparation. Have two or three specifics ready: something from their engineering blog, an observation from using the product, and the stack from the JD. "You're doing [specific technical thing], the product does [X] well, and the role's a React-and-Spring full-stack seat." Generic mission-statement answers ("you're innovative and mission-driven") signal you didn't prepare and can score negative.

"Where do you see yourself in a few years?"

Show a considered trajectory that this role plausibly serves — that's the retention signal they want. "Full-stack staff-level: owning features from schema to pixel, and the technical direction of a team's surface area. That's why the full-stack ownership here matters to me — it's the exact muscle I want to build next." Avoid answers that imply you'll leave (founding a startup, a totally different field) or that you haven't thought about it at all. Ambition tied to their role is the sweet spot.

"How do you handle the enterprise/fintech version of 'why us'?" (awareness)

Add a domain sentence that consumer-tech loops rarely require. Optum, JPMC, Goldman and similar weight "why this industry" heavily because onboarding into a regulated domain is a real investment they're de-risking. "Beyond the tech, I want to build in [healthcare data / payments infrastructure / capital markets] because [genuine reason] — problems where correctness and reliability actually matter, not just velocity." One sincere sentence about the domain moves you from "generic tech candidate" to "wants to be here specifically."

What to remember

  • The open and the close are graded, and you control them completely. The opener sets your level anchor and frames every story that follows; the closing questions are the clearest level signal you'll emit. Nail both — they're the two moments the interviewer reads you as a colleague, not a problem-solver.
  • "Tell me about yourself" = present → past → why-here, ~90 seconds, present first. Lead with level and range, then 2–3 quantified signature wins, then one true specific reason for this role. Never chronological, never a resume recital.
  • Walk the honesty tightrope in the confident register. Deep frontend, deliberately extended, currently shipping real Java/Spring in production — not "seven years of Java." Owned-in-production for a short-but-real time, said confidently, beats a vague long claim the instant they probe. Don't over-claim; don't apologize.
  • "Why here" must survive the name-swap test. If your answer would be equally true of a competitor, it's not an answer. Spend 30–45 minutes on their eng blog / product / JD and name one specific, researched hook. Braid: something about them + your full-stack thesis + the fit.
  • Always ask questions — 2–3 per interviewer, matched to their role. Senior questions probe how the machine runs: path-to-production, incident handling, decision-making, where the team is under strain. The MVP: "from merged PR to real traffic — and what happens when it breaks." Keep logistics for the recruiter.
  • Weakness = real → fix in motion → evidence it's working. No humblebrags, nothing disqualifying. Your backend-depth answer doubles as inoculation against the "how much Java?" gotcha. The evidence clause is the senior half.
  • Gaps and hops: honest, brief, forward-looking, and never bitter. Own them as choices (even the response to a layoff you didn't choose). Over-explaining signals a wound; badmouthing predicts you'll trash-talk them next — the single fastest negative signal in the loop.
  • These scripts are shapes, not scripts. Drop your real company, stack, and true numbers into every skeleton; keep the structure and the beat lengths. Then rehearse out loud and time it — the gap between the pitch in your head and the pitch out of your mouth is the whole thing.
