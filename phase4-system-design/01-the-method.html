01 — The System Design Interview: a repeatable method

Six steps, a time budget, and the sentences to say out loud. Learn this before you learn a single architecture.

Why this matters in the interview

Most system design rounds are lost in the first five minutes, and almost nobody realises it while it's happening. The interviewer says "design a URL shortener," and the candidate — nervous, eager to look productive — starts drawing boxes. Client, load balancer, app server, database, cache. It feels like progress. It is actually the moment the round died, because that architecture was not derived from anything. It was recalled. And an architecture that was recalled instead of derived cannot be defended: when the interviewer asks "why a cache there?" the honest answer is "because the diagram I memorised had one," and everything after that is improvisation on top of a foundation you can't justify. The interviewer isn't grading your diagram. They're grading whether the diagram follows from the requirements — and if you never established the requirements, there's nothing for it to follow from.

So this page teaches a script. Not a design, a process: six steps you run in order, with a time budget, that turn a deliberately vague prompt into a defensible system. The script matters most exactly when you need it most — under pressure, on a problem you haven't seen, when your brain wants to reach for a memorised picture. Every case study in this phase (07 through 18, and 20 through 23) is this same script executed on a different problem, so if you learn the script now, the case studies stop being twelve things to memorise and become one thing applied twelve times. And at 7 years of experience the process is the signal. Nobody expects you to have operated Kafka at a million events per second. They expect you to ask what the read:write ratio is before you pick a database, to say what your design costs, and to know what breaks first. That is a learnable, rehearsable discipline, and it starts here.

1. The failure mode this page fixes

Here is the transcript of a losing round, compressed. It is not a strawman; it is the median. 

0:00  Interviewer: "Design Twitter."
0:20  Candidate:   "Okay. So we'd have clients, going through a load
                    balancer, to a set of app servers…"     ← already lost
0:20-0:35          [draws boxes; every box is generic; none of them
                    were forced by anything the candidate knows]
0:35  Interviewer: "How would you handle a user with 40 million
                    followers?"
0:36  Candidate:   [has no framework for this, because they never
                    asked how many users there are, never asked the
                    read:write ratio, and never established that
                    fan-out is the problem]

The candidate's instinct is understandable. Silence feels expensive, drawing feels like doing, and the box diagram is the only artifact they've ever seen from a system design round — so they produce the artifact. But the artifact is the output of a process, and they've skipped the process. The result is a design with no because in it. Ask "why?" of any box and there's no answer that starts with "because the requirements said."

Concretely, the boxes-first candidate cannot answer any of the questions that actually decide the round:

  • "Why that database?" — They don't know the access patterns, because they never asked what the reads look like.
  • "Do you need a cache?" — They don't know the read:write ratio, so "yes, caching is good" is the best they have. That's a slogan, not an argument.
  • "What breaks first at 10×?" — They have no numbers, so they can't identify a bottleneck. Everything is equally hypothetical.
  • "Is eventual consistency acceptable here?" — They never asked, so they can't say. And this single question is often the fork that decides the entire architecture.

The discipline that fixes it is embarrassingly simple to state and genuinely hard to execute when adrenaline is up: understand, scope, estimate, then design. You do not touch the whiteboard until you can say, in one sentence, what you are building, for how many users, with what read:write ratio, and what you are deliberately leaving out. Everything downstream is then forced — and "forced" is what a defensible design means.

The single line that inverts the whole dynamic, said in the first thirty seconds: "Before I draw anything, let me ask a few questions to pin down scope and scale — I'd rather spend five minutes getting the requirements right than design the wrong system beautifully." That sentence alone reads as senior, because it says you've been burned by building the wrong thing before. You have. That's what seven years buys you.

2. The six-step method & the time budget

Six steps, always in this order, on every backend/distributed prompt you will ever get. Run them like a script. The order is not arbitrary — each step's output is the next step's input, which is exactly why skipping one leaves you with a design you can't defend. 

  1. Requirements   ──▶  what am I building, and what am I NOT building?
        │                (functional + non-functional + explicit cuts)
        ▼
  2. Estimation     ──▶  QPS, storage, bandwidth. One number will
        │                dominate. Find it and say which.
        ▼
  3. API            ──▶  the 3-5 endpoints. Freezes the contract.
        │
        ▼
  4. Data model     ──▶  entities, keys, access patterns → SQL or NoSQL,
        │                justified out loud.
        ▼
  5. High-level     ──▶  boxes — but now every box is FORCED by 1-4.
     design         │    Walk one write and one read end to end.
        ▼
  6. Bottlenecks    ──▶  what breaks first at 10×, and the deep dive
     & scale             the interviewer is steering you toward.

The time budget (45-minute round)

Assume ~40 usable minutes after intros and before the candidate-questions bit at the end. Here is where it goes, and — more importantly — where it's safe to overrun and where overrunning is fatal.

StepBudgetWhat "done" looks likeOverrun risk
1. Requirements~5 min You can state the core flow in one sentence, and you've named what you're cutting. Safe to overrun. Going to 7–8 minutes here is fine and often reads as rigour. Nobody has ever failed for asking too many good clarifying questions.
2. Estimation~5 min You have a QPS number, a storage number, and you've said which one drives the design. Safe-ish. Don't do long division on the board. Round hard, out loud.
3. API~5 min 3–5 endpoints with request/response shapes. Not 15 endpoints. Low risk, but easy to waste time bikeshedding field names. Don't.
4. Data model~5 min Entities + keys + the SQL-vs-NoSQL call, defended. Low. Keep it to the 2–4 tables that matter.
5. High-level design~15 min A diagram, plus one write path and one read path walked end to end. FATAL to overrun. This is the step that expands to fill all available time if you let it. Every extra minute here is stolen from step 6, which is where the actual grading happens.
6. Bottlenecks & scale~10 min You've named what breaks first, fixed it, and gone deep on one thing. You will never get here if step 5 ran long. Protect this budget above all else.

Why step 5 overrun is fatal and step 1 overrun is not. Step 1 is cheap because a longer requirements phase makes every later step faster — you're not exploring, you're eliminating. Step 5 is expensive because a longer high-level design phase produces no additional signal. Once the interviewer has seen your boxes and your request walkthrough, more boxes teach them nothing new about you. The deep dive in step 6 is where they find out whether you actually understand fan-out, or contention, or consistency — the things that separate a 7-YOE hire from a 3-YOE one. A candidate who spends 25 minutes lovingly drawing microservices and then has 4 minutes for "how do you handle a celebrity user" has, functionally, not been interviewed.

The clock is not your friend and the interviewer will not always rescue you. Some will let you drown in step 5 on purpose — time management is part of the rubric. Watch the clock yourself. At the ~22-minute mark, if you're still drawing boxes, say: "I want to leave time for the interesting part, so let me lock the high-level design here and go deep on [the hard thing] — unless there's a piece of the architecture you'd like me to expand first."
Not every round is 45 minutes and not every round is backend. A 60-minute round just stretches steps 5–6 (more deep dives, usually two). A 30-minute screen usually means requirements → estimation → high-level → one bottleneck, and you compress the API and data model into a couple of sentences. The order never changes; only the budget does.

3. Functional vs non-functional requirements

Split them explicitly, out loud, and write them in two columns on the board. The split is not academic pedantry — it's the load-bearing distinction of the whole round, because functional requirements tell you what to build, and non-functional requirements tell you how to build it. The architecture is determined almost entirely by the second column.

FunctionalNon-functional
Asks What can a user do? How well must it do it?
Examples "Shorten a URL." "Post a tweet." "See a feed." "Book a seat." 100M DAU · 100:1 read:write · p99 < 200ms · 99.99% available · globally distributed · eventual consistency OK for the feed, strong for the booking
Determines Your API surface and your entities. Steps 3 and 4. Your entire architecture. Steps 5 and 6.
If you skip it You build the wrong feature. Recoverable — the interviewer will redirect you. You build a system that cannot be defended. Not recoverable.

Watch how a single non-functional answer forces the design, while the functional requirement stays identical:

Same functional requirementNon-functional answerArchitecture it forces
"Users can see a feed of posts from people they follow." 1M DAU, 10:1 read:write, eventual consistency fine Fan-out on write into a per-user feed cache. Precompute. Reads are a single Redis lookup.
Same, but some users have 40M followers Hybrid: fan-out on write for normal users, fan-out on read (pull at query time) for celebrities. Completely different write path.
"Users can buy a ticket for a seat." Low traffic, but never oversell Single Postgres, a unique constraint on (event_id, seat_id), optimistic locking. Boring, correct, done.
Same, plus 500k people hit "buy" in the same 10 seconds Queue + virtual waiting room + seat holds with TTL + Redis reservation layer in front of the DB. A different system entirely.

That's the point. The functional requirement is the same sentence in both rows. Everything that changed came from a non-functional answer you only get by asking. This is why asking scores points — not as a politeness ritual, but because the interviewer is watching to see whether you know that the answer to "which database" lives in the requirements, not in your memory.

The clarifying questions — ask these, in roughly this order

Six questions. You can ask all six in about three minutes. Memorise them; they work on literally any backend prompt.

#AskWhat you're actually fishing for
1 "How many users are we talking about — DAU, roughly? And is that where we are today or where we want to be?" The scale tier. 10k DAU means one Postgres and you're done. 100M DAU means sharding, caching, and a CDN are all forced. Design for the wrong tier and everything after is wrong.
2 "What's the read:write ratio? Is this read-heavy like a URL shortener, or write-heavy like a metrics pipeline?" The single highest-value question in the round. Read-heavy → caching, read replicas, denormalisation, precomputation. Write-heavy → LSM-tree store, partitioning, async ingestion, batching. Nearly every architectural fork downstream hangs off this one ratio.
3 "What's the latency target? Is this a p99 < 100ms user-facing path, or is a few seconds acceptable?" Whether you can do work synchronously. A tight p99 forces precomputation and caching and pushes everything non-essential onto a queue.
4 "How strong do the consistency guarantees need to be? Is it okay if a user sees a slightly stale view for a second or two?" The CAP fork. "Stale is fine" unlocks replication, caching, async fan-out, multi-region actives. "Never stale, never double-spend" forces single-writer, transactions, quorum reads — and usually kills your clever caching layer.
5 "Is this global, or single-region? Are users concentrated anywhere?" Whether you need a CDN, geo-routing, multi-region replication, and the whole cross-region consistency headache. Also whether the interviewer wants to talk about that at all.
6 "What's explicitly out of scope? I'd assume auth, spam/abuse, payments and analytics are out unless you say otherwise." Permission to cut. This is the question that buys you your 40 minutes back. See §4.

A seventh, prompt-specific one is usually worth asking too — the one that names the hard part. For a chat system: "Do we need delivery receipts and read receipts, or just message delivery?" For a feed: "Is the feed chronological or ranked?" For a booking system: "How bad is overselling — is it a refund, or is it illegal?" Asking the prompt-specific question proves you understand where the difficulty is before you've designed anything, and that is a very strong signal.

Don't just ask — propose an answer and ask them to correct it. "I'll assume 100 million DAU, read-heavy at maybe 100:1, and that eventual consistency is fine for reads — does that match what you had in mind?" This is strictly better than an open question. It's faster, it shows you know what the plausible numbers are, and it hands the interviewer a cheap way to steer you. Open questions make them do the work; proposals make them a collaborator.
Do not ask questions you won't use. "What's our team size?" "What's the tech stack?" "Do we have a budget?" — these are real-job questions that score zero in an interview, and asking a string of them burns your requirements budget while signalling that you don't know which inputs actually drive a design. Every question you ask should be one whose answer would visibly change your architecture. If you can't say how the answer would change your design, don't ask it.

4. Scoping ruthlessly

"Design Twitter" is not a 45-minute problem. It's a 4,000-engineer problem. The prompt is deliberately too big, and the first thing being tested is whether you notice. A candidate who tries to cover posting, the feed, follows, search, notifications, DMs, media upload, trending topics, and moderation will produce nine shallow boxes and zero depth, and will be graded — correctly — as someone who can't prioritise. A candidate who cuts eight of those and goes genuinely deep on the feed will be graded as senior.

Cutting is not weakness. Cutting is the demonstration. In your actual job, the highest-value thing you do in a design review is say "we're not solving that this quarter." The interview is testing for exactly that instinct, compressed into 45 minutes.

The sentence

Learn this one verbatim. Say it at roughly the four-minute mark, right after the clarifying questions:

"This is a big surface, so I'm going to scope it. I'll cut authentication, search, notifications, and media upload — those are real but they're standard, and I don't think they're where the interesting design is. I'll focus on the core flow: [write a post → fan it out → read a feed]. That's where the scale problem actually lives. Tell me if you'd rather I cover something else instead."

Unpack why that sentence works, because every clause is load-bearing:

  • "I'm going to scope it" — you're taking control. You're not asking permission to think; you're announcing a decision. That's the driving-the-room signal.
  • "I'll cut X, Y, Z" — naming what you're cutting proves you know it exists. This is the crucial bit. Silently omitting auth looks like you forgot auth. Explicitly cutting auth looks like you triaged auth. Same diagram, opposite grade.
  • "those are standard / not where the interesting design is" — you're demonstrating a judgment about where difficulty lives. That judgment is the thing being graded.
  • "I'll focus on the core flow: …" — you commit to one narrow, deep path. Now step 5 has a natural boundary and can't sprawl.
  • "Tell me if you'd rather I cover something else" — the check-in. It costs you nothing, and it converts a unilateral decision into a shared one. If the interviewer did want to see search, you find out in minute four instead of minute forty. Enormous insurance for one clause.

What to cut, almost always

Cut by defaultWhy it's safe to cutUnless…
Authentication / authorizationSolved problem. JWT or session token at the gateway. Nobody learns anything about you from it.The prompt is about auth (design SSO, design a permissions system).
Analytics / reportingAlmost always "fire an event onto Kafka, land it in an OLAP store." One sentence, then move on.The prompt is an ad-click aggregator or a metrics pipeline (tutorial 18).
Spam, abuse, moderationAn enormous ML-shaped problem orthogonal to the architecture.Rarely. Mention it exists, then cut it.
Payments / billingIts own hard problem (tutorial 13). Don't drag it into a feed design.The prompt is a payment or order system.

What you must never cut

The thing the prompt is secretly about. Every classic prompt has one hard core, and cutting it is the one unrecoverable scoping error — it reads as either not knowing where the difficulty is, or dodging it.

PromptThe hard core you cannot cut
URL shortenerKey generation + the read:write ratio driving the cache
News feed / TwitterFan-out, and the celebrity problem
Chat / WhatsAppConnection state at scale + message ordering & delivery guarantees
TicketmasterInventory contention — never oversell a seat
Rate limiterThe distributed counter race
Payment systemIdempotency and exactly-once-ish semantics
Uber / nearbyThe geospatial index over moving objects
The mirror-image mistake to over-scoping: cutting so aggressively that nothing hard is left. "I'll assume a single region, a single database, no cache, and 1,000 users" technically produces a correct design and scores nothing, because you've defined the interview out of existence. Cut the breadth (features), never the depth (the hard core). If your scoped problem could be solved with one Postgres and a Spring Boot app, you cut wrong — put the difficulty back.

5. The rubric — what's actually being graded

Interviewers at most large companies fill in a scorecard with named dimensions. You will never see it, but it is remarkably consistent across companies, and it is not "did the candidate produce a correct architecture" — because there is no correct architecture. It is a set of behavioural signals. Here they are.

SignalWhat earns itWhat loses it
Requirements & scoping Asked scale, ratio, latency, consistency. Named explicit cuts. Checked in on scope. Started designing immediately. Asked no numbers. Tried to cover everything.
Estimation Produced a QPS/storage number and said which number drives the design. Skipped it, or produced numbers and then never used them. (Unused numbers score almost nothing.)
Design coherence Every component traces back to a requirement. Data flows end to end without gaps. Boxes with no justification. A cache that caches nothing in particular. A queue with no consumer story.
Trade-off articulation ★ Names the cost of every choice unprompted. "I'm picking eventual consistency here, which means a user can see a stale count for a couple of seconds — I think that's acceptable for a like counter." Presents choices as if they were free. Never uses the word "but."
Depth / bottleneck identification ★ Knows what breaks first, and why. Goes genuinely deep on one hard thing. Uniformly shallow. Deflects the deep dive back to more boxes.
Communication & drive Thinks out loud, structures the answer, states the plan, manages the clock, checks in. Long silences. Needs to be dragged forward. Interviewer ends up running the round.
Handling pushback Treats a challenge as information. Updates the design or defends it with a reason. Defensive, or instantly capitulates and changes the design without understanding why.
Technical breadth Knows the right tool exists and roughly why. Nothing — but note this is the least weighted line on the card, and candidates over-invest here by a mile.

3 YOE vs 7 YOE — the same round, graded differently

This is the part to internalise, because you are being graded on the right-hand column and most public prep material teaches the left-hand one. A mid-level candidate who does everything in the left column gets a "hire" at mid-level and a "no hire" at senior. Same performance, different bar.

DimensionPassing at ~3 YOEPassing at ~7 YOE (your bar)
Requirements Asks a couple of clarifying questions when prompted to. Drives the requirements phase unprompted, proposes numbers, and cuts scope with a stated rationale.
The design Produces a reasonable, working architecture. Produces a reasonable architecture and can explain why each alternative was rejected.
Trade-offs Can discuss a trade-off when the interviewer raises one. Raises them first. Never presents a choice without its cost. This is the single biggest gap between the two levels.
Failure "We'd add monitoring and retries." Knows what breaks first, at what load, and what the blast radius is. Names a specific component and a specific number.
Conversation Answers the interviewer's questions well. Drives the conversation. The interviewer mostly listens and occasionally steers. If the interviewer had to pull the round along, that's a senior-bar miss even if every answer was right.
Not knowing something Says "I don't know." Says "I haven't used that in anger — but here's how I'd reason about it," and then reasons correctly from first principles.
Technologies Names appropriate ones. Names fewer, and justifies each. Naming ten technologies is a negative signal at senior; it reads as vocabulary substituting for judgment.

Read that last row twice. It is the most counter-intuitive thing on this page and it is the trap most experienced candidates walk into. You are not being graded on how many technologies you can name. A candidate who says "Postgres, and here's why it's enough at this scale, and here's the specific load at which I'd have to leave it" beats a candidate who scatters Kafka, Cassandra, Redis, Elasticsearch, Flink and Kubernetes across a whiteboard without a single because. The second candidate sounds impressive to themselves and thin to the interviewer.

The highest-scoring habit, and you can install it mechanically: never let a design choice leave your mouth without a "which costs us…" attached. Build it as a reflex. "I'll cache the redirect in Redis — which costs us a staleness window, so if someone edits a link the old target can serve for up to the TTL. At a 100:1 read ratio I think that trade is obviously worth it." That's one sentence containing a choice, a cost, and a justification. String twenty of those together and you have a senior system design round.

6. How to drive the room

The interviewer's private note at the end is usually a sentence like "I had to pull this out of them" or "they ran the session and I just watched." Those two sentences are different hiring decisions. Driving is a skill and it's coachable.

Think out loud — the interviewer can only grade what they hear

Your internal monologue is invisible. If you spend forty silent seconds correctly ruling out a hash-based key generator because of collisions, you scored zero for it. Say it: "I could hash the URL and take the first seven characters, but then I have to handle collisions on every write, which means a read-before-write. Let me think about a counter-based scheme instead…" Same thinking, forty seconds, now it's worth points. Externalising your reasoning is not a communication nicety — it is literally how the transcript gets written.

Silence also destroys the interviewer's ability to help you. They can't redirect a candidate whose thoughts they can't see. A candidate who says their wrong idea out loud gets rescued in ten seconds; a candidate who thinks it silently spends five minutes on it and then presents it.

Propose, then check in

The rhythm of a well-driven round is a loop: state the plan → do the thing → check in → move on. The check-in is a single short question at each phase boundary, and it costs you five seconds. 

  "I'm going to spend a few minutes on requirements first."   ← state the plan
  [asks the six questions]                                     ← do the thing
  "Does that scope sound right, or would you rather I
   cover X?"                                                   ← check in
  "Great — moving to estimation."                              ← move on

Check-ins do three things at once. They prove you can collaborate. They give the interviewer a low-friction moment to steer you (interviewers want to steer; they're often just waiting for an opening). And they cap your downside — you can never drift more than one phase away from what they wanted to see.

Pushback is a hint, not an attack

When the interviewer says "hmm, are you sure about that?" or "what happens if the cache goes down?" — that is almost never a trap and almost always a gift. They have seen the flaw and they are handing you the chance to find it. The two ways to lose the point are symmetric and both common:

Wrong responseWhy it failsDo this instead
Capitulate. "Oh — you're right, let me change it." (Changes the design instantly, without knowing what was wrong.) Reads as no conviction. You'd fold in a real design review too. Also: sometimes they're testing whether you'll defend a correct choice. "Let me think about what happens there…" — actually engage, then either fix it or defend it.
Get defensive. "No, that's fine, caches don't go down that often." Reads as unable to take feedback. Instantly disqualifying at senior level, where you're expected to run design reviews. "That's a fair concern. If Redis is down, every read falls through to Postgres — at 10k QPS that flattens it. So I'd want a fallback: […]."

The good response has a shape: acknowledge → reason → resolve. "That's a good catch" / "here's what would actually happen" / "so I'd do X, or if you'd rather keep it simple, Y with this cost." You end up ahead of where you started, and — this is the part candidates miss — you get credit for the fix even though they found the bug. Finding your own bug when prompted is worth nearly as much as not having it.

When you genuinely don't know

You will hit something you don't know. You're pivoting from frontend; you have not run a Cassandra cluster. This is fine and it is survivable, as long as you don't bluff. Bluffing is the one unrecoverable move, because the interviewer almost certainly does know, and a confident wrong answer poisons everything else you said — now they have to wonder which of your other confident statements were also invented.

The recovery script, which scores better than a memorised right answer would have:

"Honestly, I haven't operated Cassandra at that scale, so I don't want to fake the details. But let me reason about it: it's an LSM-tree store, so writes are cheap appends and reads may have to check multiple SSTables — which means it's a good fit for our write-heavy workload but I'd expect read latency to be less predictable than Postgres. If read p99 turned out to matter more than write throughput, I'd want to revisit that choice. Does that match your experience?"

Look at what that does. It admits the gap (honesty — a strong senior signal), then reasons from first principles (which is the actual thing being graded), then arrives at a correct conclusion anyway, then invites the interviewer to contribute. You have converted an unknown into a demonstration of how you think. That's a better outcome than knowing the answer, because knowing the answer only proves you read a blog post.

The two silences that kill rounds. The stall: you go quiet for ninety seconds because you're stuck. Don't — narrate the stuckness: "I'm weighing two options here and I don't love either; let me talk through both." The drift: you go quiet while drawing for four minutes, and the interviewer disengages. Narrate every box as you draw it, and say why it's there. A whiteboard filled in silence is worth almost nothing.
Practical mechanics for a remote round: you'll be on Excalidraw, a shared doc, or occasionally just talking. Keep the requirements and the numbers visible in a corner of the canvas the whole time and point back at them when you justify a choice — "we said 100:1 reads, so…" That physical gesture of tying a decision back to a requirement is the entire method made visible, and interviewers notice it.

7. The three different rounds — and telling them apart in 60 seconds

Your résumé — React, 7 years, pivoting full-stack — will get you into all three of these, sometimes in the same loop. They are graded on different rubrics and answering one in the style of another is a fast fail. The good news: the prompt itself tells you which room you're in, if you know what to listen for.

Backend / distributed (HLD)Frontend system designLow-level design (LLD / OOD)
The prompt sounds like "Design a URL shortener." "Design Twitter." "Design a rate limiter." "Design a news feed UI." "Design an autocomplete component." "Design a design system." "Design a parking lot." "Design an elevator system." "Model a chess game."
The tell A system or company name. The question is about scale. The words UI, component, page, widget, client. Or: the interviewer is a frontend engineer and the loop is a frontend loop. A physical object or a game. No scale in the prompt at all — you'll never be asked the QPS of a parking lot.
You're graded on Scale, trade-offs, bottlenecks, data modelling, distributed failure. Component decomposition, state management, API/data contract with the server, rendering performance, a11y, network optimisation. Class design, SOLID, encapsulation, choosing the right pattern, extensibility, thread-safety.
The method The six steps on this page. RADIO — Requirements, Architecture/components, Data model, Interface/API, Optimizations. → tutorial 19 Nouns → classes, verbs → methods, variation points → interfaces. → tutorial 24
Estimation? Yes — QPS, storage, bandwidth. Central. Rarely numeric. "How many items in the feed, how big is a payload, what's the bundle budget." No. Estimation is meaningless here.
Fatal mistake Boxes before requirements. Answering it like a backend round. Drawing a load balancer and a Cassandra cluster when they asked you to design an autocomplete component is an instant fail — you've shown you don't know what round you're in. Writing procedural code with one god class. Or drawing infrastructure.

The 60-second disambiguation. If it's genuinely ambiguous — "design Instagram" could be either — just ask, and asking is itself a point:

"Quick check before I start: do you want me to focus on the distributed backend — storage, fan-out, scale — or on the client architecture: components, state, data fetching, rendering? I'm happy either way, and I'd approach them quite differently." This is a 10-second question that eliminates the single most expensive failure available to you in this phase, and it demonstrates that you know the two rounds are different disciplines. Most candidates don't.

For you specifically: the frontend round is your edge, not your fallback. Seven years of React means you have real, hard-won opinions about virtualised lists, optimistic updates and cache invalidation that a backend candidate simply does not have. Do not treat it as the easy round — treat it as the round you should be outstanding in. Tutorials 19–23 are there to convert that experience into interview-shaped answers.

Enterprise and fintech loops (Optum, JPMC, Goldman and peers) frequently run LLD as a separate round — often instead of, or alongside, a distributed round — and it's the one most FAANG-oriented prep material skips entirely. If you're interviewing at those companies, tutorials 24 and 25 are not optional. Their HLD rounds also skew pragmatic: idempotency, transactions, schema and caching, not web-scale fan-out.

8. Common failure modes

Six ways to lose a round you were technically capable of passing. Each one is a habit, and each one has a specific antidote. Read these before every mock.

8a. Designing for scale nobody asked for

The candidate is asked to design an internal tool for a company with 5,000 employees, and produces a globally replicated, multi-region, Kafka-backed, consistently-hashed monster. This is not "aiming high." It is failing to derive the design from the requirements — the exact same sin as the boxes-first candidate, just with fancier boxes. And it exposes something worse: an engineer who over-engineers in an interview will over-engineer in production, and that costs real money and real on-call pages.

The senior move is often the boring answer, delivered with the reasoning that makes it defensible: "At 5,000 users and maybe 50 QPS, this is one Postgres instance and a stateless app tier behind a load balancer. I want to be clear that's a deliberate choice, not a lack of ambition — I'd rather ship something operable and scale it when the numbers say to. The point at which I'd have to change this is roughly when writes exceed what a single primary can absorb, call it a few thousand a second." That answer scores higher than the distributed one, because it demonstrates you know when not to.

Complexity you can't justify with a number is complexity you're guessing at. Before you add a component, ask yourself: which requirement forces this? If the honest answer is "it's what real Twitter does," delete the box. You are not designing real Twitter; you are designing the system the stated requirements demand.

8b. Not asking the read:write ratio

This one deserves its own heading because it's both the most common omission and the most expensive. The ratio is the hinge on which the whole architecture swings, and skipping it means every subsequent choice is unmoored:

If it's…The design goes…
Read-heavy (100:1 — URL shortener, feed, product catalogue)Cache aggressively. Read replicas. Denormalise. Precompute at write time. CDN. Your write path can be slow and nobody cares.
Write-heavy (1:100 — metrics, logs, IoT ingest, ad clicks)LSM-tree store (Cassandra) not B-tree. Batch and buffer. Async ingestion via a queue. Partition by write key. Caching buys you almost nothing.
Balanced (chat, collaborative editing)Usually the hardest. You can't precompute everything and you can't just cache. Expect the real difficulty to be ordering and consistency, not throughput.
If you take one question into every interview, take this one. A candidate who adds a Redis cache without ever having established that the workload is read-heavy has added a component on faith. The interviewer notices, and the follow-up — "what's your expected hit rate?" — has no answer.

8c. Jumping straight to microservices

"I'd have a User Service, a Post Service, a Feed Service, a Notification Service…" — drawn in minute three, before any requirement justified splitting anything. Microservices are an organisational solution (independent deploys, team autonomy, independent scaling of a hot path) that you're paying for with distributed transactions, network failure between every call, and an operational burden. In a 45-minute interview, none of the benefits are visible and all of the costs are things the interviewer will make you pay.

Start with logical components and split only when something forces it: "I'll start with a single service — I don't have a reason to split it yet. The one thing I would peel out is the fan-out worker, because it has a completely different scaling profile from the read path and I don't want a fan-out spike to take down reads. That's a real reason to split; team boundaries aren't visible to me here." That's how a senior engineer talks about service boundaries.

Drawing six microservices and then being unable to explain how a write that touches two of them stays consistent is a self-inflicted wound. You have just handed the interviewer the 2PC-vs-saga question, and if you don't know that material cold (tutorial 05), you have manufactured your own failure.

8d. Naming technologies as if naming were justifying

"I'll use Kafka here." Full stop. Next box. This is the single most common way candidates fool themselves into thinking they're doing system design. A technology name is not an argument; it is a label on a hole where an argument should be. The interviewer's entire job at that moment is to ask "why?" — and if your answer is a shrug or a recital of Kafka's marketing page, you've lost more than you gained by mentioning it.

The fix is a fixed sentence template. Problem → property → tool → cost.

Weak (naming)Strong (justifying)
"I'll use Kafka." "Fan-out is spiky and I don't want it in the write path, so I need durable async buffering with replay — that's a log, and Kafka is the obvious one. It costs me end-to-end latency and an extra system to operate; a simple SQS-style queue would do if I didn't need replay."
"I'll use Cassandra." "Writes are 50k/s and the access pattern is a single-key lookup — no joins, no ad-hoc queries. That's an LSM-tree, write-optimised store, so Cassandra. I'm giving up transactions and flexible queries, which I'm fine with because nothing here needs them."
"I'll add Redis." "100:1 reads on a small hot set means a cache buys me most of the read load for very little memory. Redis, cache-aside, TTL of an hour. It costs staleness and it introduces a stampede risk on eviction, which I'd handle with a short lock or a probabilistic early refresh."
Say the property before you say the product. "I need a durable, ordered, replayable log" then "so, Kafka." Not the other way round. This ordering proves you'd have arrived at the right shape even if the product didn't exist — which is exactly the transferable judgment being tested. It also protects you: if the interviewer says "we don't have Kafka, what else?", you already stated the requirement and can reach for the alternative in one move.

8e. Never mentioning a single trade-off

A design presented as a series of unambiguously correct decisions is a design presented by someone who hasn't run one in production. Every real system is a pile of compromises, and the interviewer knows it. A round with zero trade-offs spoken aloud caps out at "mid-level" no matter how good the architecture is — because trade-off articulation is the dimension that defines the senior bar (see §5).

If you get to minute 30 and haven't used the words "the cost of that is," "the alternative would be," or "I'd flip this decision if," you are actively failing the round even if your architecture is flawless. Set that as a tripwire in your head.

8f. Running out of time before the deep dive

You spent 25 minutes on the high-level design. The interviewer asks about the celebrity fan-out problem with six minutes left. You get out two sentences before the round ends. Your scorecard reads: "never got to depth; couldn't assess distributed systems knowledge." That's a no-hire on a round where you actually knew the answer.

This is a time-management failure, not a knowledge failure, which makes it the most infuriating way to lose — and the easiest to prevent. The deep dive is where the points are. Everything before it is setup. Budget accordingly, watch the clock yourself, and cut your own high-level design short rather than let it eat the deep dive.

The tell that you're about to fail this way: you're adding boxes that aren't in dispute. A load balancer, a CDN, an auth gateway — nobody was ever going to challenge those, so drawing them slowly earns you nothing. Sketch the uncontroversial parts in ten seconds ("standard stuff — LB, stateless app tier, CDN for static assets") and spend your minutes where the disagreement lives.

9. Worked mini-example: design a pastebin

The method, executed end to end on a deliberately small problem, so you can see the whole shape at once. This is what the six steps look like when they're actually run. A real 45-minute answer is this with 5× the depth — but not with more steps. Everything in tutorials 07–18 is this page, expanded.

Step 1 — Requirements (~5 min)

The prompt: "Design a pastebin. Users paste text, get a link, share it."

You ask, and let's say the interviewer answers:

QuestionAnswerWhat it just forced
How many users?~10M pastes created per month.Modest write volume. Single-primary Postgres is probably enough — check in step 2.
Read:write ratio?Each paste is read ~10 times on average, but it's long-tailed — a few go viral.Read-heavy, and there's a hot-key problem. Caching is now justified, not assumed.
Latency target?Reads should feel instant. p99 under 200ms.Cache on the read path; the write path can be slower.
Consistency?A paste must be readable immediately after creation. Otherwise eventual is fine.Read-your-writes on create. This kills naive async replication on the read path — an easy trap.
Global?Yes, but a single primary region is acceptable.CDN/edge cache for reads; no multi-region write story needed. Big simplification, and I said it out loud.
How big is a paste?Text. Cap it at 1 MB, typical is a few KB.The 1 MB cap matters — it decides whether text lives in the DB or in blob storage.
Out of scope?Your call.Take it.

Functional: create a paste (text + optional expiry) → get a short URL. Fetch a paste by that URL. Pastes expire.

Non-functional: ~4 writes/s average, read-heavy with a long tail, p99 < 200ms reads, read-your-writes on create, single write region, 1 MB cap.

And the scoping sentence:

"I'm going to cut user accounts, private/password-protected pastes, syntax highlighting, and editing — those are all real but they're standard CRUD and they'd eat the time. I'll focus on the core create-and-read flow, the key generation scheme, and how reads stay fast under a viral paste. Sound right, or is there something else you'd rather see?"

Step 2 — Estimation (~5 min)

Out loud, rounding aggressively, and saying so:

Writes:   10M pastes / month
          ≈ 10M / 2.5M seconds     (a month ≈ 2.6M s — I'll call it 2.5M)
          ≈ 4 writes/sec           ← trivially small. One DB handles this.
          Peak, say 10x average    ≈ 40 writes/sec. Still trivial.

Reads:    10 reads per paste       ≈ 40 reads/sec average.
          But the tail: one viral paste could be thousands/sec ON ITS OWN.
          ← THE AVERAGE IS A LIE. The hot key is the real problem.

Storage:  10M pastes/mo × 10 KB avg  = 100 GB / month
          × 12 months                = ~1.2 TB / year
          With expiry + a 5yr horizon: single-digit TB.
          ← fits on one machine. No sharding needed. SAY THIS.

Bandwidth: 40 reads/s × 10 KB ≈ 400 KB/s average. Nothing.
           A viral paste at 5k reads/s × 10 KB = 50 MB/s. ← CDN territory.

Now the senior move — say which number drives the design:

"The interesting thing here is that none of the aggregate numbers are hard. 4 writes a second and a couple of terabytes is one Postgres box — I don't need sharding and I'm not going to pretend I do. The number that actually drives the design is the tail: a single viral paste can pull thousands of reads a second on one key. So this isn't a scale problem, it's a hot-key problem, and my design should be shaped around caching and edge delivery rather than around partitioning." That reframing — from "big system" to "one specific pressure" — is the whole reason estimation exists. Numbers you compute and then ignore score nothing.

Step 3 — API (~5 min)

Three endpoints. Not fifteen.

POST /api/pastes
  body:  { "content": "…", "expiresIn": 86400 }   // seconds, optional
  201 →  { "key": "aB3xY9z", "url": "https://pb.co/aB3xY9z",
           "expiresAt": "2026-07-15T10:00:00Z" }
  413 →  content exceeds 1 MB

GET /api/pastes/{key}
  200 →  { "key": "aB3xY9z", "content": "…", "createdAt": …,
           "expiresAt": … }
  404 →  not found OR expired  // deliberately indistinguishable

GET /{key}
  302 →  the human-facing HTML view. Same lookup underneath.

Two decisions worth voicing while you write those. Expired and missing both return 404 — collapsing them avoids leaking the existence of a paste to someone guessing keys, which is a small security instinct that reads well. And the key is server-generated, not client-supplied — if clients could pick keys you'd have a collision-and-squatting problem, and you'd be forced into a check-and-set on every write.

Step 4 — Data model (~5 min)

CREATE TABLE pastes (
  key         VARCHAR(8)   PRIMARY KEY,   -- base62, the shard key if we ever need one
  content     TEXT         NOT NULL,      -- ≤ 1 MB; Postgres TOASTs this out of the row
  created_at  TIMESTAMPTZ  NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
  expires_at  TIMESTAMPTZ                  -- NULL = never
);
CREATE INDEX idx_pastes_expires ON pastes (expires_at)
  WHERE expires_at IS NOT NULL;   -- partial index: only for the reaper job

The SQL-vs-NoSQL call, made explicitly:

"The only access pattern is get-by-primary-key, which is exactly what a KV store is for — so a case could be made for DynamoDB or Cassandra. But at 4 writes a second and a few terabytes, I don't need the horizontal scale that buys me, and I do want the operational simplicity and the transactional guarantees. So: Postgres. The specific load at which I'd flip is when writes outgrow a single primary, or when storage growth makes a single box painful — neither is close. I'd rather be able to point at the number that would change my mind than pick the 'scalable' option reflexively."

Key generation is where the interviewer will actually push, so pre-empt it. Three options, and the reason one wins:

SchemeHowCost
Hash the contentMD5/SHA of content, take 7 base62 charsCollisions. Every write becomes a read-then-write to check. Also: identical content collapses to one key, which you may or may not want.
Random 7 chars62⁷ ≈ 3.5 trillion keysStill needs a uniqueness check, but at 10M/month the collision probability is negligible for a long time. Simple. Unguessable — which we want.
Counter → base62A distributed counter (or per-node ranges), encodedNo collisions at all, guaranteed. But the keys are sequential and therefore enumerable — anyone can walk your pastes. Fatal here; fine for a URL shortener.

Pick random, and say why the counter loses: "For a URL shortener I'd take the counter, because guessability doesn't matter and I get collision-freedom for free. Here, pastes are semi-private by obscurity — so enumerable keys are a security hole, and I'll pay the small cost of a unique-constraint retry on insert to get unguessable ones." Same primitive, opposite choice, because the requirement differs. That is the entire thesis of this phase in one paragraph.

Step 5 — High-level design (~15 min)

                    ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
                    │  CDN / edge cache                │  reads of hot
   Browser ────────▶│  (cache GET /{key}, TTL 5 min)   │  pastes never
      │             └──────────────┬───────────────────┘  reach us at all
      │  POST (write)              │ miss
      │  bypasses CDN              ▼
      │                   ┌────────────────┐
      └──────────────────▶│ Load balancer  │
                          └───────┬────────┘
                                  ▼
                          ┌────────────────┐   cache-aside
                          │  App servers   │◀──────────────▶ Redis
                          │  (stateless)   │                 (paste by key,
                          └───────┬────────┘                  TTL 1 hour)
                                  │
                                  ▼
                          ┌────────────────┐
                          │   Postgres     │◀── reaper job deletes
                          │  (primary +    │    expired rows hourly
                          │   read replica)│
                          └────────────────┘

Write path, end to end:

  1. Client POSTs content. LB → any app server (stateless, so it doesn't matter which).
  2. App validates size (reject > 1 MB with a 413), generates a random 7-char base62 key.
  3. INSERT into Postgres. If the primary key collides — vanishingly rare — catch the constraint violation and retry with a new key. Bounded retry, not a loop forever.
  4. Write it into Redis immediately (write-through on create). This is what buys read-your-writes: the creator's immediate read hits the cache, so it can't be served a stale miss by a lagging replica.
  5. Return the key. ~1 DB round trip. Easily under 200ms.

Read path, end to end:

  1. CDN edge: hit → served in ~20ms, never touches your infrastructure. This is where the viral paste is absorbed.
  2. Miss → LB → app server → Redis (cache-aside).
  3. Redis hit → return. Redis miss → read from a Postgres read replica, populate Redis, return.
  4. Not found or expires_at < now() → 404, and cache the negative result briefly so a hammered bad key doesn't hit the DB every time.

Notice that every box traces to a requirement: the CDN exists because of the viral tail, Redis exists because reads are 10:1 and the hot set is small, the write-through exists because the interviewer said readable immediately after creation, and there is no shard router because the storage estimate said we don't need one. Nothing is there because a diagram somewhere had it.

Step 6 — Bottlenecks & scale (~10 min)

What breaks firstWhenFix
The hot key. One viral paste, thousands of reads/sec on a single Redis key and a single row. The first time something hits the front page. Already mitigated by the CDN — that's the whole point of putting it there. Second line of defence: a small in-process LRU on each app server, so even a CDN miss storm is absorbed locally.
Cache stampede. The hot key's TTL expires, and 5,000 concurrent requests all miss and all hit Postgres at once. Exactly at TTL boundaries, which is to say: at the worst possible moment. Probabilistic early expiry (refresh slightly before TTL, jittered), or a per-key lock so exactly one request repopulates and the rest wait. → tutorial 06.
Storage growth if pastes never expire. Slowly, then all at once, in year 3. Enforce a default TTL; the reaper job deletes on the partial index. If content genuinely must live forever, move the blob to S3 and keep only metadata in Postgres.
Write primary saturates. Not at 40 writes/s peak. Nowhere close. Say this out loud — declining to fix a non-problem is a signal. If it ever did: shard by key (it's a random uniform key, so hash sharding is trivially even — no hot partition).
Redis dies. Eventually. Everything does. All reads fall through to the replica. At our real load that's survivable — which is a genuinely nice property to be able to state, and I only know it because I did the estimation.

That's the whole method in about 1,500 words. The case studies are longer because the deep dives are longer — but the skeleton never changes. When you're on a whiteboard and your mind blanks, you are not trying to remember an architecture. You are trying to remember six words: requirements, estimation, API, data model, design, bottlenecks. The design falls out.

Interview Q&A

The interviewer says "design Twitter" and goes quiet. What are your literal first 30 seconds?

Do not touch the whiteboard. Say, roughly: "Okay — Twitter's a big surface, so before I design anything I want to pin down scope and scale. Let me ask a few questions, then I'll propose a scope and check it with you." Then run the six clarifying questions: DAU, read:write ratio, latency target, consistency, global or not, what's out of scope. Then the prompt-specific one: "Is the feed chronological or ranked?"

Then scope out loud: "I'll cut search, DMs, notifications, media and moderation, and focus on post → fan-out → read-a-feed, because that's where the scale problem lives. Sound right?" Now — and only now — pick up the pen. You've spent about four minutes and you've already earned marks on requirements, scoping, communication and drive before drawing a single box.

Why is the read:write ratio the single most valuable question you can ask?

Because it determines the shape of the system, not just a parameter of it. Read-heavy (say 100:1) means your job is to make reads cheap: cache aggressively, add read replicas, denormalise, precompute at write time, push to a CDN. You can afford an expensive write path because writes are rare. Write-heavy (1:100) inverts everything: caching buys you almost nothing, a B-tree store starts to struggle with write amplification so you want an LSM-tree engine, and you need batching, async ingestion through a queue, and partitioning by write key.

Those are not two versions of the same design. They're different databases, different data models, and different bottlenecks. Any component you place before you know which world you're in — a Redis box, a Kafka topic — you placed on faith. And the follow-up ("what hit rate do you expect from that cache?") will expose it immediately.

You're 25 minutes in and still drawing the high-level design. What do you do?

Stop, immediately and visibly, and take control of the clock rather than letting the interviewer take it from you:

"I want to make sure we get to the interesting part, so I'm going to lock the high-level design here. The pieces I haven't drawn — auth, the CDN for static assets, analytics — are all standard and I don't think they're where the signal is. I'd rather spend the remaining time going deep on the celebrity fan-out problem, which I think is the actual hard part of this design. Unless there's a piece of the architecture you'd rather I expand first?"

This is strictly better than either finishing the diagram (you'll run out of time and be graded as shallow) or going quiet. It also demonstrates the time management and prioritisation the rubric explicitly scores. Note that you're naming what you're skipping — that's what stops it reading as forgetting.

The interviewer challenges a decision you're confident about. Do you defend it or change it?

Engage first, decide second. Both reflexes — instant capitulation and instant defence — lose the point. Instant capitulation reads as no conviction (and sometimes they were testing whether you'd defend a correct choice). Instant defensiveness reads as someone who can't take feedback, which is disqualifying at a level where you're expected to run design reviews.

The shape is acknowledge → reason → resolve: "That's a fair challenge — let me think about what actually happens there. If Redis goes down, every read falls through to Postgres; at 10k QPS that flattens the primary. So you're right that I need a fallback, and I'd add [X]." Or, if you still think you're right: "I'd actually still keep it, and here's why — [reason]. But I'd change my mind if [specific condition]. Does that reasoning hold up, or am I missing something?" Both outcomes score. Only the reflexes lose.

What actually separates a "hire" at 7 YOE from the same performance at 3 YOE?

Four things, and none of them is "knows more technologies":

  1. Unprompted trade-offs. A mid candidate discusses a trade-off when asked. A senior candidate never presents a choice without its cost attached, before anyone asks.
  2. Driving. A mid candidate answers questions well. A senior candidate runs the session — states the plan, manages the clock, checks in, and leaves the interviewer mostly listening.
  3. Knowing what breaks first. "We'd add monitoring" is a mid-level answer. "The write primary saturates at roughly 5k writes/s and that's what forces sharding" is a senior one — a named component and a number.
  4. Justified restraint. Choosing the boring option and being able to say precisely what would change your mind. Naming ten technologies is a negative signal at senior; it reads as vocabulary standing in for judgment.
The interviewer asks about something you've genuinely never used — say, Cassandra's compaction. What do you say?

Never bluff. The interviewer almost certainly knows the answer, and a confident wrong answer retroactively poisons everything correct you said — they now have to wonder what else you invented.

Admit it, then reason: "I haven't operated Cassandra, so I won't pretend to know the compaction internals. But I can reason about the shape: it's an LSM-tree, so writes are appended to an in-memory table and flushed to immutable files, which means reads can have to check several files. Compaction must be the background process that merges those to keep read amplification down — which tells me it's a background I/O cost I'd need to plan capacity for, and a tuning knob if reads get slow. Is that roughly right?"

That answer scores higher than a memorised correct one, because it demonstrates exactly the transferable skill being tested: deriving behaviour from first principles when you don't have the fact cached. It also invites the interviewer to teach you something, which almost every interviewer enjoys.

Is it ever right to say "one Postgres instance and we're done"?

Frequently — and it's often the highest-scoring answer available, provided you show the arithmetic that justifies it and name the exit condition. "At 50 QPS and 200 GB, this is a single Postgres primary with a read replica. That's a deliberate choice: I'd rather ship something operable than pay the distributed-systems tax I don't need yet. The point where I'd have to change is when writes exceed what one primary absorbs — call it a few thousand a second — and then I'd shard on user_id."

What makes it score is the pairing: the boring choice plus the number that would overturn it. The bare statement "just use Postgres" with no numbers behind it reads as not knowing the alternatives. The same statement with an estimation behind it and a named exit condition reads as someone who has actually operated systems and learned what complexity costs.

How do you tell within the first minute whether you're in a backend, frontend, or LLD round?

Listen to the noun in the prompt. A system or company ("design a rate limiter," "design Twitter") is backend/distributed — run the six steps. A UI artifact ("design a feed UI," "design an autocomplete component") is frontend system design — run RADIO (tutorial 19). A physical object or game ("design a parking lot," "design an elevator") is LLD — nouns to classes, verbs to methods (tutorial 24). Context helps too: a frontend loop with a frontend interviewer is very unlikely to want a Cassandra cluster.

If it's genuinely ambiguous — "design Instagram" can be either — ask, and get credit for asking: "Do you want me to focus on the distributed backend, or on the client architecture? I'd approach those quite differently." Ten seconds, and it eliminates the most expensive failure in the phase: answering a frontend question with a load balancer.

What's wrong with saying "I'll use Kafka here"?

Nothing, if a because follows within five seconds. Everything, if it doesn't. A technology name is a label, not an argument, and the interviewer's next word is going to be "why?" — so answer it before they ask.

The template is problem → property → tool → cost, and note that the property comes before the product: "Fan-out is spiky and I don't want it in the synchronous write path, so I need durable, ordered, replayable buffering — that's a log. Kafka. It costs me end-to-end latency and one more system to operate; if I didn't need replay, a simpler queue would do."

Stating the property first proves you'd have arrived at the right shape even without knowing the product's name — which is the transferable judgment being graded. It also protects you: when the interviewer says "we don't have Kafka, what else?", you've already stated the requirement and can pivot in one move.

Why is over-engineering treated as a failure rather than as ambition?

Because it's the same sin as boxes-first, just better dressed: a design that wasn't derived from the requirements. Multi-region replication on a 5,000-user internal tool means you either didn't do the estimation or you did it and ignored it. Both are the failure the whole method exists to prevent.

It also predicts on-the-job behaviour, which is really what an interview is for. An engineer who reaches for consistent hashing when a single Postgres would do will do the same thing in production, and someone will carry that pager. Interviewers are explicitly listening for whether you know when not to. The safest way to prove you do is to name the complexity, price it, and decline it: "I could shard this, but at 40 writes a second that's complexity I'd be paying for with no return. Here's the load at which I'd revisit."

You've computed the QPS and the storage. Now what? Most candidates waste this.

They compute numbers and then never refer to them again — which scores almost nothing, because the numbers weren't the point. The point is the conclusion you draw from them.

So immediately after the arithmetic, say the sentence that converts numbers into a design constraint: "So the number that actually drives this design is X." Examples: "4 writes a second is nothing — this isn't a throughput problem, it's a hot-key problem, so my design is shaped around caching, not partitioning." Or "500 TB doesn't fit on one machine, so sharding isn't optional and the shard key is now the most important decision I make today." Or "100:1 reads means the cache hit rate is my architecture; everything else is secondary."

That single sentence is the whole return on the estimation step. And once you've said it, keep pointing back at it — every later choice should be visibly justified by it.

How do you avoid long silences without just narrating noise?

Narrate decisions and the reasons for them, not keystrokes. "Now I'm drawing a box" is noise. "I'm putting a cache here because reads are 100:1 and the hot set is small — the cost is a staleness window" is signal. The test: if a sentence doesn't contain a because, a but, or a so, it probably wasn't worth saying.

When you're genuinely stuck, narrate the stuckness rather than going quiet: "I'm weighing two options here and I don't love either — let me talk through both and see which one the requirements favour." That buys you thinking time, keeps the interviewer engaged, and often causes them to hand you a hint. Silent thinking is unscored thinking: if forty seconds of correct reasoning happens inside your head, it did not happen.

  • "The number that actually drives this design is …"
  • "I'll do [choice] — which costs us [cost]. I'd flip it if [condition]."
  • "I haven't used that in anger, but let me reason about it from first principles…"

The graded signals, in weight order: trade-offs named unprompted · depth on one hard thing · driving the conversation · design coherence (every box traces to a requirement) · requirements & scoping · handling pushback. Technical breadth is the lightest line on the card. Naming ten technologies is a negative signal.

The six ways to lose: designing for scale nobody asked for · never asking the read:write ratio · reflexive microservices · naming technologies as if naming were justifying · never speaking a single trade-off · running out of time before the deep dive.

The tripwire: if it's minute 30 and you haven't said "the cost of that is," "the alternative would be," or "I'd change this if" — you are failing the round regardless of how good the architecture is.

The compressed version, for when your mind blanks at the whiteboard: understand, scope, estimate, then design — and never let a choice out of your mouth without its cost attached. That's the whole page. Everything from tutorial 07 onward is this method run on a different problem.
