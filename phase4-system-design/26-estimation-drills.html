26 — Back-of-Envelope Estimation

Numbers you can produce in ten seconds, out loud, without stalling — and how to know which one matters.

Why this matters in the interview

Estimation is the one part of a system design round where you can be objectively, checkably right or wrong, and interviewers use it as a cheap signal for seniority. Not because the exact numbers matter — nobody cares whether you said 12,000 QPS or 15,000 — but because a candidate who can say "a billion writes a day is about ten thousand writes a second, and at 1 KB each that's ten megabytes a second, so roughly a terabyte a day, call it a petabyte over three years" in one unbroken breath has clearly done this before. The candidate who freezes, reaches for a calculator, or multiplies 86,400 by hand for thirty seconds has told you the opposite. The arithmetic is trivial; the fluency is the tell.

The point of estimation is never the number itself — it's finding the number that drives the design. You run the arithmetic to discover that this system is 100:1 read-heavy (so the whole game is caching and read replicas), or that it stores a petabyte (so it can't live on one Postgres box and you need blob storage plus sharding), or that the write QPS is 50 (so any single database handles it and you should stop over-engineering). A senior candidate does the math and then says "so the thing that drives this design is X" — the math is just how you earn the right to that sentence. This page gives you the numbers to memorize, the mechanical method to turn a DAU figure into QPS/storage/bandwidth, the verbal script to do it without stalling, and 15 timed drills to make it reflex. Do the drills out loud on a timer. Talking is the skill being graded.

Part 1 — The numbers to know cold

There is a small set of numbers that you should be able to recall the way you recall your phone number — no derivation, no hesitation. Everything else in estimation is multiplication and rounding built on top of these. Memorize this part. It's maybe fifteen facts, and they pay for themselves in every single design round you'll ever sit.

1.1 The latency ladder — orders of magnitude, not exact numbers

This is the famous "latency numbers every programmer should know" table, compressed to what actually gets used in an interview. Do not memorize the exact nanoseconds — memorize the ratios and the handful of round numbers you'll actually quote (RAM ~100 ns, SSD ~100 µs, disk seek ~10 ms, same-datacenter round trip ~0.5 ms, cross-country ~50–70 ms). The lesson of the table is the gaps: memory is ~1000× faster than SSD, SSD is ~100× faster than spinning disk, and a round trip across the country is enough time to read from RAM a million times.

OperationRough latencyRelative to L1What it implies
L1 cache reference~1 ns1×Free. The CPU baseline.
L2 cache reference~4 ns~4×Still effectively free.
Branch mispredict~5 ns~5×Why tight-loop micro-opt exists — rarely your interview concern.
Main memory (RAM)~100 ns~100×The number to anchor on. A cache hit in Redis is basically this plus a network hop.
Read 1 MB sequentially from RAM~10 µs~10,000×Memory bandwidth is huge; scanning RAM is cheap.
SSD random read~100 µs (0.1 ms)~100,000×~1000× slower than RAM. This is why a DB "disk" hit still isn't catastrophic on SSD.
Round trip within a datacenter~0.5 ms~500,000×The cost of one network hop. Every service-to-service call, every Redis call, pays this.
Read 1 MB sequentially from SSD~1 ms~1,000,000×~100× slower than the same read from RAM.
Disk seek (spinning HDD)~10 ms~10,000,000×~100× slower than SSD. This is why random I/O on HDD is the classic bottleneck.
Read 1 MB sequentially from HDD~20–30 ms—Sequential is fine; the seek is the killer.
Round trip, same continent~10–20 ms—Speed of light in fibre is real and unbeatable.
Round trip, cross-country (US)~50–70 ms—Why you put a CDN near the user. You cannot make this faster; you can only avoid it.
Round trip, intercontinental~150 ms+—Why multi-region write coordination is painful — every consensus round is 150 ms.
The interview-useful compression of this whole table is three sentences: "RAM is nanoseconds, SSD is microseconds, disk seek and cross-country network are both milliseconds — so a network round trip and a disk seek cost about the same, and both are ten thousand times more expensive than reading from memory." That last equivalence is the one that scores: it's why a cache hit (RAM + one network hop ≈ 0.5 ms) beats a database read that seeks (10 ms) by ~20×, and it's why the answer to "make it faster" is almost always "remove a hop or remove a seek," not "buy a faster CPU."

1.2 Powers of two and the data-size ladder

You need to convert between "number of things" and "bytes" constantly, so you need the powers of two that map to data sizes cold. The trick every senior engineer uses: 2¹⁰ ≈ 10³ (a thousand), 2²⁰ ≈ 10⁶ (a million), 2³⁰ ≈ 10⁹ (a billion), 2⁴⁰ ≈ 10¹² (a trillion). That is the entire conversion table between binary and decimal orders of magnitude, and it lets you jump straight from "2³² user IDs" to "about 4 billion."

Power of 2Exact-ishDecimal shorthandData-size name
2¹⁰1,024~1 thousand (10³)1 KB
2²⁰1,048,576~1 million (10⁶)1 MB
2³⁰~1.07 billion~1 billion (10⁹)1 GB
2³²~4.29 billion~4 billionthe classic "max unsigned 32-bit int" — run out of IPv4/int IDs here
2⁴⁰~1.1 trillion~1 trillion (10¹²)1 TB
2⁵⁰—~10¹⁵1 PB (petabyte)
2⁶⁴~1.8 × 10¹⁹~2 × 10¹⁹max unsigned 64-bit — effectively unbounded; you will never exhaust it

The byte-size building blocks you multiply with all the time:

  • ASCII char / small int — 1 byte. A short string of N chars ≈ N bytes.
  • Timestamp, 64-bit ID, long — 8 bytes. Round it to 10 bytes in your head; it makes the arithmetic cleaner and the error is noise.
  • UUID — 16 bytes raw, 36 chars as a string. A short URL code, a tweet ID: call it ~10–20 bytes.
  • A "row" of metadata (a few IDs, a timestamp, some flags) — ~100 bytes. A good default when you don't want to itemize fields.
  • A tweet / short text post — ~200–300 bytes of text; call it ~1 KB with all metadata and indexing overhead.
  • A typical web page / API JSON response — ~1–100 KB.
  • A thumbnail ~10 KB · a photo ~200 KB–2 MB (use 1 MB) · a high-res photo ~5 MB.
  • A minute of video — very roughly ~50 MB at decent quality (varies wildly with bitrate; state your assumption). An hour ≈ a few GB.

1.3 The one time-conversion that unlocks everything: 86,400 ≈ 10⁵

The single most useful rounding in all of estimation. There are 86,400 seconds in a day, and you round that to 10⁵ (100,000). That's it. Once you internalize "seconds in a day ≈ 100,000," every per-day-to-per-second conversion becomes a matter of subtracting five from the exponent. 

  Seconds in a day = 24 × 3600 = 86,400  ≈  10⁵   (you are ~15% high — say so, it doesn't matter)

  So, to go from "per day" to "per second", divide by 100,000:

     1,000,000 requests/day     →  1e6 / 1e5   =  10  requests/sec
     100,000,000 requests/day   →  1e8 / 1e5   =  1,000  requests/sec
     1,000,000,000 requests/day →  1e9 / 1e5   =  10,000  requests/sec

  Memorize the anchor:   1 billion/day  ≈  ~10,000 per second.
  (Real answer is 11,574/s. Rounding 86,400 to 100,000 makes you ~15% low on QPS,
   which is a conservative, safe direction to be wrong in. Mention it once, move on.)

Other time anchors worth having ready so you never compute them live:

  • Seconds in a day ≈ 10⁵ (86,400). The one that matters.
  • Seconds in a month ≈ 2.5 million (~2.6 × 10⁶). Handy for monthly bandwidth bills.
  • Seconds in a year ≈ 31.5 million ≈ 3 × 10⁷. Also: π × 10⁷ is a famous mnemonic (31.4M vs 31.5M actual).
  • Days in a year ≈ 365, round to ~400 or ~300 depending on which side you want to err. Many people just use ×400 for day→year so it cancels nicely with other roundings.
  • 3 years ≈ 1,000 days · 5 years ≈ ~2,000 days. Storage questions love "over 5 years"; multiply daily storage by ~2,000.
Why round 86,400 up to 100,000 rather than down to 80,000? Because dividing by a bigger number gives a smaller QPS, which is the conservative direction — you'd rather slightly under-state load in the arithmetic and then explicitly add a peak multiplier on top than accidentally over-state it and design for traffic that isn't there. More importantly, 10⁵ is a clean power of ten, so the exponent arithmetic stays trivial and you never stall. Clean beats precise. Every time.

1.4 The standard assumptions you're allowed to just declare

Interviewers expect you to invent the input numbers — that's part of the exercise. The move is to state a reasonable assumption confidently and move on, not to agonize. These are the defaults that no interviewer will argue with:

QuantityDefault to assumeNotes
Peak-to-average QPS ratio×2 to ×3 (use ×2, sometimes ×5 for spiky consumer apps)Traffic isn't flat over 24h; design for the peak, not the average.
Read:write ratio (social / content)100:1 (sometimes 10:1 or 1000:1)The number that decides whether the design is about caching or about write throughput. Ask for it or assume it, always.
Active hours in a dayOften modeled as ~10⁵ s (whole day), or ~50,000 s if you want to concentrate trafficUsing the whole day and then applying a peak multiplier is cleaner.
DAU as fraction of total users~20–50% of registered users are daily-activeIf given "1B registered," maybe 200M DAU. State it.
Actions per user per daya handful to a few dozen — e.g. reads 20–50/day, writes 1–5/dayTwitter: opens feed ~10×/day, posts ~0.1×/day. Declare it per app.
Bytes per typical request/object~1 KB text row · ~1 MB photo · ~50 MB per min of videoRound to a clean power of ten; the object size drives storage, the request rate drives QPS.
Cache hot-set fraction~20% of data serves ~80% of reads (Pareto)You only need to cache the hot 20% to get most of the benefit — this sizes your Redis.
What one commodity server handles~1,000–10,000 QPS for simple requests; ~64–256 GB RAM; a few TB SSDDivide total QPS by ~1,000 for a rough server count. Very workload-dependent — say so.
Say the assumption out loud as an assumption, with a number attached, and then never look back: "I'll assume 100 million daily active users, each opening the feed 10 times a day, so a billion feed reads a day — and I'll assume a 100:1 read-to-write ratio, so ten million writes a day. Stop me if you'd rather use different numbers." That last clause is the senior move: it signals you know these are levers, invites the interviewer to steer if they have a specific case in mind, and lets you proceed without waiting. Never ask "um, how many users should I assume?" — supplying the assumption yourself is the thing being tested.

Part 2 — The method

Every estimation you'll ever be asked to do runs down the same assembly line. Learn the pipeline once and you can apply it to a URL shortener, a chat app, or a video platform without thinking about the structure — you just plug in the app-specific numbers at the top and turn the crank. The pipeline is: 

  DAU
   │  × actions per user per day
   ▼
  requests/day
   │  ÷ 10⁵  (seconds in a day)
   ▼
  average QPS
   │  × 2–3  (peak multiplier)
   ▼
  peak QPS  ────────────────▶  drives compute: server count, connection pools, cache sizing
   │
   │  (separately) requests/day × bytes/object
   ▼
  storage/day
   │  × ~400 (→ /year)  or  × ~2000 (→ /5yr)
   ▼
  total storage  ───────────▶  drives storage tier: one DB vs sharding vs blob store
   │
   │  (separately) QPS × bytes/object
   ▼
  bandwidth (bytes/sec)  ────▶  drives network / CDN decisions

Three independent outputs — QPS, storage, bandwidth — off one set of inputs. Notice they branch: QPS comes from the request rate, storage comes from rate × object size × time, bandwidth comes from rate × object size. You almost never need all three to full precision; you compute the one or two that will drive the design and wave at the rest.

2.1 Worked example, end to end — a Twitter-like service

Watch the whole pipeline run once with real numbers. This is the pattern you'll reuse in every drill below. 

  ASSUMPTIONS (declare these, don't derive them):
    DAU                 = 200,000,000        (200M)
    feed reads / user   = 10 / day
    posts / user        = 0.1 / day          (most users lurk)
    bytes per post      = 1 KB                (text + metadata)

  ── QPS ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    read requests/day   = 200M × 10   = 2 × 10⁹   (2 billion reads/day)
    avg read QPS        = 2e9 / 1e5   = 20,000 reads/sec
    peak read QPS       = 20,000 × 2  = ~40,000 reads/sec

    write requests/day  = 200M × 0.1  = 2 × 10⁷   (20 million writes/day)
    avg write QPS       = 2e7 / 1e5   = 200 writes/sec
    peak write QPS      = 200 × 2     = ~400 writes/sec

    read : write  =  20,000 : 200  =  100 : 1   ← THE DRIVING NUMBER

  ── STORAGE (of the posts themselves) ────────────────
    per day   = 2e7 writes × 1 KB      = 2 × 10¹⁰ bytes = 20 GB / day
    per year  = 20 GB × 365            ≈ 7 TB / year
    per 5 yr  = 20 GB × ~2000          ≈ 40 TB over 5 years

  ── BANDWIDTH (serving reads) ────────────────────────
    read egress = 20,000 reads/sec × 1 KB = 2 × 10⁷ B/s = 20 MB/sec
                (per post; a real feed returns ~20 posts, so ×20 → ~400 MB/s)

Now the payoff sentence, which is the entire reason you did the arithmetic: the read:write ratio is 100:1, so this is a read-heavy system and the design is dominated by serving reads cheaply — caching, read replicas, fan-out-on-write so reads are pre-computed. Meanwhile 40 TB over five years is small enough that storage is a non-issue you can handle with a sharded database; you don't need anything exotic. Two sentences, both earned by the math, both steering the rest of the interview.

2.2 How to round — the actual technique

Rounding is not sloppiness; it's the skill. The goal is to keep every intermediate number to one significant figure and a power of ten so the arithmetic stays in your head. The rules:

  • Round inputs to one significant figure first. 86,400 → 100,000. 365 → 400 (or 300). 24 → 25 or 20. 512 → 500. Do the rounding before you multiply, not after.
  • Work in powers of ten via exponents. Don't multiply 200,000,000 × 10; write 2×10⁸ × 10¹ = 2×10⁹. Adding exponents is the whole trick — it's why you converted everything to scientific notation in your head.
  • Let roundings cancel. If you rounded seconds/day up to 10⁵ (making QPS low), and you round the peak multiplier a bit high, they offset. Being deliberately loose in opposite directions keeps the final answer honest without precision.
  • Carry units, not just numbers. "2×10⁹ reads/day ÷ 10⁵ s/day = 2×10⁴ reads/s." The units divide out and tell you the answer is a rate. This catches the classic error of dividing when you should multiply.
  • Sanity-check against an anchor. You have "1 billion/day ≈ 10,000/s" memorized — so any per-day figure lets you eyeball the QPS instantly and catch an order-of-magnitude slip before you commit to it.
The most common estimation errors in interviews are order-of-magnitude slips, not arithmetic mistakes — dropping or adding a zero, confusing millions with billions, or forgetting a unit (writing "20 GB" when you meant "20 GB per day," which is a 2000× difference over five years). Defend against this by always writing the unit and the time window on every number — "20 GB/day", "40,000 QPS peak" — and by keeping numbers in scientific notation so a missing zero is visibly a wrong exponent rather than an invisible extra digit. Say the exponent out loud: "two times ten to the nine" is much harder to get wrong than "two billion... or was it two hundred million?"
You do not have to compute all three outputs for every question. Read the prompt for which one is being probed: a URL shortener question is really a QPS + read:write question (storage is trivial); a photo-storage question is a storage question (QPS is modest); a video-streaming question is a bandwidth question (that's the whole point). Spend your ninety seconds on the number that decides the architecture and name the others in one line.

Part 3 — Estimating out loud without stalling

Here is the thing nobody tells you: the estimation section is graded on fluency and narration, not on the answer. An interviewer watching you compute 40,000 QPS in silence for ninety seconds learns nothing and gets bored; an interviewer listening to you talk through the same computation in thirty seconds sees exactly how you think. The failure mode isn't a wrong number — it's the stall: the long silence, the visible mental long-division, the "hang on, let me just...". You avoid it with a script.

3.1 The three-beat script for every number

Each number you produce follows the same three beats. Say them in this order, every time:

  1. State the assumption — "I'll assume 100 million daily actives, each posting twice a day." Own it. Don't ask permission.
  2. Round and compute out loud — "so that's 200 million posts a day; seconds in a day is about ten to the five; so ~2,000 writes a second average, call it ~5,000 at peak." Narrate the rounding as you round.
  3. Name what it means and move on — "that's low, a single database handles it, so writes aren't my problem here — the reads are." Then stop. Don't refine.

The connective phrases that keep you moving and buy you thinking time without sounding like stalling:

  • "Let me round aggressively — precision doesn't matter here, the order of magnitude does." (Grants you permission to be loose, out loud.)
  • "Call it roughly..." / "on the order of..." / "ballpark..." (Signals you know it's an estimate.)
  • "Seconds in a day is about ten to the five..." (You've memorized this; deploying it fast looks expert.)
  • "I'm rounding 86,400 up to 100,000, so I'll be about 15% low on QPS, which is fine — it's the conservative direction." (Naming your own error is a flex.)
  • "So the number that actually matters here is..." (The transition to the payoff. This is the sentence they're waiting for.)

3.2 A full narration, transcribed

This is what the whole thing sounds like spoken. Read it aloud once; it's ~40 seconds of talking.

  "Let me size this before I design it.

   I'll assume 100 million daily active users, and each opens the app
   about 20 times a day — so that's 2 billion reads a day.

   Seconds in a day is roughly ten to the five, so 2 billion over
   ten-to-the-five is about 20,000 reads per second average. Peak is maybe
   2 to 3x, so call it ~50,000 QPS at peak. I'm rounding hard — the shape
   is what matters.

   Writes: say each user posts once a day, so 100 million writes a day,
   which is ~1,000 a second. So my read-to-write ratio is about 20 to 1.

   So the number that drives this design is the read:write ratio — this is
   read-heavy, so I'm going to spend my effort on caching and read replicas,
   and writes will comfortably fit on a primary. Storage I'll check quickly:
   100 million posts a day at ~1 KB is ~100 GB a day, ~35 TB a year — big
   enough that I'll need to shard eventually, not big enough to worry about
   today."

  → ~40 seconds. Three numbers, one driving insight, zero stalls.
The single highest-leverage habit: always end the estimation by naming the driving number and what it changes about the design. Juniors compute QPS and storage because the template says to, then move on having learned nothing from their own arithmetic. Seniors compute exactly enough to say "so this is a read-heavy / storage-bound / write-bound system, which means the interesting part is X." The estimation isn't a checkbox you tick before the real design — it's how you discover what the real design is about. If you finish the numbers and can't say what they changed, you did the arithmetic for nothing.
Do not reach for a calculator, and do not do long division on the whiteboard. Both read as "I can't do this in my head," which is exactly the thing the section tests. If you catch yourself about to compute 86,400 × 365 by hand, stop — that's a signal you skipped a rounding step. Round the inputs to clean powers of ten first, and the arithmetic collapses to adding exponents, which nobody needs a calculator for. The mental move is "make it easy, then compute," never "compute the hard thing precisely."

Part 4 — 15 timed drills

This is the gym. Cover the answer, set a 90-second timer, and do each one out loud using the three-beat script — assumption, round-and-compute, name the driver. The answers show the full arithmetic and end by naming the number that drives the design, because reproducing that final sentence is the actual goal. Your numbers won't match mine exactly and that's fine; if you're within one order of magnitude and you named the right driver, you passed. Do these until the pipeline is muscle memory.

Drill 1 — QPS for a Twitter-like feed. 200M DAU. What's your read and write QPS, and which drives the design?

Assumptions: 200M DAU · each opens the feed ~10×/day (reads) · each posts ~0.1×/day (writes) · most users lurk.

  reads/day   = 200M × 10   = 2 × 10⁹
  avg read QPS = 2e9 / 1e5   = 20,000/s
  peak (×2)    = ~40,000/s

  writes/day  = 200M × 0.1  = 2 × 10⁷
  avg write QPS = 2e7 / 1e5  = 200/s
  peak (×2)    = ~400/s

  read : write = 100 : 1

Driver: the 100:1 read:write ratio. This is a read-heavy system — the design is about serving reads cheaply (caching, read replicas, fan-out-on-write to pre-compute feeds). 400 writes/sec is trivial and fits on a single primary; stop worrying about the write path. "So the whole game here is reads."

Drill 2 — Storage for a photo service. 100M users upload 2 photos/day. How much storage over 5 years?

Assumptions: 100M DAU · 2 uploads/user/day · ~1 MB per photo (after compression; add thumbnails ~10% overhead, ignore for now).

  photos/day  = 100M × 2    = 2 × 10⁸  (200 million/day)
  bytes/day   = 2e8 × 1 MB  = 2 × 10⁸ MB = 2 × 10¹⁴ B = 200 TB/day
  per year    = 200 TB × 365 ≈ 73 PB/year   (round: ~70 PB)
  per 5 years = 200 TB × ~2000 ≈ 400 PB

Driver: storage, and it's enormous — hundreds of petabytes. This immediately rules out storing blobs in a database: photos go to object storage (S3/blob store), and only the metadata (owner, URL, timestamp — ~100 bytes/photo → ~7 TB over 5 years, tiny) goes in a database. "The petabyte number forces the metadata/blob split — that's the first architectural decision this makes for me." Add a replication factor of 3 for durability and you're at ~1.2 EB raw; tier cold photos to cheaper storage.

Drill 3 — Bandwidth for video streaming. Netflix-scale: 100M concurrent streams at 5 Mbps. What egress bandwidth?

Assumptions: 100M concurrent viewers at peak · average bitrate 5 Mbps (1080p-ish, adaptive).

  total = 100M × 5 Mbps
        = 10⁸ × 5 × 10⁶ bits/s
        = 5 × 10¹⁴ bits/s
        = 5 × 10¹³ bytes/s   (÷8, round 8→10)
        ≈ 50 TB/s of egress

Driver: bandwidth, and it's astronomical — tens of terabytes per second. No origin datacenter serves this; it's physically why video is a CDN problem, not a compute problem. The design is: encode once, push copies to thousands of edge caches near users (even into ISPs — Netflix's Open Connect appliances live inside ISP networks), and serve ~95%+ of bytes from the edge. "The bandwidth number is so large it makes the CDN the entire system — origin serves almost nothing."

Drill 4 — Cache memory for a hot set. 500M items, each 1 KB, 80/20 access. How much Redis RAM to cache the hot set?

Assumptions: 500M total items · ~1 KB each · Pareto: ~20% of items serve ~80% of reads, so cache the hot 20%.

  full dataset  = 5 × 10⁸ × 1 KB = 5 × 10¹¹ B = 500 GB
  hot 20%       = 500 GB × 0.2   = 100 GB
  + overhead    ≈ 100 GB × ~1.3  ≈ 130 GB  (Redis per-key metadata, fragmentation)

Driver: the hot-set size, ~100 GB. That doesn't fit one commodity Redis node comfortably (~64 GB is a common ceiling before you're nervous), so you shard across ~2–4 Redis nodes (or use Redis Cluster), and you get to serve ~80% of reads from RAM while only paying for 20% of the data in memory. "The 80/20 rule is what makes the cache affordable — I don't cache 500 GB, I cache 100 GB and still catch most traffic." If you needed to cache everything, you'd re-examine whether Redis is even the right tier.

Drill 5 — Number of app servers. 50,000 peak QPS, each server handles ~1,000 QPS. How many servers (with headroom)?

Assumptions: 50,000 QPS at peak · one commodity server sustains ~1,000 QPS for a lightweight request (very workload-dependent — say so) · you never run servers at 100%.

  bare minimum = 50,000 / 1,000        = 50 servers
  run at ~50%  = 50 / 0.5              = 100 servers  (headroom for spikes + failures)
  + N+2 across 3 AZs, round up         ≈ ~120 servers

Driver: the per-server QPS assumption, which you should state and caveat loudly — "assuming ~1,000 QPS per box, which depends entirely on what the request does; if each request makes 3 DB calls it's more like 200 QPS and I need 5× the fleet." The senior moves here: never size to 100% (leave headroom for traffic spikes and for losing a node), spread across availability zones so one AZ failure doesn't halve you, and note that the real bottleneck is usually the downstream (database connections, not app CPU) — so the honest answer is "~100 app servers, but the constraint is probably the database, let me check that next."

Drill 6 — URL-shortener keyspace. How many short codes do you need over 10 years, and how long is the code?

Assumptions: 100M new URLs/day (generous — this is a big service) · retain for 10 years · codes are base62 (a–z, A–Z, 0–9).

  urls/day    = 10⁸
  per year    = 10⁸ × 365   ≈ 3.65 × 10¹⁰  (round ~4 × 10¹⁰)
  10 years    ≈ 4 × 10¹¹    (~400 billion codes needed)

  how many chars of base62?  each char = 62 values ≈ ~10^1.79
    62⁶ = 5.6 × 10¹⁰   (56 billion  — not enough)
    62⁷ = 3.5 × 10¹²   (3.5 trillion — plenty)

  → 7 base62 characters covers it with ~1000× headroom.

Driver: the keyspace vs code length trade-off. 7 characters gives ~3.5 trillion codes — comfortably more than the ~400 billion you need, with room to spare so you're not forced to lengthen codes later. "I'll use 7-character base62 codes; that's 62⁷ ≈ 3.5 trillion, which covers a decade at 100M/day with 1000× headroom, and 7 characters keeps the URL short." Note the read side is where the real load is — a URL shortener is ~100:1 read-heavy, so the shortening is trivial and the redirect path is what you cache.

Drill 7 — Storage QPS vs bandwidth for a chat app. 1B messages/day. What write QPS and storage/year?

Assumptions: 1 billion messages/day · ~1 KB per message stored (text + metadata; media stored separately as blobs).

  writes/day   = 10⁹
  avg write QPS = 10⁹ / 10⁵ = 10,000/s
  peak (×3)    = ~30,000/s

  storage/day  = 10⁹ × 1 KB = 10¹² B = 1 TB/day
  storage/year = 1 TB × 365 ≈ 365 TB/year  (~0.4 PB/yr)

Driver: the write QPS (~10,000–30,000/s) — this is a write-heavy system, the mirror image of the feed. That rules out a single primary comfortably taking all writes; you shard messages (by chat ID or user ID) and you reach for a write-optimized store (Cassandra's LSM-tree, per tutorial 03) over a B-tree Postgres if the write rate keeps climbing. "1 billion messages a day is ~10k writes/sec — write-heavy, so I shard by conversation and pick an LSM-based store; the read pattern (recent messages in one chat) shards cleanly by the same key." Storage (~0.4 PB/yr) is real but secondary; tier old messages to cold storage.

Drill 8 — Cache memory for active sessions. 50M concurrent users, 1 KB session each. How much RAM?

Assumptions: 50M concurrent active sessions at peak · ~1 KB per session (user ID, token, a few flags, expiry).

  memory = 50 × 10⁶ × 1 KB
         = 5 × 10⁷ × 10³ B
         = 5 × 10¹⁰ B
         = 50 GB   (+ ~30% overhead ≈ 65 GB)

Driver: the total, ~50–65 GB — which comfortably fits in RAM on one or two Redis nodes, so session storage is a solved problem, not an architectural challenge. The insight to voice: session data is small (1 KB) and the count is bounded by concurrent users (not total registered), so it's always modest. "Sessions are tiny and bounded by concurrency, so ~50 GB — a single replicated Redis holds all of it; I'll set a TTL equal to the session timeout so expiry is automatic." Contrast with drill 4: same 1 KB objects, but 500M of them needed sharding — the count is what moves the answer.

Drill 9 — Bandwidth for an image-heavy feed (Instagram). 500M DAU, 50 images viewed/day at 200 KB. Egress?

Assumptions: 500M DAU · ~50 images viewed per user per day · ~200 KB per image (feed-resolution, not full-res).

  images/day  = 500M × 50   = 2.5 × 10¹⁰
  bytes/day   = 2.5e10 × 200 KB = 2.5e10 × 2e5 B = 5 × 10¹⁵ B = 5 PB/day
  avg egress  = 5e15 B / 1e5 s  = 5 × 10¹⁰ B/s = 50 GB/s
  peak (×2)   ≈ 100 GB/s

Driver: bandwidth, ~50–100 GB/s. Like video (drill 3) this is a CDN problem — you cannot and should not serve 5 PB/day of images from origin. The design: images live in blob storage, are served through a CDN, and you generate multiple resolutions on upload so the feed pulls the 200 KB version, not the 5 MB original. "5 petabytes a day of egress means the CDN hit ratio is the single most important number in this system — every percent of cache-miss is another ~50 GB/s hammering the origin." (Echoes the hit-ratio math from tutorial 06 — a 99% vs 95% CDN hit rate is a 5× difference in origin load.)

Drill 10 — Database rows and index size. 1B users, 100 rows each. Row count, table size, and does the index fit in RAM?

Assumptions: 1B users · ~100 rows/user (orders, events, whatever) · ~200 bytes/row · index is on an 8-byte key + pointer ≈ ~20 bytes/entry.

  rows        = 10⁹ × 100    = 10¹¹  (100 billion rows)
  table size  = 10¹¹ × 200 B = 2 × 10¹³ B = 20 TB
  index size  = 10¹¹ × 20 B  = 2 × 10¹² B = 2 TB

Driver: the row count (10¹¹) and the 2 TB index. A 20 TB table with 100 billion rows does not live on one machine — you shard (by user ID), and each shard holds a slice whose index fits in that node's RAM. The point to make: "the B-tree index is 2 TB, which won't fit in a single box's RAM, so lookups would hit disk — that alone forces sharding so each shard's index stays memory-resident." This is the estimate that justifies partitioning (tutorial 04): you shard not because you ran out of disk but because you ran out of RAM for the index, and once the index spills to disk your P99 falls off a cliff.

Drill 11 — Peak QPS from a spiky event. A flash sale: 10M users hit "buy" in a 60-second window. Peak QPS?

Assumptions: 10M users all converge in ~60 seconds · each retries ~2–3× when it's slow (so effective load is higher) · this is NOT an "average over a day" problem — the whole point is the spike.

  naive avg   = 10M / 60 s ≈ 167,000 requests/s
  but they cluster in the first ~10 s, not uniform:
              ≈ 10M / 10 s = 10⁶ requests/s at the true peak
  × retries (×3 under load)  ≈ 3 × 10⁶ requests/s

Driver: the peak, and the lesson is that averaging over the window hides it — real flash-sale traffic isn't uniform, it front-loads into the first few seconds, and failed requests retry and amplify. "The daily-average framing is a trap here; this is a thundering-herd problem where peak is 10–30× the naive average, so I design for the spike: a queue to absorb it, a rate limiter / virtual waiting room to shed it, and inventory reservations so I'm not taking 3 million writes/sec at the database." Naming that the peak, not the average, drives everything is the entire point of this drill (ties to tutorial 14, ticket booking).

Drill 12 — Bandwidth into your ingest pipeline. IoT: 10M devices each send a 100-byte reading every 10 seconds. Ingest QPS and bandwidth?

Assumptions: 10M devices · one 100-byte reading every 10 s each.

  QPS        = 10M / 10 s   = 10⁷ / 10 = 10⁶ readings/s   (1 million writes/sec)
  bandwidth  = 10⁶ × 100 B  = 10⁸ B/s  = 100 MB/s ingest
  storage/day = 10⁶ /s × 10⁵ s × 100 B = 10¹¹ × 100 = 10¹³ B = 10 TB/day (raw)

Driver: the ingest write QPS (~1 million/s) — that is far past any single database, so this is a streaming-aggregation problem (tutorial 18): readings land in Kafka (millions/sec is what it's built for), a stream processor rolls them up into time-windowed aggregates, and you store the aggregates, not every raw point. "A million writes a second means I never touch a database on the hot path — Kafka absorbs it, and I down-sample to per-minute aggregates so my 10 TB/day of raw becomes megabytes of rollups I actually query." The bandwidth (100 MB/s) is modest; the QPS is the whole story.

Drill 13 — Does it fit in memory? "Design an autocomplete." 10M search terms, avg 20 chars. Can the whole index live in RAM?

Assumptions: 10M distinct terms · ~20 bytes/term · a trie roughly ~2–5× the raw string size for node pointers (call it ~3×).

  raw terms  = 10⁷ × 20 B   = 2 × 10⁸ B = 200 MB
  trie (~3×) ≈ 600 MB
  + top-k completions cached per prefix, metadata ≈ ~1–2 GB total

Driver: the total, ~1–2 GB — which easily fits in RAM on one box, and that's the whole answer. "The entire autocomplete index is a couple of gigabytes, so it fits in memory on every node — I replicate the trie to each server rather than sharding it, and rebuild it from a batch job periodically." The senior instinct: before designing a distributed anything, check whether it fits in memory, because "it all fits in RAM on one machine" collapses an entire category of complexity. Most datasets that feel huge (a dictionary, a product catalogue, a geo-index of a city) are actually a few GB.

Drill 14 — Availability budget. Your SLA is 99.9%. How much downtime is that per year, and what does it force?

No arithmetic to derive — these are worth memorizing outright (the "nines" table):

  99%     "two nines"   →  ~3.65 days/year   (~14 min/day)
  99.9%   "three nines" →  ~8.76 hours/year  (~1 min/day)
  99.99%  "four nines"  →  ~52 minutes/year  (~8.6 s/day)
  99.999% "five nines"  →  ~5.3 minutes/year

  Mnemonic: each extra 9 divides downtime by 10. Anchor on
  "four nines ≈ 52 minutes/year" and multiply/divide from there.

Driver: what the budget forbids. 99.9% is 8.76 hours/year — a human can be paged and fix things by hand. 99.99% is 52 minutes/year — no human is that fast, so recovery must be automated (health checks, auto-failover). "Four nines forces automated failover because 52 minutes a year is less than one bad on-call response." And the multiplication trap: serial dependencies multiply, so five 99.9% services in series ≈ 99.5% overall (~1.8 days/year) — you cannot hit four nines by chaining three-nines components, which is an argument for fewer hops or for making a dependency non-blocking (put it behind a queue and it leaves your availability equation entirely). Ties to tutorial 06's availability math.

Drill 15 — The reverse drill: given "storage = 100 PB," what must the inputs have been? (Sanity-checking backwards.)

This is the skill that catches order-of-magnitude slips: run the pipeline backwards to see if a result is even plausible. Suppose you computed 100 PB/year and want to check it.

  100 PB/year = 10¹⁷ B/year
  per day     = 10¹⁷ / 365 ≈ 3 × 10¹⁴ B/day  (~300 TB/day)

  if objects are ~1 MB (photos):  3e14 / 1e6 = 3 × 10⁸ objects/day  (300M/day)
  → 300M uploads/day. At 2/user that's 150M DAU. Plausible for a big photo app? Yes.

  if objects are ~1 KB (text rows): 3e14 / 1e3 = 3 × 10¹¹ rows/day
  → 300 billion text rows/day. Plausible? NO — that's 100× any real service.
    So if you got 100 PB/yr from 1 KB objects, you slipped a few zeros somewhere.

Driver: the plausibility check itself. "Let me sanity-check that backwards — 100 PB a year is 300 TB a day, which at 1 MB a photo is 300 million photos a day; that's a top-five photo service, so the number's in the right ballpark." Doing this once, out loud, after a big estimate is a strong senior signal: it shows you don't trust your own arithmetic blindly and you know what a reasonable answer looks like. If the reverse check produces something absurd (300 billion rows/day), you caught a zero-slip before the interviewer did.

Interview Q&A

The drills above are the practice. These are the meta-questions about the technique — how to deploy estimation in a real round.

1. How precise do my estimates actually need to be?

Within one order of magnitude is a pass; the exact figure is irrelevant. No interviewer checks whether you said 40,000 or 45,000 QPS — they're checking whether you know it's ~10⁴ and not ~10⁶, because the design for 10,000 QPS (one beefy database) is completely different from the design for a million QPS (shard everything). The precision that matters is the exponent. Getting the exponent right and the leading digit roughly right is the entire bar. Spending time refining 40,000 to 42,300 is actively bad — it's precision the problem doesn't support, and it signals you don't understand that estimation is about orders of magnitude.

2. The interviewer didn't give me any numbers. What do I do?

Invent them, out loud, confidently — that's the test. "How many users?" is not a question to ask the interviewer; it's a number you supply: "I'll assume 100 million daily active users — stop me if you had a specific scale in mind." Vagueness is deliberate; the interviewer wants to see you take an ambiguous prompt and pin it down to concrete numbers you can compute with. Asking "what should I assume?" hands the work back to them and reads as not knowing that assuming is the job. The one thing you should genuinely ask is anything that flips the design — "is this consumer-scale or internal-tool-scale?" — because 100M DAU and 10K DAU are different problems. Otherwise, declare and proceed.

3. Should I compute QPS, storage, AND bandwidth every time?

No — that's the junior tell (mechanically filling in the template). Compute the one or two numbers that drive this specific design and name the rest in a sentence. A URL shortener is a QPS + read:write question; storage is trivially small, so you say "storage is a non-issue, a few TB, moving on." A photo service is a storage + bandwidth question; the QPS is modest, so you don't belabor it. Video is a pure bandwidth question. Reading the prompt to know which number is load-bearing — and skipping the arithmetic on the ones that aren't — is itself a seniority signal. It shows you're estimating to make a decision, not to fill a checklist.

4. I froze mid-calculation and lost my place. How do I recover gracefully?

Restate the anchor and rebuild from it — never sit in silence. Out loud: "Let me reset — a billion a day is about ten thousand a second, and I've got two billion reads a day, so that's ~20,000 a second." The memorized anchors ("1 billion/day ≈ 10⁴/s", "seconds/day ≈ 10⁵", "4 nines ≈ 52 min/yr") exist precisely so you always have a known-solid place to restart from. The recovery move is to narrate the reset — "let me redo that cleanly" — which reads as composure, not failure. What kills you is the silent stall; talking through a restart is completely fine and interviewers barely register it.

5. When does estimation actually change the architecture vs. just being a ritual?

It changes the architecture at the order-of-magnitude thresholds, and knowing those thresholds is the real value. A few worth carrying: ~tens of thousands of QPS is where a single database stops coping and you need caching + replicas or sharding. ~single-digit TB is where data stops fitting comfortably on one node and sharding enters. ~tens of GB/s of egress makes it a CDN problem, full stop. ~1M writes/sec means you can't touch a database on the hot path and it's a streaming problem. When your estimate lands near one of these thresholds, the number genuinely decided something; when it lands far from all of them ("500 QPS, 2 TB"), the honest and senior thing to say is "these numbers are comfortable, nothing here is stressed, so I won't over-engineer it" — which is itself a valuable conclusion the arithmetic earned you.

6. Do I need to memorize the exact latency numbers?

No — memorize the ratios and a handful of anchors, not the table. The four anchors that carry their weight: RAM ~100 ns, SSD ~100 µs, disk seek ~10 ms, same-DC round trip ~0.5 ms, cross-country ~50–70 ms. The insight you're actually deploying is the gaps between them — "memory is ~1000× faster than SSD," "a network round trip costs about the same as a disk seek," "cross-country latency is unbeatable, so cache near the user." You quote latency numbers to justify a decision ("a cache hit is 0.5 ms vs a 10 ms disk read, so the cache saves ~20×"), and for that you need the order of magnitude and the ratio, never the precise nanoseconds. Anyone reciting exact figures is signalling they memorized a blog post, not that they understand the hierarchy.

What to remember

The numbers to have memorized cold

  • Seconds in a day ≈ 10⁵ (86,400). The master key. To go per-day → per-second, subtract 5 from the exponent.
  • 1 billion/day ≈ 10,000/second. The QPS anchor — eyeball every per-day figure against it.
  • Powers of 2 → 10: 2¹⁰ ≈ 10³ (KB), 2²⁰ ≈ 10⁶ (MB), 2³⁰ ≈ 10⁹ (GB), 2⁴⁰ ≈ 10¹² (TB), 2⁵⁰ ≈ 10¹⁵ (PB). 2³² ≈ 4 billion, 2⁶⁴ ≈ effectively infinite.
  • Latency ladder: RAM ~100 ns · SSD ~100 µs · disk seek ~10 ms · same-DC round trip ~0.5 ms · cross-country ~50–70 ms. Memory ~1000× faster than SSD; a network hop ≈ a disk seek.
  • Object sizes: text row ~1 KB · metadata row ~100 B · photo ~1 MB · thumbnail ~10 KB · minute of video ~50 MB · 64-bit ID/timestamp ~10 B.
  • The nines: 99% ≈ 3.6 days/yr · 99.9% ≈ 8.8 h/yr · 99.99% ≈ 52 min/yr · 99.999% ≈ 5 min/yr. Each 9 divides downtime by 10.
  • Standard assumptions: peak = ×2–3 average · read:write often 100:1 · 20% of data serves 80% of reads · one commodity box ~1,000–10,000 QPS · ~64 GB RAM before you shard a cache · 5 years ≈ ~2,000 days.

The method, compressed

  • The pipeline: DAU × actions → requests/day ÷ 10⁵ → avg QPS × (2–3) → peak QPS. Separately: requests/day × object size × time → storage; QPS × object size → bandwidth.
  • Round to one significant figure and a power of ten, before multiplying. Work in exponents. Carry units. Let opposite roundings cancel.
  • Three-beat script per number: state the assumption → round and compute out loud → name what it means, then stop.
  • Always end with the driving number: "so this is read-heavy / storage-bound / write-bound / bandwidth-bound, which means the interesting part is X." The arithmetic exists to earn that sentence.

The thresholds where a number actually changes the design

  • ~tens of thousands of QPS → one database stops coping → caching + replicas or sharding.
  • ~single-digit TB / index bigger than one node's RAM → sharding (you shard for RAM-resident indexes, not just for disk).
  • ~tens of GB/s egress → it's a CDN problem; origin serves almost nothing.
  • ~1M writes/sec → no database on the hot path; Kafka + stream aggregation, store rollups.
  • Numbers far from every threshold → say "nothing here is stressed, I won't over-engineer it." That restraint is also a senior signal.
The one habit that makes all of this land: never present an estimate without immediately saying what it decides. A number on the whiteboard with no consequence attached is noise; a number followed by "so the design is dominated by X" is judgment. Do the arithmetic fast and loose to earn the right to that second sentence, then say it. Fluent numbers plus a named driver, in under a minute, out loud, with no calculator and no stall — that is the entire skill this page exists to build. Drill it until it's reflex.
