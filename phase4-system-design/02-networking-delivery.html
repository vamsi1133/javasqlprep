02 — Networking & Delivery

Everything between the user's finger and your first line of application code — and how to explain it under pressure.

Why this matters in the interview

You already know most of this page. Seven years of frontend work means you have operated every box in it: you've watched a cold DNS lookup add 100ms to your Lighthouse trace, you've fought a CORS preflight at 11pm, you've deleted your domain-sharding hack after moving to HTTP/2, you've purged a CDN because a stale index.html was pinning users to a deleted JS chunk, you've opened a WebSocket and then discovered it doesn't reconnect. What you have not had to do is stand at a whiteboard and say why any of it exists, in the right order, in ninety seconds. That translation — working intuition into stated mechanism and trade-off — is the entire content of this tutorial. The gap between you and a strong system design answer here is smaller than you think, and it closes faster than any other topic in Phase 4.

It matters because networking is the connective tissue of every case study that follows. You cannot design a chat system without defending WebSockets over SSE and then owning the connection-state problem that choice creates. You cannot design video streaming without a CDN strategy. You cannot design any multi-region system without knowing that a Mumbai→Virginia round trip costs ~150ms, which is the number that decides whether your protocol can be chatty. Interviewers rarely ask "what is TCP" as a question — they ask it as an ambush, three levels into a design, when you say "the client connects to the server" and they say "walk me through what actually happens there." A 3-YOE candidate names the boxes. A 7-YOE candidate names the boxes, the round trips each one costs, and what he'd remove if latency were the top-line requirement. Everything below is aimed at that second answer.

1. The full request path

Before any individual box, hold the whole path in your head. Every question in this tutorial is "what does this hop cost, what can it see, and what happens when it dies." Draw this from memory once and the rest of the page is annotation.

Two things to notice, because they're the ones interviewers reward. First, the expensive hops are at the top: DNS, TCP, TLS and the cross-continent leg dominate, and none of them are your application code. Second, every box after the CDN exists to be crossed as rarely as possible — the whole delivery stack is a series of increasingly desperate attempts to answer the request before it reaches your database.

The framing sentence for this entire topic: "Before I talk about services, let me account for the request path — because at international scale the network is the latency budget, not the application. A cold connection from Mumbai to us-east-1 costs a DNS lookup plus a TCP handshake plus a TLS handshake before a single byte of my payload moves. That's why we terminate TLS at an edge PoP 10ms away and keep a warm pooled connection back to origin." Say that in the first two minutes of a design and the interviewer recalibrates who they're talking to.

2. DNS

What it is. A distributed, cached, hierarchical lookup from a name to an IP address. You type api.example.com; something must return 52.14.x.x before a packet can move.

Why it exists. Obviously "humans can't remember IPs" — but that's the 3-YOE answer and it's not the useful one. The system-design answer is: DNS is the last layer of indirection you control before the client's packet leaves its ISP. It is the only place you can influence routing before the user has connected to anything of yours. That makes it a load-balancing and failover mechanism, not just a phone book. That reframing is the whole point of this section.

The resolution path

  browser cache ──▶ OS cache ──▶ stub resolver
        │                              │
        │ (all misses fall through)    ▼
        │                       recursive resolver (ISP / 8.8.8.8 / 1.1.1.1)
        │                              │  ← THIS is where nearly all caching happens
        │                              ├─▶ root NS      "who handles .com?"
        │                              ├─▶ TLD NS       "who handles example.com?"
        │                              └─▶ authoritative NS  "A record for api = 52.14.x.x"
        ▼
   IP address (cached for TTL seconds at every layer above)

Cold, that's up to four round trips and can cost 20–120ms. Warm, it's ~0ms — and it's warm the overwhelming majority of the time, which is why DNS almost never shows up in your p50 but disproportionately shows up in your p99 and in first-visit metrics. This is why <link rel="dns-prefetch"> and preconnect exist, and you've shipped both.

TTL: the knob, and the trade-off

Every DNS record carries a TTL. Resolvers may cache the answer for that long. This is a pure, clean trade-off and interviewers love it:

Low TTL (30–60s)High TTL (1–24h)
Failover speedFast — you can move traffic off a dead region in ~a minuteSlow — stale clients keep hammering a dead IP for hours
Query volumeHigh — resolvers re-ask constantly; you pay for it and it's a DDoS surfaceLow

Use it forRecords you intend to steer: failover targets, blue/green cutovers, GeoDNSStable records: MX, static apex, anything you won't move
TTL is a hint, not a contract. Plenty of resolvers, corporate middleboxes and JVMs (the JVM historically cached DNS forever by default — networkaddress.cache.ttl) ignore it. So never make DNS your only failover mechanism. The correct answer is layered: DNS steers users to a region; an anycast IP or a load balancer with health checks handles failures inside the region within seconds, without waiting for any client to re-resolve. Candidates who say "we'll just lower the TTL and repoint DNS" and stop there get pushed on it every time.

The part interviews actually want: geo-routing

"A user in Mumbai should reach your Mumbai region, a user in Ohio should reach us-east-1." How? Three real mechanisms, and you should be able to name all three and pick one.

The soundbite: "For 'nearest region' I'd use latency-based DNS rather than pure GeoDNS, because geographic proximity is a bad proxy for network latency — and I'd back it with anycast at the edge so failover doesn't depend on TTL expiry. Real anchor you already know: your CDN's CNAME. When you point www at d1234.cloudfront.net, you're handing geo-routing to someone who does it better — their authoritative NS answers with the PoP nearest the resolver, and the PoP's IP is anycast on top of that. You have already deployed a two-layer geo-routing system. You just never had to say so.

3. TCP vs UDP

What they are. Both are transport protocols on top of IP. TCP gives you a reliable, ordered byte stream with congestion control. UDP gives you "here is a datagram, good luck." Everything else follows from that sentence.

Why TCP exists. IP is allowed to drop, duplicate, and reorder packets. Almost every application wants none of that, so rather than each app re-implementing reliability, TCP does it once: sequence numbers to reorder, ACKs and retransmission to recover loss, a sliding window plus congestion control so you don't melt the network. The price is state and round trips.

The handshake, and why it costs you a round trip

  Client                                Server
    │ ── SYN  (seq=x) ──────────────────▶ │   ┐
    │ ◀───────────── SYN-ACK (seq=y,ack) │   │ 1 full RTT before you can send data
    │ ── ACK  ──────────────────────────▶ │   ┘
    │ ── GET /api/thing ────────────────▶ │   (data can ride with the final ACK in theory)

Three messages, but only one full round trip of waiting before the client may send application data. Add TLS on top and you're at 2–3 RTTs. From Mumbai to Virginia at ~150ms per RTT that's 300–450ms of pure setup before your server sees the first byte. This single fact is why connection reuse, keep-alive, connection pooling and edge termination all exist — and why you should say it out loud.

Two more TCP behaviours that show up as follow-ups:

  • Slow start. A new TCP connection doesn't start at full speed — it ramps its congestion window. So a fresh connection is slow even after the handshake completes. Reused, warm connections are faster than new ones for two separate reasons.
  • Head-of-line blocking at the transport layer. Because TCP guarantees ordered delivery, one lost segment stalls delivery of every byte behind it — even bytes for a totally unrelated stream sharing that connection. Remember this; it is the entire reason HTTP/3 exists, and we'll come back to it in §5.

When UDP is right

UDP is the right choice when a late packet is worth less than no packet. If your data is only valuable if it's timely, TCP's "I will stall everything to redeliver that dropped frame from 400ms ago" is actively harmful.

Why DNS on UDP is the cleanest example: a query and its answer each fit in one small packet, so you'd be paying a full TCP handshake — a round trip — to reliably deliver a message whose total cost to retry is one round trip anyway. Reliability is cheaper at the application layer here: just ask again. (DNS falls back to TCP for responses too large for a datagram, e.g. zone transfers and some DNSSEC responses. Knowing the fallback exists is a nice detail.)

Why live video and gaming: if a frame from 300ms ago arrives now, it's garbage — the game has moved on. You want the current position, not a faithful replay of a stale one. TCP would deliver the stale one and hold up the current one behind it. That's the exact wrong behaviour, and it's the sentence to say.

Nuance worth having ready, because a good interviewer will spring it: video streaming (Netflix, YouTube VOD) runs over TCP/HTTP, not UDP. It's buffered, so a retransmit that lands within the buffer window is free — reliability is cheap and quality matters. It's real-time video (calls, live low-latency) that goes UDP, because there's no buffer to hide a retransmit in. Candidates confidently say "video → UDP" and get taken apart on it. The real distinction is buffered vs interactive.
Soundbite: "TCP trades latency for correctness — it will stall the whole stream to redeliver a lost byte. That's the right trade for an API and the wrong one for a live call, where a 300ms-old audio frame is worthless. So the question isn't 'which is faster', it's 'is stale data worth more than no data?'"

4. TLS

What it is. A handshake that (a) authenticates the server via a certificate chain, (b) negotiates a cipher, and (c) agrees on a symmetric session key — after which everything is encrypted with cheap symmetric crypto. The expensive, asymmetric part happens once per connection.

Why it exists in a system design conversation — not "because security", but because it costs round trips and CPU, and both are things you architect around.

The cost

  TLS 1.2:  TCP handshake (1 RTT)  +  TLS handshake (2 RTT)  = 3 RTT before data
  TLS 1.3:  TCP handshake (1 RTT)  +  TLS handshake (1 RTT)  = 2 RTT before data
  TLS 1.3 + session resumption:                                1 RTT  (0-RTT possible)
  QUIC (HTTP/3), new connection:   transport + crypto merged = 1 RTT
  QUIC, resumed:                                               0 RTT

Multiply by 150ms for a cross-continent connection: TLS 1.2 to a far origin is ~450ms of setup. TLS 1.3 to a nearby edge PoP (10ms) is ~20ms. That difference — 450ms vs 20ms, for the identical request — is the argument for edge termination, in one line.

TLS termination at the load balancer / edge

"Terminating TLS" means the LB (or CDN edge) is the endpoint of the encrypted connection: it holds the certificate and private key, decrypts, and forwards plaintext (or re-encrypts) to your backends. Four reasons this is the default:

  • Latency. The expensive handshake happens against a PoP that's 10ms away, not an origin that's 150ms away. Combined with a warm, pre-established, pooled connection from edge to origin, the user pays handshake cost against the near box only.
  • CPU. Asymmetric crypto is expensive. Offloading it (often to dedicated hardware) keeps it off your application servers, which you'd rather spend on business logic.
  • Certificate management. One place to rotate certs and renew Let's Encrypt, rather than N services each with a keystore. Anyone who has done cert rotation across a fleet will feel this one.
  • Visibility — and this is the L7 story in §7. A load balancer cannot route on a URL path, read a header, or inject a request ID if it can't decrypt the traffic. TLS termination is the precondition for L7 routing. That's the link between these two sections and it's the thing candidates miss.
"Terminate TLS at the LB, then plaintext to the backends" is fine if the internal network is trusted. In fintech, healthcare and anything under PCI/HIPAA — i.e. Optum, JPMC, Goldman — it very often isn't, and the expected answer is re-encryption (TLS termination at the edge, a fresh TLS connection to the backend) or full mTLS between services, usually via a service mesh. Saying "I'd terminate at the LB for L7 routing, then re-encrypt to the backend — in a regulated environment I'd assume the internal network is hostile and use mTLS" is a direct scoring line at those companies. Volunteer it there; skip it at a consumer-scale FAANG loop unless asked.

Session resumption

A returning client shouldn't redo the expensive handshake. Two mechanisms:

  • Session IDs — the server stores session state and hands the client an ID. Problem: it's server-side state, so with N load-balanced terminators the client must land on the same one (or you need a shared session cache). Sound familiar? It's the sticky-session problem again, one layer down.
  • Session tickets — the server encrypts the session state with a key only it knows and gives the blob to the client to store. Stateless, so it works across a fleet — provided every terminator shares the ticket-encryption key and you rotate it properly. This is the modern default and it's the same "move state to the client, signed" trick as a JWT.

TLS 1.3 additionally allows 0-RTT resumption: the client sends application data with the first packet. Free speed, with one catch that interviewers like: 0-RTT data is replayable by an attacker, so you must only allow it for idempotent requests. A replayed GET is harmless; a replayed POST /transfer is a fraud incident. (Idempotency is a theme that will return with force in tutorial 13.)

Soundbite: "TLS is a latency problem before it's a security problem. Terminating at the edge turns a 3-RTT handshake against a distant origin into a 1–2 RTT handshake against a PoP 10ms away, and it's also what lets the load balancer do L7 routing at all — you can't route on a path you can't decrypt."

5. HTTP/1.1 → HTTP/2 → HTTP/3

This is the section you already know from web-perf work — you just know it as tactics ("we sharded domains", "we inlined critical CSS", "we stopped concatenating after H2"). Here is the causal chain those tactics came from. The entire evolution of HTTP is one problem, solved at three different layers: head-of-line blocking. If you can tell that story straight, this whole topic is done.

HTTP/1.1 — head-of-line blocking at the request layer

One connection carries one request at a time. Response #2 cannot start until response #1 finishes. Browsers worked around this by opening ~6 parallel TCP connections per origin — hence the great hack of your early career, domain sharding: serve assets from static1., static2., static3. to multiply the browser's 6-connection cap. (HTTP pipelining was supposed to fix this and was a disaster in practice — broken proxies, and responses still had to come back in order, so it just moved the blocking. Nobody enabled it.)

Every HTTP/1.1 performance tactic you ever shipped was a workaround for this one limit: concatenate your JS bundles (fewer requests), sprite your images (fewer requests), inline critical CSS (fewer requests), shard domains (more connections). Say that out loud in an interview and it lands, because it's true and it's yours.

HTTP/2 — multiplexing kills it at the HTTP layer

H2 splits the connection into frames tagged with a stream ID. Many logical requests interleave over a single TCP connection, and responses can come back in any order. Plus:

  • Header compression (HPACK) — HTTP/1.1 re-sent your 800-byte cookie header on every one of 60 asset requests. HPACK keeps a shared dynamic table so repeated headers cost a few bytes.
  • Stream prioritization — the client can hint that CSS matters more than a footer image.
  • Server push — send resources the client hasn't asked for yet. It was clever, it was hard to use without wasting bandwidth (you push what's already in the client's cache), and it is now deprecated / removed from Chrome. Early Hints (103) replaced it. Saying that is a fresh, current detail most candidates don't have.

And this is why domain sharding became actively harmful: it fragments what should be one multiplexed connection into six, defeating HPACK's shared table, splitting congestion-control state, and multiplying handshakes. You already deleted your sharding config for exactly this reason. That's the anecdote — use it.

…but H2 still head-of-line blocks — at the TCP layer

Here is the punchline the whole topic builds to, and it's the question that separates people who read a blog post from people who understand the stack.

HTTP/2 removed blocking between HTTP requests. But all those streams ride on one TCP connection, and TCP guarantees ordered delivery of the byte stream. So if a single TCP segment is lost, the kernel will not hand any subsequent bytes to the application until it's retransmitted — including bytes belonging to completely unrelated streams. One dropped packet stalls all 100 in-flight requests. 

  HTTP/1.1 — blocking between requests, at the HTTP layer
    conn: [ req1 ........ resp1 ][ req2 ........ resp2 ]   ← strictly serial

  HTTP/2 — multiplexed over ONE TCP connection
    TCP:  [ s1 ][ s3 ][ s1 ][ s2 ][ s3 ][ s2 ]   ← interleaved frames, great
                       ✗ packet lost
    TCP:  [ s1 ][ s3 ][ XX ][ ?? ][ ?? ][ ?? ]   ← ALL streams stall waiting
                                                    for one retransmit (TCP HOL)

  HTTP/3 — multiplexed over QUIC (on UDP), independent streams
    QUIC: [ s1 ][ s3 ][ XX ][ s2 ][ s3 ][ s2 ]   ← only stream 1 stalls;
                                                    s2 and s3 are delivered

On a clean fibre connection you never feel this. On a lossy mobile network — which is most of your users — it is the dominant cost, and H2's advantage over H1 narrows or even inverts, because H1's six connections at least fail independently.

HTTP/3 / QUIC — why it had to move to UDP

You cannot fix TCP head-of-line blocking inside TCP. Ordered delivery is TCP's defining guarantee, and TCP is implemented in every OS kernel and every middlebox on earth — you cannot ship a change to it. So QUIC does the only available thing: it rebuilds a transport in userspace on top of UDP, because UDP is the one thing middleboxes will pass without opinions.

QUIC then re-adds everything worth keeping — reliability, congestion control, ordering — but per stream instead of per connection. Loss on stream 1 stalls only stream 1. It also:

  • Merges the transport and TLS handshakes → 1 RTT to first byte on a new connection, 0 RTT on resumption. TLS 1.3 isn't optional in QUIC; it's baked in.
  • Uses connection IDs instead of the 4-tuple, so the connection survives a network change — walk out of your house, phone flips Wi-Fi → LTE, IP changes, and your QUIC connection keeps going instead of tearing down and re-handshaking. This is called connection migration and it is a huge deal on mobile.
  • Costs more CPU (userspace, per-packet crypto, less kernel/NIC offload) and gets blocked or throttled by some corporate firewalls that distrust UDP — hence every H3 client keeps a TCP fallback.

In practice you don't "implement HTTP/3" — you check a box on your CDN and it negotiates H3 with clients that support it (via the Alt-Svc header) and falls back to H2 for the rest. The full soundbite, and it's one of the highest-value 30 seconds on this page: "HTTP's whole evolution is one bug fixed at three layers. H1.1 blocked between requests, so we opened six connections and concatenated everything. H2 multiplexed streams over one connection and killed request-level blocking — which is why domain sharding became an anti-pattern. But those streams still rode one TCP connection, and TCP's ordering guarantee means a single lost packet stalls every stream behind it. You can't fix that inside TCP, so HTTP/3 rebuilt the transport on UDP: independent streams, merged TLS handshake at 0–1 RTT, and connection IDs so it survives a Wi-Fi-to-LTE switch."

6. CDNs & edge caching

What it is. A globally distributed fleet of caching reverse proxies (PoPs — points of presence), sitting in front of your origin, close to users.

Why it exists. Two reasons, and candidates only ever give the first. (1) Latency — you cannot beat the speed of light, so move the bytes closer to the user. (2) Origin offload — a 95% cache hit ratio means your origin sees 1/20th of the traffic, which is a capacity and cost story, not a latency one. And a third, quieter one: (3) a CDN is your DDoS absorber — it has more capacity than your origin ever will, and it's the only thing standing between an attacker and your app servers.

Mitigations — request coalescing (the edge collapses N concurrent misses for the same key into one origin fetch — Nginx calls it proxy_cache_lock, most CDNs do it automatically), stale-while-revalidate (serve the stale copy instantly, refresh in the background), and TTL jitter. This whole family of problems gets a proper treatment in tutorial 06.

Cache keys

The cache key is what the edge hashes to decide "is this the same object?" By default it's roughly the URL. You can add headers to it — and this is where the whole thing lives or dies, so make it explicit:

  • Every dimension you add to the key multiplies your object count and divides your hit ratio. Key on Accept-Language and you now cache 20 copies. Key on the raw Cookie header and your hit ratio is effectively zero, because every user's session cookie is unique — congratulations, you built an expensive proxy.
  • The disciplined move is to normalize before keying: strip marketing query params (utm_*) so they don't fragment the cache, sort remaining params, and vary on a derived low-cardinality value (a country or device-class header the edge computes) rather than the raw high-cardinality one.
  • Vary: Accept-Encoding is fine (2–3 values). Vary: User-Agent is a classic self-inflicted wound (thousands of values).

TTL & invalidation — and why "cache invalidation is hard" is a real answer

Three mechanisms, in increasing order of how much you should like them:

  1. TTL expiry (Cache-Control: max-age=…). Passive, cheap, eventually consistent. You accept staleness for up to the TTL. Everything else is a workaround for a TTL you set too long.
  2. Explicit purge / invalidation. Tell the CDN to drop a key. It works, but it's slow (seconds to minutes to propagate across hundreds of PoPs), often rate-limited, and it is a distributed operation — so it can partially fail, and now some PoPs serve new content and some serve old. Purge-by-surrogate-key/tag ("purge everything tagged product:123") is much better than purge-by-URL, because one write usually invalidates many URLs and you don't want to enumerate them.
  3. Versioned/immutable URLs — the one that actually works. Never invalidate; change the name. app.a1b2c3.js with Cache-Control: max-age=31536000, immutable. A new deploy produces a new filename, so there is nothing to invalidate — the old object simply stops being requested and ages out. You have shipped this a hundred times: it's content-hashed bundle filenames from Webpack/Vite. You've been solving cache invalidation by sidestepping it since 2018.

The corollary you also already know: the one file you cannot version is index.html, because its name is the URL. So it gets a short TTL or no-cache, and it's the pointer to all the immutable assets. That two-tier pattern — a short-TTL mutable pointer to long-TTL immutable content — is a genuinely general caching design and it generalizes far beyond the browser.

Say this and it reads as scar tissue, not theory: "I'd avoid invalidation as a design goal rather than solve it. Content-hashed, immutable URLs with a one-year TTL and a short-TTL HTML pointer means there's nothing to purge — a deploy changes the names. Purge APIs are a distributed operation across hundreds of PoPs; they're slow and they can partially fail, so I don't want correctness depending on them. Where I must invalidate — a product price changed — I'd use surrogate-key purging, not URL purging, and I'd design the read path to tolerate a few seconds of staleness."

The interesting part: a CDN for a dynamic API

Everyone can say "the CDN caches my static assets." The senior answer is what the CDN does for an API that returns per-user JSON and can't be cached at all. Four things, and none of them are "cache the response":

  • Connection termination alone is worth it. Even at a 0% hit ratio, the CDN edge terminates TCP+TLS 10ms from the user instead of 150ms from origin, and forwards over a warm, pre-established, pooled connection on optimized backbone routes. You can cut hundreds of milliseconds off an uncacheable request. This is the single most under-said fact about CDNs in interviews. The CDN is not just a cache; it is a latency-optimized on-ramp to your origin.
  • Micro-caching. A 1–5 second TTL on a hot, non-personalized endpoint (/trending, /prices, a leaderboard) sounds useless and is transformative: at 10,000 RPS, a 1-second TTL means your origin serves 1 request per second instead of 10,000. Nobody notices one second of staleness on a trending list. Have this number ready — it's a great "10× traffic" answer.
  • Split the response. Most "personalized" pages are 90% shared content and 10% user-specific. Cache the shared part at the edge with a long TTL, fetch the personal slice separately (or stitch it in at the edge). Cache hit ratio is a function of how well you've decomposed your responses — not a property handed to you.
  • Edge compute (Cloudflare Workers, Lambda@Edge, Fastly Compute). Run code at the PoP: validate a JWT and reject unauthorized traffic 10ms from the user instead of 150ms away; A/B bucket a user and rewrite the cache key; do geo-based redirects; serve a personalized shell from cached fragments; rate-limit abusive IPs before they ever reach your infrastructure. The mental model: edge compute is where you put logic that must be fast and must not require your database. That constraint — no strongly-consistent state at the edge, because the edge is 300 PoPs and your DB is in one region — is exactly the trade-off to name, and naming the constraint is what makes it a senior answer.
The classic CDN horror story, and it's worth telling as a "what breaks" answer: caching a response that had a Set-Cookie header or a personalized body, because someone forgot Cache-Control: private. User A's session lands in the shared edge cache and gets served to User B. This is a real, recurring, career-defining outage class — anything user-specific must be private/no-store, and your cache key must never accidentally be shared across identities. Volunteering this unprompted in a design where you've put a CDN in front of an API is a strong signal.

Health checks

The LB pings each backend on an interval; N consecutive failures eject it from the pool, M consecutive successes bring it back. Two things that make this a senior answer instead of a bullet point:

  • Shallow vs deep. A shallow check ("is the port open? does /health return 200?") catches a dead process. A deep check ("can I reach the DB and Redis?") catches a useless process. But a deep check is dangerous: if the shared database has a hiccup, every backend fails its health check simultaneously, the LB ejects the entire fleet, and a degraded system becomes a total outage. The mature position: "shallow checks for liveness so I never eject the whole fleet on a shared dependency, plus separate deep checks that page a human and shed load — but don't remove capacity." If you know Kubernetes, this is exactly the liveness-vs-readiness probe distinction, and naming it lands.
  • Connection draining / graceful shutdown. When you deploy, the LB must stop sending new requests to an instance but let in-flight ones finish before the process exits. Without it, every deploy is a burst of 500s. Interviewers love this because it shows you have actually operated something.

  • Aggregation / BFF. The mobile app needs one screen's worth of data that lives in four services.   • Protocol translation. The outside world speaks REST/JSON over HTTP/1.1; your internal fleet speaks gRPC. The gateway is the seam. (This is the natural bridge into §10.)
Two failure modes to volunteer, because they're the follow-ups. (1) The gateway is a single point of failure — everything goes through it, so it must be replicated and horizontally scaled, and it must have a very fast, boring, non-blocking path. (2) The gateway becomes a monolith. Business logic creeps in ("just this one little transform"), and five years later the gateway is a distributed ball of mud that every team is scared to deploy — the ESB problem, reborn. The rule you state: the gateway does cross-cutting concerns only; if it needs to know what a "purchase order" is, it's in the wrong place. Saying that is a strong, opinionated, enterprise-flavoured answer.
Soundbite: "A reverse proxy is a front door, a load balancer is a reverse proxy that distributes and health-checks, and an API gateway is a reverse proxy that owns cross-cutting concerns — auth, rate limiting, routing, aggregation — so thirty services don't reimplement them thirty ways. In a design I'd draw the LB and the gateway as one edge tier and say so, rather than drawing boxes I can't justify."

The hard part isn't the protocol — it's connection state

Anyone can open a WebSocket. What makes this an interview topic is what a persistent connection does to your architecture, and it is a genuinely deep problem:

  • Your servers become stateful. A stateless HTTP fleet lets any box serve any request; that's why you can autoscale it freely. A WebSocket pins a specific user to a specific process for hours. That single fact breaks load balancing, breaks deploys (every restart drops every connection — you need drain windows and client reconnect with jitter, or a deploy becomes a self-inflicted thundering herd), and breaks autoscaling (scaling in means severing live users).
  • Routing a message becomes a lookup problem. User A on server 7 sends to user B — who is connected to server 23. Server 7 must find out where B is (a registry: userId → serverId, typically Redis) and hand the message to server 23 (via a pub/sub bus or a direct RPC). Your fan-out now depends on a distributed connection registry that must be kept correct through crashes, which is much harder than it sounds.
  • Connections are expensive in aggregate. Each open socket costs a file descriptor and kernel + userspace buffers — call it tens of KB. A million concurrent connections is a real memory and FD-limit engineering problem, and it's why chat systems have a dedicated, thin, horizontally-scaled connection tier that owns nothing but sockets, sitting in front of stateless business services.

All three of these get built out properly in tutorial 11 (Design a Chat System) — the connection registry, presence, and the fan-out path. What you need here is to be able to say "and choosing WebSockets means I've made my edge tier stateful, which is the real cost" — because naming the cost of your own choice is the single most reliable senior signal in this entire phase.

gRPC: why it's the internal answer

Three properties, and they all point the same way. Binary Protobuf means less bytes and dramatically cheaper serialization — which matters enormously when one user request fans out to fifteen internal calls and every one of them is paying JSON parse cost. HTTP/2 gives you multiplexing over one long-lived connection between services, so you're not paying handshakes per call. And the .proto contract is enforced: it generates the client and server stubs, so an incompatible change fails at build time instead of at 3am. The trade-offs, honestly: it's not human-readable (you can't curl it, and debugging is worse), it's awkward through browsers and some middleboxes, and it adds a codegen step to your build. That's exactly why it lives behind the API gateway (§8) and the gateway translates REST/JSON at the edge.

The soundbite that gets used in half the case studies in this phase: "REST at the edge, gRPC internally. The public API is REST/JSON because it's cacheable, debuggable and every client speaks it — but service-to-service I'd use gRPC: binary Protobuf over a multiplexed HTTP/2 connection, with an enforced schema so a breaking change fails at compile time rather than in production. The gateway is the translation seam."

GraphQL: argue about it like a systems engineer, not a frontend dev

You know why you liked GraphQL: no more begging a backend team for a new endpoint, no more fetching a 40-field user object to render a name and an avatar, no more three round trips to paint one screen. That's real, and on a 150ms-RTT mobile link (§11) collapsing three sequential round trips into one is worth ~300ms of perceived load time. Say that — it's the strongest argument for GraphQL and it's a latency argument.

But in a system design interview, the interviewer is testing whether you know what it costs on the server. Own all four:

  • N+1 queries. { posts { author { name } } } — resolvers are per-field, so a naive implementation fetches 100 posts, then issues 100 separate queries for the authors. This is the single most-asked GraphQL follow-up. The fix is DataLoader: batch all the author lookups within one tick into a single WHERE id IN (…), and cache per request. You have hit this exact problem from the Postgres side in Phase 1 — it's the same N+1 as a lazily-loaded JPA relation, and saying so connects two phases in one sentence.
  • Caching is genuinely hard. Everything is a POST to one URL with a different body, so HTTP caching — CDN, proxy, browser — is dead. You're pushed to persisted queries (register the query server-side, send a hash, make it a cacheable GET — this is the real answer) and to normalized client caches. You gave up the single greatest free win in web architecture. Name it as a cost.
  • You handed clients an unbounded query budget. A deeply nested or recursive query can be a self-inflicted DoS. So you need query depth limiting, complexity/cost analysis, and rate limiting by computed cost rather than by request count — which is a much harder rate limiter than "100 requests/minute" (tutorial 09).
  • Observability degrades. Every request is POST /graphql 200. Your per-endpoint latency dashboards, your per-route rate limits, your "which endpoint is slow" instincts — all gone. You have to rebuild observability at the resolver level.
The trap: proposing GraphQL for internal service-to-service communication. It's a client-facing aggregation layer — the whole value proposition is "an unknown client wants an unpredictable shape of data." Between two of your own services, the shape is known at compile time, so all you've bought is parsing overhead, N+1 risk and lost caching, and you've given up the schema enforcement you'd have got free from Protobuf. Use gRPC there. Equally: don't propose GraphQL for a system with one client and stable screens — you've taken on all of the cost and none of the benefit. GraphQL earns its keep when you have many heterogeneous clients whose data needs you can't predict. That's the sentence.
The pragmatic middle you should have in your pocket: a BFF (Backend-For-Frontend) REST endpoint that aggregates services inside the datacenter and returns exactly the shape one screen needs. You get GraphQL's round-trip savings, keep HTTP caching, and skip the entire GraphQL server. In a design interview, proposing the BFF instead of GraphQL — and explaining that you're choosing it because your client set is small and stable — is often the stronger answer, because it shows you can turn a technology down.

  • Speed of light is a hard floor. ~300,000 km/s in vacuum, ~200,000 km/s in fibre — and real routes are not straight lines. Deriving that out loud from the latency budget is a top-percentile move (and it's the method from tutorial 01 in action).
  • Memory is ~1000× faster than SSD, and a same-DC round trip costs about as much as a disk read. That's why a Redis lookup over the network still beats hitting the database — you're trading a cheap network hop for an expensive disk hop, plus you're offloading the DB.
  • Sequential beats random, everywhere. Reading 1 MB sequentially from SSD (~1ms) is far cheaper than 10,000 random 100-byte reads. It's why batching wins, why Kafka is fast, and why LSM-trees turn random writes into sequential ones.
Use the numbers as an argument, never as a recital. "Requirement is a 200ms p99 for users in Europe and our origin is us-east-1 — that's a 90ms round trip before we do any work, so nearly half the budget is gone on physics. Either we serve reads from an EU region, or we accept that 200ms is only achievable in North America. Which is it?" That is what estimation is for, and tutorial 26 drills it until it's automatic.
Nobody expects exact figures. Round hard and say you're rounding: "call it a millisecond for a same-DC round trip, call it 150ms across the world, memory is roughly a thousand times faster than disk." Confident approximations beat hesitant precision every single time, and hesitation is what actually costs you points.

Interview Q&A

Answer each out loud before opening it. If your spoken answer is shorter than the written one, you have found a gap.

Walk me through everything that happens when a user in Mumbai types your URL and hits enter.

The one they open with. Structure it as hops with costs, not as a list of nouns:

  1. DNS resolution. Browser cache → OS cache → recursive resolver → root → TLD → authoritative NS. Cold that's up to four round trips (20–120ms); warm it's ~0ms. Our authoritative NS does latency-based routing, so it hands back an IP for the nearest edge PoP rather than the origin.
  2. TCP handshake to that PoP — one RTT, but the PoP is ~10ms away, not 150ms.
  3. TLS handshake — 1 RTT on TLS 1.3, or 0 on resumption. TLS terminates at the edge.
  4. The CDN checks its cache. Hit → we're done, the request never touched our infrastructure. Miss → the edge forwards to origin over an already-warm, pooled connection on an optimized backbone route.
  5. Load balancer at origin: health-checked pool, least-connections, deep L7 routing on the path.
  6. API gateway: authenticate the JWT, apply rate limits, route to the right service, possibly aggregate.
  7. Service → cache (~1ms) → database on miss (~5–20ms).
  8. Response back up the chain; the edge caches it if the headers allow.

Then the closing line that makes it a senior answer: "Note that most of the elapsed time is in steps 1–3 and the origin leg — the network, not my code. That's why the edge exists."

Why does HTTP/3 use UDP? Isn't UDP unreliable?

UDP isn't the goal — it's the escape hatch. The real goal is eliminating TCP head-of-line blocking: HTTP/2 multiplexes many streams over one TCP connection, but TCP guarantees ordered delivery of the byte stream, so one lost packet stalls delivery of every stream behind it. You can't fix that inside TCP — ordered delivery is TCP's defining guarantee, and TCP lives in every kernel and middlebox on earth, so you can't ship a change to it.

So QUIC rebuilds a transport in userspace on top of UDP, because UDP is the only thing middleboxes will pass without opinions. It then re-adds reliability, ordering and congestion control per stream instead of per connection — so loss on stream 1 stalls only stream 1. It also merges the transport and TLS handshakes (1 RTT new, 0 RTT resumed) and uses connection IDs instead of the IP 4-tuple, so a connection survives a Wi-Fi→LTE switch. HTTP/3 is not less reliable than HTTP/2; the reliability just moved up a layer, where it could be made per-stream.

L4 or L7 load balancer — how do you choose?

It's a question of what the balancer needs to see. L4 works on TCP packets: it knows source and destination IP and port and nothing else, so it can only pin an entire connection to a backend. L7 terminates TLS and parses HTTP, so it can route on path, header or cookie, distribute per request rather than per connection, do deep health checks (GET /health, not just "is the port open"), retry, rewrite headers, inject request IDs, and rate limit.

L7 is the default for HTTP because you almost always want path-based routing and per-request distribution. L4 wins where you need raw throughput, non-HTTP protocols, or where you must not decrypt (regulatory or end-to-end encryption). In practice, run both: a cheap, fast, DDoS-absorbing L4 front door fanning out to a fleet of L7 balancers doing the intelligent work.

The connective insight to state: TLS termination is the precondition for L7 — you cannot route on a URL you can't decrypt.

WebSockets or SSE for a live notification feed?

SSE, and the reasoning is the answer. Notifications are strictly server-to-client, so full duplex buys nothing. SSE is plain HTTP: it passes through every proxy, load balancer, CDN and API gateway I already operate, it works with normal auth headers and compression, and the browser gives me automatic reconnect with Last-Event-ID replay for free — which is precisely the layer I'd otherwise have to write, badly, myself for a WebSocket.

I'd take WebSockets when the client genuinely pushes at high frequency — chat, typing indicators, presence, collaborative cursors, live trading — because then I'm actually buying duplex. And I'd say what it costs: my edge tier becomes stateful, deploys sever live connections, and I need a connection registry to find which server a given user is attached to.

Long polling stays in my pocket as a fallback for hostile corporate networks that kill long-lived connections.

You've put a CDN in front of a personalized, dynamic API. The cache hit ratio is 0%. Is the CDN useless?

No — and this is the question that separates people who've only read about CDNs. Even at 0% hit ratio the CDN terminates TCP and TLS ~10ms from the user instead of ~150ms from origin, then forwards over a pre-established, warm, pooled connection on an optimized backbone path. You've removed 2–3 expensive round trips against a distant origin and replaced them with cheap ones against a nearby PoP. On an uncacheable request that can still be hundreds of milliseconds. A CDN is a latency-optimized on-ramp before it is a cache. It's also your DDoS absorber and your TLS/cert layer.

Then I'd attack the 0%: micro-caching hot non-personalized endpoints with a 1–5 second TTL (at 10k RPS, a 1s TTL means the origin serves 1 request/second instead of 10,000, and nobody notices one second of staleness on a trending list); splitting responses so the 90% shared content caches long and only the 10% personal slice is dynamic; and edge compute to validate JWTs and reject bad traffic at the PoP. Hit ratio is a design output, not a fixed property.

Why is cache invalidation hard, and what do you actually do about it?

It's hard because a purge is a distributed operation across hundreds of PoPs: it takes seconds to minutes, it's usually rate-limited, and it can partially fail — leaving some PoPs serving new content and others serving old, with no easy way to tell which. So any design where correctness depends on a purge landing promptly and everywhere is fragile.

What I actually do: avoid invalidation rather than solve it. Content-hashed immutable URLs (app.a1b2c3.js, max-age=31536000, immutable) mean a deploy changes the name, so there is nothing to purge — the old object simply stops being requested. The one file that can't be versioned is the HTML entry point, because its name is the URL, so it gets a short TTL and acts as a pointer to immutable assets. That two-tier pattern — a short-TTL mutable pointer to long-TTL immutable content — generalizes far beyond the browser.

Where I genuinely must invalidate (a price changed), I'd use surrogate-key/tag purging ("purge everything tagged product:123") rather than URL purging, since one write invalidates many URLs, and I'd use stale-while-revalidate so users get an instant response while the refresh happens in the background.

Round robin or least connections?

Least connections, for anything where request cost varies — which is every real API. Round robin is blind: a server that gets unlucky and receives three ten-second report requests in a row is buried while its neighbours idle, and round robin will happily send it a fourth. Least connections is feedback-driven — a struggling backend accumulates open connections and therefore naturally receives less traffic.

The caveat worth adding: a freshly restarted server has zero connections and will get instantly flooded, so you want a slow-start ramp. And if the backend holds any state keyed by something — a cache node, a shard, a live WebSocket session — neither applies: you want consistent hashing, so the same key always lands on the same node and adding or removing a node remaps only ~1/N of keys instead of nearly all of them.

What's the difference between an API gateway, a reverse proxy, and a load balancer?

They overlap in implementation — Nginx or Envoy can be all three — but they are three jobs. A reverse proxy is the single public front door that hides your backend topology (TLS, compression, static caching, header rewriting). A load balancer is a specialized reverse proxy whose job is spreading traffic across N identical instances of the same service and routing around dead ones via health checks. An API gateway is a reverse proxy that owns the cross-cutting concerns of an API across many different services: authN/authZ, rate limiting and quotas, per-API routing, request/response transformation, aggregation, versioning, per-consumer metrics.

The gateway's reason to exist, said plainly: without it, thirty microservices each implement JWT validation and rate limiting thirty slightly different ways, one of which is wrong. It's your Express middleware stack or Spring filter chain, extracted into infrastructure. The danger is that business logic creeps into it and it becomes a monolith everyone is afraid to deploy — the old ESB problem. My rule: if the gateway needs to know what a "purchase order" is, it's in the wrong place.

Would you use GraphQL here? Sell me on it, then argue against it.

For it: when you have many heterogeneous clients — web, iOS, Android — whose data needs you can't predict, GraphQL kills over- and under-fetching and collapses several sequential round trips into one. On a 150ms mobile link, turning three dependent requests into one is ~300ms of real perceived load time. That's a latency argument and it's the strongest one.

Against it: (1) N+1 queries — per-field resolvers turn { posts { author } } into 1 + 100 queries; you need DataLoader-style per-request batching and it's a footgun forever. It's the same N+1 as a lazily-loaded JPA relation. (2) You lose HTTP caching — everything is a POST to one URL, so CDNs, proxies and browsers can't cache it; you're pushed to persisted queries and normalized client caches. That's giving up the single greatest free win in web architecture. (3) You've handed clients an unbounded query budget — a deeply nested query is a self-inflicted DoS, so you need depth limiting and cost-based rate limiting, which is a much harder rate limiter than "100 req/min". (4) Observability collapses — every request is POST /graphql 200, so per-endpoint dashboards and route-level limits are gone.

My call: if the client set is small and the screens are stable, I'd use a BFF REST endpoint that aggregates inside the datacenter instead — same round-trip savings, keep HTTP caching, skip the GraphQL server entirely. And I'd never use GraphQL service-to-service; that's gRPC's job.

Your service is in us-east-1. A requirement says p99 under 200ms for users in Singapore. What do you say?

I do the arithmetic before I draw anything. Singapore to us-east-1 is roughly a 200ms+ round trip — over the stated budget on physics alone, with zero server time. Even a single request can't make it. So the requirement is telling me, in the requirements phase, that this is a multi-region design; there is no caching or query optimization that fixes it.

Then I'd separate reads from writes, because they have different answers. Reads: serve from a Singapore region — a read replica, or edge caching if the data tolerates staleness. That's usually achievable and it's most of the traffic. Writes: if they must be strongly consistent against a single primary in us-east-1, that 200ms round trip is unavoidable and I'd go back to the interviewer and say so — either the write-latency SLO is relaxed, or we accept a multi-primary design and its conflict resolution, or writes are made async from the user's perspective. Naming which requirement has to give is the answer, not silently pretending I can meet all of them.

Why is TLS termination at the load balancer important?

Four reasons, and the fourth is the one candidates miss. Latency: the expensive handshake happens against a PoP 10ms away instead of an origin 150ms away — turning ~450ms of setup into ~20ms — and the edge-to-origin leg rides a warm pooled connection. CPU: asymmetric crypto is expensive and offloading it keeps it off your app servers. Certificate management: one place to rotate certs, not N services with N keystores. Visibility: an LB cannot route on a path, read a header, or inject a request ID if it can't decrypt — TLS termination is the precondition for L7 routing at all.

And the caveat that matters in fintech and healthcare: terminating and then sending plaintext to backends assumes a trusted internal network. Under PCI or HIPAA it isn't, and the expected answer is re-encryption to the backend, or full mTLS between services via a service mesh.

Why is DNS not a sufficient failover mechanism on its own?

Because TTL is a hint, not a contract. Resolvers, corporate middleboxes and some runtimes (the JVM historically cached DNS forever by default) ignore it, so "lower the TTL and repoint the record" leaves a long tail of clients hammering a dead IP for minutes or hours after you've moved. Lowering TTLs also costs you: more cold lookups, worse tail latency, higher query volume, and a bigger DDoS surface.

So failover has to be layered. DNS (ideally latency-based) steers users to a region — it's coarse-grained, minutes-scale, and that's fine for what it's for. Inside a region, health checks at the load balancer eject a dead instance in seconds without any client re-resolving. Across regions, anycast lets the internet's own routing move traffic to a healthy PoP in seconds, again with no DNS change. DNS is a steering wheel, not an airbag.

What's the hardest part of running WebSockets at scale?

Not the protocol — the state. A WebSocket pins a specific user to a specific process for hours, which undoes the thing that makes an HTTP tier easy: statelessness.

  • Routing becomes a lookup. User A on server 7 messages user B, who is connected to server 23. Server 7 has to discover that (a userId → serverId registry, typically Redis) and hand the message over via pub/sub or direct RPC. That registry has to stay correct through crashes, which is the genuinely hard part.
  • Deploys sever every connection. Restarting the fleet drops a million sockets, all of which reconnect at once — a self-inflicted thundering herd. You need connection draining and client reconnect with exponential backoff plus jitter.
  • Autoscaling fights you. Scaling in kills live users; scaling out doesn't help existing connections, since they're already pinned.
  • Raw capacity. Each socket costs a file descriptor and kernel buffers — tens of KB. A million concurrent connections is a real memory and FD engineering problem.

Which is why chat systems run a thin, dedicated, horizontally-scaled connection tier that owns nothing but sockets, in front of stateless business services. Tutorial 11 builds exactly that.

What to remember

  • The request path, in order: DNS → TCP → TLS → CDN edge → LB → API gateway → service → cache → DB. The expensive hops are at the top and none of them are your code. Every box after the CDN exists to be crossed as rarely as possible.
  • DNS is a routing layer, not a phone book. It's the last indirection you control before the packet leaves the user's ISP. Latency-based routing beats GeoDNS (geography is a bad proxy for latency). Low TTL = fast failover but more lookups; TTL is a hint, so never make DNS your only failover — anycast plus LB health checks are what make failover fast.
  • TCP costs a round trip before your first byte (plus 1–2 more for TLS). Pick UDP when stale data is worthless: live voice/video, gaming, DNS. Buffered video streaming is TCP — the distinction is interactive vs buffered, not "video".
  • TLS is a latency problem before it's a security problem. Terminate at the edge: 450ms of setup becomes 20ms. Termination is also the precondition for L7 routing. Session tickets (stateless) beat session IDs (sticky). 0-RTT is replayable — idempotent requests only.
  • HTTP's whole history is head-of-line blocking, fixed at three layers. H1.1 blocks between requests (→ sharding, concat). H2 multiplexes streams and kills that (→ sharding becomes an anti-pattern), but TCP's ordering still stalls all streams on one lost packet. H3/QUIC rebuilds the transport on UDP for independent streams, 0–1 RTT handshakes, and connection migration across network changes.
  • A CDN is a latency-optimized on-ramp before it's a cache. Even at a 0% hit ratio it terminates TLS near the user and forwards over a warm connection. Pull by default; push for predictable spikes. Every dimension you add to the cache key divides your hit ratio.
  • Don't solve cache invalidation — sidestep it. Content-hashed immutable URLs with a short-TTL HTML pointer. Purges are distributed, slow, and can partially fail. Where you must purge, purge by surrogate key, not URL.
  • L4 sees IP+port and pins whole connections; L7 decrypts, sees the request, and distributes per request. L7 is the HTTP default. Use least connections, not round robin. Use consistent hashing when the backend holds per-key state. Keep health checks shallow so a shared DB blip doesn't eject the entire fleet.
  • Reverse proxy = front door. Load balancer = distribute + health-check. API gateway = cross-cutting concerns (auth, rate limiting, routing, aggregation) so N services don't reimplement them N ways. It's middleware, extracted into infrastructure — and it must not grow business logic.
  • SSE for server→client, WebSockets for genuinely bidirectional, long polling as a fallback. Most "real-time" needs are one-way and SSE gives you reconnect and proxy-friendliness for free. WebSockets make your edge tier stateful — that's the real cost, and naming it is the senior move.
  • REST at the edge, gRPC internally, GraphQL only for many heterogeneous clients — and if you say GraphQL, you must immediately own N+1, lost HTTP caching, unbounded query cost, and collapsed observability. Never GraphQL service-to-service.
  • The numbers: memory ~100ns · SSD read ~0.1ms · Redis ~1ms · same-DC RTT ~0.5ms · same-region ~1–5ms · US coast-to-coast ~70ms · intercontinental ~150ms. Memory is ~1000× faster than disk (that's why you cache). Five sequential round trips from Mumbai is 750ms of pure physics — a chatty protocol is fatal internationally, and no database optimization saves it.
  • The habit: every time you place a box, say what it costs. "I'll terminate TLS at the edge — that buys me 400ms of handshake but it means the edge holds my private key, so in a regulated environment I'd re-encrypt to the backend." Design, then price it. That's the whole grading rubric.
