17 — Design a Web Crawler & Search Index

Archetype: crawl → index → query. Large-scale batch ingestion feeding an inverted index. And underneath all of it: a breadth-first search you already know how to write, run on a graph that doesn't fit on one machine.

Why this matters in the interview

This question looks enormous. "Design Google" is the intimidation play, and most candidates react to it by trying to remember things — PageRank, MapReduce, some half-recalled blog post about Bigtable — and end up reciting nouns. Here is the reframe that makes the whole page easy, and it is the single most valuable thing you will take away: a web crawler is BFS on a graph. The graph is the web. The nodes are URLs. The edges are <a href> links. You pop a URL off a queue, you visit it, you discover its neighbours, you push the unvisited ones back onto the queue, and you keep a visited-set so you don't loop forever. You wrote that exact function in Phase 3 tutorial 10. It was twelve lines.

Everything that makes this a hard system design problem is a consequence of scaling those twelve lines beyond one machine. The frontier (the BFS queue) doesn't fit in memory — so it becomes a distributed queue. The visited-set doesn't fit in memory — so it becomes a bloom filter, and you accept a false-positive rate. Visiting a node isn't a pointer dereference, it's 200ms of network I/O — so you need thousands of concurrent workers. The graph is adversarial and effectively infinite — so you need trap detection and depth limits. And you are not allowed to visit a node's neighbours as fast as you like, because hammering one web server with 1,000 concurrent requests is a denial-of-service attack — so politeness, of all things, becomes the constraint that dictates your partitioning scheme. Every hard part of this problem falls out of one familiar algorithm meeting physics. Say that out loud in minute two and you have converted an intimidating question into a familiar one.

And the archetype is far more common than "design Google" suggests. Crawl → index → query is: a code-search tool over your monorepo, a "search documents" feature in an enterprise app, product-catalogue search on an e-commerce site, log indexing in Elasticsearch, a legal-discovery system, the search box on your own company's website. All of them ingest a corpus in bulk, build an inverted index, and serve ranked keyword queries. The scale differs by nine orders of magnitude; the shape does not. Learn the shape here and you can answer "design search over our 10M internal documents" — which, at Optum or JPMC, is the version you will actually get.

1. The prompt

The interviewer says:

"Design a web crawler and a search engine. Users type keywords, we return relevant pages."

That single sentence contains two entirely different systems that share almost nothing but a storage bucket:

  • The crawler — an offline, batch, write-heavy, I/O-bound pipeline. Its problems are politeness, deduplication, hostile input, and freshness. Nobody is waiting on it. It can be down for an hour and no user notices.
  • The search service — an online, read-heavy, latency-critical query system. Its problems are the inverted index, sharding, fan-out, ranking, and p99 latency. If it's down for an hour, the product is down.

Their only coupling is the artefact in the middle: the crawler produces documents, an indexer turns those documents into an index, and the search service reads it. That's it. They have different failure modes, different scaling axes, different consistency needs, and in a real company they'd be owned by different teams.

The first thing out of your mouth: "This is really two systems — an offline crawling and indexing pipeline, and an online query-serving system. They're coupled only through the index artefact. In 45 minutes I can't do both justice, so let me sketch both in five minutes and then go deep on whichever one you care about. If you don't have a preference, I'll go deep on the crawler, because the politeness and dedup constraints there are the more interesting engineering." Two things just happened. You demonstrated that you can decompose an ambiguous prompt, and you handed the interviewer the steering wheel — which is exactly what they want, because they usually have a specific deep-dive in mind and are waiting to see if you'll ask.

Do not skip that. Candidates who try to cover both systems at equal depth cover neither, run out of time in the inverted index, and leave the interviewer with no signal on anything. Breadth first, then depth on one thing, chosen collaboratively. That is tutorial 01's method applied to a prompt that is deliberately twice as large as the time allows — and the oversized prompt is itself the test.

2. Requirements

The clarifying questions to ask first

AskAssume, if they shrugWhy it changes the design
"Crawler, search service, or both?" Both at a sketch, then deep-dive the crawler. The most important question on the page. These are two systems. Committing to one is what buys you the depth that gets you hired.
"Scale? The open web, or a bounded corpus?" 1 billion pages. The open web. 1B pages is what forces bloom filters, a distributed frontier and a sharded index. 10M internal documents forces none of it — the honest answer there is "install Elasticsearch."
"Text only, or images/video/PDFs too?" HTML text only. Cut it. Multi-modal indexing is a different problem and a time sink.
"Do I need to render JavaScript?" No — parse static HTML. Flag it as a real gap. A headless browser costs ~50–100× more CPU per page than an HTML parse. This decision alone changes your fleet size by two orders of magnitude. See follow-ups.
"How fresh must the index be?" Days for the long tail; hours for news. Freshness dictates the re-crawl budget, and re-crawling competes with new discovery for the same finite polite bandwidth.
"How good must ranking be?" Reasonable keyword relevance. Not Google. Scope this hard and say so. Ranking is a decade of ML research; you have four minutes for it.

Functional requirements

  • Crawl — start from a seed set of URLs, fetch pages over HTTP, extract text and outbound links, follow the links. Repeat, forever.
  • Index — turn the fetched text into a structure that can answer "which documents contain the word kafka?" in milliseconds, over a billion documents.
  • Query — accept a keyword query, return a ranked list of matching pages with titles, URLs and snippets.
  • Re-crawl — pages change and pages die. The index must not decay into a museum.

Explicitly out of scope, and say so: personalisation, ads, images/video, spelling correction, query understanding and synonyms, the ML ranking model itself, and the crawler's legal-compliance/takedown machinery. Each is real; none is this system.

Non-functional requirements — and one of them is a hard constraint

The line that separates you from every other candidate on this question: "The interesting thing about a crawler is that its throughput ceiling isn't set by my hardware — it's set by politeness. I could fetch a million pages a second from one site with enough machines, but doing that would be an attack. Most candidates mention robots.txt as a checkbox. You are treating politeness as the load-bearing constraint of the architecture — because it is.

3. Estimation

Round aggressively, say you're rounding, and drive at the number that changes the design.

Crawl rate

Target:            1,000,000,000 pages in 30 days
30 days            ≈ 2.6M seconds  (30 × 86,400 = 2,592,000 — call it 2.5M)

Pages per second   = 1e9 / 2.5e6  ≈ 400 pages/sec sustained

400/sec sounds trivially small. It is not, and here is why — this is the arithmetic that actually matters: 

One fetch = DNS + TCP + TLS + request + response  ≈ 200ms, and it is
            almost entirely waiting. Near-zero CPU.

By Little's Law:  concurrency = throughput × latency
                              = 400/sec × 0.2 sec
                              = 80 fetches in flight at all times — minimum

80 concurrent sockets is nothing; a single machine with async I/O (or 80 threads — this is the textbook case for the I/O-bound thread pool from Phase 1) handles it. So why does Google run tens of thousands of crawler machines? Three reasons, and naming them is the senior move:

  1. Politeness caps per-domain throughput. If you allow yourself 1 request/sec to any single domain, then hitting 400 pages/sec requires you to be actively working on at least 400 distinct domains simultaneously. The number that matters isn't your concurrency — it's your domain concurrency.
  2. Real fetches are worse than 200ms. Slow servers, timeouts (you'll wait 10s to learn a host is dead), retries. Your effective average latency is more like 1–2s, which multiplies the required concurrency by 5–10×.
  3. Parsing isn't free. HTML parse, text extraction, link extraction, dedup hashing — call it 50–100ms of CPU per page. At 400 pages/sec that's ~40 cores just to parse, before you index anything. And if you render JavaScript, it's 1–5 seconds of CPU per page, and your fleet grows by 50×.

Storage

Raw HTML:      1e9 pages × 100 KB   = 100 TB   (compresses ~5× → ~20 TB stored)
Extracted text: 1e9 pages ×  10 KB   = 10 TB    (the useful part; ~10% of the page)
Inverted index: roughly 20–30% of the text corpus after compression
                                     ≈ 2–3 TB
Link graph:    1e9 pages × 50 links × 8 bytes ≈ 400 GB (for PageRank)

100TB of raw HTML is a blob-storage problem, not a database problem — S3/GCS, exactly as in 16. The index is 2–3TB, which is the number that drives the online system: 2–3TB does not fit on one machine's RAM, and you want the index in RAM. At ~64GB of usable index RAM per box, that's on the order of 50–100 index shards, which is precisely why sharding the index is the deep dive on the search side.

The visited-set — the number that forces the interesting decision

URLs seen (not just crawled — seen, including all the links you found):
   easily 10× the page count → ~10 billion URLs. Take 1e9 for the arithmetic.

A HashSet<String> of 1e9 URLs:
   avg URL ~60 bytes + Java object/entry overhead (~40–60 bytes)
   ≈ 100+ bytes/entry × 1e9 = ~100 GB of RAM   ← and that's the optimistic version

A bloom filter for 1e9 items at 1% false positives:
   ≈ 10 bits per item → 1e9 × 10 bits = ~1.2 GB
100GB versus 1.2GB. That ratio — nearly two orders of magnitude — is the entire reason bloom filters exist, and it is why tutorial 06 promised you this page as one of the three places they earn their keep. Hold that number; we spend it in deep dive 1.

Query side

Assume 100M queries/day  → 100e6 / 86,400 ≈ 1,200 QPS average
Peak ≈ 3× average        → ~3,500 QPS
Each query fans out to all ~100 index shards (see deep dive 2)
   → internal QPS = 3,500 × 100 = 350,000 shard-reads/sec

That last line is the one to say out loud. The fan-out multiplies your internal load by the shard count. It's also why the query p99 is governed by your slowest shard, not your average one — a classic tail-latency-amplification problem, and the reason caching popular queries matters so much.

Land the estimation with the punchline: "The number that drives this design isn't QPS or storage — it's that my crawl throughput is bounded by politeness, not by hardware. I need 400 pages/sec, and at one request per second per domain, that means I must be working on 400+ distinct domains at any instant. So my frontier can't be a single FIFO queue — it has to be partitioned by domain, with each partition rate-limited independently. That constraint, not scale, is what makes the architecture what it is." Deriving the architecture from an estimate is the whole point of doing the estimate. Most candidates compute numbers and then design as if they hadn't.

4. API design

Note something unusual before you draw endpoints: the crawler has almost no API. It is not a request/response service. Its "API" is a pipeline contract — messages on queues — plus a thin admin surface. Saying that is a small, cheap credibility win, because it shows you understand that not every box in a system design is a REST service.

The public query API (this is the one that matters)

Two details worth naming out loud. totalHits is approximate — computing an exact count means fully materialising the intersection of two enormous posting lists, which defeats the early-termination optimisations that make search fast. Google shows "About 1,280,000 results" for exactly this reason. And pagination is cursor-based, not offset-based: offset=10000 forces every shard to rank and discard 10,000 results, so deep pagination is quadratic. In practice you cap the reachable depth (Google gives you ~1,000 results and then stops), which is a lovely piece of pragmatism to mention.

Mentioning an operational kill-switch is the kind of detail that reads as "this person has run something in production."

5. Data model

Four stores, and each one is a different technology because each has a different access pattern. Making that reasoning explicit — rather than defaulting everything to Postgres or everything to Cassandra — is what 03 was for.

StoreHoldsAccess patternChoice
Frontier URLs waiting to be crawled, with priority Enqueue heavy; dequeue by domain partition, highest priority first Kafka (partitioned by domain hash) or a sharded priority queue in Redis. Not a relational table — you'd be doing 400 SELECT … ORDER BY … FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED per second forever.
Seen-URL filter "Have I ever seen this URL?" Membership test, ~10k/sec, must be O(1) and tiny Bloom filter, sharded, in memory. Backed by a durable set (RocksDB/Cassandra) if you can't tolerate loss on restart.
Document store Raw HTML + extracted text, 100TB Write once, read in bulk by the indexer. Never point-queried by users. Blob storage (S3/GCS/HDFS). Cheap, sequential, infinitely scalable. This is not a database question.
Metadata / URL table Per-URL crawl state, timestamps, content hash, change rate Point read/write by URL hash. 1B rows, no joins. Wide-column KV (Cassandra/Bigtable/DynamoDB). Classic LSM-tree write-heavy workload — the exact case 03 said to pick Cassandra over Postgres for.
Inverted index term → posting list Read-only at serve time; rebuilt in batch. Latency-critical. Immutable segment files, memory-mapped, document-sharded. This is what Lucene/Elasticsearch is.

The URL metadata row

-- Cassandra-shaped. Partition key = url_hash. No joins, no scans.
CREATE TABLE url_meta (
  url_hash        blob       PRIMARY KEY,   -- md5(normalized_url), 16 bytes
  url             text,
  domain          text,
  last_crawled_at timestamp,
  last_status     int,                     -- 200 / 404 / 500 / timeout
  content_hash    blob,                    -- for content-level dedup
  simhash         bigint,                  -- for NEAR-duplicate detection
  etag            text,                    -- conditional GET → free freshness check
  change_freq_est double,                  -- drives the adaptive re-crawl schedule
  page_rank       double,                  -- drives frontier priority AND query ranking
  depth           int                      -- BFS depth from seed — the trap defence
);

Three fields there are load-bearing and each earns a mention. etag lets you issue a conditional GET with If-None-Match and get back a 304 Not Modified — a re-crawl that costs you almost no bandwidth and tells you the page is unchanged. That is free freshness, straight out of HTTP caching (02), and it is the cheapest optimisation in the entire system. depth is not decoration — it is your defence against infinite crawler traps. And page_rank is used in two places, which surprises people: it ranks results at query time, and it prioritises the frontier at crawl time, because you'd rather crawl an important page first.

URL normalisation — small, boring, and it prevents a catastrophe

Before any dedup check, normalise. Lowercase the scheme and host; strip the fragment (#section is client-side and never changes the document); remove default ports; sort query parameters; strip known tracking junk (utm_*, fbclid, sessionid); resolve . and ..; decide on the trailing slash and be consistent.

Skip normalisation and example.com/p?a=1&b=2, example.com/p?b=2&a=1, EXAMPLE.com/p?a=1&b=2#top and example.com/p?a=1&b=2&utm_source=twitter are four distinct URLs to your crawler and one page to the world. Your seen-set silently stops working. Now add a site that appends a fresh session ID to every internal link, and you have a URL generator: one page produces infinite unique URLs, your frontier grows without bound, and you crawl a single site forever while the rest of the web starves. Normalisation isn't a hygiene detail; it's the first line of defence against the frontier exploding.

6. High-level design

Start from the twelve lines you already know

Before you draw a single box, write this on the board. It costs you thirty seconds and it buys you the whole page:

void crawl(Set<String> seeds) {
  Queue<String> frontier = new ArrayDeque<>(seeds);   // the BFS queue
  Set<String>   seen     = new HashSet<>(seeds);      // the visited-set

  while (!frontier.isEmpty()) {
    String url  = frontier.poll();
    String html = fetch(url);                            // ← 200ms of network I/O
    store(url, extractText(html));

    for (String link : extractLinks(html)) {          // ← the neighbours
      if (seen.add(link)) frontier.add(link);        // ← enqueue if unvisited
    }
  }
}

That is a correct web crawler. It is also, line for line, the BFS from Phase 3 tutorial 10. Every box in the architecture you are about to draw is one of these four lines, broken because of scale:

The lineWhat breaks at 1B pagesWhat it becomes
Queue<String> frontierDoesn't fit in RAM; one machine can't drain itA distributed, prioritised, domain-partitioned queue
Set<String> seen~100GB of RAM for 1B URLsA bloom filter (~1.2GB) — and you accept false positives
fetch(url)200ms of blocking I/O; and you must not hammer one hostThousands of async fetchers + per-domain rate limiting
while (!frontier.isEmpty())It is never empty. The web is infinite and adversarial, and it changes.A perpetual loop with trap detection and a re-crawl scheduler
Say it exactly like this: "At its core this is breadth-first search on a graph — the web is the graph, URLs are nodes, links are edges. If the whole web fit in memory, this is a twelve-line function with a queue and a visited-set. Everything hard about this problem is a consequence of the frontier not fitting in memory, the visited-set not fitting in memory, a node visit costing 200 milliseconds instead of a pointer dereference, and the graph being written by adversaries. So let me take those four things one at a time." That sentence structures your entire answer, proves you see the underlying algorithm rather than a memorised diagram, and — not incidentally — makes the interviewer's job of grading you very easy. Give them a spine to hang the answer on.

One page, end to end (the write path)

  1. Select. The frontier picks the next URL — not FIFO, but by priority (PageRank, freshness need, depth) within a domain queue whose politeness clock says it's allowed to be hit right now.
  2. Route. The URL goes to the fetcher that owns hash(domain). Always the same fetcher for a given domain. This is the whole trick, and deep dive 1 explains why.
  3. Check robots. The fetcher looks up example.com/robots.txt in a local cache (TTL ~24h). If the path is disallowed, drop the URL and record why. If the cache is cold, fetch robots first — and that fetch itself counts against politeness.
  4. Resolve DNS. Almost certainly a cache hit. If not, this is a 50–200ms round trip, and it is the sleeper bottleneck of the whole system (see §8).
  5. Fetch. Conditional GET with If-None-Match if you have an ETag. Cap the response size (a 10GB "HTML" file is an attack, not a page). Cap redirects at ~5. Time out at 10s.
  6. Store raw. Blob storage. The queue message carries only the key, never the bytes — a rule worth stating: queues carry pointers, not payloads.
  7. Parse. Extract text. Extract links, resolve them to absolute URLs, normalise them. Compute a content hash and a simhash.
  8. Dedup content. If the content hash is already known under a different URL, it's a mirror or a session-ID duplicate: record the alias, don't index it twice.
  9. Dedup URLs. For each discovered link, ask the bloom filter. "Definitely not seen" → enqueue. "Probably seen" → drop. That drop is where a false positive costs you a page you'd never crawled — and you have to decide, out loud, whether that's acceptable. (It is. Deep dive 1.)
  10. Schedule the re-crawl. Write next_crawl_at into the URL metadata based on the estimated change rate.

7a. Deep dive: the crawler

This is where the interview really happens — if the interviewer chose the crawler. Five things, in the order they'll be pushed on: the frontier, politeness, URL dedup, content dedup and traps, and freshness.

The frontier — BFS's queue, when the queue is a terabyte

Naively the frontier is a FIFO queue: pop the oldest URL, crawl it. Two things kill that immediately.

First, it doesn't fit in memory. Hundreds of millions of pending URLs at ~100 bytes each is tens of gigabytes, and a single machine draining it is a single point of failure and a throughput ceiling. So the frontier is a distributed queue — Kafka with many partitions, or a sharded Redis/Cassandra-backed priority structure. Kafka is the natural fit: durable (a fetcher crash loses nothing — offsets aren't committed until the page is stored), horizontally partitioned, and it gives you replay for free.

Second, FIFO is the wrong order. Plain BFS treats every URL as equally worth crawling. It is not. You have a finite, politeness-capped crawl budget and an infinite web; the order you spend that budget in is the quality of your index. So the frontier is really a priority queue, scored on:

  • PageRank / inbound links — a page linked from 10,000 sites is worth crawling before a page linked from one. (Chicken-and-egg: you need the link graph to compute PageRank, and the crawl to build the link graph. In practice you use last iteration's PageRank, seeded by domain authority. Say that; the bootstrapping question is a classic follow-up.)
  • Freshness need — a re-crawl of a news homepage outranks the first crawl of a dead forum from 2007.
  • Depth — shallow pages (near the homepage) tend to matter more, and depth is also your trap cap.
Don't say "I'll use a priority queue" and move on. The interviewer's next question is "a priority queue where? You have a billion URLs." The real answer is a two-level structure: a small number of priority bands (say 5 Kafka topics, or 5 queues per domain), and you dequeue from the high band far more often than the low one. You get approximate priority ordering without ever needing a global sorted structure over a billion items — and "approximate priority is fine here" is exactly the kind of judgment call being graded. A globally-ordered distributed priority queue is a research project; a weighted band selector is an afternoon.

Politeness — the constraint that shapes the entire architecture

Here is the scenario. Your fetchers pull from a shared queue, round-robin. A large site — say wikipedia.org — has ten million pages in your frontier. At any instant, several hundred of your fetchers happen to be holding Wikipedia URLs. You now have 500 concurrent connections open to one server.

You have just DDoS'd Wikipedia. Not metaphorically. Their ops team sees a traffic spike from your IP range, your user agent gets blocked, and if you're unlucky the conversation involves lawyers. This is the single most common way a naive crawler design fails, and it is a correctness failure, not a performance one.

The rules you must respect, in order of seriousness:

  1. robots.txt — fetch https://domain/robots.txt before crawling any domain, cache it (TTL ~24h; the cache is essential — you don't re-fetch it per URL), and obey both its Disallow paths and its Crawl-delay directive. It's a voluntary standard with no enforcement, which is exactly why respecting it is a signal about you.
  2. Rate limit per domain — at most 1 request every N seconds to a single host. A common default is ~1/sec, adaptive: back off when you see 429s, 503s, or rising latency (the host is telling you it's struggling). This is the token bucket from tutorial 09, keyed by domain — except here you're the one being polite, not the one enforcing a quota. Same algorithm, the other side of the table.
  3. Identify yourself — a real User-Agent with a URL and a contact address, so the site owner can email you instead of blocking you.
  4. Back off on failure — exponential backoff with jitter (06). Retrying a struggling server aggressively is how you turn its bad day into an outage.

The architectural consequence — and this is what they're actually testing

Enforcing "1 request/sec to example.com" is trivial if exactly one worker is allowed to fetch example.com. It is a distributed-consensus problem if any of 1,000 workers might. So:

The line to say: "Politeness means I can't round-robin URLs across my fetcher pool. If any worker can pick up any URL, then enforcing a per-domain rate limit requires a distributed counter that every worker checks on every fetch — that's a shared-state bottleneck and a race condition, and it's the hard way to solve this. The easy way is to make it a partitioning problem instead: route URLs to workers by hash(domain), so one worker owns all of example.com. Now the rate limit is a local variable in that worker. No coordination, no locks, no shared counter. I've turned a distributed-coordination problem into a data-partitioning problem — and I'd use consistent hashing (04) so that adding or losing a fetcher only remaps 1/N of the domains instead of reshuffling everything." That paragraph is the single highest-value thing you can say on the crawler side of this question. It shows you can eliminate coordination by choosing the right partition key — which is, honestly, most of distributed systems.
WRONG — shared queue, any worker takes any URL:

  [ one big queue ] ──▶ W1  W2  W3 ... W1000
                         ↓   ↓   ↓        ↓
                    all hammering example.com at once

RIGHT — partition by domain:

Two consequences to volunteer before you're asked, because they're the natural next questions:

  • Hot domains. Wikipedia has millions of pages; a random blog has twelve. Domain-partitioning gives you a skewed workload — the classic hot-partition problem from 04. Fix: a worker owns many domains and interleaves them, so while it's waiting out Wikipedia's crawl delay it fetches from its 5,000 other domains. Politeness delay isn't idle time; it's just a reason to work on something else. A worker with enough domains is never blocked.
  • Sites behind one IP, and one site behind many. Politeness is really per-server, not per-hostname. 10,000 domains on one shared host means "1 req/sec per domain" is 10,000 req/sec to that poor machine. The rigorous fix is to rate-limit by resolved IP, not hostname. Mention it. Most candidates never think of it, and it's a genuinely nice catch.

URL dedup at scale — where bloom filters earn their keep

Tutorial 06 taught you bloom filters and named three places they genuinely pay for themselves. This is the best of the three. Here is the payoff.

Every page you parse yields ~50 links. At 400 pages/sec that's 20,000 URLs per second arriving at the question "have I seen this one before?" Get that wrong and BFS revisits nodes — which in a cyclic graph (and the web is nothing but cycles: every page links back to the homepage) means you loop forever and never make progress. The visited-set isn't an optimisation. It's what makes the algorithm terminate.

The obvious implementations, and why each one dies:

ApproachCost at 1B URLsVerdict
HashSet<String> in memory ~100 GB RAM (60-byte URLs + object overhead) Correct, and absurdly expensive. You are buying a 128GB machine to answer a yes/no question.
Store 16-byte hashes instead of URLs ~16–30 GB RAM Better! Genuinely a valid answer, and worth saying. Still a big dedicated cluster, and it doesn't shrink as you grow.
A database lookup per URL 20,000 point-reads/sec, forever Works, costs a Cassandra cluster, and adds a network round trip to the hottest path in the system. Now dedup is your bottleneck.
Bloom filter ~1.2 GB RAM at 1% FP Fits in a corner of one machine's heap. O(k) hashes, no network, no allocation. The catch: it lies, occasionally, in one specific direction.

What the bloom filter actually promises

bloom.mightContain(url):

   returns FALSE  →  definitely never seen.  Guaranteed. No false negatives, ever.
   returns TRUE   →  probably seen ... but maybe not (that's the ~1% false positive)

So the crawler's logic is:

   if (!seen.mightContain(url)) {   // definitely new
       seen.add(url);
       frontier.enqueue(url);
   }                                // else: probably seen → DROP IT

Follow the consequence through, because this is the judgment the question exists to test. A false positive means the filter says "probably seen" about a URL you have never actually crawled. You drop it. That page never enters your index. You have silently lost a page.

And the correct engineering response to that is: so what?

Say this, and say it with conviction: "A bloom filter gives me 'definitely new' or 'probably seen' for a billion URLs in about a gigabyte, versus a hundred gigabytes for an exact hash set. The cost is that roughly 1% of the time it tells me I've seen a URL I haven't, and I skip a page I've never crawled. For a web crawler, that is a completely acceptable loss. I'm crawling a billion pages out of a web that has trillions — I am already not crawling almost everything. Missing one page in a hundred, at random, changes nothing about the quality of my index, and the page will almost certainly get rediscovered through a different link anyway. I'm trading a 1% recall loss for a 100× reduction in memory. That trade is obviously correct here — and I want to be precise that it's correct because of what this system is, not because bloom filters are always fine."

The contrast that proves you understand the tool — not just its name

Take the exact same data structure to tutorial 14, ticket booking. Use a bloom filter to answer "is seat 14C already sold?" A false positive there means the filter says "sold" about a seat that is free — you'd wrongly refuse a sale. Annoying, not fatal. But flip the polarity of the check, as an unwary engineer easily might, and a false positive says "not sold" about a seat that is sold — and you have just sold seat 14C twice. Two people, two valid tickets, one chair. That is an incident, a refund, and at a fintech loop, a compliance conversation.

This is what "engineering judgment" actually means, and it's what a 7-YOE candidate is being graded on that a 3-YOE candidate isn't. The junior answer is "bloom filters are memory-efficient." The senior answer is "bloom filters trade a bounded, one-directional error for an enormous space saving, so the only question that matters is what does that specific error cost in this specific system?" Two things about bloom filters that interviewers love to probe. (1) You cannot delete from a standard bloom filter — clearing bits would break other entries that share them. If you need deletion, you need a counting bloom filter (4-bit counters instead of bits, so 4× the space). For a crawler you almost never delete, so this doesn't bite — but know it. (2) It's not durable. A restart with an empty filter means you re-crawl everything you've ever seen. Fix: periodically snapshot the bit array to blob storage (it's only a gigabyte), and/or back the filter with a durable KV store that you only consult on a "probably seen" hit — which converts your 1% false-positive rate into a 1% rate of cheap extra database lookups and zero lost pages. That last variant is a very strong answer to "how do you avoid losing pages at all?": the bloom filter absorbs 99% of the traffic and the database provides exactness for the remaining 1%. Bloom filter as a cache in front of the truth, rather than as the truth itself.

One more scaling note: at 10B+ URLs even 1.2GB grows, and a single filter is a single point of failure. Shard the filter by hash(url) across the fetcher fleet — and if you've already partitioned by domain, the natural move is for each worker to hold the bloom filter for its own domains. The dedup check then never leaves the process. No network call in the hottest loop in the system. That's the design falling pleasantly into place.

Content dedup — the same page at a thousand URLs

URL dedup catches /a arriving twice. It does nothing for the same content served at different URLs, and the web is drowning in that: mirrors and CDN copies, ?sessionid= and ?utm_source= variants, printer-friendly versions, HTTP and HTTPS versions, www and bare-domain versions, and syndicated articles republished on forty sites. A large fraction of the crawlable web is duplicate or near-duplicate content. Index it all and your results page shows the same article ten times, which is a visibly broken product.

  • Exact duplicates: hash the extracted text (SHA-256 of the normalised content). Same hash → same page. Keep one canonical URL, record the others as aliases. Cheap, effective, do this always.
  • Near duplicates (the same article with a different ad rail, timestamp, or comment count — so the exact hash differs on every fetch): you need a hash where similar documents produce similar hashes. That's SimHash — hash each word, weight it, sum the weighted vectors, take the sign of each dimension to get a 64-bit fingerprint. Two documents that differ by 3% differ in only a few bits, so "near-duplicate" becomes "Hamming distance ≤ 3" — and Hamming distance is a popcount of an XOR, which is the bit manipulation from Phase 3 tutorial 14. (Shingling + MinHash is the other classic technique; same goal, different mechanism.)
SimHash is awareness-level and you should say so. Nobody expects you to derive it at a whiteboard. The gradeable insight is one sentence: "an exact content hash catches perfect duplicates but breaks on a single changed byte, so for near-duplicates I'd use a locality-sensitive hash like SimHash where similar documents land close together in Hamming distance — I know the shape of it, I'd read up before implementing." Naming the technique and honestly bounding your depth beats bluffing every single time, and interviewers can always tell the difference.

Crawler traps — the web is adversarial

This is the lesson that makes crawling weird, and it's worth stating explicitly because nothing else in this course is like it: your input is written by people, some of whom want to hurt you. Your React app's inputs come from your own frontend. A crawler's input comes from the open internet, including people who profit from wasting your budget.

That is the same instinct as a circuit breaker or a bulkhead from 06 — you cannot prevent every failure, so you contain what one failure can cost you. Interviewers love seeing a foundations pattern reappear in a new costume, because it proves you learned a principle rather than a recipe.

Freshness & re-crawl — the loop that never ends

while (!frontier.isEmpty()) was a lie. The frontier is never empty, because a crawled page is not done — it's just currently accurate. Pages change, pages 404, new pages appear. Your index decays from the moment you write it.

The naive answer is "re-crawl everything every 30 days." That's wrong in both directions at once, and saying why is the point:

  • A news site's homepage changes every few minutes. Crawling it monthly means your index is a month stale on the content people search for most.
  • A blog post from 2009 has not changed in seventeen years and never will. Re-crawling it monthly burns politeness budget — your scarcest resource — to fetch a byte-identical page.

So the re-crawl interval must be adaptive, per URL. Concretely:

Track, per URL:  did the content_hash change between the last N crawls?

   changed every time      → crawl more often   (interval ÷ 2, floor ~5 min)
   never changed in 10x    → crawl less often   (interval × 2, cap ~90 days)

Then:  next_crawl_at = last_crawled_at + interval

The re-crawl scheduler = "give me all URLs where next_crawl_at < now()",
which is exactly the distributed scheduler from tutorial 12 — a time-bucketed
scan that emits due work onto the frontier. Reuse it; don't reinvent it.

Two cheap wins to volunteer. Conditional GETs: send If-None-Match: <etag> or If-Modified-Since; a 304 Not Modified costs a few hundred bytes instead of 100KB and still refreshes your "this page is alive" signal. You just did a re-crawl for ~1% of the bandwidth. And sitemaps: many sites publish /sitemap.xml with <lastmod> timestamps — the site is telling you what changed. Free freshness signal, and you should take it.

Notice that re-crawls and new discovery compete for the same politeness-capped budget. That's a genuine, forced product trade-off, and naming it is worth real points: "Every re-crawl is a page I didn't discover. So freshness and coverage are in direct tension, and I'd split the budget explicitly — say 70% discovery, 30% refresh — and tune the ratio from search-quality metrics rather than pretending the trade-off doesn't exist." A candidate who sees that two features of the same system are fighting over one resource is thinking like an owner, not an implementer.

7b. Deep dive: the search index

This is where the interview really happens — if the interviewer chose the search side. And this is the half you're more likely to get in an enterprise loop, because "search over our documents" is a real thing your team will build, whereas "crawl the open web" is not.

The inverted index — the single most important data structure in search

Derive it from first principles rather than naming it. Start with the naive approach so the interviewer sees you understand why the answer is the answer.

You have a billion documents. A user searches for kafka. The obvious approach: scan every document, check if it contains "kafka". That is a full-text scan of 10TB per query — hundreds of seconds if you're lucky, and it does not get better with caching, because the next query is a different word. Dead on arrival.

The problem is that your data is stored document → words, and your query asks word → documents. You are querying against the grain. So invert it. That's the whole idea, and the name is admirably literal: 

FORWARD index (how documents naturally are):

   doc1 → "the kafka broker stores the log"
   doc2 → "a distributed log is a kafka concept"
   doc3 → "the broker handles partitions"

INVERTED index (what search needs):

   term        posting list (sorted doc IDs)
   ─────────────────────────────────────────
   broker    → [1, 3]
   concept   → [2]
   distributed → [2]
   kafka     → [1, 2]
   log       → [1, 2]
   partitions→ [3]
   stores    → [1]

Now the query kafka log is not a scan. It is: 

postings("kafka") = [1, 2]
postings("log")   = [1, 2]

AND query → intersect the two sorted lists → [1, 2]

Query "broker log":
   [1, 3] ∩ [1, 2] = [1]        ← doc1 only

Two sorted lists, walk both with two indices, advance whichever is smaller, emit on a match. That is literally the two-pointer merge from Phase 3 tutorial 02, and it's O(n + m), not O(n × m): 

List<Integer> intersect(List<Integer> a, List<Integer> b) {
  List<Integer> out = new ArrayList<>();
  int i = 0, j = 0;
  while (i < a.size() && j < b.size()) {
    if      (a.get(i).equals(b.get(j))) { out.add(a.get(i)); i++; j++; }
    else if (a.get(i) < b.get(j))        { i++; }   // advance the smaller
    else                                { j++; }
  }
  return out;
}

Always intersect the shortest posting list first — "the" appears in 900M documents and "kafka" in 2M, so start from kafka's list and you do 2M comparisons instead of 900M. (Better still: skip pointers inside posting lists let you binary-search forward instead of stepping one by one, which is Phase 3 tutorial 05 showing up uninvited. Awareness level; mentioning it is a nice flourish, not a requirement.)

The sentence that shows you understand search rather than having memorised it: "An inverted index is just the recognition that your query direction and your storage direction are opposite. Documents are stored doc→words; queries ask word→docs. So you precompute the transpose. Everything after that — intersecting posting lists, ranking, sharding — is engineering on top of that one inversion." Deriving the structure in thirty seconds beats naming it in five, because naming it proves you read a blog post and deriving it proves you could have invented it.

What actually lives in a posting list

Not just doc IDs. To rank and to support phrase queries you need more:

"kafka" → [ (docId=1, tf=3, positions=[2, 17, 40]),
            (docId=2, tf=1, positions=[7]),
            ... ]

  tf        = term frequency — how many times the word appears in that doc  → ranking
  positions = where in the doc                                              → PHRASE queries

Positions are what let "distributed systems" in quotes mean adjacent, not merely both present: intersect the doc lists, then check whether some position of "systems" equals a position of "distributed" + 1. They roughly double index size, which is exactly the kind of cost/feature trade you should name out loud rather than swallow.

Building it: this is a MapReduce, and you should say so

MAP    (runs over every crawled document, embarrassingly parallel):
   for each doc:
     for each term in tokenize(doc.text):
        emit( term, (docId, position) )

SHUFFLE  — the framework groups all emitted pairs by key (the term)

REDUCE (one reducer per term-range):
   for each term:
     sort the (docId, position) pairs by docId
     compress → write the posting list to an index segment

That is the canonical MapReduce example for a reason: the map is trivially parallel across a billion documents, the shuffle does the transpose, and the reduce writes sorted, compressed segments. In 2026 you'd write it in Spark or Flink, but the shape is unchanged. Segments are immutable — you never edit a posting list in place. New documents go into new small segments, which are periodically merged into bigger ones.

Immutable segments + background merge is an LSM-tree in all but name — the same structure 03 taught you for Cassandra's write path, and the same reason: sequential writes are fast, in-place updates of a compressed sorted structure are miserable. Lucene (and therefore Elasticsearch, and therefore most search you'll ever touch) is built exactly this way. Spotting that two "different" systems are the same structure underneath is precisely the pattern-recognition being graded.

Compression — awareness level, but a nice detail

Raw posting list:      [ 5, 12, 19, 40, 41, 60, ... ]   ← 4 bytes each

Delta encoding (store gaps, not absolutes — the list is sorted, so gaps are small):
                       [ 5,  7,  7, 21,  1, 19, ... ]

Varint / VByte (small numbers take fewer bytes — 1 byte for values < 128):
                       most gaps now cost 1 byte instead of 4

Result: posting lists compress ~4×. On a 3 TB index that's 2+ TB of RAM
you didn't have to buy — and since the whole game is keeping the index in
memory, compression is not a micro-optimisation. It's capacity planning.

Mention it in one sentence and move on. Nobody will ask you to implement varint, but knowing that posting lists are delta-encoded and that this is why they must be sorted by doc ID is a real detail that separates people who've looked inside Lucene from people who haven't.

Sharding the index — a genuinely good interview question

3TB of index does not fit on one machine, and you want it in RAM. So you shard. There are exactly two ways, and choosing between them, with reasons, is one of the highest-signal moments available on this page. 

DOCUMENT-PARTITIONED  (shard by docId — each shard holds a subset of DOCS,
                        and the COMPLETE index for just those docs)

   Shard 1: docs 1–10M   → its own full inverted index over those 10M docs

"On paper, term-partitioning looks better — a two-word query only touches one or two shards instead of fanning out to a hundred. But it loses badly in practice for three reasons. Hot keys: term frequency follows a Zipf distribution, so the shard holding common words is hit by nearly every query while the shard holding rare words idles — it's the celebrity-partition problem from 04, in its purest form, and you can't fix it by adding shards. Writes: adding one document means touching every shard that owns any word in it, so indexing becomes a scatter-write across the entire cluster. And multi-term queries need cross-shard joins — you either ship a posting list with millions of entries across the network, or you make two round trips. Document-partitioning has real costs too: every query hits every shard, so my p99 is set by my slowest shard, not my average one. But that cost is bounded and fixable — replicate shards, hedge requests, cache. So I'd choose document-partitioning, and I'd budget for tail latency." That answer names the alternative, gives three concrete reasons, admits what its own choice costs, and says how it'd mitigate that. That is the complete trade-off shape, and it is the shape every answer in this phase should have.

Two consequences worth saying: replicate every shard (3×) — not just for durability but because replicas cut tail latency (send to two replicas, take the first response — "hedged requests"). And a shard is a unit of scaling: doubling your corpus means doubling shards, and because the index is immutable and rebuilt in batch, resharding is easy here in a way it never is for a live OLTP database. A rare case where the batch nature of the pipeline actually buys you something.

Ranking — be honest about scope

Intersection tells you which documents match. It says nothing about which are good. A query for "kafka" matches ten million pages; the entire product is in choosing the ten to show.

TF-IDF, in one idea

score(term, doc) = TF × IDF

  TF  (term frequency)          = how often the term appears IN THIS DOC
                                  → "kafka" 40 times? This doc is probably about kafka.

  IDF (inverse document frequency) = log( totalDocs / docsContainingTerm )
                                  → "the" is in 900M docs → IDF ≈ 0 → worthless signal
                                  → "kafka" is in 2M docs → IDF is high → strong signal

The one-sentence intuition, and this is the whole concept: a term that is rare across the corpus but frequent in this document is a strong signal that the document is about that term. TF alone would rank a page that says "the the the" at the top. IDF is what kills the stopwords — automatically, without a hand-maintained stopword list. That's the elegance of it.

BM25 is TF-IDF with two fixes, and it's what Lucene/Elasticsearch actually use, so it's worth naming: term-frequency saturation (the 50th occurrence of "kafka" adds almost nothing over the 10th — TF-IDF grows linearly, which is wrong) and document-length normalisation (a 10,000-word page shouldn't outrank a focused 500-word one just for having more room to contain the word). Both are corrections for how real documents behave. Awareness level: know what it fixes and why, not the formula.

PageRank, honestly, in one paragraph

TF-IDF is entirely about the page's own text — which means anyone can game it by stuffing keywords into their page. PageRank's insight was to use a signal the page's author doesn't control: treat a link as a vote. A page is important if important pages link to it — a recursive definition, resolved by iterating to a fixed point (equivalently: the stationary distribution of a random surfer clicking links forever). The link graph you built during the crawl is the input, it's a big sparse matrix-vector multiply run to convergence, and it is query-independent — computed once in batch, stored per URL, and mixed into the score at query time. Final score ≈ BM25(query, doc) × f(PageRank(doc)).

Then close the topic deliberately, because knowing where to stop is itself a skill: "In reality, modern ranking is a learned model — hundreds of features (BM25, PageRank, click-through rate, dwell time, freshness, page speed, spam scores) fed into a learning-to-rank model, retrained continuously, with a cheap first-pass retrieval to get a thousand candidates and an expensive re-ranker on just those. I can't design Google's ranker in a system design interview and I don't think anyone can. What I can do is get the retrieval right, get BM25 plus a link-based prior as a baseline, and build the click-logging pipeline that a learned model would need as training data — because the feedback loop is the actual moat, not the formula." That last clause is a genuinely senior observation, and being explicit that you're bounding the scope rather than bluffing through it reads as confidence, not as a gap.

Query serving

  1. Parse & normalise. Tokenize, lowercase, stem ("running" → "run", so it matches "runs"), drop stopwords, expand synonyms. The query must be processed with exactly the same pipeline as the documents were — if you stemmed at index time you must stem at query time, or nothing matches. This is a real bug people ship, and naming it is a nice detail.
  2. Cache. Query popularity is Zipf-distributed to an extreme degree — a small number of queries are a huge fraction of traffic. A simple Redis cache of query → results, TTL of minutes to hours, absorbs a large share of load. Cache the rendered result page, not just the doc IDs. See 06 — and mind the stampede on a newly-trending query, where a million people search the same thing at the same instant and every one of them misses the cache.
  3. Scatter. Fan out to all N shards. Each shard fetches posting lists, intersects, scores, and returns only its own top-10 — never its full match list. Sending 10 results instead of 2 million is the difference between a working system and a melted network.
  4. Gather. Merge N×10 candidates into a global top-10 with a heap. Set a deadline: if a shard hasn't answered in 100ms, return without it and serve slightly degraded results. Partial results beat a timeout — and being willing to say that out loud is a real judgment call, because it's an availability-over-completeness choice you should defend, not sneak past.
  5. Snippets. Fetch the matched documents' text and cut a passage around the query terms. This is a separate service reading the doc store, and it's surprisingly expensive — you're doing 10 random reads into a 10TB corpus per query. Cache it hard.
Autocomplete is the other half of the query box, and it's a whole tutorial: 21 covers the frontend side — debounce vs throttle, out-of-order response races, ARIA comboboxes. The backend is a trie over popular query prefixes, with the top-k completions precomputed and stored at each node so a lookup is a walk down the prefix and a read, not a subtree search. That's Phase 3 tutorial 14, and it is one of the very few places a raw trie is genuinely the production answer rather than an interview curiosity. Note also that autocomplete is ranked by query popularity (what people type), not document relevance — a different corpus entirely, built from your query logs.

What breaks first, and what you do about it.

It's a real, specific, non-obvious answer, it shows you've thought about the physics of the thing rather than the boxes, and it's the kind of detail interviewers remember when they write up your feedback.

10. Follow-ups you should expect

"How do you crawl JavaScript-rendered pages?"

You will get this one, because you're a frontend engineer, and you should have a strong opinion. Half the modern web renders its content client-side; curl on a React SPA returns <div id="root"></div> and a script tag. Your parser extracts zero text and zero links, and to the crawler that page — and everything reachable only through it — does not exist. This isn't an edge case; it's a large fraction of the web, and it's a graph reachability problem, not just a content problem: you lose whole subgraphs.

The fix is to render: run a headless browser (Puppeteer/Playwright over headless Chrome), execute the JS, wait for the network to go idle, then read the resulting DOM. The catch is the cost. A static HTML parse is ~10ms of CPU and a few MB. A headless render is 1–5 seconds of CPU and 100–300MB of RAM per page. Call it 50–100× more expensive. You cannot render a billion pages; the fleet arithmetic simply doesn't close.

So you render selectively, and here's the pipeline that gets you the points:

  • Pass 1 — cheap. Fetch and parse the raw HTML for everything.
  • Detect. Heuristics: is the text content near-empty? Is there a single root div and a big JS bundle? Does the DOM look like a known SPA framework's shell?
  • Pass 2 — expensive, on a separate fleet. Queue the suspects to a much smaller render tier, budgeted and rate-limited independently. Prioritise by importance — render high-PageRank SPAs, skip the long tail.
  • Look for the cheap way out first. Many SPAs ship server-side rendering, prerendered __NEXT_DATA__ / hydration JSON, an og: meta description, or a sitemap. Sometimes the content is sitting right there in a JSON blob in the HTML and you never need a browser at all. Check for that before you spin up Chrome.
And the observation that only a frontend engineer makes: "This is also why SSR and SSG exist as SEO features, not just performance ones. When I chose Next.js with server-side rendering on a project, part of the justification was exactly this — I was making the crawler's job cheap, because a crawler that has to spin up Chrome to see my content might just decide I'm not worth the CPU. The economics of the crawler are the reason SEO advice tells you to server-render." That connects your actual career experience to the system you're designing, from the other side of the wire. It is a genuinely distinctive thing to be able to say in this interview, and almost no backend candidate can say it. Use it.

"How fresh is fresh enough?"

There is no universal answer, and the correct response is to refuse the premise and segment: news and social — minutes to hours (and you'd special-case them: dedicated high-frequency crawlers, plus push-based ingestion like RSS/sitemap pings/PubSubHubbub rather than polling). E-commerce prices and stock — hours. Documentation and reference — days. The archival long tail — months, and you'd mostly rely on a cheap conditional GET to confirm nothing changed. Then the honest close: "freshness costs politeness budget, which is the same budget as discovery, so 'fresher' always means 'less coverage'. I'd set the split from search-quality metrics — if users are clicking stale results, buy freshness; if they're getting no results, buy coverage."

"How do you detect spam and SEO manipulation?"

Awareness level, and say so — this is an adversarial ML arms race, not a system design problem, and anyone who claims to solve it in an interview is bluffing. But have the vocabulary: keyword stuffing and invisible text (text the same colour as the background, display:none divs) are detectable with content heuristics. Link farms — thousands of sites linking to each other to inflate PageRank — are detectable as suspiciously dense subgraphs, and PageRank variants like TrustRank propagate trust from a hand-vetted seed set to resist them. Cloaking — serving different content to Googlebot than to users — is caught by crawling occasionally from an unlabelled IP with a browser user-agent and diffing. The structural point, which is the actually gradeable one: any ranking signal the page's author controls will eventually be gamed, which is precisely why link-based and behaviour-based signals (which they don't control) are worth so much more than on-page ones.

"Scope this down: search over my company's 10 million documents."

This is the version you will actually be asked at an enterprise loop, and the answer that scores highest is the one that refuses to build any of this.

"At 10 million documents, I'd use Elasticsearch or OpenSearch and I would not build a single thing on this whiteboard. Ten million docs is maybe 50GB of index — that fits on a handful of nodes with room to spare. There's no crawler, because there's no link graph: I'd ingest via connectors and CDC from the source systems (05's outbox pattern, straight into a queue). There's no politeness problem, because I own the servers. There's no bloom filter, because an exact hash set of 10M IDs is 300MB. Nearly every hard problem on this page is a consequence of the open web, and none of them exist inside a company. What actually matters at that scale is relevance tuning, permissions filtering — the genuinely hard part, because a user must never see a document they lack access to, and that means access control has to live inside the query, not as a post-filter, or your pagination silently breaks — and the ingestion pipeline's reliability. I'd spend my engineering budget there, not on rebuilding Lucene." Knowing when not to build is a senior skill, and interviewers grade it heavily. A candidate who cheerfully designs a distributed frontier with bloom filters for 10M internal PDFs has just demonstrated exactly the judgment failure that gets expensive in real jobs.

"Incremental index updates vs full rebuilds?"

Full rebuild: simple, atomic (build the new index, then flip a pointer), and it lets you change the schema, tokenizer or scoring model freely — every document gets reprocessed. But it's expensive and slow (hours), so your freshness floor is your rebuild period. Incremental: new documents go into small new segments that are queryable immediately; segments merge in the background; deletes are tombstones filtered at query time rather than in-place edits. That's near-real-time, but it's more complex, segments fragment, and merge is a permanent background cost. The real answer is both: incremental for freshness, plus a periodic full rebuild to compact, to garbage-collect tombstones, and — crucially — because changing your tokenizer or your ranking features requires reprocessing every document anyway. That reprocessing need is what forces a rebuild path to exist no matter how good your incremental path is, and saying that shows you've thought past the happy case.

"What about the link graph and PageRank at 1B pages?"

The link graph (~400GB of edges) doesn't fit on one machine, so PageRank is a distributed iterative computation — classically a series of MapReduce jobs, in practice a graph-processing framework (Pregel/Giraph/Spark GraphX) because iterative graph algorithms in raw MapReduce re-read the entire graph from disk on every iteration, which is brutal. It converges in ~20–50 iterations, it's run in batch (daily/weekly, not per-page), and the result is a single float per URL. The bootstrapping question — you need PageRank to prioritise the crawl, but you need the crawl to compute PageRank — is resolved the boring way: use last iteration's values, and seed new domains with a prior from the domain's overall authority. It's a fixed-point iteration that never finishes, which is fine, because neither does the crawl.

Interview Q&A

What data structure is a web crawler, really?

A BFS on a graph. The web is the graph, URLs are nodes, <a href> links are edges. The frontier is the BFS queue; the seen-set is the visited-set. That's it.

Every hard part of the system is one of those two structures breaking under scale: the queue doesn't fit in memory (→ a distributed, domain-partitioned, prioritised queue), the visited-set doesn't fit in memory (→ a bloom filter), visiting a node costs 200ms of network I/O instead of a pointer dereference (→ thousands of concurrent fetchers), and the graph is infinite and adversarial (→ depth limits, budgets, trap detection). Say this in the first two minutes. It gives your whole answer a spine and it tells the interviewer you see the algorithm underneath the boxes.

Why can't you just round-robin URLs across your fetcher pool?

Politeness. If any worker can grab any URL, then at any instant hundreds of workers may independently hold URLs for the same domain — and you've opened 500 concurrent connections to one web server. That's a DDoS, and it will get you blocked or sued.

Enforcing "1 request/sec to example.com" across 1,000 independent workers requires a shared distributed counter checked on every fetch: a coordination bottleneck and a race condition. The fix is to make it a partitioning problem instead of a coordination problem: route by hash(domain), so exactly one worker owns all of example.com. The rate limit becomes a local variable in that worker's heap — no locks, no shared state, no network call. Use consistent hashing (04) so that adding or losing a fetcher remaps only 1/N of domains. This is the key architectural consequence of politeness, and it's the thing interviewers are listening for.

Why a bloom filter for URL dedup, and what does it cost you?

An exact HashSet of 1B URLs is ~100GB of RAM (60-byte URLs plus object overhead). A bloom filter answering the same membership question at a 1% false-positive rate is ~1.2GB — roughly 10 bits per item, independent of how long the URLs are. Two orders of magnitude.

The cost is precise and one-directional: no false negatives, but ~1% false positives. "Not in the set" is a guarantee; "in the set" is a strong probability. So the crawler occasionally believes it has seen a URL it has never crawled, and silently skips that page.

For a web crawler that is an acceptable loss, and being able to say why is the whole point: you're crawling a billion pages out of a web with trillions — you're already sampling. Dropping 1% at random doesn't measurably change index quality, and the page will likely be rediscovered via another link. You've traded 1% recall for 100× less memory.

You said bloom filters are great here — but you rejected them for ticket booking. Why?

Because the tool didn't change; the cost of being wrong did. That's the entire lesson.

In a crawler, a false positive means one page out of a billion doesn't get indexed. Bounded, invisible, self-healing (another link will find it).

In ticket booking (14), a probabilistic answer to "is this seat taken?" can mean you sell seat 14C twice. Unbounded consequence — a refund, a furious customer, and at a fintech loop a compliance conversation. One error is a full-blown incident. There you use the database's uniqueness constraint, which cannot be wrong.

Never name a probabilistic data structure without immediately pricing the error it introduces in that specific domain. "Bloom filters save memory" is the junior answer. "Bloom filters trade a bounded, one-directional error for an enormous space saving, so the only question is what that error costs here" is the senior one — and it's the judgment being graded.

Explain an inverted index from scratch.

Documents are naturally stored doc → words. Queries ask word → docs. You're querying against the grain, so scanning is O(corpus) per query — 10TB, dead on arrival. So precompute the transpose.

For each term, store a posting list: the sorted list of doc IDs containing it (plus term frequency and positions). kafka → [1, 2, 17, 40]. A query for kafka log becomes the intersection of two sorted lists — the two-pointer merge from Phase 3 tutorial 02, O(n+m). Always start with the shortest list.

You build it with a MapReduce: map emits (term, docId) for every word in every document, the shuffle groups by term (that's the transpose), and the reduce writes a sorted, compressed posting list per term. Segments are immutable and merge in the background — which is an LSM-tree, and is exactly what Lucene does.

Document-partitioned or term-partitioned index? Defend it.

Document-partitioned, essentially always. Each shard holds a subset of documents and the complete index over just those; a query fans out to every shard, each returns its local top-10, and you merge.

Term-partitioning looks better on paper — a query only touches the shards holding its terms, so low fan-out. It loses for three reasons:

  • Hot keys. Term frequency is Zipf-distributed. The shard holding "the" is hit by nearly every query while the shard holding "kafka" idles. That's the celebrity-partition problem, and you cannot fix it by adding shards.
  • Writes are a scatter. Adding one document touches every shard owning any word in it — i.e. most of them. Incremental indexing becomes a cluster-wide write.
  • Multi-term queries need cross-shard joins. You'd ship a posting list with millions of entries over the network to intersect it.

Then admit the cost of your own choice: with document-partitioning, every query hits every shard, so my p99 is set by my slowest shard, not my average one. Mitigate with replicas + hedged requests, deadlines with partial results, and caching popular queries. Naming what your choice costs is what makes it an answer rather than an opinion.

What's TF-IDF, in one sentence — and what does BM25 fix?

A term that is rare across the corpus but frequent in this document is a strong signal that the document is about that term. TF (how often it appears here) × IDF (log(totalDocs / docsContaining), which crushes "the" to near zero and rewards "kafka"). IDF is what kills stopwords automatically, with no hand-maintained list.

BM25 fixes two ways TF-IDF misbehaves on real documents: term-frequency saturation (the 50th occurrence of a word adds nearly nothing over the 10th, but TF-IDF grows linearly and rewards keyword stuffing) and document-length normalisation (a 10,000-word page shouldn't outrank a focused 500-word one merely for having more room). BM25 is what Lucene and Elasticsearch actually use. Awareness level — know what it fixes, not the formula.

What's a crawler trap and how do you defend against one?

A page (or site) that generates unbounded unique URLs: an infinite calendar with a "next month" link running to the year 3000; session IDs baked into every link so every crawl yields "new" URLs; faceted e-commerce navigation with a combinatorial explosion of filter permutations; deliberately hostile bot mazes.

The defence is not a clever detector — the general case is undecidable. You cannot reliably distinguish an infinite calendar from a genuinely large site by inspection. The defence is budgets: a depth limit from the seed, a URL-length and query-param-count cap, a hard per-domain page budget, a byte cap on responses (zip bombs), a redirect-chain cap, and monitoring for any domain consuming a disproportionate share of the crawl. Then the worst a trap can do is exhaust one domain's budget rather than your entire crawler.

Say the principle: "I don't try to be smart about hostile input; I bound the blast radius." Same instinct as a bulkhead or a circuit breaker.

The same article is on 40 sites. How do you avoid indexing it 40 times?

Two layers, because they catch different things:

  • Exact duplicates: SHA-256 of the normalised extracted text. Same hash → same document. Keep one canonical URL, record the rest as aliases. Cheap; always do it.
  • Near duplicates (same article, different ad rail / timestamp / comment count — so the exact hash differs on every single fetch): you need a hash where similar documents produce similar hashes. SimHash — a locality-sensitive hash producing a 64-bit fingerprint where "near-duplicate" becomes "Hamming distance ≤ 3", which is a popcount of an XOR. Shingling + MinHash is the other classic.

SimHash is awareness level and you should say so: name it, describe the property that makes it work (similar docs land close together in Hamming space), and be honest that you'd read up before implementing. Bluffing depth you don't have is far more damaging than bounding it.

What's the surprising bottleneck in a crawler?

DNS. Everybody budgets for HTTP fetch latency; almost nobody budgets for the fact that you're resolving millions of distinct hostnames. DNS is UDP, often single-threaded in client libraries, and a cold lookup costs 50–200ms — comparable to the page fetch itself. At scale you can overwhelm your resolver, or your ISP's, and it silently becomes the dominant term in your latency budget.

Fixes: run your own recursive resolvers, cache aggressively with an extended TTL floor, cache per-fetcher and in a shared tier, and prefetch the resolution when a URL enters the frontier rather than when a fetcher picks it up.

The second, less surprising but more fundamental one: politeness caps your throughput. Adding machines does nothing if you only have 100 domains queued. Your only real lever is domain diversity.

Half the web is client-rendered React. How does your crawler see it?

curl on an SPA returns <div id="root"></div> and a script tag: zero text, zero links. Worse than losing one page — you lose every page reachable only through it. It's a graph-reachability failure, not just a content failure.

To see it, you must render: headless Chrome via Puppeteer/Playwright, execute the JS, wait for network idle, read the DOM. But that costs 1–5 seconds of CPU and 100–300MB of RAM per page — 50–100× a static parse. You cannot render a billion pages.

So render selectively. Pass 1: cheap-parse everything. Detect SPA shells heuristically (near-zero text + a lone root div + a large JS bundle). Pass 2: queue only those to a much smaller, separately-budgeted render fleet, prioritised by page importance. And always look for the cheap escape first — SSR output, a __NEXT_DATA__ JSON blob, og: meta tags, a sitemap. Often the content is right there in the HTML and you never need a browser.

And the thing only you can say: this is precisely why SSR/SSG are pitched as SEO features and not just performance features. When you reach for Next.js server rendering, part of what you're buying is making the crawler's job cheap enough that it bothers with you at all.

"Build search over our 10 million internal documents." What do you do?

You use Elasticsearch, and you say so immediately. Almost every hard problem on this page is a consequence of the open web, and none of them exist inside a company:

  • No crawler — there's no link graph. Ingest via connectors / CDC / an outbox from the source systems.
  • No politeness problem — you own the servers.
  • No bloom filter — an exact hash set of 10M doc IDs is ~300MB. Just use the hash set.
  • No custom index — 10M docs is maybe 50GB, which fits comfortably on a few nodes.

What is hard at that scale, and where your effort should go: relevance tuning, ingestion-pipeline reliability, and permissions filtering — a user must never see a document they lack access to, which means access control has to be part of the query itself, not a post-filter, or your result counts and pagination silently break.

Knowing when not to build is a senior skill and it is graded. A candidate who happily designs a distributed frontier with bloom filters for 10M internal PDFs has just demonstrated exactly the judgment failure that gets expensive in a real job.

How do you keep the index fresh without rebuilding 3TB every hour?

A two-tier index. A large, immutable base index rebuilt periodically in batch, plus a small, in-memory fresh index holding the last few hours of documents. Query both and merge the results; periodically fold fresh into base.

That is the LSM-tree pattern (memtable + SSTables) from 03, in a new costume — and also the lambda-architecture shape you'll meet again in 18. Deletes are handled as tombstones filtered at query time, not in-place edits, because segments are immutable.

Note that you still need the full-rebuild path even with a great incremental one, and here's the reason people miss: changing your tokenizer, your stemmer or your ranking features requires reprocessing every document. The rebuild isn't legacy; it's the mechanism by which the index can ever change shape.

Your crawler dies mid-crawl. What have you lost?

Almost nothing, and this is a requirement you should state early: a crawler needs to be fault-tolerant, not highly available. Nobody is waiting on it. Being down for an hour costs you an hour of throughput and zero user-visible impact — which is a genuinely unusual profile in this course, and telling the two properties apart is worth saying out loud.

Mechanically: the frontier is durable (Kafka, offsets committed only after the page is stored — so an in-flight URL is simply redelivered), fetched pages land in blob storage before the offset commits, and URL metadata is in a durable KV store. The one genuinely volatile thing is the bloom filter, which lives in RAM: lose it and you re-crawl everything you've ever seen. Snapshot the bit array to blob storage periodically (it's only ~1GB) and/or back it with a durable set.

Re-delivery means you may fetch the same URL twice. That's fine — crawling is idempotent. You overwrite the same blob key and the same metadata row. Design for at-least-once and make the operation idempotent is the same lesson as 12 and 13; here it's free, because fetching a page has no side effects.

What to remember

  • A crawler is BFS on a graph. Web = graph, URLs = nodes, links = edges, frontier = the queue, seen-set = visited. Every hard part of this problem is a consequence of scaling BFS beyond one machine. Say this in the first two minutes and the question stops being intimidating.
  • It's two systems. An offline, batch, write-heavy crawl+index pipeline, and an online, read-heavy, latency-critical query service, coupled only by the index artefact. Sketch both, then ask which one to go deep on. Trying to do both at depth in 45 minutes gives no signal on either.
  • Politeness is a hard constraint, and it dictates the architecture. Your throughput ceiling is set by manners, not hardware: (domains in flight × per-domain rate). Therefore you partition the frontier by hash(domain) so one worker owns a domain and the rate limit becomes a local variable — turning a distributed-coordination problem into a data-partitioning one. This is the single highest-value insight on the crawler side.
  • Bloom filters earn their keep here: 1B URLs in ~1.2GB instead of ~100GB. The cost is ~1% false positives → you occasionally skip a page you've never crawled. That's acceptable because of what this system is — you're already sampling a fraction of the web. In ticket booking the identical error would be a double-booking. Same tool, opposite verdict; always price the error in the specific domain.
  • The web is adversarial. Infinite calendars, session-ID URL generators, zip bombs, link mazes. You cannot detect traps in general — so you bound the blast radius: depth caps, URL-length caps, per-domain page budgets, response-size caps. Normalise URLs before anything else, or dedup silently stops working.
  • Freshness must be adaptive. A news homepage changes hourly; a 2009 blog post never will. Estimate per-page change frequency from content-hash history and schedule accordingly. Use conditional GETs (ETag → 304) for near-free re-crawls. And remember: re-crawls and discovery compete for the same politeness budget — freshness is bought with coverage.
  • The inverted index is the transpose. Documents are doc→words; queries are word→docs; so precompute word→[docIds]. Query = intersecting sorted posting lists = the two-pointer merge. Build it with a MapReduce. Segments are immutable and merged — i.e. an LSM-tree, i.e. Lucene.
  • Shard the index by document, not by term. Term-partitioning has lower fan-out on paper but dies on hot keys (Zipf: the "the" shard melts), on writes (adding one doc touches every shard), and on cross-shard joins for multi-term queries. Document-partitioning costs you fan-out and tail latency — say so, and mitigate with replicas, hedged requests, deadlines and query caching.
  • Ranking: know the idea, bound the scope. TF-IDF = rare in the corpus but frequent in this doc = strong signal. BM25 adds TF saturation and length normalisation. PageRank treats a link as a vote and is query-independent, computed in batch. Then be honest: real ranking is a learned model, you can't design Google's ranker in 45 minutes, and the click-log feedback loop is the actual moat.
  • DNS is the bottleneck nobody predicts. Millions of unique hostnames, 50–200ms per cold lookup. Run your own resolvers, cache hard, prefetch at enqueue time.
  • Same archetype, everywhere: code search, document search, product-catalogue search, log indexing (Elasticsearch), the search box on any site. Crawl → index → query. And at 10M internal documents the right answer is "use Elasticsearch" — knowing when not to build is the most senior thing you can say in this interview.
