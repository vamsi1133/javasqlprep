14 — Design Ticket Booking (Ticketmaster / BookMyShow)

Archetype: inventory under contention. A finite set of things, and far too many people trying to claim them at the same instant. The whole interview is one question: how do you not sell the same seat twice?

Why this matters in the interview

Every other case study in this block is fundamentally a scale problem: too many reads, too much fan-out, too many bytes. You solve them by adding machines. This one is a correctness problem, and correctness does not get easier when you add machines — it gets harder. You cannot cache your way out of overselling. You cannot shard your way out of it. A design that serves 10M concurrent users beautifully and sells seat 14C to two people is a failed design, and the interviewer will fail it, because in the real world that outcome ends with a refund, a furious customer standing in an aisle holding a valid ticket for an occupied seat, and — at the kind of enterprise and fintech loops you're targeting — a compliance conversation. This is the hardest correctness problem in the course, and it is a favourite at exactly the companies on your list. Optum, JPMC and Goldman do not ask you to design Twitter. They ask you how two threads don't both get the last unit of something.

The trap is that ticket booking looks like a CRUD app. Events, venues, seats, bookings — you have built this table layout a hundred times. Junior candidates draw those five boxes in four minutes, feel good, and then spend the remaining forty minutes being slowly dismantled. Because the moment 50,000 people press "buy" on 10,000 seats, every naive design breaks, and it breaks in a way that your local testing will never reveal. Concurrency bugs are invisible at one request per second. The single skill this page teaches is recognising, out loud, in the first two minutes, that this is not a CRUD problem — it is a concurrency problem wearing a CRUD costume. Say that sentence and the tone of the interview changes.

And the archetype is everywhere. Strip the word "seat" out and what you have is: a finite resource, many claimants, exactly one winner per unit, and being wrong is unacceptable. That is flight seats. Hotel rooms. Flash-sale stock on a e-commerce site. Ad impressions in an auction. Parking spots. Warehouse inventory in an order system (13). Domain-name registration. Even a distributed lock itself is this problem — a lock is an inventory of exactly one, and everything 05 told you about why locks are hard is a special case of what you're about to learn. This page assumes 03 (isolation levels, optimistic vs pessimistic concurrency) and 05 (distributed locks, sagas, why the invariant belongs in the database). Those pages built the vocabulary. This page spends it.

1. The prompt

The interviewer says, and this is roughly verbatim at half a dozen companies:

"Design Ticketmaster. Users should be able to browse events, see which seats are available, pick one, and buy it."

That is the entire prompt. It is deliberately, almost insultingly, simple — and it is simple on purpose, because the interviewer is watching to see what you notice. There are two candidates in this room:

  • The one who hears "browse, see, pick, buy" and starts drawing an events table, a seats table, a REST API and a React frontend. He is designing a CRUD app. He will be fine for fifteen minutes.
  • The one who hears "pick one, and buy it" and immediately asks: "how many people are trying to buy at the same time?" — because that single number determines whether this is a weekend project or one of the genuinely hard problems in applied distributed systems.

Here is the thing to understand and to say. A cinema booking system for a 200-seat screen with 30 concurrent users is a Postgres table and an afternoon. The same system for a stadium tour on-sale — 10 million people refreshing at 10:00:00 for 50,000 seats — is a fundamentally different machine. Same requirements document, different system. Nothing about the functional spec changed; the traffic shape changed, and the traffic shape is the design.

Open with this, before you draw anything: "Before I design anything — this looks like a CRUD app, and I want to say up front that I don't think it is one. The read path is easy: it's a catalogue, I can cache it at the edge. The write path is a concurrency problem, because the defining event in this system is 50,000 people trying to claim 10,000 seats in the same second. Everything I design is going to be shaped by not selling the same seat twice under that load. Can I confirm the traffic profile you have in mind?" That is ninety seconds, and it establishes that you have seen this problem before and know where the bodies are buried. Most candidates take twenty minutes to arrive where that sentence puts you immediately.

The interviewer will now, almost always, lean in and give you the hard version. That is what you want. You have just traded the boring version of the question for the one where you get to show judgment.

2. Requirements

The clarifying questions to ask first

Ask these before you draw a single box. Each one has a right answer you can assume out loud if the interviewer waves you on, and each one visibly changes the design — which is the point of asking.

AskAssume, if they shrugWhy it changes the design
"Is overselling ever acceptable?" No. Never. Assigned seats, concert venue. This is the most important question on the page. See below — the answer flips the entire architecture.
"Are seats assigned (seat 14C) or general admission (a number of tickets)?" Assigned. Specific seats, a seat map. General admission is a counter, and a counter can be decremented atomically. That is a dramatically easier problem. Assigned seats are what make this hard.
"Is there a flash-sale / on-sale moment, or is demand smooth?" Flash sale. 10:00:00, everybody at once. Smooth demand needs none of the waiting-room machinery. The flash sale is the design constraint.

"Do I own payments, or do I call a provider?" Call Stripe. Payments are 13's problem. Scopes the question. But the seam between hold and payment is mine, and it's nasty.
"Single region or global?" Single region for inventory. Reads global via CDN. Cut it explicitly. Multi-region inventory is a genuinely different (and much worse) problem — see follow-ups.

The question that actually matters: "is overselling ever acceptable?"

This is the strongest opening move available on this page, and almost nobody makes it. It sounds like a pedantic clarification. It is not — it is a fork in the architecture, and here is why:

  • Airlines deliberately oversell. They know a predictable percentage of passengers won't show up, so they sell 105% of the seats, and when everyone does show up they run a compensation auction at the gate ("$800 voucher for anyone who takes the next flight"). Overselling is a revenue optimisation with a known, priced-in cost. If overselling is acceptable, you don't need strict serialisation at all — you can take writes optimistically, reconcile asynchronously, run the whole thing eventually consistent, and let a compensation workflow clean up the overlap. The system gets radically simpler and faster.
  • A concert does not oversell. Seat 14C is a physical chair. There is exactly one of it. Two valid tickets for one chair is not a business trade-off, it's an incident. Here you need a hard invariant, enforced, with no wiggle room. Overselling by one is a bug of the same severity as overselling by a thousand.
Say it like this: "My first question is whether overselling is ever acceptable, because the answer changes the whole system. Airlines oversell on purpose and compensate at the gate — if that's the model, I can run this eventually consistent and reconcile later, and it's a much cheaper design. For a concert with assigned seats, seat 14C is a physical chair and overselling it is an incident, not a trade-off. I'll assume the concert model: never oversell is a hard correctness constraint, not a soft target. That means I'm willing to be slow, and I'm willing to make people queue, but I'm not willing to be wrong." That last clause — slow is acceptable, wrong is not — is the thesis of the entire page, and stating it in minute three means every subsequent decision you make has a justification the interviewer already agreed to.

Functional requirements

  • Browse events — search/list events by city, date, artist. (Low-stakes; it's a catalogue.)
  • View a seat map — for an event, see which of the ~50,000 specific seats are available. This is the heaviest read in the system by an enormous margin.
  • Hold a seat — claim seat 14C temporarily while you enter payment details. The hold has a TTL and a visible countdown.
  • Pay and confirm — convert the hold into a booking, via a payment provider. Issue a ticket.
  • Release / cancel — the user backs out, or the hold expires, and the seat returns to inventory.

Seats are specific. Not "a ticket" — seat 14C. Say this explicitly, because it is the single requirement that makes the problem hard, and you want the interviewer to see that you know that.

Explicitly out of scope (say so, and say why): dynamic pricing, recommendations, the ticket-scanning /turnstile system at the venue, refunds and the secondary resale market, the promoter-facing event-creation CMS. Each is a real system; none of them is this system. Cutting scope out loud is free credibility.

Non-functional requirements — and these are the interesting ones

RequirementTargetWhat it really means

Seat map freshness "Reasonably fresh" — seconds of staleness is fine Deliberately weak, and that's the trick. The seat map is a hint, not a contract. The write path is where truth lives. Accepting stale reads is what lets you cache aggressively and survive the read load.

Notice what just happened. By ranking correctness > availability on the write path and availability > freshness on the read path, you've split the system into two halves with opposite consistency requirements. That split — a wildly available, happily-stale read path in front of a fiercely consistent, deliberately bounded write path — is the architecture. You just derived it from the requirements instead of memorising it, which is precisely what tutorial 01 said the job was.

3. Estimation

Forget DAU. Forget monthly actives. There is exactly one number that matters in this system and it is the shape of the on-sale spike. Say that out loud, because the standard estimation ritual — "let's say 100M DAU, divide by 86,400 seconds…" — is actively misleading here and a good interviewer knows it.

The scenario that defines the system

A stadium tour goes on sale at 10:00:00 on a Friday. Round everything; I'm rounding hard and I'll say so.

  Stadium capacity ................. 50,000 seats
  People who want a ticket ......... 10,000,000   (a big tour; this is real)
  On-sale window ................... they ALL arrive within ~60 seconds
  Demand : supply ratio ............ 200 : 1

Read load — enormous

  10M users arrive in ~60s
    → ~170,000 users/sec arriving                (10M / 60)
    → each loads the event page + the seat map
    → and then REFRESHES, because the map is changing and they're anxious
    → assume ~3 seat-map fetches per user in the first minute

  Seat-map reads ≈ 10M x 3 / 60s  ≈  500,000 reads/sec   ← peak
  Payload: 50,000 seats x ~20 bytes (id, status, price tier) ≈ 1 MB raw
           → compressed / delta-encoded, call it ~100 KB

  Naive bandwidth: 500,000/s x 100 KB  =  50 GB/sec       ← absurd

50 GB/sec is not a number you serve from an origin. It is not a number you serve from a hundred origins. This number alone tells you the seat map must be cached at the CDN edge, and that you must accept staleness to do it. The read path's entire design falls out of this one calculation. (And note the second-order effect: users refresh during a flash sale, so your read multiplier is worse than your instinct says.)

Write load — and here is the whole insight

  Bookings that can POSSIBLY succeed ........  50,000    ← total. Ever. Full stop.
  Sold out in .............................. ~5 minutes (300s), typically much faster

  Successful booking writes ≈ 50,000 / 300s  ≈  ~170 writes/sec

  Hold ATTEMPTS, though, are a different story:
  10M people all clicking "buy" on the same 50k seats
    → hold attempts could be 100,000+/sec
    → of which >99% MUST FAIL, because there is nothing to sell them
This is the sentence that wins the estimation section, and I want you to say it more or less verbatim: "The read load is enormous and unbounded — call it 500k requests a second at peak. But the write load has a hard ceiling: I literally cannot sell more than 50,000 tickets, because there are only 50,000 chairs. No matter how much traffic arrives, the number of successful writes is bounded by the inventory, not by the demand. That asymmetry is the most useful fact about this system — it means I can protect the write path extremely aggressively, because I know exactly how much work it will ever have to do. Everything else is load I'm allowed to shed." Almost no candidate says this, and it is the observation that makes every subsequent decision — the queue, the aggressive caching, the single Postgres — obviously correct rather than arbitrary.

Sit with that for a second, because it inverts the usual system-design instinct. Normally traffic drives capacity: more users, more write throughput needed. Here, 10 million users and 100 million users produce the same 50,000 successful writes. The extra demand doesn't create work — it creates failed attempts. And failed attempts are something you are allowed to reject, delay, queue, or shed, because rejecting them is correct: those people were never going to get a ticket anyway. There are 10M people and 50k seats. 99.5% of your users are going home disappointed no matter how good your engineering is. Your job is to make sure they go home disappointed quickly and politely, rather than by taking your database down on their way out.

Storage — trivially small, and say so

  Seats:     50,000 rows/event x ~100 bytes            =  5 MB per event
  10,000 events/year                                   = 50 GB/year of seat rows
  Bookings:  ~500M tickets/year x ~200 bytes           = 100 GB/year
  Events, venues, users: rounding error.

  Total: comfortably a few hundred GB/year.
Storage is a non-issue and you should say so in one sentence and move on. "A few hundred gigabytes a year fits on a single Postgres instance with room to spare; storage is not driving this design, so I'm not going to spend time on it." Candidates who lovingly compute five-year storage projections for a system whose actual problem is a 60-second concurrency spike are demonstrating that they run the ritual without understanding why it exists. The senior move in estimation is knowing which number to ignore.

Do not average the peak away. A candidate who computes "10M users over a day = 116 requests/sec, that's easy" has computed a number that is true and completely useless. The peak is not an outlier to be smoothed; the peak IS the product. Nobody books concert tickets at 3am on a Tuesday — the on-sale moment is when the entire system's traffic happens, and a design that only works at the average is a design that only works when nobody is using it.

4. API design

Five endpoints. The one that matters is POST /holds, and the design decision to defend is that a hold is a first-class resource with its own lifetime — not a flag you flip on a seat, not a field in a session. It has a URI, it can be fetched, it can be deleted, and it expires on its own. Modelling it as a resource is what makes the lifecycle (deep dive 2) tractable. 

// ---- READ PATH: cacheable, stale-tolerant, never touches the write DB ----

GET /events?city=london&from=2026-08-01
  // → the catalogue. Cache for minutes. Boring on purpose.

GET /events/{eventId}/seats
  // → the seat map. THE hot read. Served from CDN/edge.
  // Cache-Control: public, max-age=5, stale-while-revalidate=30
  // Response is a HINT, not a contract. Explicitly say so in the payload.
{
  "eventId": "evt_88",
  "asOf": "2026-08-01T10:00:03Z",     // ← tell the client how stale this is
  "sections": [
    { "id": "A", "seats": [
        { "id": "A-14C", "status": "AVAILABLE", "priceCents": 12000 },
        { "id": "A-14D", "status": "BOOKED",    "priceCents": 12000 }
    ]}
  ]
}

// ---- WRITE PATH: authoritative, contended, protected ----

POST /holds
Idempotency-Key: 9f2c-...          // ← from 13. A double-clicked button must not take two holds.
{ "eventId": "evt_88", "seatIds": ["A-14C"], "userId": "u_7" }

  // 201 Created — you got it
  { "holdId": "hold_123",
    "seatIds": ["A-14C"],
    "expiresAt": "2026-08-01T10:10:00Z",   // ← the countdown the UI renders
    "totalCents": 12000 }

  // 409 Conflict — somebody beat you. THE most common response during an on-sale.
  { "error": "SEAT_UNAVAILABLE",
    "seatIds": ["A-14C"],
    "message": "Sorry, that seat just went." }

POST /bookings                       // convert hold → booking, taking payment
Idempotency-Key: 4a71-...
{ "holdId": "hold_123", "paymentMethodId": "pm_x" }
  // 201 → { bookingId, tickets: [...], status: "CONFIRMED" }
  // 410 Gone → the hold expired while you were typing your card number.
  //            (This edge case is nastier than it looks — see deep dive 2.)

DELETE /holds/{holdId}              // user backed out; release the seat immediately

GET    /holds/{holdId}              // how long have I got left? (client resync after a reload)

Three decisions in that API worth defending

  • The hold is separate from the booking. Two calls, not one. You could imagine a single POST /bookings that grabs the seat and charges the card in one shot — and for a low-contention system that's genuinely fine. But it forces you to hold the seat for the entire duration of a payment call you don't control, which is exactly the failure mode that kills the pessimistic-locking design in deep dive 1. Splitting the calls is what lets you release the database the instant the seat is claimed, and pay afterwards.
  • expiresAt is returned to the client, and it's absolute. Not "600 seconds remaining" — an absolute timestamp. Relative durations drift across a slow network and a client that got backgrounded. The client renders a countdown from it; the client's countdown is cosmetic. The server never trusts it.
  • 409 is a normal, expected, high-volume response — not an error. During an on-sale, the overwhelming majority of POST /holds calls will 409, by design, because there are 200 people per seat. Your monitoring must not page on it, your client must render it gracefully ("sorry, that seat just went — here are three others"), and your rate limiter must not treat it as abuse. Failure is the common path here. Design for it as a first-class outcome and say so — it's an unusual observation and interviewers notice it.
Idempotency keys on both write endpoints, straight from 13. A user on a flaky phone connection during a flash sale will absolutely double-tap "buy", and their browser will absolutely retry the POST. Without a key, a retried POST /bookings can charge them twice. This is the one piece of machinery you can lift wholesale from the payments page — don't re-derive it, just name it and move on. Spending 90 seconds re-explaining idempotency keys in this interview is spending time you need for the overselling problem.

5. Data model

SQL. Postgres. Made explicitly, defended immediately: this system's defining requirement is a hard correctness invariant under concurrent writes, and a relational database with ACID transactions and unique constraints is the single best tool humanity has built for exactly that. 03 made this point in the abstract; here it's concrete. Reaching for Cassandra or DynamoDB here means re-implementing ACID badly, in application code, and the interviewer will make you prove it can't oversell — and it can. And remember estimation: the write ceiling is 170/sec. A single Postgres node does that in its sleep. There is no scale argument for NoSQL here, only a scale argument against it, because you'd be giving up the exact guarantee you need in exchange for throughput you don't. 

CREATE TABLE venues (
  id            BIGINT PRIMARY KEY,
  name          TEXT NOT NULL,
  seat_map      JSONB            -- static geometry: rows, sections, coordinates
);

CREATE TABLE events (
  id            BIGINT PRIMARY KEY,
  venue_id      BIGINT REFERENCES venues(id),
  name          TEXT NOT NULL,
  starts_at     TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL,
  onsale_at     TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL   -- the 10:00:00 that ruins your day
);

-- ★ THE TABLE THE WHOLE INTERVIEW IS ABOUT ★
CREATE TABLE seats (
  id            BIGINT PRIMARY KEY,
  event_id      BIGINT NOT NULL REFERENCES events(id),
  label         TEXT   NOT NULL,        -- 'A-14C'
  price_cents   INT    NOT NULL,
  status        TEXT   NOT NULL,        -- AVAILABLE | HELD | BOOKED
  held_by       BIGINT,                  -- user holding it, if HELD
  hold_expires_at TIMESTAMPTZ,           -- ← the lazy-expiry trick lives here
  version       INT NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,   -- optimistic concurrency counter

  CONSTRAINT uq_seat_label UNIQUE (event_id, label),
  CONSTRAINT ck_status    CHECK (status IN ('AVAILABLE','HELD','BOOKED'))
);
-- One row per physical chair. 50,000 rows for a stadium. Pre-generated when
-- the event is created — NOT created on demand at booking time.
CREATE INDEX ix_seats_avail ON seats (event_id, status);

CREATE TABLE holds (
  id            BIGINT PRIMARY KEY,
  user_id       BIGINT NOT NULL,
  event_id      BIGINT NOT NULL,
  status        TEXT   NOT NULL,        -- ACTIVE | CONVERTED | RELEASED | EXPIRED
  expires_at    TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL,   -- created_at + 10 minutes
  created_at    TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);
CREATE INDEX ix_holds_expiry ON holds (status, expires_at);  -- for the sweeper

CREATE TABLE bookings (
  id            BIGINT PRIMARY KEY,
  user_id       BIGINT NOT NULL,
  event_id      BIGINT NOT NULL,
  seat_id       BIGINT NOT NULL REFERENCES seats(id),
  payment_id    TEXT,                    -- the provider's charge id
  status        TEXT NOT NULL,          -- CONFIRMED | CANCELLED
  created_at    TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),

  -- ★★ THE BACKSTOP. The database will PHYSICALLY REFUSE to double-book. ★★
  CONSTRAINT uq_seat_booked UNIQUE (event_id, seat_id)
);

The punchline: the seat row IS the lock

Look hard at the seats table. It has a status column, a held_by column, an expires_at and a version. Now compare that with a Redis distributed lock from 05: a key (the resource), a value (who holds it), a TTL (when it expires), and — if you were doing it properly — a fencing token (the version).

They are the same object. You have just built a lock. The difference is that this one lives inside a database with a linearizable lock manager, a durable WAL, and real transactions, and it costs you nothing extra because you needed the row anyway.

This is the highest-value sentence on the page and it is worth rehearsing until it's automatic: "I want to point out what the seats table actually is. A row with a status, a holder and an expiry is a lock — it's the same shape as a Redis lock key, except it's stored in a database that already gives me atomicity, durability and a real concurrency-control mechanism for free. So when someone asks me later whether I'd add a distributed lock to protect the seat, my answer is that the seat row is already the lock, and adding a second, weaker lock over the top of a correct one can only make things worse." That reframing — the resource is the lock — is the through-line from tutorial 05, it is the reason this design is correct, and it is the answer to about four of the follow-up questions before they're asked.

6. High-level design

The architecture is the requirements made physical: a fat, stale, wildly-available read path, and a thin, guarded, fiercely-consistent write path. Draw them as two separate flows, because they are two separate systems that happen to share a database, and the single most common way to fail this question is to let the read load anywhere near the write path.

Note the proportions, because they're the argument. The read path is enormous and does not talk to Postgres. The write path is tiny and does nothing but talk to Postgres. Between them sits a queue whose entire job is to guarantee the write path never sees more load than it can absorb — and since you know from estimation that it will never need to do more than 50,000 units of work total, you can size that admission rate with confidence rather than hope.

Walkthrough: a read (the seat map)

  1. Browser requests GET /events/88/seats.
  2. CDN hit — 99%+ of the time, during an on-sale, this returns a blob that is up to 5 seconds old. Zero backend load. This is where half a million requests per second go to die.
  3. On a miss, the Seat Map Service returns a pre-baked JSON blob out of Redis. It does not query Postgres to build it per-request — that would be 50,000 rows scanned per request, and at even 1% miss rate that's 5,000 req/s × 50k rows and your primary is on fire.
  4. A background job rebuilds that blob from Postgres every ~1–5 seconds and pushes it to Redis. One reader of the database, serving everyone. (This is fan-out-on-write applied to a cache — the same trick as 10, at a different layer.)
  5. The browser renders the map. Some of those "available" seats are already gone. That is expected, accepted, and designed for.

Walkthrough: a write (claiming a seat)

  1. User clicks seat A-14C. Browser fires POST /holds with an idempotency key.
  2. The request carries a waiting-room token. No valid token → 403, back to the queue. During an on-sale this rejects the overwhelming majority of requests at the edge, before they cost you anything.
  3. Rate limiter (09) — per-user and per-IP. Scalper bots are the real adversary here.
  4. Booking Service issues one statement: UPDATE seats SET status='HELD', … WHERE id=? AND status='AVAILABLE'. It checks the affected-row count. 1 → you won. 0 → someone beat you, return 409. No explicit lock. No SELECT first. No transaction spanning anything slow. The statement is the entire critical section, and it lasts under a millisecond.
  5. Insert a holds row with expires_at = now() + 10 minutes, in the same transaction. Commit. The database connection is released. The seat is claimed. Nothing is locked.
  6. Return the hold and its expiry. The UI starts a 10:00 countdown.
  7. User enters card details — taking however long they take, while your database sits idle.
  8. POST /bookings → re-validate the hold is still ACTIVE and unexpired → call Stripe (3 seconds, and this happens outside any lock) → on success, transactionally flip the seat to BOOKED, insert the bookings row (where the UNIQUE (event_id, seat_id) constraint stands as the final backstop), mark the hold CONVERTED.
  9. Async: issue the ticket, email it, invalidate the seat-map cache.
The line that makes an interviewer nod: "The critical section is a single UPDATE statement that completes in under a millisecond, and the slow thing — the payment call — happens completely outside it. That's the entire trick. Every design that fails this question fails because it holds something across the payment." Keep that sentence in your pocket; it is the compressed form of the next section and you can deploy it the moment anyone mentions locking.

7a. Deep dive: the overselling problem

This is where the interview really happens. Everything before this was setup. If you get thirty minutes into a ticket-booking question and haven't been pushed here, the interviewer is either being kind or has stopped paying attention. Every other section on this page can be a little wrong. This one cannot.

The question is one sentence: two users click seat 14C at the same millisecond. How does exactly one of them get it? There are five answers people give. Four of them are wrong or bad, and knowing why — precisely, mechanically why — is the whole assessment. Walk them in escalating order. Do this out loud; the walk itself is the signal.

Approach 1 — Read-then-write. The naive one. It is broken.

This is what everyone writes the first time, and it is what a CRUD instinct produces:

-- Thread A                                    Thread B
SELECT status FROM seats WHERE id=14;    -- 'AVAILABLE'
-- application: if (status == AVAILABLE) {
UPDATE seats SET status='BOOKED' WHERE id=14;

Looks fine. It is not fine. Here is the interleaving, and draw this timeline on the whiteboard — do not just describe it, because the picture is the proof:

  time ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────▶

  Thread A   SELECT id=14 ──▶ 'AVAILABLE'
                                    │
                                    │        ┌── Thread B  SELECT id=14 ──▶ 'AVAILABLE'
                                    │        │             (!!! still available — A hasn't
                                    │        │              written yet. B's read is CORRECT.)
                                    ▼        │
  Thread A   if (available) ✓ ──▶ UPDATE ... │
                                  status='BOOKED'
                                             ▼
                                    Thread B  if (available) ✓ ──▶ UPDATE
                                                                   status='BOOKED'

  Result: TWO people hold a valid booking for seat 14C.
          Neither thread did anything "wrong". The DB did exactly what it was told.

This is a TOCTOU race — time-of-check to time-of-use. The check ("is it available?") and the use ("take it") are two separate operations, and anything can happen in the gap between them. The window is microseconds wide. At 5 requests/sec you will never, ever see this bug — not in dev, not in staging, not in a load test you wrote yourself. At 100,000 requests/sec on one seat, it happens constantly.

Wrapping the two statements in BEGIN … COMMIT does not fix this. This is the single most common wrong answer in the entire question, and candidates say it with real confidence. A transaction gives you atomicity (both statements or neither) and isolation at the level you configured — and Postgres's default is Read Committed, under which both transactions cheerfully read 'AVAILABLE' before either writes. You have wrapped a lost update in a transaction. It is still a lost update. (See 03's anomaly table: this is precisely the "lost update" row, and Read Committed does not prevent it.) If you say "I'd use a transaction" and stop there, the interviewer's next question is "at what isolation level?", and if you don't have an answer, the round is effectively over.

Approach 2 — Pessimistic locking. Correct, and it will still take your site down.

BEGIN;
SELECT status FROM seats WHERE id = 14 FOR UPDATE;  -- row lock, held until COMMIT
-- Thread B blocks HERE until A commits. No race. Genuinely correct.
UPDATE seats SET status='BOOKED' WHERE id = 14;
COMMIT;

SELECT … FOR UPDATE takes an exclusive row lock. Thread B's SELECT blocks until A commits, then sees 'BOOKED' and correctly declines. This is correct. Say that out loud — do not dismiss it, because dismissing a correct solution without understanding it is a worse look than proposing it. Millions of systems run this way and are fine.

But look at what happens when you put the payment inside the transaction, which is exactly what a candidate under time pressure does:

  // The design that kills the company
  BEGIN
    SELECT ... FOR UPDATE          // row lock acquired  ← t=0ms
    charge = stripe.charge(card)   // ⏳ 3 SECONDS. Network. Third party.
                                   //    THE ROW LOCK IS STILL HELD.
                                   //    THE DB CONNECTION IS STILL HELD.
    UPDATE seats SET ...
  COMMIT                           //                    ← t=3000ms

Do the arithmetic, because the arithmetic is the argument:

  Connection pool ................. 100 connections (a generous default)
  Time each booking holds one ..... 3 seconds (the Stripe call)
  Max booking throughput .......... 100 / 3s  =  ~33 bookings/sec

  Incoming during on-sale ......... 100,000 requests/sec

  → Pool exhausted in ~1 millisecond.
  → Every subsequent request BLOCKS waiting for a connection.
  → The seat-map service shares the pool. It blocks too.
  → Health checks need a connection. They block. The LB marks
    every node unhealthy and pulls them out of rotation.
  → The entire site is down, including the parts that had nothing
    to do with booking.

And it's worse than throughput collapse. If Stripe hangs for 30 seconds, you hold a row lock for 30 seconds. Locks are a shared, finite, global resource, and a slow third party you don't control is now holding them. You have coupled your database's health to Stripe's p99 latency. That is a catastrophic piece of coupling, and it is invisible in a code review.

Here is the distinction that separates a 7-YOE answer from a 3-YOE one, and it's the most important thing in this section: "Pessimistic locking with SELECT FOR UPDATE is correct — it will never oversell. I'm rejecting it for a throughput reason, not a correctness reason, and I want to be precise about which. It serialises every contender on a hot seat and, more dangerously, it tempts you into holding a row lock and a pooled connection across a three-second call to a payment provider you don't control. Under a flash sale my connection pool is exhausted in about a millisecond and the whole site goes down — including browsing, which didn't need the database at all. The lock isn't the problem; the duration of the lock is the problem." Naming why a correct solution is still the wrong solution is exactly the judgment being graded. A candidate who says "pessimistic locking is bad" has memorised a slogan. A candidate who says "it's correct but it holds a connection across a third-party call and that's an outage" has understood the system.

Could you keep pessimistic locking and just move the payment outside the transaction? Yes — and now you have re-invented holds, which is exactly what the two-phase hold/booking API does. That's a fine thing to say out loud: it shows the design isn't arbitrary. But once the payment is outside the transaction, the lock is only being held for a single-row status flip, and at that point you don't need the explicit lock at all — which brings us to:

Approach 3 — Optimistic concurrency. The answer. ★

Stop reading first. Make the check part of the write:

-- ★ THE ONE STATEMENT THIS ENTIRE PAGE IS ABOUT ★
UPDATE seats
   SET status          = 'HELD',
       held_by         = :userId,
       hold_expires_at = now() + INTERVAL '10 minutes',
       version         = version + 1
 WHERE id     = :seatId
   AND status = 'AVAILABLE';      -- ← THE CHECK *IS* THE WRITE

-- Then look at the affected-row count:
--   1  → you got the seat. It's yours for 10 minutes.
--   0  → someone beat you. Return 409. No harm, no lock, no retry storm.

Why this is safe, stated precisely — and you must be able to state it precisely, because "optimistic locking" as a phrase is worth nothing without the mechanism:

A single UPDATE … WHERE <predicate> is atomic within the database engine. When two transactions target the same row, the engine takes a row-level write lock internally, for the duration of that one statement — microseconds. The second writer blocks momentarily, and then — critically — under Read Committed, Postgres re-evaluates the WHERE clause against the newly committed version of the row. It sees status = 'HELD', the predicate no longer matches, and the update affects zero rows. The database's own concurrency control does the work. You didn't write a lock; you used the one that was already there, correctly, for the shortest possible time.

There is no window between check and use, because there is no separate check. That's the whole idea. The TOCTOU race is not mitigated — it is eliminated by construction, because there is no gap in which to interleave.

The Java / Spring version

@Repository
public interface SeatRepository extends JpaRepository<Seat, Long> {

    @Modifying
    @Query(value = """
        UPDATE seats
           SET status = 'HELD',
               held_by = :userId,
               hold_expires_at = now() + interval '10 minutes',
               version = version + 1
         WHERE id = :seatId
           AND (status = 'AVAILABLE'
                OR (status = 'HELD' AND hold_expires_at < now()))
        """, nativeQuery = true)
    int tryHold(@Param("seatId") long seatId, @Param("userId") long userId);
    // ↑ returns the AFFECTED ROW COUNT. That integer is the entire answer.
    // Note the OR clause — that's lazy expiry, deep dive 2. It costs nothing here.
}

@Service
public class HoldService {

    @Transactional
    public Hold hold(long seatId, long userId) {

        int rows = seatRepo.tryHold(seatId, userId);   // ← atomic. sub-millisecond.

        if (rows == 0) {
            // Someone beat me. This is the COMMON case during an on-sale —
            // it is not an error, it is the expected outcome for 99% of users.
            throw new SeatUnavailableException(seatId);   // → HTTP 409
        }

        return holdRepo.save(Hold.active(seatId, userId, Duration.ofMinutes(10)));
    }
    // TRANSACTION ENDS HERE. Connection returned to the pool.
    // NOTHING is locked. The user can now take 9 minutes to find their wallet.
    // Payment happens LATER, in a completely separate request. That is the point.
}
Two things that will bite you in the code, not the design. First: if you use JPA's @Version annotation and a normal save(), Hibernate does a read-modify-write and throws OptimisticLockException on conflict. That is also correct, but it costs you a read, and at 100k contended attempts/sec you're generating a hundred thousand exceptions per second — exceptions with stack traces are genuinely expensive in the JVM. The raw conditional UPDATE returning a row count is strictly better under contention: no read, no exception, just an integer. Knowing that difference is a real Java-plus-Postgres signal, and you just spent a whole phase on both. Second: @Modifying queries bypass the persistence context — the entity in your L1 cache is now stale. Don't read it back and trust it in the same transaction.

The retry storm — and why it doesn't materialise here

The standard objection to optimistic concurrency, and 03 raises it explicitly, is: "under high contention, the losers all retry, and you livelock." That objection is correct in general and does not apply to this system, and knowing why is a genuinely senior distinction:

  • The losers don't retry the same row. If you lose seat 14C, retrying 14C is pointless — it's gone forever, not temporarily busy. The correct client behaviour is to fail fast and pick a different seat, which is exactly what the UI does. Compare this with an optimistic update on a bank balance, where the row is still there and you genuinely must retry until you succeed. Seat contention is not retryable contention. That's a lovely thing to say out loud.
  • Losing is cheap. One statement, zero rows affected, sub-millisecond, no lock held, connection back in the pool. You can afford to lose 99,830 times a second. Contrast with pessimistic locking, where losing costs you a blocked thread and a held connection.
Deliver approach 3 like this: "I'd make the check and the write a single atomic statement: UPDATE seats SET status='HELD' WHERE id=? AND status='AVAILABLE', and then look at the affected-row count. One means I won; zero means someone beat me and I return a 409. The database guarantees that a single UPDATE with a predicate is atomic — it re-evaluates the WHERE clause against the committed row — so there is no window between the check and the write, because there is no separate check. And critically, no lock is held across the payment: the seat is claimed in under a millisecond, and Stripe gets called afterwards, outside any transaction. That gives me pessimistic locking's correctness with optimistic locking's throughput, which is the combination this problem needs."

Approach 4 — The unique constraint. The backstop that cannot be bribed. ★

Everything so far has been your application logic being careful. Application logic has bugs. Someone will add a code path in eighteen months, an admin tool will do a bulk seat reassignment, a migration script will run at 2am, a developer will "just quickly" write a raw UPDATE without the WHERE clause. So put the invariant somewhere it cannot be bypassed: 

ALTER TABLE bookings
  ADD CONSTRAINT uq_seat_booked UNIQUE (event_id, seat_id);

-- Now the database will PHYSICALLY REFUSE to record two bookings for one seat.
-- Not "will validate". Not "should catch". REFUSE. The INSERT errors out at
-- the storage layer, inside the engine, regardless of what your application
-- code believes, regardless of which service wrote it, regardless of any bug
-- anyone introduces at any point in the future.

try {
    bookingRepo.save(new Booking(eventId, seatId, userId, chargeId));
} catch (DataIntegrityViolationException e) {
    // The constraint fired. This should be IMPOSSIBLE if the hold logic is
    // correct — which is exactly why catching it matters. It is a canary.
    // Refund the charge (saga compensation — see 13), page someone, and
    // be extremely grateful that the customer never found out.
    payments.refund(chargeId);
    alerts.critical("DOUBLE BOOKING PREVENTED BY CONSTRAINT", eventId, seatId);
    throw new SeatUnavailableException(seatId);
}
This is the through-line from 05, and it is worth landing hard: "Push the invariant into the database. An application-level check is advisory — it holds only as long as every code path that ever touches this table remembers to perform it. A constraint is enforced — the database will reject the write no matter which service, script, or intern produced it. The conditional UPDATE is my primary mechanism because it's fast; the unique constraint is my backstop because it's unbypassable. I want both, and if the constraint ever fires in production I want it to page me, because it means my primary mechanism has a bug I don't know about." That last clause — treating the constraint violation as a monitoring signal, a canary for bugs in your own logic, rather than just an error to swallow — is a genuinely senior thought and I have watched it change the temperature of an interview.

Approach 5 — A distributed lock in Redis. The trap. ✗

Ask a candidate how to stop a double-booking and nine out of ten say "a distributed lock in Redis." It is the reflex answer. It is on every blog. Here, it is the worst of the five options, and being the tenth candidate is worth a lot. 

  // The reflex answer
  String token = UUID.randomUUID().toString();
  if (redis.set("lock:seat:" + seatId, token, nx().px(10_000))) {
      // ... check seat, book seat ...   ← and now you have a WORSE system
  }

Why it's bad here — and give all three reasons, in this order:

  1. It is not safe for correctness. Straight from 05: a GC pause longer than the TTL and two clients hold the lock; clock skew expires it early; Redis's asynchronous replication means a routine primary failover can hand the same lock to two clients, and nobody did anything wrong. Redlock is contested (Kleppmann's critique) and still issues no fencing token. You would be protecting a hard correctness invariant with a mechanism that is explicitly not safe for correctness invariants.
  2. You already have a correct lock and you're paying for it. Postgres has a linearizable lock manager, a durable WAL, and real transactions. The seat row is the lock. Adding Redis means layering a weaker, best-effort lock on top of a stronger one that you're using anyway. It cannot make the system more correct. It can only add a new failure mode.
  3. It adds a new dependency on the critical path. Now "can I book a seat?" depends on Redis being up, reachable, and un-failed-over. You've reduced availability to buy nothing.
The exact sentence, and it is a genuine differentiator: "I'd deliberately not use a distributed lock here, and I want to explain why, because I think it's the trap in this question. A Redis lock isn't safe for a correctness invariant — GC pauses, clock skew, and async replication on failover can all hand the same lock to two clients, which is exactly the Kleppmann critique of Redlock. But the deeper point is that I don't need one: I already have a database with a linearizable lock manager, and the seat row is the lock. Reaching for Redlock when a WHERE status='AVAILABLE' clause would do is adding an unsafe distributed lock on top of a safe local one. That's strictly worse on correctness, availability and complexity." Volunteering this before you're asked — pre-empting the trap rather than falling into it — is one of the strongest moves available anywhere in this course.
When would you use a distributed lock? Be fair to it, because a candidate who can only criticise looks dogmatic. If the inventory did not live in one transactional database — say the seat state is sharded across a system with no cross-shard transactions, or it lives in a third-party system with no conditional write — then you have no atomic primitive to lean on and you need coordination from outside: an etcd/ZooKeeper lock (which is consensus-backed, unlike Redis) plus a fencing token the resource itself enforces. The rule from 05 holds: use a lock only when the resource can't enforce the invariant itself. Here, it can. So don't.

7b. Deep dive: holds, TTLs & the reservation lifecycle

Deep dive 1 solved claiming a seat. Now the awkward part: a human being has to type in a card number, and humans are slow. You are stuck between two unacceptable options:

  • Hold the seat forever while they fumble for their wallet → someone opens 500 tabs, holds every seat in the stadium, and buys none of them. Your inventory is now zero and your revenue is also zero. This is not hypothetical; it is a trivially easy denial-of-inventory attack and scalpers do it.
  • Release the seat instantly → the seat gets taken from under a paying customer mid-checkout. Their card is charged and they have no seat, or the payment fails at the last step. Terrible, and it's the exact failure that makes people hate ticket sites.

So: a hold with a TTL. Typically 10 minutes — that's the countdown timer you've seen on every ticket site you've ever used, and now you know it isn't a UX flourish, it's a distributed systems lease rendered as a clock. It's the same idea as a lock TTL in 05: you get exclusive access, but only for a bounded time, so that your crash (or your abandonment) doesn't deadlock everyone else forever. The countdown is a lease made visible to a human.

How does the hold actually expire? Three options, and one is quietly the best.

This is a beautiful little interview question in its own right, because the obvious answer (a cron job) is the worst one, and the elegant answer is nearly free. An interviewer who asks "so how does the seat come back?" is checking whether you reach for a background job reflexively.

Option A — A background sweeper job

-- Every 30 seconds, a scheduled job (see 12 — distributed scheduler):
UPDATE seats
   SET status = 'AVAILABLE', held_by = NULL, hold_expires_at = NULL
 WHERE status = 'HELD'
   AND hold_expires_at < now();

Simple, and it has the significant virtue that the seat map becomes correct on its own — an expired seat actually reads as AVAILABLE, so the cached map shows it and users can see it come back. But: it's another moving part, it needs leader election or a lock so two instances don't both run it (12), and it introduces a window of wrongness — for up to 30 seconds a seat is expired-but-still-marked-HELD, and therefore unbuyable by anyone. In the last minutes of a sold-out on-sale, 30 seconds of unbuyable inventory is real money.

Option B — Lazy expiry at read/claim time ★

Don't clean up. Just don't believe the status column on its own. Make the predicate tell the truth:

-- The claim, upgraded. An EXPIRED hold is, by definition, available.
UPDATE seats
   SET status = 'HELD', held_by = :userId,
       hold_expires_at = now() + INTERVAL '10 minutes',
       version = version + 1
 WHERE id = :seatId
   AND ( status = 'AVAILABLE'
         OR (status = 'HELD' AND hold_expires_at < now()) );   -- ← ★

-- Still ONE atomic statement. Still zero-or-one rows. Still no lock.
-- No job. No scheduler. No leader election. No window of wrongness.
-- Expiry is now a PROPERTY OF THE READ, not an EVENT that must be processed.

This is genuinely elegant and it is the honest right answer for a great many systems. The expiry isn't a thing that happens — it's a thing that is true, evaluated at the only moment anyone cares: when someone tries to claim the seat. There is no window in which an expired seat is unbuyable, because the very act of trying to buy it is what recognises the expiry. And it composes perfectly with deep dive 1: it's the same single atomic UPDATE, with a slightly richer predicate. It costs you nothing.

Say this, because it generalises far beyond seats: "I'd do expiry lazily, in the WHERE clause of the claim: status='AVAILABLE' OR (status='HELD' AND hold_expires_at < now()). An expired hold is an available seat — I don't need a job to make that true, I just need to stop pretending the status column is the whole truth. That removes a scheduled job, a leader election, and a window where an expired seat is unbuyable, and it's still a single atomic statement. I'd rather derive state at read time than maintain it with a background process, wherever the predicate is cheap enough — and here it's an index lookup." Then immediately volunteer the catch, because there is one and the interviewer is about to find it.
The catch with pure lazy expiry: the seat map lies. If nothing ever writes status='AVAILABLE' back to the row, then the seats table still says HELD, so your cached seat map still renders it as unavailable — and nobody clicks a greyed-out seat, so nobody ever triggers the lazy expiry that would free it. The seat is claimable-but-invisible. The seat becomes a ghost. This is a real bug and it's exactly the kind of second-order thinking that distinguishes a strong candidate.

The fix, and it's the answer you want: use both. Lazy expiry in the claim predicate is your correctness mechanism — it guarantees no seat is ever permanently lost, even if the job dies. A sweeper is your visibility mechanism — it exists so the seat map is accurate, not so the system is correct. That split matters: if the sweeper is down for an hour, the site is still correct, it just shows fewer seats than it has. That's a degraded mode, not an outage. Designing so that your background job's failure is a cosmetic problem rather than a correctness problem is precisely the instinct being graded — and the seat-map rebuild job (deep dive 3) can do the sweep itself, so it isn't even a new component.

Option C — Redis key expiry

Store the hold as a Redis key with a 600-second TTL and let Redis evict it. Attractive, and it's what a lot of blog posts suggest — but it means your inventory's source of truth is now split across two systems with no transaction between them. If Postgres commits the hold and the Redis SETEX fails, the seat is held forever. If Redis evicts and Postgres doesn't know, you have inconsistency in the direction that oversells. You'd need a reconciliation loop to fix the drift, and now you've built more machinery than option B, to be less correct. Redis expiry is great for caches and rate limits (09), and a poor choice for inventory truth. Keep the truth in one place.

The hold → booking transition, and its genuinely nasty failure modes

Converting a hold into a booking means touching two systems — your database and a payment provider — with no transaction spanning them. That is a saga (05, 13), and it has the failure modes every saga has. Name them; do not let the interviewer find them for you. 

  1. re-validate hold ──▶ 2. charge card ──▶ 3. write booking ──▶ 4. issue ticket
     (fast, local)          (SLOW, remote,      (fast, local,        (async)
                             third party)        constrained)
                                  │                    │
                                  │                    └── ✗ FAILS?
                                  │                        → money taken, no seat
                                  └── ✗ FAILS?                COMPENSATE: refund.
                                      → no money, seat still
                                        held → release it

Failure mode 1: payment succeeds, the booking write fails

You charged the customer £120 and your database fell over before recording that they own a seat. This is the worst outcome in the system — the customer has paid and has nothing, and unlike an oversell, they'll find out immediately.

The answer is a saga with a compensating transaction, and the compensation is a refund. Concretely: before calling Stripe you write a row recording your intent to charge (a PENDING booking or an outbox row); if the confirming write fails, a reconciliation job finds charges with no confirmed booking and refunds them. This is exactly the machinery from 13 — the outbox, the idempotency key, the reconciliation loop against a provider you don't control. Don't re-derive it here, name it and move on: "That's a saga; the compensation is a refund, and I'd drive it from the outbox and reconciliation loop I'd have built for payments anyway." Thirty seconds, full credit, and you've bought yourself time for the next one, which is worse.

Failure mode 2: the hold expires during payment ★

This is the genuinely nasty one and it is worth naming unprompted, because it's the edge case interviewers keep in their back pocket. The sequence: 

  t=0:00   User holds seat 14C.       Hold expires at t=10:00.
  t=9:58   User clicks "Pay".         Hold is still valid ✓ — you check, it's fine.
  t=9:59   You call Stripe. It's slow — a 3D-Secure challenge, a bank redirect.
  t=10:00  ⏰ HOLD EXPIRES. Another user's claim hits the lazy predicate
           and legitimately takes seat 14C. It is now HELD by someone else.
  t=10:04  Stripe returns SUCCESS. You have the customer's money.
           You do not have a seat to give them.

Both users behaved correctly. Your system behaved correctly at every individual step. And you have taken money for a seat that belongs to someone else. Every check you did was valid at the time you did it, and the world moved underneath you — which is, of course, the TOCTOU race from deep dive 1 wearing a different hat, stretched across seconds instead of microseconds.

You must have an answer. There are three, and a strong candidate names the trade-off between them:

  1. Re-validate at capture time and compensate if you lost. After the charge returns, atomically re-assert that the hold is still yours: UPDATE seats SET status='BOOKED' WHERE id=? AND held_by=? AND status='HELD' AND hold_expires_at > now(). If it affects 0 rows, you lost the seat — refund immediately, apologise loudly, and offer them the nearest equivalent. The conditional UPDATE saves you again, and note it's the same primitive doing the same job. Ugly for the user, but correct, and correct is the constraint.
  2. Two-phase the payment: authorise, then capture. Authorise the card before the hold gets close to expiry (an auth is fast and reversible), then capture only after you've locked in the seat. A voided auth is far cheaper and less upsetting than a refund — the customer often never sees it. This is what mature systems actually do, and mentioning auth/capture is a strong signal that you've worked near real payments.
  3. Extend the hold when payment begins. When POST /bookings arrives, push hold_expires_at out by a few minutes — the user has demonstrably committed; they're not a bot squatting inventory. Cheap, effective, and it shrinks the window rather than closing it. Combine with #1: shrink the window, and still handle the case where it happens anyway.
The framing that makes this land: "The uncomfortable truth is that I cannot make this window zero, because I have a slow external call and a lease that has to expire. So I do three things: I shrink the window by extending the hold when payment starts, I cheapen the failure by authorising rather than capturing, and I re-validate atomically at capture so that if I lost the race anyway I detect it and compensate with a refund rather than silently overselling. I'd much rather refund one unlucky customer than seat two people in the same chair." That last sentence is the whole page in one line — a bounded, visible, compensable failure beats an unbounded, invisible, uncompensable one — and it's the sentence I'd want you to walk out of this tutorial with.

7c. Deep dive: the flash sale & the thundering herd

10 million people. 10:00:00. Sixty seconds. Your correctness problem is now solved — the conditional UPDATE will never oversell no matter how much load you throw at it. But a correct system that is on fire is still on fire. Postgres will not oversell; Postgres will simply stop responding, and every one of those 10M people will see a spinner and then a 502.

Here's the reframe that makes this tractable, and it goes straight back to your estimation: you do not need to serve 10 million people. You need to serve 50,000 people — you just don't know which 50,000 yet. Everyone else is going to be told "sold out" regardless of how good your engineering is. So the design goal is not "absorb 10M concurrent checkouts." It is: absorb 10M arrivals, admit them to the write path at a rate the write path can handle, and tell everyone else the truth, politely, without falling over.

The virtual waiting room ★

This is what Ticketmaster genuinely does, and it is the single most important structural idea in this section. When the on-sale opens, you do not let 10M people into the purchase flow. You put them in a queue and admit them at a controlled rate. 

  10:00:00 — 10,000,000 arrive
       │
       ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  VIRTUAL WAITING ROOM  (at the edge — CDN worker / gateway)  │
  │                                                              │
  │  • On arrival, get a signed token + a queue position         │
  │  • Page shows: "You are 847,203 of 10,412,388"               │
  │              "Estimated wait: 24 minutes"                    │
  │  • This page is STATIC + a polling endpoint. It costs        │
  │    you almost nothing to serve. No DB. No inventory.         │
  └───────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────┘
                              │  admit ~1,000 users/sec
                              │  (a rate YOU choose, based on
                              │   what the write path can absorb)
                              ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  PURCHASE FLOW  — seat map, holds, checkout                  │
  │  Sees a steady, BOUNDED ~1,000/sec. Never sees the herd.     │
  │  Has a short-lived admission token; without it → back to     │
  │  the queue.                                                  │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Mechanically it's simple, which is part of its beauty. Arrivals get a signed token containing a monotonic sequence number (a Redis INCR, which is atomic and does ~100k+/sec quite happily — and note this is exactly the general-admission counter problem, so it's easy). A "now serving" watermark advances at your chosen rate. Your token is valid for the purchase flow when your number is below the watermark. Everything else — the position page, the polling — is static or edge-cached and costs you nothing.

The properties are exactly what you need:

  • It converts an unbounded thundering herd into a bounded, steady stream. The write path — which you proved in estimation has a hard ceiling of 50,000 units of work — now sees a load you chose, not one the internet chose for you.
  • The admission rate is a dial. Database struggling? Turn it down. Seats moving slowly? Turn it up. You have a control knob on the single most dangerous flow in the system, and you can turn it during an incident.
  • It's fair, and fairness is a product requirement here. First-come-first-served by arrival time beats "whoever has the fastest connection and the most aggressive retry loop", which is what an unmanaged free-for-all rewards — i.e. scalpers.
  • It's honest. "You are 847,203rd for 50,000 seats" is a brutal message, but it is true, and it's infinitely better than a spinner that eventually 502s. It also lets a large fraction of users self-select out, which sheds load for free.
The observation that makes an interviewer sit up: "The waiting room is load-shedding turned into a product feature. Under this kind of load I have to shed traffic — there's no configuration of servers that serves 10M concurrent checkouts against 50k seats, and buying that capacity would be absurd for something used twice a year. My options are to shed it badly, with timeouts and 502s and a site that's down for everyone including people who just wanted to browse, or to shed it well: put people in an ordered queue, tell them where they stand, and admit them at a rate my write path can absorb. Same load-shedding, but it's fair, it's honest, and the user understands what's happening. The queue isn't a workaround for the flash sale — it is the design." That reframing — an infrastructure necessity presented as a deliberate product decision — is a distinctly senior move, and it's the sort of thing that gets remembered in the debrief.

Protecting the read path: cache hard, accept lies

Estimation said 500k seat-map reads/sec and a naive 50 GB/sec of bandwidth. That never touches your origin. Three moves:

  • Cache the seat map at the CDN edge with a short TTL (~5s) and stale-while-revalidate. One origin fetch per edge PoP per 5 seconds, instead of half a million per second. The entire flash sale's read load is served by machines you don't own.
  • Pre-bake the map; never build it per request. A single background job reads the 50k seat rows from Postgres every few seconds, serialises the blob, and pushes it to Redis/the CDN. One reader of the database, serving 10 million people. Do not build the map per-request even on a cache miss — that's the stampede (06), and a cache stampede against a 50,000-row scan is how you kill the primary you were trying to protect.
  • Compress and delta. Send the full map once; then send diffs ("these 400 seats changed"). At 5-second intervals during an on-sale the delta is a fraction of the size. Awareness-level; mention it, don't dwell.

All of which means: the seat map is stale, and stale means it lies. A seat rendered as green may have been taken 4 seconds ago. Users will click seats that are already gone — in fact, during the first seconds of a hot on-sale, most clicks will land on already-taken seats. That's not a bug to fix. It is a property of the system, and it's the correct trade: you accepted staleness on the read path in your non-functional requirements precisely so that you could survive this.

Here's where your frontend background is worth actual money, so use it: "The seat map is a hint, not a contract. It's cached at the edge and it will be seconds stale, which means users will absolutely click seats that are already gone — and during the first ten seconds of an on-sale, most of them will. So the UI has to treat 'sorry, that seat just went' as a normal, expected outcome rather than an error state: keep the user on the seat map, grey out the seat, suggest three nearby ones, and don't lose their place. This is exactly optimistic UI plus an authoritative write — the client renders the optimistic view instantly, the server is the only thing that can say yes, and the client reconciles when it's told no. The truth lives in the write path. The read path is allowed to be wrong, and designing the UI around that is what makes the aggressive caching affordable." That connects a distributed-systems consistency decision to a UX decision, out loud, and very few backend-track candidates can do it. It is your differentiator in this round — spend it.

Rate limiting and the bot problem

Your real adversary is not 10M excited fans. It is a few thousand scalper bots that are faster than any human, better at retrying, and economically motivated to take every seat. Ignoring this is a missed opportunity, because it's a genuinely interesting product-and-security dimension that most candidates never mention.

  • Rate limit per user and per IP (09 — token bucket at the gateway). A human clicks a seat every few seconds; a bot fires 500 requests/sec. That's a trivially detectable difference and it's your cheapest defence.
  • Cap concurrent holds per user. One or two seats-worth. This alone kills the "hold every seat in the stadium" denial-of-inventory attack outright, and it costs one query.
  • Make the waiting room require identity. Verified account, payment method on file, phone verification. Raising the cost per queue slot is far more effective than trying to detect bots after the fact — you're changing the economics rather than playing whack-a-mole.
  • CAPTCHA / proof-of-work at admission, not at browse. Never tax the read path with this; tax the point of scarcity.
  • Rate-limit by behaviour, not just volume: a client that requests exactly one seat 30ms after each map refresh is not a person. Awareness-level, but naming it shows you've thought past the obvious.
The honest framing to offer: "You cannot win this with engineering alone — a sufficiently motivated adversary with residential proxies and real identities will get through, and Ticketmaster's actual answer has been partly technical and partly commercial: verified fans, identity-bound tickets, and controlled resale. The engineering goal isn't to eliminate bots, it's to make them expensive enough that they can't take the whole inventory." Being realistic about the limits of a technical fix, and knowing where the real solution is commercial rather than architectural, is a mature thing to say — and it's true.

8. Scaling & bottlenecks

What breaks first: the single hot event

Here is the bottleneck, and it is beautiful in a horrible way. All the contention in this system is on one event's seats. Not spread across a million events — concentrated on the one that went on sale thirty seconds ago. Every other event in your database is idle.

So the reflex answer — "I'd shard by event_id" — is worth examining, because it sounds right and it does not help you at all: 

  Shard by event_id:

    Shard 1 ── events 1..1000     ← idle
    Shard 2 ── events 1001..2000  ← idle
    Shard 3 ── events 2001..3000  ← 🔥 THE TAYLOR SWIFT ON-SALE 🔥
    Shard 4 ── events 3001..4000  ← idle

  100% of the load is on ONE shard. By definition. Because the load IS one event.

  Adding shards 5..100 does nothing. You have not divided the load;
  you have divided the IDLE.

This is the hot-partition / celebrity-key problem from 04, in its purest and most inescapable form. In the news feed (10) the celebrity problem is bad but at least you can hybridise. Here it's worse: you cannot split the work, because the work is definitionally about one key. Sharding distributes independent load, and this load is not independent — it's 10 million people contending for one event's 50,000 rows.

Could you shard within the event? By seat range or section — seats 1–10k on shard A, 10k–20k on shard B. This actually does work, because each seat is independent of every other seat, and no transaction ever spans two seats (until group bookings, which is exactly why they're hard — see follow-ups). It's a legitimate answer and worth offering. But be honest about it: it adds cross-shard complexity to a write path that, by your own estimation, only needs to do 170 writes/sec. You'd be sharding to solve a throughput problem you don't have.

The trap here is that "shard by event_id" is the reflexively correct-sounding answer, and saying it unprompted is a tell. Instead: "Sharding by event_id is the obvious move and I don't think it helps, because the load is on one event by definition — I'd be partitioning the idle, not the hot. This is the hot-partition problem in its purest form: the key is the workload. What actually saves me is different, and it's two things. First, the write ceiling: I can only ever sell 50,000 tickets, so the hot shard's total work is bounded and small — a single Postgres primary handles a couple of hundred writes a second without noticing. Second, the queue: it guarantees that bounded work arrives at a bounded rate. I don't scale the hot partition out; I bound what arrives at it." That is the correct and non-obvious answer, and it demonstrates you understand why sharding works when it works — which is a much better signal than knowing that it exists.

10. Follow-ups you should expect

"What if it's general admission — no assigned seats?"

The problem collapses. Now it's a counter, and a counter can be decremented atomically: 

-- The ENTIRE general-admission booking system:
UPDATE events
   SET tickets_remaining = tickets_remaining - 1
 WHERE id = :eventId
   AND tickets_remaining > 0;      -- ← can't go below zero. Ever.

-- 1 row → you got a ticket.   0 rows → sold out.  That's it. That's the system.
-- Even better: Redis DECR is atomic and does 100k+/sec, and here the
-- correctness bar is low enough that you can genuinely front the DB with it.

One atomic decrement, one row, no seat map, no per-seat contention, and it can live in Redis because the invariant ("≥ 0") is enforceable by the primitive itself. You can also shard the counter — give each of 10 shards 5,000 tickets and let them run independently, which removes even the single-row contention (the classic technique, and the same one from 09). The cost is a little slop at the boundary: one shard sells out while another has stock. Trivially fixable by rebalancing.

Land the point rather than just answering: "General admission is a dramatically easier problem, and it's worth saying why — it's the assigned-seat requirement that made this hard. With GA, inventory is a single integer, and an atomic decrement with a > 0 guard is a complete and correct solution — I could even shard the counter to remove the last of the contention. Everything difficult in my design — the seat map, the per-seat holds, the map staleness, the contention on individual rows — exists only because seat 14C is a specific chair that exactly one person can sit in. The identity of the resource is what creates the difficulty." That's the archetype stated in its most general form, and it's exactly the transferable lesson: fungible inventory is a counter; identified inventory is a contention problem.

"What about group bookings — 4 adjacent seats, all or nothing?"

Now you genuinely need a transaction across multiple rows, and this is the one follow-up that legitimately makes the design harder. Single-row atomicity was doing all your work; it can't cover four rows. 

BEGIN;
  -- Claim all four in ONE statement. Atomic, all-or-nothing.
  UPDATE seats
     SET status = 'HELD', held_by = :userId,
         hold_expires_at = now() + INTERVAL '10 minutes'
   WHERE id IN (:s1, :s2, :s3, :s4)
     AND (status = 'AVAILABLE'
          OR (status = 'HELD' AND hold_expires_at < now()));

  -- ★ THE CHECK: I asked for 4. Did I get 4?
  -- If affected_rows != 4, someone took one of them → ROLLBACK.
  -- Partial success is NOT success. All or nothing.
COMMIT;   -- or ROLLBACK if affected_rows < 4

The affected-row count generalises beautifully: ask for N, check you got N, roll back otherwise. But two real complications appear, and naming them unprompted is worth a lot:

  • Deadlocks. Two group bookings overlapping on seats {5,6} and {6,5} can each hold one and wait for the other. Fix: always lock rows in a deterministic order — sort the seat IDs ascending before the statement. This is a classic and the interviewer is often specifically listening for it. (A single UPDATE … WHERE id IN (...) in Postgres will generally lock in a consistent order anyway, but say the rule, because the rule is the knowledge.)
  • Contention gets much worse. A 4-seat group needs 4 rows to simultaneously be free; under a 200:1 flash sale, most attempts fail, and now each failure wastes 4 rows' worth of work and a rollback. Retry logic needs to pick a different block of 4, not retry the same one. In practice, a real system pre-computes contiguous blocks and hands them out, rather than letting users assemble arbitrary sets.

"How do cancellations and returns work?"

A cancellation returns a seat to inventory: flip status back to AVAILABLE, mark the booking CANCELLED, refund via the payment saga (13). Three things to say:

  • Never delete the booking row. Cancellation is a state transition, not a deletion. You need the audit trail — and at a fintech loop, saying the word "audit trail" unprompted is worth real points. The UNIQUE (event_id, seat_id) constraint then needs to be a partial unique index so a cancelled booking doesn't block the seat's resale: CREATE UNIQUE INDEX … ON bookings (event_id, seat_id) WHERE status = 'CONFIRMED'. That's a genuinely nice detail — the constraint must know about the lifecycle.
  • Returned seats re-enter a sold-out event, which creates a mini flash sale of its own. This is why people sit refreshing sold-out events. A waitlist with a notification (12) is a better product than a refresh war, and it converts a thundering herd into a queue — the same move as the waiting room, which is worth pointing out.
  • Refund before returning the seat, or after? After. Return the seat first (it's cheap and reversible); the refund is the slow, external, saga-driven part. Never make inventory wait on a payment provider — that's the same lesson as deep dive 1, showing up again.

"What if the payment provider is slow?"

This is a favourite because it probes whether your design is actually decoupled. The answers, in order:

  • Nothing in your database is waiting on it — no lock, no transaction, no connection. That's the design, and it's why you can survive a slow provider at all. Lead with this.
  • Circuit breaker + timeout (06). If Stripe's p99 goes to 30 seconds, trip the breaker and fail fast rather than piling up requests.
  • Extend holds when the provider degrades. If payments are taking 30s, a 10-minute hold is suddenly much tighter for everyone. Widening the TTL under provider degradation is a lovely adaptive touch and almost nobody says it.
  • Auth now, capture later. Get a fast authorisation, confirm the seat, capture asynchronously. The customer gets their confirmation immediately; the money settles in the background.
  • Worst case, queue the payment and confirm the booking as PENDING — but be careful, because you've now promised a seat you haven't been paid for. Acceptable if your auth succeeded; not otherwise. Naming that boundary is the point of the answer.

"Dynamic pricing?"

Awareness-level; it's rarely the meat of the round, so don't sink time into it unless pushed. Price becomes a function of remaining inventory and demand velocity, computed by a separate service, cached on the seat map. The one thing worth saying: the price must be locked at hold time and stored on the hold, or the price changes between the seat map and the checkout page and you've built a system that lies to customers about what they're paying — which, at a fintech loop, is not merely a UX bug, it's a regulatory one. "I'd snapshot the price onto the hold. The quoted price is part of the offer." One sentence, full marks, move on.

"Multi-region?"

Say no, and say why, because the reasoning is the answer. Inventory must have a single writer. A seat cannot be simultaneously owned by a US primary and an EU primary without consensus on every claim — and cross-region consensus adds 100ms+ to every write, which you'd be paying on the hottest path in the system. So: reads global (CDN), writes to one region. A user in Sydney takes a 200ms round-trip to claim a seat, and that is entirely acceptable — it's a hold, not a video call. If you truly needed regional write locality, you'd have to partition the inventory by region (this block of seats is sold by the EU region only), which is really just "shard the inventory and accept that a region can sell out while another has stock." Airlines actually do this. "I'd take a 200ms write latency over a consensus protocol on my hot path" is the right instinct and the right sentence.

Interview Q&A

1. Two users click seat 14C at the same millisecond. Walk me through exactly how only one gets it.

The only question that matters. Answer in three beats.

What breaks: the naive version reads the seat, sees AVAILABLE, and writes BOOKED. Two threads both read AVAILABLE before either writes — a TOCTOU race, the classic lost update. And wrapping it in a transaction does not fix it: Postgres defaults to Read Committed, under which both transactions happily read AVAILABLE.

What works: make the check part of the write. UPDATE seats SET status='HELD', version=version+1 WHERE id=14 AND status='AVAILABLE', then check the affected-row count. 1 = I won; 0 = someone beat me, return 409. A single predicated UPDATE is atomic inside the engine — the second writer blocks for microseconds, then re-evaluates the WHERE clause against the newly committed row, sees HELD, and updates zero rows. There's no window between check and use because there is no separate check.

The backstop: UNIQUE (event_id, seat_id) on bookings. Even if my application logic has a bug, the database will physically refuse to record the second booking. If that constraint ever fires, it pages me, because it means my primary mechanism is broken.

2. Why not just use SELECT … FOR UPDATE? It's correct, isn't it?

Yes, it is correct — say that first. Dismissing it as "wrong" is a worse answer than proposing it, and the interviewer is checking whether you know the difference between a correctness objection and a throughput objection.

I'm rejecting it for throughput. It serialises every contender on a hot seat, and — much more dangerously — it tempts you into holding a row lock and a pooled DB connection across a 3-second call to Stripe. Do the arithmetic: a 100-connection pool, 3 seconds per booking, gives you ~33 bookings/sec, and 100,000 requests/sec arriving. The pool is exhausted in about a millisecond, every request then blocks waiting for a connection, health checks can't get one either, and the load balancer pulls every node out of rotation. The whole site goes down — including browsing, which didn't even need the database.

The one-liner: "The lock isn't the problem. The duration of the lock is the problem." And for a 200-seat cinema with 30 concurrent users? FOR UPDATE is completely fine. Use it and go home.

3. Would you use a distributed lock in Redis to protect the seat?

No — and this is the trap in the question. Three reasons, in order:

It isn't safe for correctness. A GC pause longer than the TTL means two clients hold the lock. Clock skew expires it early. Redis replication is asynchronous, so a routine primary failover can hand the same lock to two clients with nobody doing anything wrong. Redlock is contested — Kleppmann's critique is that it still depends on timing assumptions and issues no fencing token. I would be protecting a hard correctness invariant with a mechanism that is explicitly unsafe for correctness invariants.

I already have a correct lock. The seat row is the lock — a row with a status, a holder and an expiry is exactly the shape of a lock key, except it lives in a database with a linearizable lock manager and a durable WAL. Adding Redis layers a weaker lock on top of a stronger one I'm using anyway. It cannot make the system more correct; it can only add a failure mode and a dependency on the hot path.

When would I? If inventory lived outside a transactional store with no conditional write — then I'd need coordination from outside: etcd or ZooKeeper (consensus-backed, unlike Redis) plus a fencing token the resource itself enforces. The rule: use a lock only when the resource can't enforce the invariant itself. Here, it can.

4. How do seat holds expire? Don't say "a cron job."

Lazily, in the WHERE clause of the claim — and that's the elegant answer:

WHERE status='AVAILABLE' OR (status='HELD' AND hold_expires_at < now())

An expired hold is an available seat. Expiry isn't an event that must be processed — it's a property that's true, evaluated at the only moment anyone cares: when someone tries to claim the seat. No scheduler, no leader election, no window in which an expired seat is unbuyable, and it's still the same single atomic statement.

The catch, which you should volunteer: if nothing writes AVAILABLE back to the row, the cached seat map still shows the seat as taken — so nobody clicks it, so nobody triggers the lazy expiry. The seat becomes a ghost: claimable but invisible.

So: use both. Lazy expiry is the correctness mechanism (no seat is ever permanently lost, even if every background job dies). A sweeper is the visibility mechanism (the map is accurate). If the sweeper dies, the system is still correct — it just displays fewer seats than it has. That's a cosmetic degradation, not an outage, and designing so that a background job's failure is cosmetic is the actual signal here.

5. The hold expires while the user is on the payment page and Stripe is slow. Now what?

The nastiest edge case in the system, and naming it before you're asked is worth a lot. At t=9:58 the user clicks Pay; the hold is valid, you check, it's fine. Stripe takes 6 seconds (3D-Secure). At t=10:00 the hold expires and another user legitimately takes the seat. At t=10:04 Stripe returns SUCCESS. You have their money and no seat. Every check was valid when you made it, and the world moved underneath you — it's the same TOCTOU race, stretched across seconds instead of microseconds.

I can't make the window zero — a slow external call and an expiring lease guarantee it exists. So three moves:

Shrink it: extend hold_expires_at when payment begins. The user has demonstrably committed; they're not a bot squatting inventory.

Cheapen the failure: authorise rather than capture. A voided auth is far less upsetting (and often invisible) compared with a refund.

Re-validate atomically at capture: UPDATE seats SET status='BOOKED' WHERE id=? AND held_by=? AND status='HELD' AND hold_expires_at > now(). Zero rows → I lost the race → refund immediately and apologise. The conditional UPDATE saves me again — same primitive, same job.

The framing: "I'd much rather refund one unlucky customer than seat two people in the same chair." A bounded, visible, compensable failure beats an unbounded, invisible, uncompensable one.

6. 10 million people arrive at 10:00:00 for 50,000 seats. What do you do?

Start with the reframe: I don't need to serve 10 million people. I need to serve 50,000 — I just don't know which 50,000 yet. The other 9.95 million are getting "sold out" no matter how good my engineering is. My job is to make sure they get it quickly and politely, not by taking the database down on their way out.

A virtual waiting room. Everyone gets a signed token with a monotonic sequence number (a Redis INCR — atomic, 100k+/sec). A "now serving" watermark advances at a rate I choose — say 1,000/sec. Below the watermark, your token admits you to the purchase flow. Above it, you see "you are 847,203 of 10,412,388," which is a static page plus a polling endpoint and costs me almost nothing.

This converts an unbounded thundering herd into a bounded, steady stream that my bounded write path can absorb. Crucially, the admission rate is a dial I can turn during an incident.

And the senior framing: the waiting room is load-shedding made into a product feature. I have to shed traffic — there is no server configuration that serves 10M concurrent checkouts against 50k seats. My choice is to shed it badly (timeouts, 502s, the whole site down) or shed it well (an ordered, fair, honest queue). The queue isn't a workaround for the flash sale. It is the design.

7. Shard by event_id?

It doesn't help, and saying it unprompted is a tell. All the contention is on one event by definition — the one that went on sale thirty seconds ago. Sharding by event_id puts 100% of the load on one shard and distributes the idle across the others. This is the hot-partition problem in its purest form: the key IS the workload. Sharding divides independent load, and this load is not independent.

What actually saves me is two things. First, the write ceiling: I can only ever sell 50,000 tickets, so the hot shard's total work is bounded and small — a single Postgres primary does ~170 writes/sec without noticing. Second, the queue: it guarantees that bounded work arrives at a bounded rate. I don't scale the hot partition out; I bound what arrives at it.

Could I shard within the event, by seat range? Yes — seats are independent, so it works. But I'd be adding cross-shard complexity to solve a throughput problem I don't have.

8. Is overselling ever acceptable? Why is that your first question?

Because the answer forks the entire architecture, and asking it is the strongest opening move available.

Airlines oversell deliberately. They know a percentage won't show, so they sell 105% and run a compensation auction at the gate. If overselling is acceptable, I don't need strict serialisation at all — I can take writes optimistically, run the whole thing eventually consistent, reconcile asynchronously, and let a compensation workflow clean up. Radically simpler and faster.

A concert does not. Seat 14C is a physical chair; two valid tickets for it is an incident, not a trade-off. Overselling by one is as bad as overselling by a thousand. That means a hard invariant, enforced in the database, with no wiggle room.

The thesis it sets up: "never oversell is a hard correctness constraint, not a soft target. I'm willing to be slow, and I'm willing to make people queue. I'm not willing to be wrong." Every subsequent decision on the page follows from that sentence — which is why it's worth ninety seconds up front.

9. Your seat map is cached and stale. Users will click seats that are already gone. Isn't that a bug?

No — it's a deliberate trade, and it's what makes the read path survivable. 500k reads/sec at ~100KB is 50 GB/sec naive; that must be served from the CDN edge, and edge caching means staleness. I accepted that in my non-functional requirements precisely so I could afford this.

The seat map is a hint, not a contract. The truth lives in the write path. During the first seconds of a hot on-sale, most clicks will land on already-taken seats — that's not a failure, it's the expected behaviour at 200:1 contention.

Which makes it a UI problem: "sorry, that seat just went" must be a normal, expected outcome rather than an error state. Keep the user on the map, grey the seat out, suggest three nearby ones, don't lose their place. That's optimistic UI plus an authoritative write: the client renders optimistically, the server is the only thing that can say yes, and the client reconciles when told no. And it's why the 409 response is a high-volume, first-class path in my API — my monitoring must not page on it and my rate limiter must not treat it as abuse.

10. Postgres or Cassandra?

Postgres, and it isn't close. The defining requirement of this system is a hard correctness invariant under concurrent writes. ACID transactions, conditional updates and unique constraints are the best tools ever built for exactly that.

Choosing Cassandra here means re-implementing ACID badly in application code, and the interviewer will make me prove it can't oversell — and it can, because eventual consistency and last-write-wins are precisely the semantics that lose one of two concurrent claims silently. (Lightweight transactions with Paxos exist, and they're slow and still not what I want.)

And the scale argument evaporates on contact with the estimation: the write ceiling is ~170/sec, because I cannot sell more than 50,000 tickets. There is no throughput case for NoSQL here — only a case against it, because I'd be trading away the exact guarantee I need for throughput I will never use. Storage is a few hundred GB a year. One Postgres primary. Boring and correct.

11. How would you handle a group booking — 4 adjacent seats, all or nothing?

Single-row atomicity was doing all my work; now I need a real multi-row transaction. The pattern generalises cleanly: ask for N, check you got N.

UPDATE seats SET status='HELD' … WHERE id IN (s1,s2,s3,s4) AND (status='AVAILABLE' OR expired) — then check the affected-row count. If it isn't 4, roll back. Partial success is not success.

Two complications worth naming unprompted. Deadlocks: two overlapping group bookings can each hold one row and wait for the other — always claim rows in a deterministic order (sort the seat IDs ascending). That rule is what the interviewer is listening for. Contention gets much worse: a 4-seat group needs 4 rows simultaneously free, so under a flash sale most attempts fail, and the retry must pick a different block, not the same one. Real systems pre-compute contiguous blocks and hand them out rather than letting users assemble arbitrary sets.

12. What's the transferable lesson? Where else is this the same problem?

Any finite resource that many people try to claim at once. Strip the word "seat" out and the shape is: a finite resource, many claimants, exactly one winner per unit, and being wrong is unacceptable.

Flight seats. Hotel rooms. Flash-sale stock. Ad impressions in an auction. Parking spots. Warehouse inventory in an order system. Domain-name registration. Limited-edition drops. And a distributed lock itself — a lock is an inventory of exactly one, which is why everything that makes locks hard shows up here too.

The mechanism is always the same three-layer answer: (1) make the check part of the write — a single atomic conditional update, never read-then-write; (2) push the invariant into the database as a constraint, so no application bug can violate it; (3) bound what arrives at the contended resource, because you cannot scale a hot key out.

And the meta-lesson, which is the one to carry into every other design: identified inventory is a contention problem; fungible inventory is a counter. The moment the resource has an identity — seat 14C, not "a ticket" — you're here, and you need this page.

What to remember

The one-sentence version

Make the check part of the write — a single atomic conditional UPDATE whose affected-row count tells you if you won — back it with a unique constraint the database cannot be talked out of, and bound what arrives at the contended resource with a queue, because you cannot scale a hot key out.

The archetype

  • Inventory under contention. A finite resource, many claimants, one winner per unit, and being wrong is unacceptable. Concert seats, flight seats, hotel rooms, flash-sale stock, ad impressions, parking spots, warehouse inventory — and a distributed lock, which is an inventory of exactly one.
  • Identified inventory is a contention problem; fungible inventory is a counter. Seat 14C is hard. "A ticket" is an atomic decrement. The identity of the resource is what creates the difficulty, and noticing that out loud is the transferable insight.
  • It looks like CRUD and it is concurrency. Say so in the first two minutes.

The mechanics — know these cold

  • Read-then-write is broken. TOCTOU / lost update. And a transaction does not fix it — Read Committed lets both transactions read AVAILABLE. This is the most common wrong answer in the question.
  • SELECT … FOR UPDATE is correct and doesn't scale. Say both halves. It dies because it holds a row lock and a pooled connection across a 3-second payment call; your pool is gone in a millisecond and the whole site follows. The lock isn't the problem — the duration is.
  • The conditional UPDATE is the answer. UPDATE seats SET status='HELD' WHERE id=? AND status='AVAILABLE' → check the affected-row count. 1 = won, 0 = 409. No window between check and use because there is no separate check.
  • The unique constraint is the backstop. UNIQUE (event_id, seat_id). Application checks are advisory; constraints are enforced. And if it ever fires, page someone — it's a canary for a bug in your own logic.
  • Redis locks are the trap. Unsafe for correctness (pauses, clock skew, async-replication failover, no fencing token) — and unnecessary, because the seat row IS the lock, in a database that already does this correctly and for free.
  • Lazy expiry beats a cron job. OR (status='HELD' AND hold_expires_at < now()). An expired hold is an available seat. Add a sweeper for visibility, not for correctness — so that its death is cosmetic.
  • Nothing slow happens inside the critical section. The claim is one sub-millisecond statement; payment is a separate request. Every design that fails this question fails by holding something across the payment.

The numbers

  • The write path has a hard ceiling. 50,000 seats means at most 50,000 successful writes — ~170/sec — no matter how much demand arrives. 10M users and 100M users produce the same number of writes. This asymmetry is the most useful fact in the system, and it's what lets you use one boring Postgres.
  • The read path is unbounded. ~500k/sec, ~50 GB/sec naive → CDN edge, a pre-baked blob, one background reader of the database serving 10 million people.
  • Contention is 200:1. >99% of write attempts must fail, so failing must be cheap — which is precisely why optimistic beats pessimistic here.
  • Ignore storage. A few hundred GB/year. Say so in one sentence and move on; knowing which number to ignore is the senior move in estimation.
