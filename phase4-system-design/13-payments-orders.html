13 — Design a Payment / Order System

The archetype where a lost update isn't a bug — it's a financial loss. Ledgers, idempotency, sagas, and the discipline of being right.

Why this matters in the interview

This is the most important page in the phase for the companies you are targeting. Optum, JPMC, Goldman — these are not companies that will ask you to shard Twitter. They will ask you to move money, or to move a claim, or to move an order, and what they are really testing is whether you can be trusted with a system where being wrong costs something. Every other case study in this course has a forgiving failure mode. Drop a tweet from a timeline and nobody files a regulatory report. Serve a stale view count and the world continues. Here, the same class of mistake — a lost update, a double-applied write, a dropped event — is a customer charged twice, a merchant paid twice, a ledger that no longer balances, and in the worst case a conversation with a regulator. This is also the design that sits directly on top of your Phase 1 capstone: the Spring Boot + Postgres order-management service you already built is literally this system, simplified. You are not learning a new domain here. You are learning what that domain looks like when it has to be correct under failure.

So here is the thesis, and it is the whole page: in every other system design, correctness bows to availability and scale. Here it is the other way around. You design for auditability and correctness first, and only then, grudgingly, for scale. The candidate who opens with "first let's estimate QPS so we know how to shard" has already lost this round — not because estimation is wrong, but because it signals that they've pattern-matched a payments question onto a feed question. Payments are low volume. A thousand transactions per second is a very large payments business. Scale is not the hard part and saying so is itself a scoring move. The hard part is that money is conserved, history is immutable, retries are guaranteed, third parties are unreliable, and you must be able to prove — months later, to someone who is not an engineer — exactly what happened and why. Get that framing out in the first ninety seconds and the rest of the interview is you being right in public.

The opening line for this round, and you should have it memorised: "Before I draw anything: this is a correctness-first system, not a scale-first system. The volumes here are modest — payments are orders of magnitude lower-volume than reads — so scale won't drive my design. What will drive it is that every state change must be idempotent, auditable, and reconstructible, because a bug here isn't a bad user experience, it's a financial loss. So I'd rather be briefly unavailable than briefly wrong." That last sentence inverts the instinct that every other system design round trains into you, and it is the single best thing you can say in a fintech loop.

1. The prompt

The interviewer says, and this is genuinely all they say:

"Design the payment and order system for an e-commerce site. A customer should be able to place an order and pay for it."

Two sentences, and almost every load-bearing decision is unstated. Are you Stripe, or are you a shop using Stripe? Does "pay" mean you touch a card number (in which case you have just walked into a PCI-DSS compliance conversation) or does it mean you hand off to a provider? Is an order one item or a basket from three different merchants? Refunds? Partial refunds? Chargebacks? Multiple currencies?

The vagueness is deliberate and it is the first thing being graded. A mid-level candidate starts drawing boxes: a load balancer, an order service, a database. A senior candidate spends the next four minutes making the problem smaller and sharper, and — this is the part people miss — says out loud what they are cutting and why, so the interviewer knows the omission is a choice rather than a gap.

Notice what this prompt is not. It doesn't mention scale, DAU, or latency. In a feed or a shortener prompt, the interviewer usually hands you a number ("100M users") because scale is the puzzle. Here they don't, because it isn't. If you find yourself inventing a scale figure so you can do the estimation ritual, stop — you're answering a different question than the one you were asked.

2. Requirements

The clarifying questions to ask first

Ask these in this order. The first one restructures the entire design, so it goes first and you do not draw anything until you have the answer.

QuestionWhy it mattersThe answer to assume
"Are we the merchant, or are we the payment processor?" Completely different systems. The processor (Stripe, Adyen) speaks to card networks, holds PCI Level 1 scope, does fraud scoring and settlement. The merchant orchestrates an order and delegates the money movement. If you design the processor by accident you will spend 45 minutes in the wrong system. The merchant, integrating with a third-party PSP (payment service provider). That's the realistic scope, it's what almost every company actually builds, and it's what you have shipped.
"Do we ever see a raw card number?" Determines whether PCI-DSS scope lands on you. If a PAN (primary account number) touches your servers, your entire infrastructure is in audit scope. No. The card is collected by the PSP's iframe/SDK in the browser; we receive a token. Say the word "tokenization" here.
"Single currency, or multi?" Multi-currency doubles the ledger's complexity — you need FX rates, and a rate is a point-in-time fact you must store, not re-derive. Single currency (USD) for the core design; I'll say how I'd extend it in the follow-ups.
"Refunds — full only, or partial?" Partial refunds mean a payment has many refunds, so refunded_amount ≤ captured_amount becomes an invariant you must enforce under concurrency. Partial refunds are in scope. Ask for them — it's a better design and it shows you know the domain.
"Chargebacks and disputes?" A chargeback is money reversed by the bank, weeks later, without asking you. It's an inbound event you cannot refuse. Out of scope for the core flow, but I'll model the disputed state and cover it in follow-ups.
"Digital goods or physical?" Physical means authorize-now/capture-at-shipment (and legally, in many jurisdictions, you may not capture before you ship). Digital means capture immediately. Physical — it forces the authorize/capture split, which is the more interesting and more commonly probed design.

Functional requirements

  • Place an order. A customer submits a basket; we create an order, reserve inventory, and take payment.
  • Authorize a payment at checkout (hold the funds on the card) and capture it later (actually take the money), at shipment.
  • Refund a payment, in full or in part, possibly several times against one payment.
  • Handle failure at every step — declined card, out-of-stock item, PSP timeout, our own crash mid-flow. "Handle failure" is a functional requirement here, not an operational afterthought.
  • Query order and payment status — for the customer, for support, and for finance.
  • Produce an auditable history of every state change, forever.

Explicitly out of scope (say this): the cart/basket service, the product catalogue, shipping-carrier integration, fraud modelling internals, and tax calculation. Each is a real system; none is what's being tested.

Non-functional requirements — this is where the round is won

RequirementWhat it actually means
Correctness > availability ★ It is better to be down than to be wrong. If the ledger cannot be written consistently, refuse the payment. A customer who sees "please try again in a minute" is annoyed; a customer charged twice is a support ticket, a refund, a chargeback fee, and possibly a compliance incident. This inverts the usual instinct — in a feed you'd serve something stale rather than nothing — and stating the inversion deliberately is the highest-value sentence in this round.
Auditability Every state change is reconstructible. Not "we log it" — the log is not a source of truth. The database itself is append-only for financial facts: you can replay the entries and re-derive any balance at any past instant. If finance asks "what was this account's balance on March 3rd at noon", you must be able to answer with a query, not a guess.
Idempotency everywhere Every mutating endpoint, every queue consumer, every call out to the PSP. Retries are not an edge case — they are the steady state (05: "duplicates are the weather"). A system where a retry can double-charge is not a payment system; it is a liability.
Durability Once we tell the customer "paid", that fact survives anything. Synchronous replication for the payment DB, not async. Losing 30 seconds of writes on failover is acceptable for a chat app and unacceptable here.
Regulatory / PCI-DSS Never store raw card numbers. Collect via the PSP's hosted field, store the returned token (pm_1Ab2…), and your PCI scope collapses from "everything" to "almost nothing" (SAQ-A). Financial records are retained for years (7 is a common figure) and never deleted.
Latency Checkout should feel fast, but a payment taking 2 seconds is fine — users expect a spinner at checkout. Do not trade correctness for 100ms here. Meanwhile order reads should be fast, and they can be, because they're not on the money path.
Availability Target high (99.9%+) — being down costs revenue — but it is explicitly the second priority, and you should say so rather than reciting "five nines" out of habit.
The most common self-inflicted wound in this round: reaching for the standard playbook. "We'll denormalize for read speed, go eventually consistent, and use a NoSQL store so we can scale writes." Every one of those is a defensible instinct in a feed design and every one of them is wrong here, because each trades correctness for throughput you do not need. If you catch yourself saying "eventually consistent" about a balance, stop and restart the sentence.
Frame the CAP decision explicitly, because they're waiting for it: "On the money path I'm choosing CP over AP. Under a partition, the payment service refuses writes rather than accepting them into a state it can't reconcile. On the read path — order history, receipts — I'm happy to be AP and serve slightly stale data from a replica, because a receipt that's five seconds behind harms nobody. So this isn't one system with one consistency model; it's two paths with two." Splitting the CAP answer by path rather than by system is a 7-YOE move (03).

3. Estimation

Do the arithmetic — but do it to make a point, and announce the point before you do the sums, so the interviewer knows you're not just performing the ritual.

Assume a large e-commerce business: 100M registered users, 10M daily active, 1M orders per day.

ORDERS
  1,000,000 orders/day ÷ 86,400 s  ≈  12 orders/sec average
  Peak (Black Friday, ~10× a normal peak, and peaks are ~3× average):
      12 × 3 × 10  ≈  360 orders/sec  →  round up hard: call it 1,000 TPS

PAYMENT OPERATIONS per order
  1 authorize + 1 capture + ~0.1 refunds + ~2 webhook callbacks  ≈  4 ops
  → ~4,000 payment-related writes/sec at peak.  Still tiny.

READS (order status, order history, receipts, the "where's my stuff" page)
  Every customer checks their order 5–10× before it arrives, and support
  looks too:
      ~50M reads/day  ≈  600 reads/sec avg,  ~5,000/sec peak
  → READ:WRITE ≈ 50:1 on orders … but ONLY on the read path.

STORAGE  (this is the number that actually matters)
  Ledger entries: ~6 entries per order (see section 7) × ~200 bytes
      1M orders/day × 6 × 200B  ≈  1.2 GB/day  ≈  440 GB/year
  Orders + payments + events + outbox + idempotency keys: call it 3× that.
      ≈ 1.5 TB/year, growing MONOTONICALLY — we never delete.

Now say the thing the numbers are there to license you to say:

A thousand transactions per second is a workload a single well-tuned Postgres instance handles comfortably. Scale is not the hard part of this system. Correctness is. So I'm not going to shard for throughput — I'm going to spend my design budget on making every write idempotent, every state change auditable, and every failure recoverable.

That is a genuinely disarming thing to say, because it is true and because most candidates cannot bring themselves to say it. Visa's global peak is on the order of tens of thousands of transactions per second. The entire card-payments volume of planet Earth would fit, throughput-wise, into a handful of database servers. What makes payments hard has never been the QPS.

The number that does drive the design: storage that only grows

Every other case study has a retention story — expire the cache, roll up the metrics, delete after 90 days. Here you have the opposite: the ledger is append-only and retained essentially forever, because a regulator or an auditor can ask about a transaction from six years ago and "we deleted it" is not an available answer. So:

  • You never UPDATE and you never DELETE a ledger entry. A correction is a new, compensating entry, exactly like a saga compensation (05). The mistake stays visible forever, and that is the feature, not the bug.
  • Growth is linear and predictable (~1.5 TB/year), which is easy — but it means you need a partitioning/archival story from day one. Partition the ledger by month; keep hot months on fast storage; move cold partitions to cheap storage but keep them queryable. Postgres declarative partitioning does this without an extra system.
  • Cost is real but boring. Terabytes are cheap. Wrong numbers are expensive.
Close the estimation section with this: "The read:write ratio here is about 50:1, which normally would push me toward read replicas and aggressive caching — and I will do exactly that, but only on the order-history path. I'm deliberately not letting the read volume influence the write path, because the write path's constraint isn't throughput, it's correctness. Those are two different systems wearing one name, and I'll design them separately."
Don't cache balances. It will feel natural — you'll have just finished saying "50:1 read ratio, so cache" — and it is the exact trap. A cached balance is a stale balance, and a stale balance used in a "do they have enough funds?" check is a lost update with a dollar sign attached. Cache order summaries and receipts (immutable facts, safe to cache forever). Never cache a number you're about to make a decision with.

  • paymentMethodToken — the browser talked to Stripe directly and got back an opaque token. Our servers never saw a PAN, so we are not in PCI scope for card data. Say "tokenization keeps us at SAQ-A" and watch a fintech interviewer relax.

Design decisions in that surface, defended

  • POST /refunds, not DELETE /payments/{id}. This is the most important line in the API and it encodes the entire philosophy of the page. A refund is not a deletion and not an undo — it is a new, forward transaction that moves money in the opposite direction. The original charge still happened, still appears on the customer's statement, and you probably still paid the processing fee on it. Modelling a refund as DELETE is not just an aesthetic mistake; it's a statement that you think history can be rewritten, and in this system it cannot.
  • Capture is a separate call from authorize. It has to be, because they happen days apart.
  • Refund returns PENDING, not DONE. The PSP takes days to actually move the money back. Anything that lies about being synchronous when it isn't will eventually be caught by reconciliation, and then you get to explain why your API said "refunded" and the bank disagrees.
  • Cursor pagination on history, not OFFSET — offsets drift when rows are inserted, and here that means a customer's order silently vanishing from page 2.
  • Everything mutating is a POST with an idempotency key. PUT is idempotent by HTTP spec, but "charge this card" isn't a resource replacement, so don't contort it. Use POST and carry the key.
Worth 10 seconds of interview time: Stripe holds idempotency keys for 24 hours and returns the original response, including the original error, on a replay. Both halves matter — replaying a stored 402 card_declined is just as important as replaying a success, because otherwise a retry of a declined charge could get lucky, charge the customer, and confuse everyone. Say "I'd replay the original response, success or failure."

5. Data model

The SQL vs NoSQL decision, made explicitly

Make this call early, loudly, and with a reason — because the interviewer is watching to see whether you'll reflexively reach for a NoSQL store because that's what "scalable" designs use.

Relational. Postgres. Because I need multi-row ACID transactions, real foreign keys, unique constraints as a concurrency primitive, CHECK constraints to enforce financial invariants, and SERIALIZABLE when I need it. Those are not conveniences here — they are the mechanism by which the system is correct.

The full argument is in §10, but plant the flag now. Every one of those features is doing load-bearing work in the schema below.

Orders

CREATE TABLE orders (
    id              UUID        PRIMARY KEY,
    customer_id     UUID        NOT NULL,
    status          TEXT        NOT NULL,        -- see the state machine, §9
    total_cents     BIGINT      NOT NULL CHECK (total_cents > 0),
    currency        CHAR(3)    NOT NULL,        -- ISO 4217. Always store it.
    version         INT         NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,  -- optimistic locking (@Version)
    created_at      TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
    updated_at      TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);
CREATE INDEX idx_orders_customer ON orders (customer_id, created_at DESC);

CREATE TABLE order_items (
    order_id        UUID   NOT NULL REFERENCES orders(id),
    sku             TEXT   NOT NULL,
    qty             INT    NOT NULL CHECK (qty > 0),
    unit_price_cents BIGINT NOT NULL,   -- the price AT THE TIME OF ORDER.
    PRIMARY KEY (order_id, sku)              -- NEVER join to the live catalogue price.
);

-- Every status change, forever. The order row tells you WHERE it is;
-- this table tells you HOW IT GOT THERE. Auditability is a table, not a log file.
CREATE TABLE order_status_history (
    id          BIGSERIAL   PRIMARY KEY,
    order_id    UUID        NOT NULL REFERENCES orders(id),
    from_status TEXT,
    to_status   TEXT        NOT NULL,
    reason      TEXT,
    actor       TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- 'saga', 'webhook:psp', 'support:u_44'
    occurred_at TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);

unit_price_cents on the order line is the kind of detail that reads as experience. The price is copied into the order, not referenced. If you join to the catalogue at read time, then a price change next Tuesday retroactively rewrites what the customer paid last week — and now your receipt disagrees with your ledger. Same instinct as the FX-rate rule in the follow-ups: a financial fact is captured at the moment it becomes true, and never re-derived.

Payments and refunds

CREATE TABLE payments (
    id                  UUID        PRIMARY KEY,
    order_id            UUID        NOT NULL REFERENCES orders(id),
    status              TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- pending|authorized|captured|…
    amount_cents        BIGINT      NOT NULL CHECK (amount_cents > 0),
    captured_cents      BIGINT      NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,
    refunded_cents      BIGINT      NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,
    currency            CHAR(3)    NOT NULL,
    psp_payment_intent  TEXT        UNIQUE,     -- Stripe's id for this charge
    payment_method_token TEXT       NOT NULL,   -- 'pm_1Ab2…'. NOT a card number.
    version             INT         NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,
    created_at          TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),

    -- The invariants, enforced BY THE DATABASE. You cannot refund more than you
    -- captured, no matter what bug your service layer has this week.
    CHECK (captured_cents <= amount_cents),
    CHECK (refunded_cents <= captured_cents),
    CHECK (refunded_cents >= 0)
);

CREATE TABLE refunds (
    id            UUID        PRIMARY KEY,
    payment_id    UUID        NOT NULL REFERENCES payments(id),
    amount_cents  BIGINT      NOT NULL CHECK (amount_cents > 0),
    status        TEXT        NOT NULL,      -- pending | succeeded | failed
    psp_refund_id TEXT        UNIQUE,
    reason        TEXT,
    created_at    TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);
Point at those CHECK constraints deliberately: "I want the refund invariant enforced in the database, not in the service layer. Service-layer checks are check-then-act — two concurrent partial refunds can both read refunded_cents = 0, both pass validation, and together over-refund. A CHECK constraint plus a row lock makes over-refunding structurally impossible, and it stays true even when someone runs a manual UPDATE from a support tool at 2am." That parenthetical about the support tool is what makes it land — it's the failure everyone has actually seen.

The ledger — the tables that make this a payment system

This is §7's subject, but the schema belongs here so the shape of the whole model is visible at once. 

-- An ACCOUNT is any place money can sit. Customers, merchants, and — crucially —
-- our own internal accounts (revenue, fees, cash-in-transit). Every movement of
-- money is BETWEEN accounts. Money never appears; it only ever moves.
CREATE TABLE accounts (
    id          UUID        PRIMARY KEY,
    type        TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- CUSTOMER | MERCHANT | REVENUE | FEES |
                                          -- PSP_CLEARING | CASH
    owner_id    UUID,                    -- null for internal accounts
    currency    CHAR(3)    NOT NULL,
    created_at  TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
    -- ⚠ NOTE WHAT IS NOT HERE: there is NO `balance` COLUMN. On purpose. §7.
);

-- A TRANSACTION is a group of entries that must balance to zero together.
CREATE TABLE ledger_transactions (
    id           UUID        PRIMARY KEY,
    type         TEXT        NOT NULL,  -- CAPTURE | REFUND | FEE | CHARGEBACK
    reference_id UUID        NOT NULL,  -- the payment/refund that caused it
    idem_key     TEXT        UNIQUE,    -- ← the same transaction can never be
    occurred_at  TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()   --   posted twice
);

-- An ENTRY is one leg of a transaction: this much money, into or out of this
-- account. Append-only. Never updated. Never deleted. Ever.
CREATE TABLE ledger_entries (
    id             BIGSERIAL   PRIMARY KEY,
    transaction_id UUID        NOT NULL REFERENCES ledger_transactions(id),
    account_id     UUID        NOT NULL REFERENCES accounts(id),
    direction      TEXT        NOT NULL CHECK (direction IN ('DEBIT','CREDIT')),
    amount_cents   BIGINT      NOT NULL CHECK (amount_cents > 0),
    currency       CHAR(3)    NOT NULL,
    created_at     TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
) PARTITION BY RANGE (created_at);   -- monthly partitions; cold ones archive

CREATE INDEX idx_entries_account ON ledger_entries (account_id, created_at);
CREATE INDEX idx_entries_txn     ON ledger_entries (transaction_id);

-- Make the append-only rule real, not a convention people forget:
REVOKE UPDATE, DELETE ON ledger_entries FROM app_user;

The supporting cast (both from 05, both non-negotiable)

CREATE TABLE idempotency_keys (
    key           TEXT        PRIMARY KEY,     -- ← THE concurrency primitive.
    endpoint      TEXT        NOT NULL,        --   Not Redis. This.
    request_hash  TEXT        NOT NULL,
    status        TEXT        NOT NULL,        -- IN_PROGRESS | COMPLETED
    response_code INT,
    response_body JSONB,
    created_at    TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()   -- reap after 24h
);

CREATE TABLE outbox (                       -- 05 §8. Same table, same reasons.
    id             BIGSERIAL   PRIMARY KEY,
    aggregate_type TEXT        NOT NULL,
    aggregate_id   TEXT        NOT NULL,
    event_type     TEXT        NOT NULL,
    event_id       UUID        NOT NULL UNIQUE,
    payload        JSONB       NOT NULL,
    published_at   TIMESTAMPTZ
);
CREATE INDEX idx_outbox_unpub ON outbox (id) WHERE published_at IS NULL;

CREATE TABLE processed_webhooks (          -- §9. The PSP will call you twice.
    psp_event_id  TEXT        PRIMARY KEY,
    received_at   TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);
Count the unique constraints in this schema: idempotency_keys.key, ledger_transactions.idem_key, outbox.event_id, processed_webhooks.psp_event_id, payments.psp_payment_intent. Five separate places where a duplicate is stopped by the database rather than by application logic. That's not paranoia — it's the design. 05's rule ("most 'we need a distributed lock' requirements turn out to be 'we need a unique constraint'") is not a slogan here; it is load-bearing in five places.

            └───────────────┘          │      JOB        │    compares to the PSP,
                    │                  └─────────────────┘    ALARMS on mismatch
                    ▼
          notification / analytics / fraud consumers

Three things about that diagram are worth narrating rather than leaving implicit:

  • The card never touches us. That single arrow from the browser straight to the PSP is what keeps our PCI scope near zero. It is also the arrow candidates forget to draw, and drawing it unprompted is a signal.
  • The Order Service is the orchestrator, not a passive CRUD service. It owns the saga's state machine. Ask "why is order 12345 stuck?" and the answer is one row in one table (05 §7b).
  • The reconciliation job has an arrow of its own. It is not an afterthought or a cron someone added later — it is a first-class component, and in a real payments org it is the thing that catches the bugs the tests didn't. Draw it in the first pass of the diagram, not when the interviewer asks.

Two subtleties in that walkthrough that are worth calling out, because the interviewer will notice if you do and notice if you don't:

  • Step 2 returns 201 immediately. The customer is not held on an HTTP connection while three services talk to each other. The order exists in PENDING_PAYMENT, and the saga proceeds asynchronously. This is what "the intermediate states are visible to users" means in practice (05 §7b) — and it's why the UI shows "processing your order" rather than pretending the world is transactional.
  • Step 4 writes no ledger entry. An authorization moves no money — it is a promise from the issuing bank that the funds are reserved. Writing a ledger entry at authorize time is a real and frequently-made mistake, and it silently inflates your revenue by the value of every authorization that is never captured. The ledger records movements of money, not intentions to move money.

      → In-flight orders are read from the replica, uncached. They change too often

That's it. The read path is a solved problem and you should spend 60 seconds on it, say "replicas and a cache on immutable terminal states," and get back to the money. Spending your deep-dive minutes on read caching in a payments interview is a misallocation the interviewer will read as a lack of judgment.

Say this as you finish the diagram, because it sets up the whole rest of the interview: "I've deliberately kept the happy path simple, because in this system the happy path isn't where the design lives. I'd like to go deep on those." You have just told the interviewer what to ask you, and you've picked the three questions you're best at.

7. Deep dive: the double-entry ledger

This is where the interview really happens, and it is the single highest-leverage thing on this page for the companies you're targeting. A candidate who can design a correct double-entry ledger on a whiteboard is, to a fintech interviewer, immediately in a different category from one who cannot — because it is not a distributed-systems trick, it is a demonstration that you understand what money is in a computer.

7a. The mistake: a mutable balance column

Here is what almost everyone draws first, and it is wrong in three independent ways:

CREATE TABLE accounts (
    id       UUID   PRIMARY KEY,
    balance  BIGINT NOT NULL     -- ← every problem in this section starts here
);

-- and then, somewhere in a service:
UPDATE accounts SET balance = balance - 4998 WHERE id = ?;
UPDATE accounts SET balance = balance + 4998 WHERE id = ?;

Why it fails, in ascending order of how much it will hurt you:

  1. You have destroyed the audit trail. The balance says $412.00. Why? There is no answer in the database. You cannot reconstruct how it got there, you cannot answer "what was this balance last Tuesday," and when finance says "this number is wrong" you have nothing to investigate with. An auditor asking "show me the history of this account" is asking a question your schema cannot answer.
  2. You have created a lost-update race. Two concurrent refunds read the balance, both compute balance + 1500, both write. One refund vanishes. Yes, balance = balance - 4998 is atomic within one statement — but the moment there's a check ("does this account have enough?") in front of it, you're back to check-then-act, and now you need SELECT … FOR UPDATE and you've serialised every operation on a hot account.
  3. Money can be created and destroyed. Nothing in that schema says the debit and the credit have to agree. Deploy a bug where one of those two UPDATEs throws after the other commits, and you have invented money. There is no constraint that will stop you, and no query that will tell you it happened.

A balance is not a fact you store. A balance is a question you ask — it is the sum of an immutable, append-only sequence of entries. Store the entries. Derive the balance.

7b. Double-entry: the 500-year-old idea that fixes it

Double-entry bookkeeping predates computers by about five centuries (Luca Pacioli codified it in 1494, and the Venetian merchants were using it before that), and it is still, unimprovedly, how every bank on earth records money. The rule is one sentence:

Every transaction writes at least two entries, which sum to zero. Money is never created and never destroyed — it is only ever moved from one account to another.

Treat a DEBIT as +amount and a CREDIT as −amount (the accounting convention for which is which per account type is a distraction; what matters is that the two directions are opposite signs and the total is zero). Then the invariant is a query, and a query is something you can assert: 

-- The invariant. If this ever returns a row, money has been created or destroyed,
-- and that is a page-someone-at-3am event, not a rounding error to paper over.
SELECT transaction_id,
       SUM(CASE direction WHEN 'DEBIT' THEN amount_cents
                            ELSE -amount_cents END) AS imbalance
  FROM ledger_entries
 GROUP BY transaction_id
HAVING SUM(CASE direction WHEN 'DEBIT' THEN amount_cents
                            ELSE -amount_cents END) <> 0;

And the balance you refused to store is now derived, from data that cannot lie: 

-- The balance of an account, right now.
SELECT SUM(CASE direction WHEN 'CREDIT' THEN amount_cents
                             ELSE -amount_cents END) AS balance_cents
  FROM ledger_entries
 WHERE account_id = ?;

-- The balance of an account AS OF ANY PAST MOMENT — the query a mutable
-- balance column simply cannot answer:
SELECT SUM(...) FROM ledger_entries
 WHERE account_id = ? AND created_at <= '2026-03-03 12:00:00Z';
The sentence that will make a fintech interviewer put their pen down and look at you: "I would not have a balance column. A balance is a derived value — the sum of an immutable, append-only sequence of ledger entries — and the moment you store it and mutate it, you've destroyed your audit trail and introduced a lost-update race for nothing. I'd store entries, derive balances, and if reading them gets slow I'd add a periodic snapshot as a cache that can always be rebuilt from the entries, never as the source of truth." That last clause is the whole game: the optimization is allowed, but it must be re-derivable.

7c. A worked example: a $100 purchase with a $3 processing fee

Do this on the whiteboard. It takes ninety seconds and it proves more than ten minutes of talking. The customer pays $100 for goods; the PSP keeps $3; the merchant nets $97.

The accounts involved:

  • customer_receivable — what the customer owes / has paid us
  • psp_clearing — money the PSP is holding on our behalf, not yet in our bank
  • revenue — our income from the sale
  • processing_fees — an expense account

Transaction 1 — the capture ($100 moves from the customer to us, via the PSP):

txn_id = txn_A   type = CAPTURE   reference = pay_9K2

  account              direction   amount     signed
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  psp_clearing         DEBIT       10000      +10000     money arrives with the PSP
  revenue              CREDIT      10000      -10000     we recognise the sale
                                            ──────────
                                    SUM  =        0   ✓

Transaction 2 — the PSP's fee ($3 of what they hold is theirs, not ours):

txn_id = txn_B   type = FEE   reference = pay_9K2

  account              direction   amount     signed
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  processing_fees      DEBIT         300       +300     an expense we incurred
  psp_clearing         CREDIT        300       -300     the PSP keeps it
                                            ──────────
                                    SUM  =       0    ✓

Transaction 3 — payout, two days later (the PSP wires us what's left):

txn_id = txn_C   type = PAYOUT   reference = po_31

  account              direction   amount     signed
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  bank_cash            DEBIT        9700      +9700     money lands in our bank
  psp_clearing         CREDIT       9700      -9700     the PSP no longer holds it
                                            ──────────
                                    SUM  =       0    ✓

Now derive every balance from nothing but those six entries:

  psp_clearing     = +10000 - 300 - 9700  =      0   ← settled to zero. Correct:
                                                       the PSP holds nothing of ours.
  revenue          = -10000               = -10000   ← we earned $100 (credit balance)
  processing_fees  =   +300               =   +300   ← we spent $3
  bank_cash        =  +9700               =  +9700   ← we have $97 in the bank

  Sanity:  9700 (cash) + 300 (fees) - 10000 (revenue)  =  0   ✓
           The books balance. They always balance, or something is broken.

Look at what you get for free from this. The $3 fee is not a mystery. It is not "revenue minus a bit." There is a row that says exactly where it went, when, and which payment caused it. When finance asks "why did we net $97 on a $100 sale," the answer is a SELECT. And when psp_clearing does not settle to zero, you have found a bug — before the auditor did.

Notice that the fee is a separate transaction, not two extra legs bolted onto the capture. That's a modelling choice and you should be able to defend it: the fee is a distinct economic event that the PSP tells you about at a different time (often in the settlement file, days later, and sometimes at a different amount than you predicted). Modelling it separately means the capture transaction is written when the capture happens, and the fee transaction is written when you learn the fee — neither has to wait for the other, and neither is ever revised.

7d. Money is never a float

Never, ever, represent money as a float or a double. IEEE-754 binary floating point cannot exactly represent 0.1, any more than decimal can exactly represent 1/3. In Java, 0.1 + 0.2 == 0.30000000000000004, and System.out.println(1.03 - 0.42) prints 0.6100000000000001. Run that in a loop over a million ledger entries and your books do not balance — by an amount too small to notice and too large to ignore, in a system whose entire purpose is that the books balance. Use integer minor units (cents) as a long/BIGINT, or an exact decimal type (java.math.BigDecimal, Postgres NUMERIC). Never double. Never float. Never REAL. A rounding error in a ledger is a career-defining bug, and this is a real, commonly-asked interview gotcha — some interviewers will slip a double amount into a schema they show you purely to see whether you catch it.
RepresentationVerdictWhy
long amountCents (integer minor units) Preferred. Exact, fast, trivially serialisable, no library needed. A long holds ±92 quadrillion cents — enough for any conceivable amount. This is what Stripe's API does: "amount": 4998.
BigDecimal / NUMERIC(19,4) Also correct. Exact decimal arithmetic with explicit scale and rounding mode. Necessary when you need sub-cent precision — FX conversion, interest accrual, per-unit pricing. Slower, and you must set the RoundingMode explicitly or it throws.
double / float / REAL Disqualifying. Binary floating point. Cannot represent decimal fractions exactly. Errors accumulate. There is no scenario in which this is the right choice for money, and using it in an interview is a red flag the interviewer will remember.

And a corollary that trips people: minor units are not always 100. The Japanese yen has zero decimal places (¥100 is 100, not 10000); the Kuwaiti dinar has three. If you go multi-currency you cannot hardcode "divide by 100" — you look up the currency's exponent (ISO 4217 defines it). Mentioning that unprompted is a nice, cheap signal that you've worked with real money.

7e. Writing a transaction: the Java

The whole ledger write is one method and one Postgres transaction. Note that it does not read a balance, does not take a lock, and cannot half-succeed. 

@Service
public class LedgerService {

    /**
     * Post a balanced transaction. The ONLY way entries enter the ledger.
     * Idempotent on idemKey: posting the same transaction twice is a no-op.
     */
    @Transactional
    public UUID post(String idemKey, LedgerTxnType type,
                     UUID referenceId, List<Entry> entries) {

        // 1. The entries MUST balance. Assert it in code as well as in the DB —
        //    fail fast, before anything is written, with a message a human can act on.
        long sum = entries.stream()
            .mapToLong(e -> e.direction() == Direction.DEBIT
                           ? e.amountCents() : -e.amountCents())
            .sum();
        if (sum != 0) {
            throw new UnbalancedTransactionException(
                "entries sum to " + sum + ", must be 0 — refusing to post");
        }
        if (entries.size() < 2) {
            throw new UnbalancedTransactionException("need at least two legs");
        }

        // 2. Claim the transaction's idempotency key. The UNIQUE constraint on
        //    ledger_transactions.idem_key is what makes double-posting impossible —
        //    not a lock, not a check-then-act. (05 §9.)
        UUID txnId = UUID.randomUUID();
        int claimed = jdbc.update("""
            INSERT INTO ledger_transactions (id, type, reference_id, idem_key)
            VALUES (?, ?, ?, ?)
            ON CONFLICT (idem_key) DO NOTHING
            """, txnId, type.name(), referenceId, idemKey);

        if (claimed == 0) {
            // Already posted. Return the EXISTING transaction id. This is not an
            // error — it is a retry doing exactly what a retry should do: nothing.
            return jdbc.queryForObject(
                "SELECT id FROM ledger_transactions WHERE idem_key = ?",
                UUID.class, idemKey);
        }

        // 3. Append the entries. INSERT only. There is no code path in this
        //    service that UPDATEs or DELETEs a ledger entry, and the DB grant
        //    (REVOKE UPDATE, DELETE) means there never can be.
        for (Entry e : entries) {
            jdbc.update("""
                INSERT INTO ledger_entries
                    (transaction_id, account_id, direction, amount_cents, currency)
                VALUES (?, ?, ?, ?, ?)
                """, txnId, e.accountId(), e.direction().name(),
                     e.amountCents(), e.currency());
        }
        return txnId;
    }
}

And the caller, for the $100-with-$3-fee capture above:

@Transactional   // SAME transaction as the payments-table update. Both or neither.
public void recordCapture(Payment p, long feeCents) {

    payments.markCaptured(p.id(), p.amountCents());          // UPDATE payments

    ledger.post("capture:" + p.id(), LedgerTxnType.CAPTURE, p.id(), List.of(
        Entry.debit (accounts.pspClearing(), p.amountCents(), p.currency()),
        Entry.credit(accounts.revenue(),     p.amountCents(), p.currency())
    ));

    ledger.post("fee:" + p.id(), LedgerTxnType.FEE, p.id(), List.of(
        Entry.debit (accounts.processingFees(), feeCents, p.currency()),
        Entry.credit(accounts.pspClearing(),   feeCents, p.currency())
    ));

    outbox.emit("Payment", p.id(), "PaymentCaptured", PaymentCaptured.from(p));
}
// Note the idempotency keys: "capture:{paymentId}" and "fee:{paymentId}" are
// DERIVED, not random. Call this method five times and you get exactly one
// capture transaction and one fee transaction, every time. That is the point.
The deterministic-key trick deserves a sentence of its own, because it's subtle and it's the kind of thing interviewers reward: "Where I can, I derive the idempotency key from the business entity rather than generating a random one — capture:{paymentId}. A random UUID makes a retry idempotent only if the caller remembered to reuse it. A derived key makes the operation idempotent by construction, even if the caller is a buggy retry loop or a duplicate Kafka message that has no memory of the first attempt."

7f. Refunds prove the model

Now refund $15 of that $100 order. Watch what does not happen: nothing is updated, nothing is deleted, and the original capture transaction is untouched forever. 

txn_id = txn_D   type = REFUND   reference = re_77

  account              direction   amount     signed
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  revenue              DEBIT        1500      +1500     we give back the revenue
  psp_clearing         CREDIT       1500      -1500     the PSP sends it to the customer
                                            ──────────
                                    SUM  =       0    ✓

  revenue balance now:   -10000 + 1500  =  -8500   ← we earned $85 net. Derived.
  The $100 sale is STILL THERE. The $15 refund is STILL THERE. Both are true.

This is the same idea as a saga compensation (05 §7b) and it is not a coincidence — it is the same idea. You do not undo a financial event. You post an opposing one. The history is the truth, and the balance is just what the history adds up to.

7g. Reconciliation — the job that catches what your tests didn't

Your ledger says the PSP owes you $9,700. Does the PSP agree? You must check, and you must check continuously, and you must alarm — loudly — when the answer is no. 

  NIGHTLY (and, for high-value accounts, hourly):

  1. Pull the PSP's settlement report for the period
        (Stripe: the Balance Transaction API / the settlement file)

  2. Re-derive OUR view from the ledger — from the ENTRIES, never from a
     cached balance, because the whole point is to catch a wrong cached balance:
        SELECT SUM(...) FROM ledger_entries
         WHERE account_id = psp_clearing AND created_at < :cutoff

  3. Compare, line by line, on the PSP's transaction id:
        ┌────────────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────┬─────────────┐
        │ psp txn            │ ours (cents) │ theirs       │ status      │
        ├────────────────────┼──────────────┼──────────────┼─────────────┤
        │ pi_3Ab…            │        10000 │        10000 │ ✓ match     │
        │ pi_3Cd…            │         4998 │            — │ ✗ MISSING   │  ← we think
        │                    │              │              │             │    we captured;
        │                    │              │              │             │    they don't
        │ pi_3Ef…            │            — │         2500 │ ✗ ORPHAN    │  ← they charged;
        │                    │              │              │             │    we have no
        │                    │              │              │             │    record. WORST
        │                    │              │              │             │    case.
        │ pi_3Gh…            │         1999 │         1996 │ ✗ AMOUNT    │  ← a 3¢ gap.
        └────────────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────┴─────────────┘

  4. EVERY non-match writes a break record and pages someone. Nothing is
     auto-corrected. Nothing is rounded away.

The three break types map to three different bugs, and being able to name them is exactly the kind of practical detail that says "I have done this":

  • MISSING (we have it, they don't) — usually a capture we think succeeded but whose PSP call actually failed, or a webhook we processed for an event that was later reversed. Investigate; often the fix is to re-query the PSP for the authoritative state.
  • ORPHAN (they have it, we don't) — the worst one. The customer's card was charged and we have no record of it. This is money we took and cannot account for, and it is the shape of the failure that ends up in a newspaper. Usually caused by a PSP call that timed out, succeeded on their side, and was never reconciled because someone trusted the timeout.
  • AMOUNT — a fee that differed from what we predicted, an FX rate applied differently, or a genuine float bug (§7d). A 3-cent gap is not "close enough." Either you know why it's 3 cents, or your ledger is wrong and the size of the error is a coincidence.
The line, and deliver it flatly, because its flatness is the point: "Reconciliation isn't a nice-to-have — it's the control that tells me my system is correct. Every night I re-derive our balances from the ledger entries and compare them, line by line, against the provider's settlement report. A mismatch raises a break that a human investigates. I never auto-correct a break and I never write off a discrepancy as a rounding error, because a system that quietly absorbs small differences is a system that will quietly absorb a large one." Every person who has worked in payments has watched someone add a tolerance threshold to make an alarm stop firing, and every one of them has watched what happened next.
The optimization you will be tempted into, and the safe version of it: summing millions of entries to get a balance does get slow. The fix is snapshots — a materialised account_balance_snapshot (account_id, as_of, balance_cents, last_entry_id) row written periodically, so a current balance is snapshot + SUM(entries after last_entry_id). That is fine, and it is what real ledgers do. What makes it fine is the property you must state out loud: the snapshot is a cache, not a source of truth, and it is fully rebuildable from the entries at any time. The moment someone treats the snapshot as authoritative and stops being able to rebuild it, you are back to a mutable balance column with extra steps.

8. Deep dive: the distributed transaction

Placing an order touches inventory, payment, shipping and notification. Even if you have not drawn microservice boundaries, those are four separate concerns with four separate failure modes, and one of them is a third party you do not control. That last fact settles the design before you start arguing about it.

8a. Why 2PC is off the table — say this in one sentence and move on

2PC (05 §7a) gives you real atomicity by having a coordinator drive every participant through prepare-then-commit. It is not a bad protocol. It is simply unavailable to you, for a reason that has nothing to do with its blocking failure mode:

Stripe will not join your XA transaction. There is no prepare() on a third-party payment API. You cannot ask a card network to hold a lock while your inventory service makes up its mind.

That is the whole argument and it takes ten seconds. You can add the blocking critique — a coordinator crash after prepare leaves every participant holding locks indefinitely, which under load is a cascading outage rather than a stalled order — but lead with the structural reason, because it is unanswerable. And then acknowledge where 2PC does survive, because you're interviewing at banks and they run it: "Inside one datacenter, with XA datasources under Spring's JtaTransactionManager and modest volume, 2PC is real and I'd expect to meet it in a core-banking context. It's the cross-vendor, over-the-internet version that's impossible." That sentence tells a JPMC interviewer you're not going to sneer at their stack.

8b. The order saga, with its compensations

  STEP                  LOCAL TXN                        COMPENSATION
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1. Create order       INSERT orders                    UPDATE orders
                        status = PENDING_PAYMENT           SET status='CANCELLED'
                        + INSERT outbox                    + reason

  2. Reserve            UPDATE stock SET reserved += q   UPDATE stock
     inventory           WHERE (on_hand-reserved) >= q     SET reserved -= q
                        (0 rows ⇒ OUT_OF_STOCK)           ("release the hold")

  3. Authorize          POST psp/authorize               POST psp/void
     payment            [idem: auth:{orderId}]           (an auth VOID is free and
                        → payments.status=AUTHORIZED      leaves no trace on the
                        → NO LEDGER ENTRY (§6)            statement — cheap to undo)

  4. Confirm order      UPDATE orders                    UPDATE orders
                        SET status='CONFIRMED'             SET status='CANCELLED'
                        + INSERT outbox                    + issue refund if captured

  5. Ship               shipping label created           ⚠ CANNOT UNDO. Return label.

  6. Capture            POST psp/capture                 ⚠ CANNOT UNDO.
     payment            [idem: capture:{paymentId}]       ⇒ REFUND — a NEW forward
                        → LEDGER ENTRIES POSTED             transaction, not a rollback
                        → status = CAPTURED

  7. Notify             send email                       ⚠ CANNOT UNDO. Send an
                                                           apology email.

  Failure at step 3 (card declined):
      compensate 2, then 1, IN REVERSE ORDER  →  release the inventory hold,
      then cancel the order.  Step 3 never committed, so nothing to compensate there.

Two ordering rules fall straight out of that table, and both are worth saying because they are design decisions, not implementation details:

  • Compensate in reverse order, unwinding like a stack. Release inventory before you cancel the order, not after — the cancellation may cascade into logic that assumes the hold is already gone.
  • Put the irreversible steps last. Look at where capture, ship and notify sit: at the end. That is deliberate. Every step after an irreversible one is a step whose failure leaves a mess a human must clean up. Sequencing the saga so the cheap-to-compensate steps come first and the impossible-to-compensate steps come last is one of the highest-value things you can say in this section, and almost nobody says it.

8c. The honesty that defines this archetype: you cannot roll back a captured payment

A rollback erases history as if nothing happened. A refund does not. The charge appears on the customer's statement. The refund appears on the customer's statement. Both are true, forever. You paid the processing fee on the charge and, in many schemes, you do not get it back. A refund is a new forward transaction that moves money in the opposite direction — it is not an undo, and calling it one is the tell that someone has only read about payments.

This is not pedantry; it changes what you build. Because a refund is a real transaction:

  • It has its own idempotency key, its own row, its own PSP call, and its own ledger transaction (§7f). You cannot model it as "set the payment back to pending."
  • It can fail. The PSP can decline a refund (the card is closed, the account is frozen). So your compensation has a failure mode, which leads directly to the next subsection.
  • It is asynchronous. It returns PENDING and settles days later. Your saga must tolerate a compensation that has been initiated but not completed.
  • It is partial-able. One capture, many refunds — which is why the CHECK (refunded_cents <= captured_cents) constraint in §5 exists.

And note the design consequence you should point at in the table above: an authorization can be voided cleanly, but a capture can only be refunded. That is exactly why the saga captures at shipment rather than at checkout. Capturing late means that for the entire window in which things are most likely to go wrong — out of stock, address invalid, fraud check fails — the money hasn't actually moved, and the compensation is a free void instead of a costly, visible, fee-incurring refund. The authorize/capture split is not a card- network quirk you're working around; it is a saga design that the card networks handed you.

Say this and you have separated yourself from the field: "The crucial honesty about a saga is that you cannot roll back a captured payment — you can only refund it, and a refund is a new forward transaction, not an undo. The customer sees both entries on their statement and we've paid the processing fee on both. That's the whole reason compensation-based transactions exist as a concept rather than just being 'rollback over the network' — and it's why I'd order the saga so the irreversible steps happen last and capture at shipment rather than at checkout, so that most failures compensate with a free authorization void instead of a real refund."

8d. What happens when the compensation itself fails?

This is the question that separates people who have run a saga in production from people who have drawn one. The whiteboard version of a saga always assumes compensations succeed. They do not. The refund API call times out. The inventory service is down. The compensation's own compensation is undefined, because there isn't one.

So what actually happens? Say it plainly, because pretending otherwise is a junior tell: 

  Compensation fails
        │
        ├─▶ RETRY, with exponential backoff and jitter (06). Forever, or nearly.
        │   Compensations are IDEMPOTENT — a derived key, refund:{paymentId} —
        │   so retrying is always safe. This resolves the overwhelming majority
        │   of failures, because most of them are transient.
        │
        ├─▶ Still failing after N attempts / T hours?
        │   The saga row moves to  status = COMPENSATION_FAILED
        │   → DEAD-LETTER QUEUE (12)
        │   → a break record, exactly like a reconciliation break (§7g)
        │   → an ALERT, with the order id, the failing step, and the amount at risk
        │
        └─▶ A HUMAN LOOKS AT IT.
            An ops/finance operator, in a support console, with an audit trail of
            everything the system already tried, decides what to do — issue the
            refund manually, credit the customer, write it off, escalate.

That last box is not a failure of the design. It is part of the design. In a money system there is always a manual remediation path, because there is always a case the automation cannot resolve — and building the console, the break queue and the audit trail that make manual remediation safe is real engineering work that a real payments team spends real time on. A candidate who says "and eventually a human resolves it, and here's the queue and the tooling they'd use" is describing a system that can actually be operated. A candidate whose saga diagram has no such box is describing one that cannot.

Do not say "and then we roll back." There is no rollback. Do not say "the compensation retries until it succeeds," full stop — until it succeeds is doing enormous unearned work in that sentence, and an interviewer at a bank will immediately ask "and if it never succeeds?" Have the answer ready: bounded retries with jitter, then a dead-letter queue, then a break record, then a human with a console and an audit trail. Say the word "human" out loud. It is not a weakness in your design; it is evidence that you know what these systems look like at 3am.

8e. Orchestration, not choreography — and why

05 §7b lays out the general trade-off. Here is the decision for this system, and it is not close:

Orchestration. Because for money, you want exactly one place that knows the state of the world and can be queried.

The question you will be asked at 3amChoreographyOrchestration
"Why is order 12345 stuck?" Read five codebases, correlate traces across four services, and hope your distributed tracing was sampled in. SELECT * FROM saga_state WHERE order_id = 12345 → step=AWAITING_CAPTURE, since 4h, last_error='psp timeout'. One row.
"How many orders are stuck right now?" Unanswerable without building a whole analytics pipeline over the event bus. GROUP BY step. It's a dashboard.
"Retry the failed step for these 40 orders." Republish events by hand and pray they're idempotent. An admin endpoint that re-drives the saga from its recorded step. Safe, because every step is idempotent.
"Show the auditor the full lifecycle of this order." Reconstruct from logs. Good luck. order_status_history + saga_state + ledger_entries. It's already a table.

The cost is real and you should name it: the orchestrator is a central component that every flow depends on, and it couples to every participant. In a large org that's an organisational bottleneck — the team that owns the orchestrator becomes a dependency for everyone. Accept it. In a payment system the auditability is worth the coupling, and it is a trade you should make explicitly rather than by default.

The framing that wins this exchange: "For a two-step flow I'd choreograph — an orchestrator would be ceremony. For anything with money, I orchestrate, because the business process becomes an explicit state machine I can query, retry, and hand to an auditor. Choreography makes 'why is this order stuck' a research project; orchestration makes it a SELECT. I'll pay the coupling cost for that."

8f. The orchestrator, in Spring

The saga's state lives in a database row, not in memory. That is the entire trick — it means the orchestrator can crash, be redeployed, or be scaled to ten instances, and the saga resumes exactly where it was. 

CREATE TABLE saga_state (
    order_id     UUID        PRIMARY KEY,
    step         TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- RESERVING_INVENTORY | AUTHORIZING | …
    status       TEXT        NOT NULL,   -- RUNNING | COMPENSATING | DONE |
                                          -- COMPENSATION_FAILED  ← the human queue
    completed    JSONB       NOT NULL,   -- steps done so far → what to compensate
    attempts     INT         NOT NULL DEFAULT 0,
    last_error   TEXT,
    next_retry_at TIMESTAMPTZ,             -- backoff + jitter lives here
    updated_at   TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()
);

@Service
public class OrderSagaOrchestrator {

    // Driven by events off Kafka. Every handler is idempotent (05 §9): the
    // processed_event dedup row and the state change commit in ONE transaction.
    @KafkaListener(topics = "order-events")
    @Transactional
    public void on(SagaEvent evt) {
        if (!dedup.claim(evt.eventId())) return;   // INSERT … ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING

        SagaState s = sagas.lockForUpdate(evt.orderId());  // SELECT … FOR UPDATE:
        // one saga row, one writer. Cheap, uncontended (nobody else wants THIS order),
        // and it makes the state machine's transitions serialisable for free.

        switch (evt.type()) {
            case INVENTORY_RESERVED -> {
                s.complete(Step.RESERVE_INVENTORY);
                advance(s, Step.AUTHORIZE_PAYMENT);
            }
            case PAYMENT_AUTHORIZED -> {
                s.complete(Step.AUTHORIZE_PAYMENT);
                advance(s, Step.CONFIRM_ORDER);
            }
            case INVENTORY_FAILED, PAYMENT_DECLINED -> compensate(s, evt.reason());
            case SHIPMENT_DISPATCHED -> advance(s, Step.CAPTURE_PAYMENT);
            case PAYMENT_CAPTURED -> s.finish();
        }
    }

    private void advance(SagaState s, Step next) {
        s.setStep(next);
        sagas.save(s);
        // The command goes out via the OUTBOX — same transaction as the state
        // change, so we can never advance the saga without emitting the command,
        // and never emit a command without advancing the saga. (05 §8.)
        outbox.emit("Saga", s.orderId().toString(), next.commandName(),
                    Command.forStep(next, s));
    }

    private void compensate(SagaState s, String reason) {
        s.setStatus(Status.COMPENSATING);
        // Reverse order — unwind the stack of completed steps.
        for (Step done : s.completedStepsReversed()) {
            // Derived idempotency key: safe to emit this N times.
            outbox.emit("Saga", s.orderId().toString(),
                        done.compensationCommand(),
                        Compensation.forStep(done, s, reason));
        }
        sagas.save(s);
    }

    // The sweeper. This is what makes the saga actually reliable, and it is the
    // component candidates forget: a saga with no sweeper stalls silently forever
    // the first time a reply is lost.
    @Scheduled(fixedDelay = 30_000)
    public void sweepStuckSagas() {
        for (SagaState s : sagas.findRunningPastRetry(Instant.now())) {
            if (s.attempts() >= MAX_ATTEMPTS) {
                s.setStatus(Status.COMPENSATION_FAILED);   // → the human queue
                alerts.raise("saga stuck", s.orderId(), s.step(), s.lastError());
            } else {
                s.incrementAttempts();
                s.setNextRetryAt(backoffWithJitter(s.attempts()));  // 06
                outbox.emit("Saga", s.orderId().toString(),
                            s.step().commandName(), Command.forStep(s.step(), s));
            }
            sagas.save(s);
        }
    }
}

The sweeper is the part to point at. A saga is not driven by events; it is driven by its own state, and events merely nudge it. If a reply is lost — the inventory service replied and the message vanished — an event-driven-only orchestrator waits forever and the order silently never completes. The sweeper re-drives from the recorded step, which is safe precisely because every command is idempotent. That's the payoff for all the idempotency work: it makes blind retries free, which is what lets you build a system that self-heals instead of one that quietly loses orders.

8g. The outbox, and why it is not optional here

Every outbox.emit() above is doing specific work, and it is worth twenty seconds of explanation because it is the difference between a design that works and one that silently drops orders.

The naive version — sagas.save(s) then kafkaTemplate.send(...) — is a dual write (05 §8). Two systems, no shared transaction. Commit the state change, crash before the publish, and the saga has advanced to AUTHORIZING with no command ever sent. The order sits in that state forever. The customer's card is never charged, no email is sent, and nobody finds out until they complain — and in a payment system the mirror-image failure (publish, then the transaction rolls back) is worse: you have just commanded a card to be charged for an order that does not exist.

   │  INSERT INTO outbox (AuthorizePaymentCommand) │     Structurally.
   └───────────────────────┬──────────────────────┘
                           │ COMMIT
                           ▼
                  relay (poll, FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED)
                           │
                           ▼
                         Kafka  ──▶  Payment Service (idempotent consumer)

      → the command is published TWICE

Offer this unprompted, the moment you draw an arrow from a database to a queue: "That's a dual write, so I'd use the outbox pattern — the command goes into an outbox table in the same transaction as the saga state change, and a relay publishes it at-least-once. In a feed system a dropped event is a missing notification. Here a dropped command is an order that is never paid for, or a card that's charged for an order that doesn't exist. Same pattern, completely different blast radius — which is why in this system I'd treat it as mandatory rather than as an optimization."

9. Deep dive: idempotency & the authorize/capture lifecycle

9a. The problem, stated so that it cannot be dodged

Your client sends POST /payments. The network times out. Did the payment go through?

You cannot know from the client side. A lost request and a lost response are byte-for-byte identical from where you're standing: silence. (05 §1 — this is the root cause of the entire field.) So you have exactly two choices: retry and risk charging the customer twice, or don't retry and risk never charging them at all. Neither is acceptable. The idempotency key is the third option, and it is the only one there is.

The key doesn't prevent the duplicate — nothing can prevent the duplicate, because you can't prevent a network failure. It makes the duplicate harmless. And once retries are harmless, you can retry aggressively, which is what makes the entire saga in §8 work. 

  POST /v1/payments
  Idempotency-Key: 7c9e6679-7425-40de-944b-e07fc1f90ae7   ← generated by the CLIENT,
  { "orderId": "ord_01HX7…", "amountCents": 4998 }          ONCE per intent, stable
                                                            across every retry

  1st call  → key unseen  → charge → store (key → 201 + body)   → 201 Created
  2nd call  → key SEEN    → do NOT charge → replay stored body  → 201 Created
             identical paymentId, identical body. The client cannot tell.
             The customer is charged exactly once.

  ⚠ And if the 1st call FAILED with 402 card_declined?
     The replay returns the SAME 402. You do not get a second bite at the card.

9b. The implementation — and the race everyone misses

The naive implementation checks "have I seen this key?" and then acts. That is check-then-act, and two copies of the same request arriving 5ms apart will both check, both see nothing, and both charge the card. The race you were trying to eliminate is back, now with a table in front of it.

The fix is 05's fix and it is not a lock: make the insert the check. A primary key constraint is an atomic, durable, linearizable compare-and-swap that you already have, already operate, and already trust. 

@RestController
public class PaymentController {

    @PostMapping("/v1/payments")
    public ResponseEntity<PaymentResponse> authorize(
            @RequestHeader("Idempotency-Key") String idemKey,
            @Valid @RequestBody AuthorizeRequest req) {

        if (idemKey == null || idemKey.isBlank()) {
            // Reject it. A mutating money endpoint without an idempotency key is
            // a bug in the caller, and accepting it is a bug in you.
            throw new MissingIdempotencyKeyException();   // 400
        }
        Result r = paymentService.authorize(idemKey, req);
        return ResponseEntity.status(r.code()).body(r.body());
    }
}

@Service
public class PaymentService {

    @Transactional
    public Result authorize(String idemKey, AuthorizeRequest req) {

        String hash = sha256(canonicalJson(req));

        // ── 1. CLAIM THE KEY. This INSERT *is* the check-and-act, atomically.
        //    Exactly one concurrent caller can win this row. No Redis. No Redlock.
        //    No distributed-lock argument. The PRIMARY KEY already solved it.
        int claimed = jdbc.update("""
            INSERT INTO idempotency_keys (key, endpoint, request_hash, status)
            VALUES (?, '/v1/payments', ?, 'IN_PROGRESS')
            ON CONFLICT (key) DO NOTHING
            """, idemKey, hash);

        if (claimed == 0) {
            IdemRecord prior = idemRepo.get(idemKey);

            // Same key, DIFFERENT body → the client has a bug and is about to
            // charge someone the wrong amount. Fail loudly. Never guess.
            if (!prior.requestHash().equals(hash)) {
                throw new IdempotencyKeyReuseException();     // 422
            }
            // The original is still running. Do NOT run it again — that is the
            // double charge. Tell the client to come back.
            if (prior.status().equals("IN_PROGRESS")) {
                throw new RequestInFlightException();        // 409 + Retry-After
            }
            // COMPLETED → replay the ORIGINAL response, success or failure.
            return new Result(prior.responseCode(), prior.responseBody());
        }

        // ── 2. We won the key. Do the work — and pass an idempotency key DOWNSTREAM
        //    too. Stripe has its own Idempotency-Key header; use it, keyed off OUR
        //    key, so a retry of our PSP call is also safe. Idempotency is a CHAIN;
        //    it is only as strong as its weakest hop.
        PspResult psp = pspClient.authorize(
            req.pspIntent(), "idem-" + idemKey);       // ← the downstream key

        Payment p = payments.save(Payment.authorized(req, psp));

        // ── 3. Record the outcome IN THE SAME TRANSACTION as the business write.
        //    The key and the payment can therefore never disagree about whether
        //    this happened. That is the property the whole design rests on.
        Result res = Result.created(PaymentResponse.from(p));
        jdbc.update("""
            UPDATE idempotency_keys
               SET status = 'COMPLETED', response_code = ?, response_body = ?
             WHERE key = ?
            """, res.code(), json.write(res.body()), idemKey);

        outbox.emit("Payment", p.id().toString(), "PaymentAuthorized",
                    PaymentAuthorized.from(p));
        return res;
    }
}
The subtle failure in that code, and be ready for it as a follow-up: what if we crash after calling the PSP but before the transaction commits? The card is authorized at Stripe; our transaction rolled back; the IN_PROGRESS key row is gone too (it rolled back with everything else). A retry gets a fresh key claim and calls Stripe again — but the downstream idempotency key idem-{idemKey} is derived from the same client key, so Stripe recognises the retry and returns the original authorization instead of creating a second one. That's why passing the key downstream isn't a nicety; it's what closes the last hole. And the belt-and-braces answer if the PSP had no such header: you'd write an IN_PROGRESS "intent" row in its own committed transaction before calling out, so a crash leaves a breadcrumb the reconciler can find. Volunteering that shows you've actually thought about the crash window rather than reciting the pattern.

Authorize vs capture, explained the way you should explain it out loud:

  • Authorize asks the issuing bank to reserve the funds and reduce the customer's available credit. No money moves. It's a promise, and it expires (typically ~7 days, sometimes 30 for travel). This is why a charge shows as "pending" on your statement for a few days and then changes. Use that example — everyone has seen it, and connecting the abstraction to a thing the interviewer has personally observed on their own bank app makes it stick.
  • Capture is when the money actually moves. You capture at shipment, not at checkout — in many jurisdictions you're not permitted to take payment for physical goods before you dispatch them, and even where you are, capturing late is better engineering (§8c): a failed order compensates with a free void instead of a costly, visible refund.
  • You may capture less than you authorized (you shipped 2 of 3 items) but generally not more. So captured_cents <= amount_cents — which is exactly the CHECK constraint from §5. The state machine and the schema are the same object viewed from two angles, and saying that is a nice moment of coherence.
The transition rule to state explicitly, because it's the thing that makes the state machine enforceable rather than decorative: "Every transition is a conditional update, not a read-modify-write. UPDATE payments SET status='CAPTURED' WHERE id=? AND status='AUTHORIZED' — if it returns zero rows, someone else already moved it and I stop. That single line gives me optimistic concurrency, idempotency and state-machine validation in one statement, enforced by the database, with no lock and no race." That is 05's "let the database enforce it" theme paying off for the third time on this page, and pointing out the repetition is itself a signal that you see the pattern rather than memorising instances.

9d. The webhook problem

Here is where people who have shipped payments and people who have read about payments visibly diverge. The PSP does not just answer your calls — it calls you back, asynchronously, to tell you that a payment succeeded, a refund settled, a dispute was opened, an authorization expired. And webhooks are, without exaggeration, the least reliable part of the whole system.

What webhooks doWhat you must do about it
Arrive twice (or five times — the PSP retries when your 200 is slow or lost) Dedupe by the provider's event ID. INSERT INTO processed_webhooks (psp_event_id) ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING in the same transaction as the side effect. Zero rows inserted ⇒ already handled ⇒ return 200 and do nothing.
Arrive out of order. payment.captured can genuinely land before payment.authorized. Never write state blindly from a webhook. Use conditional transitions (WHERE status = 'AUTHORIZED') so an out-of-order event is simply a no-op, and/or carry a version/sequence and ignore anything older than what you have. Do not let a stale event drag a payment backwards through the state machine.
Never arrive. Your endpoint was down for 20 minutes; the PSP exhausted its retries; the event is gone. Poll. Always also poll. A reconciliation job (§7g) that re-queries the PSP for the state of anything in a non-terminal state older than N minutes. This is the one that matters most, and it is the one candidates omit.
Are forgeable. Your webhook endpoint is a public URL that accepts POSTs saying "this payment succeeded." Verify the signature (Stripe signs with an HMAC over the raw body + a timestamp) and reject stale timestamps to stop replay attacks. Verify against the raw bytes, before any JSON parsing — re-serialising the body changes it and the signature will never match.
Time out if you're slow. The PSP wants a 200 in a couple of seconds and will retry if it doesn't get one. Verify, dedupe, persist, 200 — then process asynchronously. Never do the business work inside the webhook handler. Write it to a queue (or your own outbox) and return immediately. 
@PostMapping("/v1/webhooks/psp")
@Transactional
public ResponseEntity<Void> onWebhook(
        @RequestHeader("Stripe-Signature") String signature,
        @RequestBody byte[] rawBody) {        // ← RAW bytes. Not a parsed DTO.

    // 1. Verify. HMAC over the raw payload + timestamp. Reject replays older
    //    than ~5 minutes. An unsigned webhook endpoint is an unauthenticated
    //    "mark this order paid" API exposed to the internet.
    PspEvent evt = pspVerifier.constructEvent(rawBody, signature);   // throws → 400

    // 2. Dedupe on the PROVIDER's event id. They WILL send it again.
    int fresh = jdbc.update(
        "INSERT INTO processed_webhooks (psp_event_id) VALUES (?) " +
        "ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING", evt.id());
    if (fresh == 0) return ResponseEntity.ok().build();   // seen it. 200. Done.

    // 3. Persist the intent to process, and RETURN. Do not call the ledger, do
    //    not call Kafka, do not do anything slow. The dedup row and the queued
    //    work commit together, so we can never ack an event we then drop.
    outbox.emit("PspEvent", evt.id(), evt.type(), evt.payload());
    return ResponseEntity.ok().build();               // fast 200. Stripe is happy.
}

// …and the actual handler, downstream, applies it with a CONDITIONAL update, so
// an out-of-order or duplicate event cannot drag the state machine backwards:
@Transactional
public void apply(PspEvent evt) {
    int rows = jdbc.update("""
        UPDATE payments SET status = 'CAPTURED', captured_cents = ?
         WHERE psp_payment_intent = ?
           AND status = 'AUTHORIZED'        -- ← the guard. Out of order? 0 rows.
        """, evt.amountCents(), evt.intentId());

    if (rows == 0) return;   // already captured, or not authorized yet. Fine.
                             // The reconciler will sort out anything genuinely wrong.
    ledger.post("capture:" + evt.intentId(), CAPTURE, ...);   // §7e
}
This is the paragraph that says "I have shipped this," and it is worth memorising almost word for word: "I'd treat webhooks as a hint, never as a source of truth. They arrive twice, they arrive out of order, and sometimes they never arrive at all. So: verify the HMAC signature against the raw body, dedupe on the provider's event ID, make the handler idempotent with conditional state transitions, return a fast 200 and do the work asynchronously — and always also poll. A reconciliation job re-queries the provider for anything stuck in a non-terminal state. If my system's correctness depends on a webhook arriving, then my system's correctness depends on someone else's retry logic, and that's not a bet I'm willing to make with money."
The specific webhook bug that has cost real companies real money: handling charge.succeeded by inserting a ledger entry, with no dedupe. Stripe retries the webhook because your 200 was slow. You post the entry twice. Your books now show twice the revenue and your psp_clearing account doesn't reconcile — and because a ledger is append-only, you cannot simply delete the bad entry. You must post a correcting entry, and explain the whole thing to finance. The dedupe row is one INSERT … ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING and it is not optional.

10. Consistency & isolation

10a. Why relational, and why "we'll use Cassandra for scale" fails here

Somebody in this interview is going to suggest a NoSQL store — sometimes the interviewer, as a deliberate test. Have the answer ready, and make it about requirements, not about taste.

What this system needsPostgresCassandra / Dynamo-style
Multi-row ACID transaction — the payment row, the ledger entries and the outbox row must commit together or not at all Native. BEGIN … COMMIT. No. Lightweight transactions are single-partition and use Paxos per operation; there is no multi-partition transaction. Your ledger entries and your payment row would commit independently — which is the dual-write problem, inside your own database.
Unique constraints — five of them (§5), each one preventing a duplicate charge Native, and enforced across concurrent writers. Only via IF NOT EXISTS LWT, single-partition, slow, and with well-documented edge cases. This is the constraint doing the most work in your design and it becomes your weakest link.
CHECK constraints — refunded_cents <= captured_cents Native. Does not exist. Every invariant moves into application code, where it is check-then-act and therefore racy.
Ad-hoc analytical queries — reconciliation, "sum every entry for this account", finance's questions SQL. Aggregations, joins, window functions. Query patterns must be designed into the partition key up front. "Sum all entries for an account across time" is a query you can't easily ask if you didn't model for it — and reconciliation asks new questions constantly.
Write throughput of 4,000/sec Trivial for one instance. Also trivial — and irrelevant, because we never needed the throughput.

Look at the last row. Cassandra's advantage is the one thing this system does not need. You would be trading away every mechanism that makes the design correct in exchange for headroom on an axis you're nowhere near saturating. That is the trade, stated plainly, and once you state it plainly nobody argues.

The full answer, and it works whether the suggestion came from the interviewer or from your own nerves: "I'd use Postgres, and I want to be specific about why, because 'scale' isn't the reason to pick a database — the invariants are. My correctness rests on multi-row transactions, five unique constraints and two check constraints. Cassandra buys me write throughput I don't need — 4,000 writes a second is nothing — and takes away the exact primitives my design is built on. Picking an eventually-consistent store for a ledger means reimplementing ACID in application code, badly, and then finding the bugs in production with real customers' money. If I ever outgrow one Postgres, I'd shard it by account and keep the transactions — not switch to a database that can't do them."

10b. Isolation levels — where READ COMMITTED quietly betrays you

Postgres defaults to READ COMMITTED, and for most applications it is fine. For balance checks it is not, and the reason is worth knowing precisely (03 covers the general theory; here is where it bites).

The dangerous pattern is a read-then-decide across rows: read a total, compare it to a limit, insert a row. Under READ COMMITTED, two transactions can both read the same total, both conclude they are within the limit, and both insert — and neither ever sees the other. Nothing is locked, because the row you would need to lock doesn't exist yet. This is a write skew, and it is the classic over-refund bug: 

  Payment pay_9K2:  captured = 10000, already refunded = 8000.
                    So at most 2000 more may be refunded.

  Txn A (refund 1500)                    Txn B (refund 1500)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  SELECT SUM(amount) FROM refunds
    WHERE payment_id = 'pay_9K2'
    → 8000                               SELECT SUM(amount) FROM refunds
                                           WHERE payment_id = 'pay_9K2'
                                           → 8000        ← SAME. Neither sees
                                                            the other's INSERT.
  8000 + 1500 = 9500 <= 10000  ✓ OK      8000 + 1500 = 9500 <= 10000  ✓ OK

  INSERT refund 1500                     INSERT refund 1500
  COMMIT                                 COMMIT

  Total refunded = 11000 on a 10000 capture.  We refunded MORE THAN WE TOOK.
  Both transactions individually obeyed the rule. Together they broke it.

Three fixes, and you should be able to name all three and pick one:

FixHowVerdict
Explicit row lock SELECT … FROM payments WHERE id = ? FOR UPDATE before validating. Both transactions must now queue on the payment row, so B reads A's committed total. My default. Simple, predictable, and the contention is per-payment — which is to say, essentially zero, because nobody else is refunding this payment. It costs nothing precisely because it locks something nobody else wants.
A denormalized counter + a CHECK Keep payments.refunded_cents, update it in the same transaction (UPDATE payments SET refunded_cents = refunded_cents + ? WHERE id = ?), and let CHECK (refunded_cents <= captured_cents) reject the over-refund. Also excellent, and it's what §5's schema does. The atomic increment takes the row lock implicitly and the constraint enforces the invariant — the database refuses the second refund outright. Note this is not a mutable balance in the §7a sense: it's a derived cache with a hard constraint, and the refunds table remains the source of truth.
SERIALIZABLE Postgres's SSI detects the read-write dependency cycle and aborts one transaction with a serialization failure. Correct, and the strongest guarantee available — but you must handle 40001 serialization failures by retrying the whole transaction, which every caller must now do. Use it for the ledger-posting path where the invariants are subtle and you want the database to be paranoid on your behalf. Don't reach for it globally without saying you'd measure the abort rate.
Say the general principle, not just the fix, because the principle is what transfers: "Anywhere I read a value, make a decision, and then write based on that decision, READ COMMITTED is not enough — that's a write skew and both transactions can pass their own check while jointly violating the invariant. So on the money path I either take an explicit FOR UPDATE lock on the aggregate root, or I push the invariant into a database constraint, or I run SERIALIZABLE and retry on 40001. What I never do is validate in application code under the default isolation level and assume it holds." Every over-refund, every double-spend and every oversold seat in the history of computing is that sentence being ignored.
Notice the shape of the answer: it is the same as 05's, and the same as 14's (ticket booking). Push the invariant down to something that can enforce it atomically — a unique constraint, a check constraint, a conditional update, a row lock. Different domain, identical move. When you see the same answer arrive from three different directions, that's the archetype, and archetypes are what you're actually building in this phase.

10c. Replication and durability

  • Synchronous replication on the payment/ledger database. synchronous_commit = on with at least one sync standby. Yes, it costs write latency (a round trip to the standby). Pay it. The alternative is that a failover loses the last few seconds of writes — and "we lost the record of the money we took" is not a sentence you want to be part of.
  • Async read replicas for the order-history path. Replication lag is fine there — a receipt that's 200ms stale harms nobody.
  • Read your own writes. After a customer pays, route their next few reads to the primary (or use the PSP's returned state), because the one place lag does hurt is the customer refreshing the page right after checkout and seeing "unpaid." That is a support call and a duplicate payment attempt waiting to happen.
  • Point-in-time recovery, and test the restore. An untested backup is a rumour. In a regulated environment you will be asked to demonstrate the restore, so build it before someone makes you.

11. Scaling & bottlenecks

Now — and only now, and having earned it — talk about scale. The framing matters as much as the content:

You scale the read path freely and aggressively. You scale the write path conservatively and reluctantly, and every scaling move on the write path must preserve the transactional guarantees, or it is not a scaling move — it is a downgrade wearing a scaling move's clothes.

What breaks first, in order

#BreaksSymptomFix

Which is a business decision as much as a technical one, and saying so is a maturity signal.
5 Write throughput on one Postgres You are, genuinely, at tens of thousands of TPS. Congratulations, you are Stripe. Shard. See below. But be honest that most companies never get here, and designing for it prematurely costs you correctness today for throughput you'll need in five years.

If you truly must shard the write path

Shard by account_id (or customer_id), not by order_id and absolutely not by time. The reason is the only reason that matters: a ledger transaction must be atomic across all its entries, so all the accounts it touches should live in one shard. Shard by account and the overwhelming majority of transactions are single-shard, so they keep real ACID. 

  shard = hash(account_id) % N       ← or consistent hashing (04) to make
                                       rebalancing survivable

  Customer pays merchant, and both accounts are on shard 3:
      → ONE local Postgres transaction. Real ACID. Nothing changes. ✓

  Customer on shard 3 pays merchant on shard 7:
      → a CROSS-SHARD ledger transaction.  Now what?
      → You are back to §8: a saga, with the entries posted in each shard's
        local transaction, an in-flight "clearing" account absorbing the
        imbalance, and a reconciliation job that catches any that get stuck.
      → The books balance GLOBALLY and are momentarily unbalanced LOCALLY,
        which is exactly what a clearing account is FOR. This is not a hack —
        it is how the actual banking system works, and it is why "money in
        transit" is a real accounting concept with a real account behind it.

The hot-account problem

This is the interesting one and it is 04's celebrity-partition problem in a suit. Sharding by account works beautifully for customers — a customer makes a handful of payments a month, so the load spreads perfectly. It works terribly for the accounts on the other side of those transactions. Your revenue account, your psp_clearing account, and a large merchant's account are touched by every single transaction in the system. Every write in the entire business contends on one row's worth of locality.

FixHow it worksThe cost
Because you have no balance column, there is no row to contend on ★ This is the sleeper benefit of §7 and you should point at it triumphantly. An append-only ledger has no hot row — every entry is an INSERT, and inserts don't block each other. Ten thousand concurrent payments to one merchant are ten thousand independent appends. A mutable balance column would have serialised every one of them behind a single row lock. Reading that merchant's balance means summing many entries — which is what snapshots (§7g) are for.
Sub-accounts / sharded counters Split the hot account into revenue_00 … revenue_99, write to a random one, and define the real balance as the sum. Same trick as a sharded counter (04, 18). Every balance read now fans out across 100 sub-accounts. Worth it only when appends genuinely contend, which with an append-only ledger is rare.
Give the hot account its own shard Directory-based sharding (04): internal accounts are pinned to dedicated, well-provisioned shards. More cross-shard transactions, because now customer and revenue are always on different shards. Trades one problem for another; only do it if you've measured.
The moment to reach for: "The hot-account problem is the one that would actually bite me at scale — every transaction in the business touches the same few internal accounts. But notice that the append-only ledger already solves the worst of it: because there's no mutable balance column, there's no hot row to serialise on. Inserts don't contend. If I'd stored balances, I'd have every payment in the company queuing behind one row lock. The design I chose for auditability turns out to be the one that scales — and that's not a coincidence, it's what immutability buys you." Landing that callback — the correctness decision paying a scalability dividend — is a genuinely memorable way to close a design.

Every row is a decision you made on purpose. Being able to say "and here's when I'd flip it" is the difference between having a design and having judgment.

13. Follow-ups you should expect

"How do you handle chargebacks and disputes?"

A chargeback is a customer telling their bank (not you) that a charge was wrong. The bank pulls the money back, weeks or months later, without asking your permission, and charges you a dispute fee on top. It is the one flow in the system where money leaves without you initiating it — and that single fact dictates the design.

  • It arrives as an inbound webhook (charge.dispute.created). Everything from §9d applies: verify, dedupe, apply conditionally.
  • It is a ledger transaction like any other: debit revenue, credit psp_clearing for the amount, plus a second transaction for the dispute fee (debit dispute_fees, credit psp_clearing). Nothing is deleted; the original sale remains. This is the ledger model earning its keep for the fourth time.
  • The payment moves to DISPUTED. There's a representment window in which you can contest it by submitting evidence (delivery confirmation, the customer's IP, the signed terms) — so you need a case-management workflow and a place to store evidence. Model it, don't build it in the interview.
  • If you win, the money comes back — as another new transaction, never as a rewrite of the old ones.
  • The senior note: a high chargeback rate gets you thrown off the card networks. Above roughly 1% you enter a monitoring programme; sustained, you lose the ability to accept cards at all. That makes fraud prevention a business-survival concern rather than a cost-optimisation one, and knowing that is a business-domain signal fintech interviewers notice.

"What about multiple currencies?"

Two rules, and the second is the one that gets you hired.

  • An account has exactly one currency. You never mix currencies within an account, and a ledger transaction's entries must all be in the same currency — otherwise "sums to zero" is meaningless. A conversion is two transactions plus an FX position account: debit the USD account, credit the EUR account, and book the spread as revenue or loss.
  • Store the FX rate you actually used, at the time you used it — and never re-derive it. Put fx_rate and fx_rate_at (and the source) on the transaction. If you re-fetch today's rate to explain a conversion from March, you will get a different number, your books won't balance, and you will have no way to prove what happened. This is the same rule as copying unit_price_cents onto the order line (§5): a financial fact is captured when it becomes true and is never recomputed. Say it that way — spotting that two different-looking requirements are the same principle is exactly what "judgment" means.
  • And the minor-unit trap from §7d: JPY has zero decimal places, KWD has three. No hardcoded /100.

"Where does fraud detection go?"

  • An async scoring step in the saga that can hold the order — not a synchronous gate on checkout. The order goes to UNDER_REVIEW; the saga pauses; a scoring service (rules + a model) returns a score; the saga resumes to CONFIRMED or compensates to CANCELLED.
  • This is exactly why authorize-then-capture is such a gift: fraud review happens in the window when the money is only held. A fraud rejection voids the authorization for free. If you'd captured at checkout, every fraud rejection would be a refund — visible to the customer, fee-incurring, and slow.
  • Keep a small set of synchronous hard blocks (velocity checks: this card has been tried 40 times in 5 minutes; this device has 12 accounts) — they must be fast, so Redis counters, same shape as the rate limiter in 09. The expensive model scoring goes async.
  • The trade-off, stated: false positives cost you a real customer; false negatives cost you a chargeback plus a fee plus network standing. The threshold is a business decision, and you should say that rather than pretending it's an engineering one.

"How do you reduce PCI scope?"

  • Tokenization. The card is collected by the PSP's iframe or SDK in the customer's browser and posted directly to the PSP. Your server receives pm_1Ab2C3d4E5. A PAN never touches your network, your logs, your database, or your backups.
  • This takes you from PCI-DSS SAQ-D (hundreds of controls, an annual QSA audit, network segmentation, key rotation — an entire compliance function) to SAQ-A (a short self-assessment). It is the single largest engineering-effort reduction available in payments and it is one architectural decision.
  • Watch the leaks: don't log request bodies indiscriminately on payment endpoints; scrub PANs at the log pipeline as a belt-and-braces measure; and remember that a card number pasted into a support ticket puts your ticketing system in scope. PCI scope is contagious — it spreads to every system a PAN touches, which is why the winning move is to make sure it touches none.

"GDPR says delete the user's data. Your ledger is immutable. Which wins?"

This is a genuinely good question, it is a real tension, and interviewers ask it to see whether you'll pretend it isn't. Do not hand-wave it.

  • You delete the PII. You keep the financial record. That's the answer, and it is not a compromise — it's what the law actually says. GDPR's right to erasure has an explicit exemption for data that must be retained to comply with a legal obligation, and financial and tax records carry statutory retention periods (commonly 7 years). You cannot delete a transaction record because a customer asked you to; a tax authority also has a claim on it.
  • Architecturally: separate PII from financial facts. The ledger references an account_id, not a name, an email, or an address. Those live in a customer service that can be redacted. Erasure means the customer record is anonymised and the ledger keeps referring to an account whose owner is now unnamed. The money still balances; the person is gone.
  • Crypto-shredding is the elegant version, and worth naming: encrypt each customer's PII with a per-customer key, and "delete" by destroying the key. The ciphertext remains in immutable storage and backups — which you couldn't have rewritten anyway — and is now permanently unreadable.
  • Say this: "These two requirements are genuinely in tension, and the honest answer is that neither wins entirely — I delete the PII and keep the financial record, because the financial record is legally required to be retained. Which means the architecture has to separate them from day one: the ledger must never contain a name. Retrofitting that separation into a ledger that embedded customer data is extremely painful, so this is a day-one decision."

"Can you guarantee exactly-once?"

This is a bait question and 05 §9 gives you the answer. Do not answer the question as asked.

"Exactly-once delivery doesn't exist — the sender can never distinguish a lost message from a lost acknowledgement, so it either retries and risks a duplicate or doesn't and risks a loss. What I build is exactly-once processing: at-least-once delivery plus idempotent handlers that dedupe on an event ID or an idempotency key in the same transaction as the side effect. In this system that's the idempotency_keys table on the API, the processed_webhooks table on the PSP callbacks, the event_id dedup on Kafka consumers, and the unique idem_key on ledger transactions. The customer's card is charged exactly once — not because the network is reliable, but because the fourth duplicate hits a primary key constraint and does nothing."

"What if the payment provider is down?"

  • Circuit breaker (06) — fail fast rather than exhausting your thread pool waiting on a dead dependency. A slow dependency takes down more systems than a dead one.
  • Never retry a timed-out capture blindly without the idempotency key — that's the double-charge. With the key, retry freely; it costs nothing.
  • For authorizations, you have a real choice: fail the checkout ("please try again"), or accept the order into PENDING_PAYMENT and retry the authorization asynchronously. The second is better UX and it is exactly what the saga already supports — the order exists, the saga is at AUTHORIZE_PAYMENT, the sweeper retries with backoff. You get provider-outage tolerance for free from a design you built for correctness.
  • A second PSP with failover routing is the real answer at scale — and it is expensive, because you now maintain two integrations, two reconciliation pipelines and two sets of webhook quirks. Name that cost; it's the reason most companies don't do it until they've been burned.
  • And do not, under any circumstances, "queue the charge and process it later" while telling the customer they've paid. You don't know that the card will authorize. You've just promised a customer something the bank hasn't agreed to. Tell them the order is pending, and mean it.

"How does this relate to the order-management service you built?"

Have this ready, because at 7 YOE the interviewer wants to connect the design to something you've actually shipped, and the honest version of the answer is more impressive than a fabricated one:

"The Spring Boot and Postgres order service I built has the same spine — orders, order items, a status state machine, and payment integration. What a system at this scale adds is everything that happens when things go wrong: an idempotency-key table so retries can't double-charge, an outbox so a crash between the commit and the publish can't lose an order, a saga orchestrator with real compensations and a sweeper, and a double-entry ledger so that what we believe about the money is reconstructible and checkable against what the provider says about the money. The happy path is the part I already had. The design is all in the failures."

Interview Q&A

"Why not just store a balance column and update it? It's simpler."

It's simpler right up to the point where it's wrong, and it's wrong in three independent ways.

It destroys the audit trail. The column says $412.00 and there is nothing in the database that explains why. You cannot answer "what was this balance last Tuesday," you cannot investigate when finance says the number is wrong, and you cannot show an auditor how it got there. In a system whose entire purpose is being provably correct about money, that is disqualifying on its own.

It creates a lost-update race. Any balance check in front of the update is check-then-act: two concurrent operations both read the old value, both validate, both write. To fix it you need SELECT … FOR UPDATE, which serialises every operation on that account behind one row lock — and the accounts you most care about (revenue, PSP clearing, a big merchant) are touched by every transaction in the system.

It lets money be created and destroyed. Nothing in the schema says the debit and the credit must agree. One UPDATE commits, the other throws, and you have invented currency — with no constraint to stop you and no query to detect it.

The alternative: an append-only double-entry ledger. Every transaction writes at least two entries that sum to zero, so money is conserved by construction; the balance is derived as SUM(entries); and if summing gets slow, you add a snapshot as a rebuildable cache, never as a source of truth. As a bonus, an append-only table has no hot row — inserts don't contend — so the design I chose for auditability is also the one that scales.

"A customer clicks Pay twice, or the network times out and the client retries. How do you avoid double-charging?"

Start by conceding the thing everyone tries to design around: you cannot know, from the client side, whether the first request went through. A lost request and a lost response are indistinguishable — both are silence. So you can't prevent the duplicate; you can only make it harmless.

An idempotency key, exactly as Stripe does it. The client generates a UUID once per logical intent — in a React checkout, when the form mounts, not when the fetch fires — and sends it as an Idempotency-Key header. Every retry reuses it.

Server side, the critical detail is that you do not check-then-act, because two concurrent duplicates would both check, both see nothing, and both charge. Instead the insert is the check: INSERT INTO idempotency_keys (key) VALUES (?) ON CONFLICT (key) DO NOTHING. Exactly one caller can win that row — the primary key constraint is an atomic, durable compare-and-swap I already have. The winner does the work and stores the response in the same transaction as the business write. The loser reads the stored response and replays it — including replaying an original 402 card_declined, because a retry must not get a second bite at the card.

And pass an idempotency key downstream to the PSP too. Idempotency is a chain; it's only as strong as its weakest hop. If I crash after calling Stripe but before my transaction commits, the downstream key — derived from the client's key — is what stops the retry from creating a second authorization.

"Walk me through what happens when the payment succeeds but the inventory reservation fails."

First, note that in my saga this shouldn't happen, because I deliberately ordered the steps to reserve inventory before touching payment — the cheapest-to-compensate steps go first. Being able to say "my step ordering was chosen to make this failure rare" is a better answer than handling it gracefully.

But suppose it does: the warehouse discovers the last unit is damaged after we authorized. The saga enters COMPENSATING and unwinds in reverse order — void the authorization (free, invisible on the customer's statement, no fee), release any inventory hold, cancel the order, and notify the customer.

This is precisely why I authorize at checkout and capture at shipment. In that window the money is only held, so the compensation is a free void. If I'd captured at checkout, this compensation would be a refund — and a refund is not a rollback. It's a new forward transaction: the charge stays on the customer's statement, the refund appears beside it, we've paid the processing fee on both, and the customer is annoyed. You cannot un-charge a card. You can only refund it.

And the part people skip: the compensation itself can fail. The void times out; the PSP is down. So compensations are idempotent (derived keys — void:{paymentId}) and retried with exponential backoff and jitter. If they still fail after N attempts, the saga row moves to COMPENSATION_FAILED, it goes to a dead-letter queue and a break queue, an alert fires with the amount at risk, and a human resolves it in an ops console with a full audit trail of what the system already tried. Every real payment system has that path. A design without one is a design that hasn't met production.

"Why a saga and not 2PC?"

The decisive reason is structural, not performance: Stripe will not join my XA transaction. There is no prepare() on a third-party payment API. The moment a participant in my transaction is a company I don't control, 2PC is not a choice I'm making — it's an option that doesn't exist.

The secondary reason is 2PC's failure mode. It's a blocking protocol: if the coordinator dies between "everyone voted yes" and "everyone commit," every participant sits there holding locks indefinitely — it can't commit (it doesn't know the decision) and it can't abort (it already promised). Under load, every other transaction touching those rows queues behind them, so a coordinator failure becomes a cascading outage rather than a stalled order.

But I'd be careful not to sneer at 2PC, because it is alive and well: two in-house XA datasources in one datacenter, driven by Spring's JtaTransactionManager, at modest volume, is a real and correct pattern — you'll find it in core banking. It's the cross-vendor, over-the-internet version that's impossible. What a saga costs me in exchange is real: no isolation, so intermediate states are visible to users (hence the explicit PENDING_PAYMENT status), and every compensation is code I have to write and test.

"The payment provider's webhook says the payment succeeded. Do you trust it?"

I treat a webhook as a hint, never as a source of truth. Four things are true about webhooks and each one demands a defence:

They arrive twice. The provider retries whenever your 200 is slow or lost. So I dedupe on their event ID — INSERT INTO processed_webhooks (psp_event_id) ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING, in the same transaction as the side effect. Zero rows inserted means I've handled it; return 200 and do nothing.

They arrive out of order. payment.captured can genuinely land before payment.authorized. So I never write state blindly from a webhook — every transition is conditional (UPDATE … WHERE status = 'AUTHORIZED'), and an out-of-order event is simply a no-op that affects zero rows.

They are forgeable. That endpoint is a public URL that accepts POSTs saying "this payment succeeded." I verify the HMAC signature against the raw request bytes before parsing anything, and reject stale timestamps to prevent replay.

And sometimes they never arrive at all. This is the one that matters most and the one candidates omit. I always also poll. A reconciliation job re-queries the provider for anything stuck in a non-terminal state past a threshold. If my correctness depends on a webhook arriving, then my correctness depends on someone else's retry logic — and that is not a bet I'll make with money.

"What's the difference between authorize and capture, and why do you care?"

Authorize asks the issuing bank to reserve the funds and reduce the customer's available credit. No money moves. It's a promise, and it expires — typically after about 7 days. This is why a charge shows as "pending" on your statement for a few days and then changes.

Capture is when the money actually moves. You capture at shipment. In many jurisdictions you're not permitted to take payment for physical goods before you dispatch them.

Why I care, in design terms: an authorization can be voided for free and leaves no trace; a capture can only be refunded — visibly, with fees, and irreversibly. So the authorize/capture split is really a saga design that the card networks handed me. By capturing late, I arrange for the entire window in which things are most likely to go wrong — out of stock, bad address, fraud review — to be a window in which the money hasn't moved yet and my compensation is a free void. It's also what makes async fraud scoring viable: the score can take thirty seconds because nobody has been charged yet.

One more consequence for the schema: you can capture less than you authorized (you shipped 2 of 3 items) but not more — which is exactly the CHECK (captured_cents <= amount_cents) constraint in my payments table. And critically, an authorization writes no ledger entry, because no money has moved. Posting to the ledger at authorize time silently inflates revenue by the value of every authorization that's never captured.

"Why not use Cassandra? It scales better."

It does scale better, and that's the one thing this system doesn't need. Peak here is around a thousand transactions per second — a single well-tuned Postgres handles that comfortably. I'd be trading away every mechanism that makes the design correct in exchange for headroom on an axis I'm nowhere near saturating.

Concretely, what I'd lose: multi-row ACID transactions (the payment row, the ledger entries and the outbox row must commit atomically — without that, I have the dual-write problem inside my own database); five unique constraints that are individually preventing a duplicate charge; check constraints enforcing refunded_cents <= captured_cents; and ad-hoc SQL for reconciliation, which asks new questions every time finance finds something odd.

The invariants are the design. Picking an eventually-consistent store for a ledger means reimplementing ACID in application code, badly, and discovering the bugs in production with real customers' money. And if I ever genuinely outgrow one Postgres, my answer is to shard Postgres by account and keep the transactions — not to switch to a database that can't do them.

"Two partial refunds are issued at the same moment. What stops you over-refunding?"

This is a write skew, and it's the classic Postgres READ COMMITTED trap. Both transactions run SELECT SUM(amount) FROM refunds WHERE payment_id = ?, both get 8000 out of a 10000 capture, both conclude that another 1500 is fine, and both insert. Neither ever sees the other, because there is no row to lock — the row that would conflict doesn't exist yet. Total refunded: 11000 on a 10000 capture. Both transactions individually obeyed the rule; together they broke it.

Three fixes, and I'd name all three:

1. An explicit row lock — SELECT … FROM payments WHERE id = ? FOR UPDATE before validating. Both refunds now queue on the payment row, so the second one sees the first one's total. This is my default, and it's cheap precisely because the contention is per-payment: nobody else in the world is refunding this payment.

2. A denormalized counter plus a constraint — UPDATE payments SET refunded_cents = refunded_cents + ? takes the row lock implicitly, and CHECK (refunded_cents <= captured_cents) makes the database itself reject the over-refund. This is what my schema does. Note it's not a mutable balance in the bad sense: the refunds table is still the source of truth, and the counter is a derived cache with a hard constraint.

3. SERIALIZABLE — Postgres's SSI detects the dependency cycle and aborts one transaction with a 40001, which every caller must then retry.

The general principle, which is what actually transfers: anywhere I read a value, make a decision, and write based on it, READ COMMITTED is not enough. Push the invariant into the database — a lock, a constraint, or a conditional update — and never validate in application code and assume it holds.

"What is reconciliation and why does it need to exist if your code is correct?"

Because "if your code is correct" is doing all the work in that sentence, and no payments engineer has ever believed it about their own system.

Reconciliation is a job that periodically re-derives our balances from the ledger entries — never from a cached balance, since the whole point is to catch a wrong cached balance — and compares them, line by line, against the provider's settlement report. Every mismatch produces a break record that a human investigates.

Three break types, three different bugs. MISSING (we have it, they don't) is usually a capture we think succeeded but whose call actually failed. ORPHAN (they have it, we don't) is the worst: a customer's card was charged and we have no record — money we took and can't account for, typically caused by someone trusting a timeout. AMOUNT mismatches are a differing fee, an FX rate applied differently, or a genuine float bug.

And the discipline that makes it worth anything: I never auto-correct a break and I never write off a discrepancy as a rounding error. A three-cent gap is not "close enough" — either I know why it's three cents, or my ledger is wrong and the size of the error is a coincidence. A system that quietly absorbs small differences is a system that will quietly absorb a large one, and everyone who has worked in payments has watched somebody raise a tolerance threshold to make an alarm stop firing.

"How would you store the amount? Show me the type."

BIGINT amount_cents in Postgres; long in Java. Integer minor units. Exactly what Stripe's API does — "amount": 4998.

Never a float or a double. IEEE-754 binary floating point cannot exactly represent 0.1, any more than decimal can exactly represent one third. In Java, 0.1 + 0.2 is 0.30000000000000004, and 1.03 - 0.42 prints 0.6100000000000001. Accumulate that across millions of ledger entries and the books stop balancing — by an amount too small to notice and too large to ignore, in a system whose entire purpose is that the books balance. A float rounding error in a ledger is a career-defining bug.

The acceptable alternative is an exact decimal type — BigDecimal / NUMERIC(19,4) — which I'd reach for the moment I need sub-cent precision: FX conversion, interest accrual, per-unit pricing. It's slower and you must set the RoundingMode explicitly, but it's exact.

And a detail that shows you've handled real money: minor units aren't always 100. The Japanese yen has zero decimal places, the Kuwaiti dinar has three. If I go multi-currency I look up the ISO 4217 exponent rather than hardcoding a divide-by-100.

"GDPR requires you to delete a customer's data. Your ledger is immutable. Which wins?"

They're genuinely in tension and I wouldn't pretend otherwise. The honest answer is that neither wins entirely: I delete the PII and I keep the financial record.

That's not a compromise I invented — it's what the law says. GDPR's right to erasure has an explicit exemption for data retained to comply with a legal obligation, and financial and tax records carry statutory retention periods, commonly seven years. I cannot delete a transaction record because a customer asked me to; a tax authority has a claim on it too.

Architecturally that has to be a day-one decision. The ledger references an account_id — never a name, an email or an address. PII lives in a customer service that can be redacted. Erasure anonymises the customer record; the ledger keeps referring to an account whose owner is now unnamed. The money still balances; the person is gone. Retrofitting that separation into a ledger that embedded customer data is extremely painful, which is exactly why it's a day-one decision and not a compliance afterthought.

The elegant version is crypto-shredding: encrypt each customer's PII under a per-customer key and "delete" by destroying the key. The ciphertext survives in immutable storage and in backups — which you could never have rewritten anyway — and is now permanently unreadable.

"Can you guarantee exactly-once processing of a payment?"

Not as asked — and refusing the question is the answer.

Exactly-once delivery doesn't exist. A sender that gets no acknowledgement cannot distinguish a lost message from a lost ack. It has exactly two options: retry, and risk a duplicate (at-least-once), or don't, and risk a loss (at-most-once). There is no third option and no amount of acknowledgement fixes it — that's the Two Generals problem, and it's turtles all the way down.

Exactly-once processing is achievable, because I control the receiver. At-least-once delivery plus idempotent handlers that dedupe in the same transaction as the side effect. In this system that's four separate mechanisms: the idempotency_keys table on the API, the processed_webhooks table on PSP callbacks, event_id dedup on Kafka consumers, and the unique idem_key on ledger transactions.

So the customer's card is charged exactly once — not because the network is reliable, but because the fourth duplicate hits a primary key constraint and does nothing. And I'd add the precise note about Kafka: its exactly-once semantics are real, but they only hold inside Kafka. The moment my consumer calls Stripe, I'm outside that transaction and back to needing my own idempotency.

"You have 10 minutes left and the interviewer says 'now make it handle 10× the traffic.' What do you do?"

I'd start by pushing back gently, because the honest answer is more impressive than the eager one: 10× of 1,000 TPS is 10,000 TPS, and that is still not a scale problem for a single Postgres on decent hardware. The first things that break are the read path and the ledger's table size, not the write throughput.

So in order: (1) read replicas plus a cache on terminal-state orders — those are immutable, so they cache forever with no invalidation problem. (2) Partition the ledger by month and add balance snapshots so a balance query sums a bounded suffix rather than the whole history. (3) Reap the outbox, or it takes down the database it was protecting. (4) A circuit breaker on the PSP, which at that volume is my real availability ceiling, not my database.

Only then would I shard the write path — by account_id, not by order_id, so that both sides of a ledger transaction usually live on the same shard and keep real ACID. Cross-shard transactions fall back to a saga with a clearing account absorbing the temporary imbalance, which is exactly how the real banking system works.

And the punchline I'd want to land: the hot-account problem is already half-solved by the ledger design. Every transaction touches the same few internal accounts, but because there is no mutable balance column, there is no hot row — every entry is an append, and appends don't contend. If I'd stored balances, every payment in the company would be queuing behind one row lock. The choice I made for auditability turned out to be the one that scales.

What to remember

If you revise one page in this phase the night before a fintech loop, revise this one. And if you remember one sentence from it, remember the first.

  • In every other system design, a lost update is a bug. Here it is a financial loss and possibly a regulatory event. Everything follows from that. You design for auditability and correctness first, and only then for scale. The candidate who leads with "let's shard for scale" has already failed this round.
  • "It is better to be down than to be wrong." Correctness over availability, said out loud, in the first two minutes. It inverts the instinct every other system design round trains into you, and it is the single best thing you can say in this interview. CP on the money path, AP on the read path — split the CAP answer by path, not by system.
  • Scale is not the hard part. Correctness is — and say so. A thousand TPS is a large payments business and a trivial database workload. The number that actually drives the design is storage that only grows, because the ledger is retained forever and you never delete.
  • Never store a mutable balance column. A balance is a derived value — the sum of an immutable, append-only sequence of entries. Storing and mutating it destroys the audit trail and creates a lost-update race for nothing. Snapshots are allowed as a rebuildable cache, never as a source of truth.
  • Double-entry: every transaction writes at least two entries that sum to zero. Money is never created or destroyed, only moved. SUM(amount) = 0 is an invariant you can check with a query, so assert it continuously and alarm when it breaks. Walk the $100-purchase-with-a-$3-fee example on the whiteboard; it takes ninety seconds and proves more than ten minutes of talking.
  • Money is never a float. Integer minor units (BIGINT cents) or an exact decimal (NUMERIC/BigDecimal). Never double. A float rounding error in a ledger is a career-defining bug, and some interviewers plant one in a schema to see if you catch it.
  • You cannot roll back a captured payment. You can only refund it — and a refund is a new forward transaction, not an undo. That distinction is the entire point of compensation-based transactions. It's why you order the saga to put irreversible steps last, and why you authorize at checkout but capture at shipment: most failures then compensate with a free void instead of a costly, visible refund.
  • Compensations fail too — say what happens. Idempotent retry with backoff and jitter, then a dead-letter queue, then a break record, then a human with a console and an audit trail. Pretending compensation always succeeds is a junior tell. Say the word "human" out loud.
  • Orchestration, not choreography, for money. You want one place that knows the state of the world and can be queried. "Why is order 12345 stuck?" should be a SELECT, not a research project across five codebases.
  • The outbox is mandatory here, not an optimization. A dual write silently drops events; in this system a dropped event is an unpaid order or a card charged for an order that doesn't exist. State change and event go into one local transaction; a relay publishes at-least-once; consumers dedupe.
  • The idempotency key is what makes a retry safe, because you cannot know from the client side whether the payment went through. Client-generated, once per intent, stable across retries — exactly as Stripe does it. The INSERT … ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING is the check; never check-then-act. Store and replay the original response, success or failure. And pass a key downstream — idempotency is a chain.
  • Webhooks are a hint, never a source of truth. They arrive twice, arrive out of order, and sometimes never arrive. Verify the signature against the raw body, dedupe on the provider's event ID, use conditional state transitions, return a fast 200 and process asynchronously — and always also poll. This is the detail that separates people who have shipped payments from people who have read about them.
  • Reconciliation is a first-class component, not a cron someone added later. Re-derive balances from the entries, compare to the provider's settlement report, and raise a break on every mismatch. Never auto-correct. Never write off a discrepancy as a rounding error — a system that quietly absorbs small differences will quietly absorb a large one.
  • Postgres, because the invariants are the design. Multi-row transactions, five unique constraints, two check constraints, ad-hoc reconciliation queries. Cassandra buys throughput you don't need and removes the primitives you're built on. If you outgrow one primary, shard Postgres by account and keep the transactions.
  • READ COMMITTED is not enough for read-then-decide. Two partial refunds can each pass their own check and jointly over-refund — that's write skew. Take a FOR UPDATE lock on the aggregate root, or push the invariant into a CHECK constraint, or run SERIALIZABLE and retry on 40001. Never validate in application code and assume it holds.
  • PCI: never store raw card numbers. Tokenize. The card goes from the browser straight to the PSP; you hold an opaque token. That single architectural decision collapses your compliance scope from SAQ-D to SAQ-A. PCI scope is contagious — the winning move is that a PAN touches nothing you own.
  • GDPR vs the immutable ledger is a real tension, and the honest answer wins. Delete the PII, keep the financial record — which is what the law actually requires. Which means the ledger must reference an account_id and never a name, from day one.
  • Exactly-once delivery is a myth; exactly-once processing is achievable. The card is charged once not because the network is reliable, but because the fourth duplicate hits a primary key constraint and does nothing.
  • The recurring move, arriving from four directions on this page: push the invariant down into something that can enforce it atomically. A unique constraint, a check constraint, a conditional update, a row lock. Not a distributed lock. Not application-layer validation. That is the archetype — and it is the same answer you'll give in 14 when they ask you not to oversell a seat.
One last calibration. Everything on this page is in service of a single impression you are trying to leave in the room: that you are someone who can be trusted with a system where being wrong costs money. Not someone who knows the most patterns. Someone who knows which failures are recoverable and which are not, who says "a human looks at it" without embarrassment, who refuses to round away a three-cent discrepancy, and who would rather be briefly down than briefly wrong. That's the hire. Everything else is decoration.
