03 — Data & Storage Engines

The database choice is the decision every system design interview turns on. Learn to derive it, not guess it.

Why this matters in the interview

Every distributed design round funnels into one moment: "what database would you use?" It is the highest-signal question in the loop because your answer reveals, in about ninety seconds, whether you understand storage or whether you have memorized a list of product names. The failure mode is famous and fatal: "I'd use NoSQL because it scales." That sentence tells the interviewer you don't know what NoSQL is, don't know that Postgres runs some of the largest OLTP workloads on earth, and — worst — that you pick technology by reputation rather than by requirement. At 3 YOE that's a shrug. At 7 YOE it's a downlevel or a no-hire, because the entire grading rubric at your level is judgment under trade-offs, and the data store is where the biggest trade-offs live.

The good news is you're not starting cold. You just spent a phase inside Postgres — B-tree indexes, EXPLAIN plans, transactions, MVCC, JSONB. That's the deepest storage knowledge most candidates in a system design round have, and most of them can't articulate it. This page extends it outward: what a different storage engine (LSM-tree) does differently and why that makes Cassandra fast at writes; what happens to all that ACID machinery when the data no longer fits on one box; what replication actually costs you; and the precise vocabulary — read-your-writes, monotonic reads, PACELC — that interviewers listen for. When you're done, "what database?" stops being a guessing game and becomes a derivation: state the access patterns, then name the store that serves them. That is the whole move, and this page is where you learn to make it.

1. How to actually justify a database choice

A database is not a general-purpose thing you "have." It is a machine that makes a specific set of queries cheap by making other queries expensive. Postgres makes arbitrary joins and multi-row transactions cheap by keeping everything on one machine's authoritative copy. DynamoDB makes "fetch the item with this key, at any scale, in single-digit milliseconds" cheap by refusing to join and by forcing you to declare your access pattern up front. Neither is better. They are different bets about what you'll ask for.

So the choice can't be made from the product name inwards. It has to be made from the queries outwards. The order is fixed: 

  1. What are the ENTITIES?          users, videos, comments, seats, payments
  2. What are the ACCESS PATTERNS?   "get video by id"        — 500k/s
                                     "list a user's uploads"  — 200/s
                                     "search video titles"    — 5k/s
  3. What's the READ:WRITE ratio?    100:1 read-heavy → cache + replicas matter
  4. What's the SHAPE of the data?   fixed cols? nested docs? time series? blobs?
  5. What CONSISTENCY does each
     pattern actually need?          seat booking: strong. like count: eventual.
  6. THEN name the store — and say
     which access pattern forced it.

Notice step 6 is the last step, and notice it comes with a justification attached. The sentence you say out loud has a fixed grammar: "Because the dominant access pattern is X, and it needs Y, I'll use Z — and I'm accepting that this makes W harder." The clause at the end is the senior clause. Candidates who name a database and stop get a follow-up. Candidates who name the database and its cost get a nod and move on.

The script, verbatim, for the moment the interviewer asks "what database?": "Let me look at the access patterns first. 95% of my traffic is 'read one item by primary key' at very high volume, and the writes are append-only with no cross-entity invariants. That's a key-value workload, not a relational one — so I'd put the hot path in DynamoDB or Cassandra. I'm giving up ad-hoc queries and joins, which means any new access pattern needs a new index or a new table, and I'd have to backfill. If the product needed flexible reporting on this data I'd flip to Postgres and shard later." That's fifteen seconds and it contains a requirement, a decision, a cost, and a falsification condition. That is what "senior" sounds like.
The two rejected answers, in order of frequency. (1) "NoSQL, because it scales." Scales what? Postgres on one large instance handles tens of thousands of writes per second and terabytes of data. If your estimate is 500 writes/s, you have no scaling problem and inventing one is a red flag. (2) Naming a product with no requirement attached — "I'll use Cassandra" as the opening move, before any numbers exist. Always earn the store from a number you computed.
The honest default, and it's a strong one: start with Postgres. It does relational, it does JSONB documents, it does full-text search, it does time-series adequately, it does geospatial via PostGIS. It is one system you can operate and reason about. The interviewer will not punish "Postgres, and here's the number at which I'd have to move off it" — they punish "MongoDB" with no reason. Introducing a second data store is a real operational cost (backups, monitoring, on-call, another consistency model in your head); make the design pay for it.

2. The store taxonomy — workload → store

There are only about eight shapes of storage. Learn the shape, not the vendor. Vendors change; the shape a workload wants does not.

Store typeThe access pattern it makes cheapWhat it can't doReal example
Key-value
CORE		 GET(key) / PUT(key, blob) at enormous scale, predictable single-digit-ms latency. Partitions trivially — the key is the shard key. Any query that isn't "by key." No joins, no scans, no ad-hoc filtering. Redis (cache, sessions, rate-limit counters) · DynamoDB (URL shortener: shortcode → longUrl)
Wide-column
CORE		 Huge write volume; queries of the form "give me a range of rows within one partition key," e.g. "the last 50 messages in chat 42." Rows are stored sorted by clustering key inside a partition. Joins, ad-hoc queries, strong multi-row transactions. Query patterns must be known before you design the table. Cassandra / ScyllaDB / HBase — chat message history, time-ordered event logs, IoT telemetry
Document
COMMON		 Fetch/store a whole nested object by id, where the object is the natural unit of read and write. Flexible per-document fields. Cross-document joins and transactions are weak or awkward. Denormalized data drifts out of sync. MongoDB · a product catalog where every category has different attributes. Also: Postgres JSONB, which gives you most of this inside a relational DB.
Relational
CORE		 Arbitrary queries you didn't anticipate; joins across entities; multi-row ACID transactions; constraints that make invalid states unrepresentable (UNIQUE, FOREIGN KEY, CHECK). Horizontal scaling of writes takes real work (sharding, and then you lose cross-shard joins and transactions anyway). Postgres / MySQL — payments, orders, ledgers, inventory, anything where correctness beats throughput
Graph
AWARENESS		 Multi-hop traversal: "friends of friends of friends," shortest path, recommendation walks. Each hop is a pointer chase, not a join. Everything else. Also hard to shard — the whole point is that edges cross partitions. Neo4j · fraud rings, social graph traversal. Rarely the right answer in an interview — most "social graph" questions are served fine by an adjacency table + fan-out.
Time-series
COMMON		 Append-only writes stamped with time; queries are always "aggregate this metric over this window." Aggressive time-based compression, automatic downsampling and retention. Updates, deletes, joins, point lookups of individual records by a non-time key. InfluxDB · TimescaleDB · Prometheus — metrics, ad-click aggregation (see 18)
Blob / object
CORE		 Store an arbitrarily large immutable byte-stream cheaply and durably; serve it via HTTP, ideally through a CDN. Query it. It has no idea what's inside the bytes. Listing is slow, and there are no transactions. S3 / GCS / Azure Blob — video files, images, user uploads, backups, data-lake parquet
Search index
COMMON		 Full-text relevance ranking, fuzzy match, faceting, typo tolerance. Inverted index: term → list of documents. Be your source of truth. It's a derived, eventually-consistent projection you rebuild from the primary DB. Elasticsearch / OpenSearch · Postgres tsvector for smaller scale — product search, log search (see 17)
The line that earns points about the search row above: "Elasticsearch is a derived index, not a system of record. The write path goes to Postgres, and a CDC stream or an outbox worker projects into Elasticsearch. If Elasticsearch loses data I rebuild it; if Postgres loses data I've lost the business." Candidates who make Elasticsearch the primary store get taken apart on durability.

Polyglot persistence is normal and you should say so — carefully. A real system uses several of these at once: Postgres for orders, Redis for sessions, S3 for uploads, Elasticsearch for search, Kafka for the event log. That's not indecision, it's fitting the store to the pattern. But every additional store is operational cost, so justify each one with the pattern it exists to serve, and say the sentence: "I'd rather run one Postgres than four stores, so each extra store here is earning its keep by serving a pattern Postgres genuinely can't."

3. SQL vs NoSQL, done properly

This is the most-asked trade-off in the entire interview and it is almost always answered with folklore. Kill the folklore first, then learn the honest framing.

3a. The myths, and what's actually true

The mythThe truth
"SQL doesn't scale." It scales vertically an absurdly long way — a modern instance with 128 cores and 1 TB of RAM will serve tens of thousands of writes/s and tens of thousands of QPS on reads with replicas, holding multiple terabytes. Most companies never outgrow it. It scales horizontally too, with effort: read replicas are nearly free; write sharding (Vitess for MySQL, Citus for Postgres, or app-level sharding) works but costs you cross-shard joins and cross-shard transactions. The truthful statement is "relational databases are harder to shard," not "they don't scale."
"NoSQL is schemaless." There is always a schema. The only question is where it lives. In Postgres it lives in the database, is enforced on every write, and one ALTER TABLE migrates every row. In MongoDB/Dynamo it lives in your application code, is enforced nowhere, and a "migration" means your code must handle v1 and v2 documents simultaneously, forever, or you write a backfill job. Schemaless writes are fast because you deferred the cost to read time. Say this out loud — it's an instant credibility marker.
"NoSQL has no transactions." Outdated. DynamoDB has TransactWriteItems, MongoDB has multi-document transactions. But they're limited and expensive (item-count caps, cross-partition cost, higher latency) and using them heavily is a signal you picked the wrong store. Single-item atomicity, however, is guaranteed everywhere — build on that.
"NoSQL is faster." Faster at the pattern it was built for, at scales where a single relational node would fall over. A primary-key lookup in Postgres with a warm cache is also sub-millisecond. What NoSQL really buys you is predictable latency at horizontal scale: p99 stays flat as you add nodes, because the query can't fan out or join.
"NoSQL means eventual consistency." Not inherently. DynamoDB offers strongly consistent reads (at 2× the read cost and higher latency). Cassandra's consistency is tunable per query via quorums (see 05). And conversely: a Postgres read replica gives you eventual consistency too. Consistency is a property of the read path and the replication config, not of the letters "SQL."

3b. The honest framing

Relational gives you joins, transactions, constraints, and the freedom to ask questions you didn't anticipate — at the cost of harder horizontal write-scaling. NoSQL gives you horizontal scale and predictable latency — at the cost of denormalization, duplicated data you must keep in sync yourself, and query rigidity: you must know your access patterns before you design the table, and adding a new one later is a migration.

DimensionRelational (Postgres / MySQL)NoSQL (Dynamo / Cassandra / Mongo)
Data modelNormalized tables; one fact stored onceDenormalized; the same fact duplicated into every table that must answer a query
Query flexibilityAd-hoc SQL; new questions need no schema changeOnly the patterns you designed for; a new pattern = new table/GSI + backfill
JoinsYes, in the engineNo — you join in the application, or you pre-joined at write time
TransactionsFull multi-row ACID; constraints enforce invariantsSingle-item atomic; multi-item is limited, costly, sometimes absent
Schema enforcementIn the DB, on every writeIn your application code (or nowhere)
Scaling writesVertical first; sharding is real work and you lose cross-shard joins/txnsAdd nodes. Linear, by design. This is the whole product.
Scaling readsRead replicas — cheap, but introduce replication lagAdd nodes / replicas; tunable consistency
Latency profileExcellent, but degrades as data outgrows RAM and as joins get complexFlat and predictable — p99 barely moves with data volume
Consistency defaultStrong on the leader; eventual on replicasUsually eventual/tunable; strong available at a price
Best forMoney, inventory, orders, anything with invariants; unknown future queries; < ~10 TB and < ~10k writes/sMassive uniform read/write on a known key; feeds, messages, telemetry, sessions, caches
The tell it's wrongYou're sharding, and every query is now a scatter-gather across shardsYou keep needing joins, or you keep discovering new query patterns
The formulation that ends the debate in one breath: "The real trade isn't SQL vs NoSQL, it's flexible queries vs horizontal write scale. Relational buys me the ability to ask questions I haven't thought of yet, and I pay for it with a sharding problem later. NoSQL buys me linear write scale, and I pay for it by having to know every question up front. For this system the access patterns are fixed and the write volume is the constraint — so I'll take the NoSQL trade." Interviewers are listening for exactly that reframing.
NewSQL exists and is worth one sentence. Spanner, CockroachDB, YugabyteDB, TiDB: relational semantics — SQL, joins, real ACID transactions — over an automatically-sharded, replicated cluster. The cost is write latency (a distributed commit requires cross-node consensus; Spanner leans on GPS/atomic-clock hardware to bound clock skew) and operational complexity. Naming it as an option — "if I truly needed cross-shard transactions at scale I'd look at CockroachDB rather than building 2PC myself" — is a strong awareness-level signal. Don't pretend to deep expertise.
Don't choose NoSQL for a system with money or inventory in it. Payments (13) and ticket booking (14) are the two case studies where the correct answer is relational, loudly, because you need a transaction and a uniqueness constraint to prevent double-spend and overselling. Candidates who reflexively reach for Cassandra there are re-implementing ACID badly in application code, and the interviewer will make them prove it can't oversell. It can.

4. Storage engines: B-tree vs LSM-tree

This is the section that makes you sound senior, because almost nobody in a system design loop can explain why one database is faster at writes than another. They just know it is. You're going to know the mechanism, and the mechanism is the answer to a question interviewers love to push into.

Underneath every database is a storage engine: the code that decides how bytes hit disk and how they're found again. There are essentially two families, and the choice of family determines the database's entire performance personality.

4a. B-tree — update in place (Postgres, MySQL/InnoDB, most relational)

You know this one from Phase 1. The index is a balanced tree of fixed-size pages (8 KB in Postgres). Every lookup walks the tree from root to leaf — typically 3–4 page reads for even a very large table, because the fan-out is enormous. A write finds the correct leaf page and modifies it in place, which means a random write to a specific location on disk. To survive a crash mid-page-write, the change is first appended to the write-ahead log (WAL); the page itself is flushed later. 

  B-tree write:  find leaf page  ──▶  modify page in place  ──▶  fsync WAL
                 (random read)        (random write)
                 ~3-4 page reads      1 page write + WAL append
  • Reads are excellent and predictable. One key lives in exactly one place. A point lookup is O(log n) page reads, full stop. Range scans are fast because leaf pages are linked in sorted order.
  • Writes are the weak spot. Every write is a random I/O against a specific page, plus a WAL append. Under heavy write load, you're issuing random writes across the whole disk. On spinning disks that was murder; on SSDs it's fine but not free, and you still pay write amplification — writing an 8 KB page to change 100 bytes, and writing it twice (WAL + heap).
  • Concurrency needs locks or MVCC. Two writers touching the same page must be serialized. Postgres's answer is MVCC — you already know this: an UPDATE writes a new row version and leaves the old one for readers, which is why VACUUM exists.

4b. LSM-tree — never update, only append (Cassandra, RocksDB, LevelDB, DynamoDB, HBase, ScyllaDB)

The LSM-tree (Log-Structured Merge-tree) starts from a different premise: random writes are the expensive thing, so never do one. Everything is an append. Here is the write path — this is the diagram to be able to draw on a whiteboard: 

  WRITE(key, value)
      │
      ├──▶ 1. Append to COMMIT LOG on disk        (sequential write — durability)
      │
      └──▶ 2. Insert into MEMTABLE in RAM         (sorted structure, e.g. skiplist)
                     │
                     │  memtable full (~64 MB)?
                     ▼
              3. FLUSH to disk as a new SSTABLE
                 (Sorted String Table: immutable, sorted by key,
                  written once, sequentially, never modified again)

  Disk over time:   [SST-1] [SST-2] [SST-3] [SST-4] ...  (newest last)
                       │       │       │       │
                       └───────┴───┬───┴───────┘
                                   ▼
              4. COMPACTION (background): merge SSTables,
                 keep newest value per key, drop tombstones,
                 write out fewer/larger SSTables.

Read that again and notice what's missing: there is no "find the old value and overwrite it." An update is just a new append with a newer timestamp. A delete is also an append — a marker called a tombstone saying "this key is gone as of time T." Nothing on disk is ever modified. That's why the write is fast: it's a sequential log append plus an in-memory insert. No seek, no read-before-write, no page to hunt down.

Now the read path, which is where you pay:

  READ(key)
      │
      ├─▶ memtable?  (RAM)                      hit → return
      │
      ├─▶ SST-4?  bloom filter says maybe → binary-search index → hit → return
      ├─▶ SST-3?  bloom filter says NO   → skip entirely  (cheap!)
      ├─▶ SST-2?  bloom filter says NO   → skip
      └─▶ SST-1?  ... newest-to-oldest until found, else key doesn't exist
  • Read amplification. A key could be in the memtable or in any SSTable, so a read may have to check several files. The fix is a bloom filter per SSTable — a tiny probabilistic bitmap that answers "is this key definitely not here?" with no false negatives. It eliminates almost all the useless file reads. (Bloom filters get their own treatment in 06; this is where they earn their keep.)
  • Compaction is the tax. Merging SSTables in the background consumes disk I/O and CPU, and a big compaction can cause a latency spike — the notorious compaction pause, visible as p99 blowouts in Cassandra. It also causes space amplification: before compaction, you're storing several versions of the same key.
  • Range scans are decent (SSTables are sorted) but need a merge across files.
  • Tombstones are a real operational hazard. Deleted data isn't gone until compaction runs; a workload that deletes heavily builds up tombstones that must still be read and merged. "Cassandra is bad at queues" is entirely this.

4c. The comparison table

The payoff sentence. Memorize it. "This is why you'd pick Cassandra over Postgres for a write-heavy workload — it's not magic, it's the write path. Postgres's B-tree does a random in-place page update per write; Cassandra's LSM-tree appends to a commit log and an in-memory memtable and flushes sorted files sequentially, so writes never seek. I'm paying for it with read amplification, compaction I/O, and worse p99 during compaction." Say that in an interview and the storage line of questioning is over — you've demonstrated you understand the engine, not the marketing.
Where you've already met an LSM-tree: Kafka is essentially an append-only log with sorted index files, and RocksDB (an LSM engine) sits underneath an enormous amount of infrastructure you use indirectly — it's the storage layer inside Kafka Streams, Flink state, CockroachDB, and TiKV. When someone says "our metrics DB fell over during compaction," this section is what they mean.
Don't overclaim. You are not expected to explain leveled vs size-tiered compaction strategies unless you're interviewing for a storage team. Knowing (a) memtable → SSTable → compaction, (b) writes are sequential appends, (c) reads check multiple files and use bloom filters, (d) compaction causes p99 spikes, is the complete 7-YOE answer. If they push further than that, "I know the compaction strategy is tunable — size-tiered favours write throughput, leveled favours read latency and space — but I haven't tuned it in production" is a perfectly strong place to stop.

5. Indexing, in the system-design frame

You know indexes from Phase 1: an index is a secondary sorted structure that turns a full table scan into a tree walk. You know EXPLAIN, you know a composite index (a, b, c) serves predicates on a, (a,b), and (a,b,c) but not on b alone (the left-prefix rule), you know a B-tree index can't serve LIKE '%foo'. That's the mechanics, and it's enough. Here's the part that matters in a system design round, which is a different set of facts.

5a. Every index is a copy of your data

An index is not free metadata. It is a second physical structure holding the indexed columns plus a pointer to the row, kept permanently in sync. Which means:

  • Indexes make reads fast and writes slow. Every INSERT/UPDATE/DELETE must update every index on the table, transactionally. Five indexes means a single-row insert is six B-tree writes. On a write-heavy table this is the difference between a healthy database and a smoking one.
  • Indexes cost RAM. The whole performance model of a B-tree database is "the upper levels of the index live in the buffer cache." Add enough indexes and you evict your hot data to make room for index pages that are rarely used.
  • Indexes cost disk and backup time. On a wide table with many indexes, the indexes can exceed the size of the table itself.

The system-design sentence: "I'll index the columns the access patterns actually filter on, and I'll say out loud that this is a write-path tax — on a table taking 20k inserts/second I'd keep the index count minimal and push secondary lookups to a derived store instead."

5b. Covering indexes

Normally an index lookup gives you a pointer, and the database must then go fetch the actual row from the heap — a second random I/O per row. A covering index contains every column the query needs, so the engine answers entirely from the index and never touches the table. In Postgres: 

-- Query: SELECT status, total FROM orders WHERE user_id = ? ORDER BY created_at DESC;

CREATE INDEX idx_orders_user ON orders (user_id, created_at DESC)
       INCLUDE (status, total);        -- payload cols: not searchable, but returned

-- EXPLAIN now shows "Index Only Scan" instead of "Index Scan + Heap Fetch".
-- Cost: the index is bigger, so writes and RAM pressure go up. Always a trade.

This is worth a sentence in an interview because it's the concrete answer to "how would you make this hot read faster without adding a cache?" — "make the index cover the query so it never touches the heap." In DynamoDB the equivalent is projecting attributes into a GSI: same idea, same trade (a projected GSI is a second full copy of those attributes, and you pay write capacity for it on every write).

5c. Why you can't index your way out of a sharding problem

This is the point of the whole section, and it's the transition into 04.

An index solves a search problem: "this data is on this machine, find the right rows fast." It does nothing whatsoever for a capacity problem: "this data no longer fits on this machine," or "this machine cannot absorb this many writes per second." Indexes don't add CPU, don't add RAM, don't add disk, and — crucially — they make writes worse, so if your bottleneck is write throughput, adding an index moves you in the wrong direction.

SymptomActual bottleneckThe fix
One query is slow, table scan in EXPLAINMissing indexIndex it. Cheap, correct.
Reads are slow across the board, CPU pinned on the primaryRead capacityCache (Redis) + read replicas
Writes are slow, disk I/O saturated, replication lag climbingWrite capacity on one nodeShard. Or move the write-heavy table to an LSM store. An index makes this worse.
Dataset exceeds the biggest instance you can buyStorage capacityShard. Or tier cold data to S3.
"Indexing is a search-cost fix, not a capacity fix. If the primary is saturated on writes, another index makes it worse — every insert now maintains one more B-tree. At that point the answer is partitioning, or an engine whose write path is cheaper. That's the next conversation." Delivering this cleanly is one of the fastest ways to show you can tell classes of problem apart, which is exactly what 7-YOE grading rewards.

6. ACID & isolation levels

ACID is four promises: Atomicity (all of the transaction or none of it), Consistency (the DB's own constraints hold before and after — note this is not the "consistency" in CAP, a genuinely unfortunate naming collision worth pointing out in an interview), Isolation (concurrent transactions don't see each other's mess), and Durability (once committed, it survives a crash — this is what the WAL and its fsync buy you).

In practice, the only letter with real dials on it is I. Isolation is expensive, so databases let you buy less of it. The levels are defined by which anomalies they still permit:

AnomalyWhat actually happens
Dirty readYou read a row another transaction wrote but hasn't committed. It then rolls back — you acted on data that never existed.
Non-repeatable readYou read a row twice inside one transaction and get two different values, because someone committed an update in between.
Phantom readYou run the same WHERE query twice and the second run returns extra rows, because someone inserted rows matching your predicate.
Lost updateTwo transactions read the same value, both compute a new one, both write. One update silently vanishes. (Classic: "increment the seat count.")
Write skewThe subtle one. Two transactions each read a set of rows, each check an invariant that currently holds, and each write a different row. Individually legal, together they break the invariant. (Two doctors each check "someone else is on call," each go off call.) Only Serializable prevents this.
Isolation levelDirty readNon-repeatable readPhantomWrite skewNotes
Read UncommittedPossiblePossiblePossiblePossibleEffectively useless. Postgres doesn't even implement it (it silently gives you Read Committed).
Read CommittedNoPossiblePossiblePossiblePostgres and Oracle default. Each statement sees a fresh snapshot. Fine for 95% of application code.
Repeatable ReadNoNoPossible in the standard; prevented in PostgresPossibleMySQL/InnoDB default. The whole transaction sees one snapshot. Postgres implements this as true snapshot isolation, so phantoms are gone — but write skew remains.
SerializableNoNoNoNoThe result is as if transactions ran one at a time. Postgres uses SSI (optimistic): it lets them run and aborts one with a serialization failure — so your code must be prepared to retry. Costs throughput.

6a. Optimistic vs pessimistic concurrency

Two ways to stop two people from grabbing the same row. You must be able to name both and pick one with a reason.

PessimisticOptimistic
IdeaAssume conflict. Lock first, then work. SELECT … FOR UPDATE.Assume no conflict. Work, then verify nothing changed at write time (a version column or a WHERE version = ? guard).
CostOther writers block. Throughput collapses under contention; risk of deadlock; a long transaction holding a lock is an outage.Other writers proceed. But on conflict the loser's work is wasted and must retry — under high contention you can livelock on retries.
Use whenContention is high and conflicts are the norm — the last 10 seats of a hot concert.Contention is low — editing your own profile, updating a document nobody else is touching.
 
-- Pessimistic: nobody else can touch this seat until I commit.
BEGIN;
SELECT status FROM seats WHERE id = 42 FOR UPDATE;   -- row lock held till COMMIT
UPDATE seats SET status = 'HELD', held_by = 7 WHERE id = 42;
COMMIT;

-- Optimistic: no lock. The WHERE clause IS the check.
-- Returns 0 rows updated if someone beat me to it -> I retry or fail the request.
UPDATE seats SET status = 'HELD', held_by = 7, version = version + 1
 WHERE id = 42 AND status = 'FREE' AND version = 3;
The clause that separates you from every candidate who just says "I'd use a transaction": "A uniqueness constraint or a conditional UPDATE is a stronger guarantee than any application-level check, because the database enforces it under concurrency and my if statement doesn't. I'd make the invariant impossible to violate at the schema level and let the write fail, rather than trying to be careful in code." That single idea prevents overselling, double-charging, and duplicate accounts, and it is the correct answer to a startling number of interview follow-ups.
Forward reference — this becomes the entire problem in 14 — Ticket Booking. Ten thousand people hit "buy" on the last seat at the same millisecond. Read Committed and a naive "check then write" oversells the seat. Pessimistic locking is correct but serializes the flash sale and tanks throughput. Optimistic concurrency is fast but 9,999 people get a retry storm. The interview is entirely about which of these you pick and why. Everything on this page is setup for that page.
Don't say "I'd use Serializable" as a reflex. It's correct and it's also a throughput cliff — and in Postgres it makes transactions abort under contention, so if your application code doesn't retry on SQLSTATE 40001, you've just turned a race condition into a 500. If you claim Serializable, be ready to say "and my write path has a bounded retry with jitter around it."

7. Replication & read replicas

Replication exists for three different reasons and candidates routinely conflate them: durability (a node dies, the data doesn't), availability (a node dies, the service doesn't), and read scale (spread reads across copies). Only the third one helps with load — and it's the one that introduces the bug in 7b.

7a. Leader–follower (single-leader)

The default everywhere: Postgres streaming replication, MySQL replication, MongoDB replica sets, Redis replicas.

                       ┌──────────────┐
   writes ────────────▶│    LEADER    │  (the only node that accepts writes)
                       └──────┬───────┘
                              │  WAL / binlog stream (the replication log)
                 ┌────────────┼────────────┐
                 ▼            ▼            ▼
           ┌──────────┐ ┌──────────┐ ┌──────────┐
   reads ─▶│ FOLLOWER │ │ FOLLOWER │ │ FOLLOWER │   ← may be MILLISECONDS
           └──────────┘ └──────────┘ └──────────┘     to SECONDS behind

All writes go to the leader. The leader ships its write-ahead log to the followers, which replay it. Reads can be served by any node. There are no write conflicts, ever — that's the entire appeal of single-leader, and it's why it's the default. If the leader dies, you promote a follower (failover), which is where the operational pain lives: detecting the failure, choosing a new leader without split-brain, and accepting that any writes the old leader had accepted but not yet replicated are lost.

Replication modeLeader waits for…DurabilityLatencyReality
AsynchronousNothing. Acks the client immediately, ships the log later.Weakest — a leader crash loses recently-acked writesBestThe common default. Fine for most workloads; unacceptable for money.
SynchronousAll replicas to confirmStrongestWorst — and one slow replica stalls every writeAlmost nobody does full sync; a single sick node takes the cluster down.
Semi-synchronousOne (or a quorum of) replicas to confirmStrong enough — a committed write exists on ≥ 2 machinesMiddle — one extra network round tripThe practical answer. Postgres synchronous_commit with ANY 1. Say this one.
"I'd run semi-synchronous replication: the leader waits for one replica to acknowledge before it acks the client. That guarantees a committed write exists on two machines, so a leader failure doesn't lose it, and it costs me one network round trip on the write path instead of stalling on the slowest replica. For the ledger table specifically I would not run fully async — losing an acknowledged payment is not a trade I'd make to save 2ms."

7b. Replication lag, and the bug you have already shipped

A follower is behind. Usually by single-digit milliseconds; under a heavy write burst or a long-running query on the replica, by seconds or minutes. And if you write to the leader and then read from a follower, you get this: 

  t=0ms   POST /comments        ──▶ LEADER    (write committed) ✓
  t=5ms   201 Created           ◀──
  t=8ms   GET /post/123/comments ──▶ FOLLOWER  (hasn't replayed the write yet)
  t=12ms  200 OK  → [ ]         ◀──            THE USER'S OWN COMMENT IS GONE

You have seen this bug. As a React developer, this is the one where the user posts a comment, the UI shows it (optimistic update), they hit refresh, and it vanishes — then reappears a second later. Frontend teams usually paper over it with an optimistic cache entry, and the actual cause is a read replica that hadn't caught up. It has a name: read-your-own-writes (or read-after-write consistency), and naming it correctly in an interview lands extremely well because it's simultaneously a distributed-systems concept and a bug the interviewer has personally debugged.

The fixes, in the order you should offer them:

FixHowCost
Read from the leader for your own dataAfter a user writes, route that user's reads to the leader for N seconds (sticky, e.g. a cookie/session flag).Simple, effective, and the standard answer. Adds leader load; needs care with multi-device users.
Read-your-writes tokenThe write returns the leader's log position (LSN); the client sends it back; the read waits for a replica that has caught up to that LSN, or falls back to the leader.Correct and precise. More plumbing. This is what "monotonic reads via a version token" looks like in practice.
Return the written objectThe POST response contains the created comment; the client renders it without re-fetching.Free, and you already do this in React. Doesn't fix the hard refresh case on its own.
Just don't use replicas for that pathSend all reads that must be fresh to the leader; use replicas for analytics, feeds, search, dashboards.The honest one. Classify your reads by freshness requirement — that's the senior move.
"I'll add read replicas to scale reads" is a correct sentence that must always be followed by the second sentence: "and I'll accept replication lag, which means reads-after-write must go to the leader." Candidates who add replicas and never mention lag get a follow-up question they can't answer, and it's the same question every time: "the user posts a comment and immediately refreshes — what do they see?"

7c. Multi-leader and leaderless AWARENESS

You need to know these exist, know what they buy, and know why you're not choosing them. That's genuinely enough.

  • Multi-leader. Several nodes accept writes (typically one leader per region, or an offline-capable client acting as a leader). Buys you local write latency and survives a region outage. The price is brutal: write conflicts are now possible and you must resolve them — last-write-wins (silently loses data, and depends on clocks you can't trust), application-defined merge, or CRDTs (see 23). Use it for multi-region write-local systems and calendar/offline sync. Avoid it if you can.
  • Leaderless (Dynamo-style). No leader at all. The client (or a coordinator) writes to N replicas and waits for W acks; reads query N replicas and wait for R responses. If R + W > N, the read set and write set overlap, so you're guaranteed to see the latest write. This is Cassandra and DynamoDB's core mechanic — consistency becomes a per-query dial. Stale replicas are healed by read repair (fix them when a read notices they're behind), anti-entropy background repair, and hinted handoff (a live node holds writes destined for a dead one). Quorums get their proper treatment in 05 and get built in 08.
The one-liner that makes the leaderless model click: R + W > N with N=3, W=2, R=2 means any read touches at least one node that took the write. Set W=1 for blazing writes and weak reads; set W=3, R=1 for a read-heavy workload that tolerates slow writes. The consistency is a config value. That's the whole Dynamo idea.

8. CAP and PACELC

CAP is the most-cited and least-understood theorem in system design interviews, and I'm going to be blunt with you about it because being blunt about it is the senior signal.

8a. What CAP actually says

Consistency (every read sees the latest write — really linearizability), Availability (every request to a non-failing node gets a non-error response), Partition tolerance (the system keeps working when the network drops messages between nodes). The theorem: you can have at most two of the three.

Here's the part everyone gets wrong. P is not optional. Networks partition. Cables get cut, switches fail, a rack loses connectivity, a GC pause makes a node look dead. If your data is on more than one machine, you will experience partitions, and "we chose CA" is not a design — it's a description of a single-node system. So the theorem collapses to something much smaller and much more useful:

The only correct way to invoke CAP: "When a network partition happens — and it will — you have exactly one choice: either the minority side keeps serving requests and risks returning stale or divergent data (AP), or it refuses to serve and returns errors until the partition heals (CP). There is no third option. For this system, [X], because [consequence of being wrong]." Then give the consequence: "a stale like-count is invisible to the user, so AP. A double-sold seat is a refund, an angry customer and a support ticket, so CP — during a partition I'd rather return 503 than sell the same seat twice."
CP — choose ConsistencyAP — choose Availability
During a partitionMinority nodes reject reads/writes → errors / timeoutsAll nodes keep serving → stale or divergent data
You're saying"Wrong is worse than down.""Down is worse than stale."
ExamplesPostgres/Spanner with sync replication · ZooKeeper/etcd · HBase · MongoDB (default) · anything holding money, inventory, or a lockCassandra · DynamoDB (default eventual reads) · Riak · DNS · a CDN · a shopping cart · a like counter
ReconciliationNone needed — you never divergedRequired — read repair, vector clocks, LWW, or app-level merge

8b. PACELC — the more useful lens

CAP only tells you what happens during the (rare) partition. It's silent about the other 99.99% of the time, which is when your system actually runs. PACELC fixes that: 

  if (Partition)  →  choose  Availability  or  Consistency     [ P: A / C ]
  Else            →  choose  Latency       or  Consistency     [ E: L / C ]

The else clause is the one that earns your salary. Even with a perfectly healthy network, consistency costs latency. If a read must reflect the latest write, it must reach a node that definitely has it — a leader, or a quorum — and that's a network round trip you could have avoided by reading a nearby possibly-stale replica. There is no way out of this; it's the speed of light plus the definition of "latest."

SystemPACELC classificationWhat it means in plain English
Cassandra / DynamoDB (default)PA/ELStay up during a partition; be fast normally. Both times, you accept staleness.
Postgres w/ sync replicationPC/ECRefuse rather than diverge; pay the round trip for correct reads. Correctness over everything.
Google SpannerPC/ECConsistent always — and it pays for it in write latency (TrueTime commit-wait). Real ACID at global scale is not free.
MongoDB (default)PC/EC (tunable)Reads from the primary; you can dial down to secondary reads and become EL.
DNSPA/ELAlways answers, possibly with a stale record until the TTL expires. That's a feature.
The upgrade move, and it's a genuine differentiator: "CAP is a starting point, not a design — it only describes the partition case, and partitions are rare. The lens I actually use is PACELC: during a partition, availability or consistency; and else — which is nearly all the time — latency or consistency. Most of my real decisions live in that else clause. Reading from a nearby replica is 2ms and possibly stale; reading from the leader across a region is 80ms and correct. That's the trade I'm actually making, every request."
Do not say "Cassandra is AP so it's eventually consistent, therefore I can't use it for anything important." Two errors. First, Cassandra's consistency is per-query tunable — QUORUM reads and writes with R + W > N give you strong-ish consistency when you need it, at the cost of latency and availability during a partition. Second, "AP vs CP" is a property of the configuration, not an immutable badge on the logo. Nuance here reads as experience.
Consistency in CAP ≠ Consistency in ACID. CAP's C is linearizability — a distributed-systems property about whether reads see the latest write. ACID's C is "the database's own constraints and invariants hold." They share a letter and nothing else. Noticing this out loud, once, costs you four seconds and is a clean expertise marker.

9. Consistency models

"Eventual consistency" is where most candidates' vocabulary stops, and it's far too coarse. There's a whole ladder between "strong" and "eventual," and every rung has a user-visible symptom. Learn the symptoms — that's how you'll recognize which model a requirement is actually demanding, and it's the vocabulary interviewers listen for.

ModelThe guaranteeWhat the user sees when you don't have itCost
Strong / linearizable Every read returns the most recent committed write. The system behaves as if there were one copy of the data. — Highest. Reads must hit the leader or a quorum. Latency, and unavailability during partitions.
Read-your-own-writes
(read-after-write)		 A user always sees their own writes. Says nothing about other users' writes. "I posted a comment, refreshed, and it's gone." The bug from §7b. Enraging, and the #1 real-world consistency complaint. Cheap. Sticky-route that user's reads to the leader, or use a version token.
Monotonic reads Once you've seen a value, you never see an older one. Time doesn't move backwards for a given reader. "I saw 43 likes, refreshed, now it says 41, refreshed again, back to 43." Caused by bouncing between replicas with different lag. Looks like a broken app. Cheap. Pin each user to one replica (hash the user id to a replica).
Consistent prefix / causal Causally-related writes are seen in order. If B was written after (and because of) A, nobody sees B without A. "I can see the reply before the message it's replying to." Comments appear out of order; an answer shows up above its question. Moderate. Needs causal metadata (version vectors / logical clocks) or careful partitioning so related writes share a partition.
Eventual If writes stop, all replicas eventually converge. Says nothing about when, or about what you see meanwhile. All of the above symptoms, potentially at once. But: if the value is a like count, nobody cares. Cheapest. Read any replica, no coordination.

The engineering move is to classify each access pattern by the weakest model it can tolerate, not to pick one model for the whole system. Real systems are consistent in some places and eventual in others, deliberately:

DataWeakest acceptable modelWhy
Account balance / seat inventory / payment statusStrongBeing wrong costs money and trust. Read from the leader.
Your own profile edit, your own posted commentRead-your-writesOther users can lag by a second; you cannot.
A chat thread / a comment threadCausalReplies must not precede their parents. Partition by thread id and it falls out for free.
Like count, view count, follower countEventualNobody can tell 10,412 from 10,409, and the count is being aggregated asynchronously anyway.
News feed, search results, recommendationsEventualFreshness is a nice-to-have; a 30-second-old feed is a fine feed.
The sentence that shows you think in gradients rather than absolutes: "I don't need one consistency model for the system — I need one per access pattern. The seat inventory is strongly consistent and reads from the leader. The user's own bookings need read-your-writes, so I sticky-route them after a write. The 'currently trending events' list is eventual and served from a replica or a cache. Picking strong consistency everywhere would work and would cost me an order of magnitude in latency and availability for no user-visible benefit."

10. Blob / object storage

Any design involving images, video, documents, avatars, or user uploads has a trap in it, and the trap has a one-line solution you must state before the interviewer asks: never put files in your database.

10a. The metadata/blob split

A 500 MB video stored as a BYTEA/BLOB column destroys your database. Every backup now copies it. Every replica ships it over the wire. It evicts your actual hot rows from the buffer cache. Your SELECT * that you thought was cheap now streams half a gigabyte. And your database — an expensive, hard-to-scale, transactional system — is being used as a very bad file server.

The split: the bytes go to object storage; a pointer to the bytes goes in the database.

  ┌─────────────────────┐          ┌───────────────────────────────┐
  │  Postgres           │          │  S3  (object storage)         │
  │  videos             │          │                               │
  │  ─────────────────  │          │  s3://vids/9f2a/master.mp4    │
  │  id, owner_id,      │          │  s3://vids/9f2a/1080p.m3u8    │
  │  title, duration,   │─────────▶│  s3://vids/9f2a/720p.m3u8     │
  │  status, created_at │  the KEY │  ...                          │
  │  s3_key ◀── pointer │          │                               │
  └─────────────────────┘          └───────────────┬───────────────┘
        queryable, small                           │
        transactional                              ▼
                                                 CDN ──▶ user
                                    (bytes NEVER pass through your API)

Object storage (S3, GCS, Azure Blob) is a different animal from a database and it's good at exactly one thing:

PropertyObject storage
InterfacePUT(key, bytes) / GET(key) over HTTP. That's basically it.
DurabilityExtremely high (S3 quotes eleven nines) — objects replicated across availability zones automatically.
CostVery cheap per GB, and tiered — hot (Standard) → warm (Infrequent Access) → cold (Glacier). Lifecycle rules move objects down automatically.
ScaleEffectively unbounded. Objects up to terabytes. No capacity planning.
Can't doQuery the contents. Transactions. Efficient listing at scale. Partial updates (you rewrite the object).
ConsistencyStrong read-after-write for PUTs of new objects (since 2020); historically eventual, which is why old designs are full of "wait and retry" hacks.

10b. Presigned URLs — get the bytes off your servers

The naive upload flow is client → your API → S3. That's wrong at scale: every byte of every upload traverses your application servers, burning their bandwidth, memory, and request-handler time on work that adds no value. A 2 GB video upload occupying an app-server thread for four minutes is a self-inflicted outage.

A presigned URL is a time-limited, cryptographically signed URL that grants permission to PUT (or GET) one specific object key. Your API mints it — a cheap signing operation, no bytes involved — and the client talks to S3 directly. 

  1. POST /videos            ──▶ API: create row (status=PENDING),
                                      generate s3_key, sign an upload URL
  2. { videoId, uploadUrl }  ◀──      (URL expires in 15 min, PUT only, this key only)

  3. PUT <uploadUrl>         ──▶ S3   (client → S3 DIRECTLY. Your API is not involved.
                                       Gigabytes never touch your servers.)

  4. S3 event / client POST  ──▶ API: mark status=UPLOADED, enqueue transcode job

The same trick runs in reverse for downloads: a presigned GET URL (or a signed CDN URL) lets a private object be served straight from S3/CloudFront to the user, with expiry, without your API proxying the bytes or the object being public.

Say this the moment any file appears in a design, before you're asked: "The blob goes in S3, the metadata goes in Postgres, and the DB row holds the S3 key. Uploads use a presigned URL so the bytes go client-to-S3 directly and never traverse my API servers; downloads go through the CDN with a signed URL. The database stays small, queryable and transactional — which is what it's for." That's three sentences and it closes out an entire category of follow-up questions.
The consistency hole in the split: the DB write and the S3 write are two systems with no shared transaction. The upload can succeed and the DB update can fail, leaving an orphaned object; or the row can say UPLOADED when S3 has nothing. The fix is the standard one — make the DB row the source of truth with an explicit status state machine (PENDING → UPLOADED → PROCESSING → READY), drive transitions from S3 events, and run a reconciliation sweep that garbage-collects objects with no READY row. Naming this before the interviewer does is a strong move; it's the same class of problem as the outbox pattern in 05.
Forward reference: this is the backbone of 16 — Design Video Streaming. Upload via presigned URL → object lands in S3 → event fires → transcoding workers produce HLS/DASH renditions at multiple bitrates → those land back in S3 → CDN serves the segments. The database holds nothing but rows of metadata and S3 keys. Every media design you'll ever be asked is a variation on that pipeline.

Interview Q&A

These are the questions, close to verbatim. Answer them out loud, standing up, before you read the answer.

"SQL or NoSQL for this system? Why?"

Never answer the question as asked. Reframe it into access patterns first, then answer.

"Let me answer that from the access patterns rather than from the label. I have three patterns: fetch one item by primary key at 50k QPS; list a user's items, up to a few hundred, sorted by time; and a monthly report that joins across users and items. The first two are key-value and partition-range patterns — trivially served by Dynamo or Cassandra. The third is a join, which they can't do, but it's low-volume and not latency-sensitive, so I'd serve it from a separate analytics store fed by CDC rather than let it dictate my primary store."

"Given that, the primary is a wide-column store partitioned by user id. If the product told me tomorrow they wanted flexible querying on the hot path, that decision flips to Postgres and I take on a sharding problem later."

Requirement → decision → cost → falsification condition. Four beats. That's the shape of every good answer here.

"Why would you pick Cassandra over Postgres for a write-heavy workload? Be specific — what's actually different?"

The whole §4 payoff. Do not say "because it's distributed." Say the write path.

"It's the storage engine. Postgres uses a B-tree: a write locates the correct leaf page and updates it in place — that's a random I/O per write, plus a WAL append, plus maintaining every secondary index. Cassandra uses an LSM-tree: a write is an append to a commit log plus an insert into an in-memory memtable, and nothing more. When the memtable fills it's flushed to disk as an immutable sorted file — sequential write, no seek. Updates and deletes are also just appends, with a newer timestamp or a tombstone. So writes are bounded by sequential disk bandwidth rather than random I/O."

"What I pay for it: read amplification, because a key may live in the memtable or in any of several SSTables — bloom filters prune most of that, but the read path is variable. Compaction runs in the background merging SSTables and it costs real I/O, which shows up as p99 latency spikes. And I lose joins and multi-row transactions. For an ingest-dominated workload like message history or telemetry, that's a trade I'll take."

"You said you'd add read replicas. A user posts a comment and immediately refreshes the page. What do they see?"

This is the trap, and it's asked constantly. The wrong answer is "their comment."

"Possibly nothing — that's the read-your-own-writes problem. The write went to the leader; the refresh read hit a follower that hadn't replayed the WAL yet, so the comment isn't there. It'll appear a second later, which is worse than a clean failure because it looks like the app lost their data."

"Three fixes, and I'd use the first: after a user writes, sticky-route that user's reads to the leader for a few seconds. Alternatively, have the write return the leader's log position and have the read wait for a replica that has caught up to it. And at the UI layer, return the created object in the POST response so the immediate render doesn't need a re-fetch at all — but that doesn't survive a hard refresh, so it's a complement, not a fix."

"More generally: I'd classify reads by freshness requirement. Reads of your own recent writes go to the leader; feeds, search and analytics go to replicas."

"Explain CAP. Which do you choose?"

Answer it, then immediately upgrade it — showing that you know its limits is worth more than reciting it.

"P isn't optional — if the data is on more than one machine, partitions will happen — so CAP really only says: during a partition, do you serve possibly-stale data (AP) or return errors (CP)? For a like counter, AP: stale is invisible. For seat inventory, CP: I'd rather return a 503 than sell the same seat twice."

"Honestly, though, CAP is a starting point, not a design. Partitions are rare; the trade I make on every single request is the PACELC one — else, in normal operation, latency versus consistency. Reading from a nearby replica is 2ms and possibly stale; reading from the leader is a round trip and correct. That's the decision that actually shapes the system."

Also worth landing: "and CAP's C isn't ACID's C — one is linearizability, the other is constraint satisfaction."

"Two users try to book the last seat at the same instant. Walk me through preventing a double-booking."

Show the naive bug first — it proves you understand the race — then the fixes with their costs.

"The naive version reads the seat, sees FREE, and writes HELD. Under Read Committed, both transactions read FREE before either writes, and both succeed — that's a lost update, and the seat is sold twice. An if in application code cannot prevent this; the check and the write aren't atomic."

"Three real options. Pessimistic: SELECT … FOR UPDATE locks the row, so the second transaction blocks until the first commits and then sees HELD. Correct, but it serializes access — under flash-sale contention that's a throughput cliff, and a long-held lock is an outage. Optimistic: a conditional update — UPDATE seats SET status='HELD' WHERE id=42 AND status='FREE'. It's atomic in the engine; exactly one transaction gets 1 row affected and the other gets 0 and fails cleanly. Cheap, no locking, ideal when contention is low. Constraint: a unique index on (seat_id) in a holds table makes the double-booking literally unrepresentable — the second insert violates the constraint and the DB rejects it."

"For a hot flash sale I'd combine them: a queue or a token gate in front to shed the thundering herd, and an optimistic conditional update at the DB as the correctness backstop. The general principle: make the invariant impossible to violate at the schema level rather than trying to be careful in application code."

"What's the difference between Read Committed, Repeatable Read and Serializable? What breaks at each?"

"Read Committed — Postgres's default — means you never read uncommitted data, but each statement gets a fresh snapshot, so reading the same row twice in one transaction can give two different values. Non-repeatable reads and phantoms are both possible."

"Repeatable Read — MySQL's default — pins one snapshot for the whole transaction, so re-reading a row is stable. The SQL standard still allows phantoms here; Postgres implements it as true snapshot isolation and prevents them. But write skew survives both: two transactions each read a set, each verify an invariant that currently holds, each write a different row, and together they break it. Classic example: two doctors each check that someone else is on call, and both go off call."

"Serializable is the only level that stops write skew — the outcome is as if the transactions ran one at a time. Postgres does it optimistically with SSI, meaning it lets them run and aborts one with a serialization failure. So the catch is that choosing Serializable is also choosing to write retry logic; if my code doesn't retry on 40001, I've converted a race into a 500."

"How do you store user-uploaded videos?"

"The bytes never go in the database. The video goes to S3; the database holds a row with the metadata — owner, title, duration, status — and the S3 key. Putting a 500 MB blob in Postgres poisons the backups, the replication stream, and the buffer cache, and it uses an expensive transactional system as a bad file server."

"The upload itself doesn't go through my API either — I mint a presigned S3 URL, scoped to one object key, PUT-only, expiring in fifteen minutes, and the client uploads straight to S3. Gigabytes never touch an app server. S3 fires an event when the object lands, and that drives the row from PENDING to UPLOADED and enqueues transcoding. Downloads go through the CDN with signed URLs."

"The one gap: the DB write and the S3 write aren't in the same transaction, so I'd model the row as an explicit state machine and run a reconciliation job that garbage-collects orphaned objects and re-drives stuck rows."

"The database is slow. Walk me through what you'd do."

Interviewers use this to see whether you can distinguish classes of problem instead of reaching for a cache immediately.

"First I'd find out what 'slow' means — is it one query, or everything? If it's one query, I'd look at the plan: a sequential scan on a filtered column is a missing index, and that's a cheap fix."

"If it's everything, I'd separate the read problem from the write problem, because they have different fixes. Reads saturating the primary: cache the hot keys in Redis, then add read replicas, and accept replication lag on the paths where it's tolerable. Writes saturating the primary: indexes make this worse, not better — every insert maintains every index. That's a capacity problem, and the answers are partitioning the table, moving the write-heavy table to a store with a cheaper write path, or batching writes through a queue to smooth the peak."

"The distinction I'd want to make explicit: indexing is a search-cost fix. It does nothing for a capacity problem. You cannot index your way out of needing to shard."

"Is 'schemaless' actually an advantage?"

"There's no such thing as schemaless — there's only 'where does the schema live.' In Postgres it lives in the database, is enforced on every write, and one migration fixes every row. In MongoDB or Dynamo it lives in my application code, is enforced nowhere, and 'migration' means my code handles v1 and v2 documents simultaneously — forever, or until I write a backfill."

"It's a genuine advantage in two cases: when the shape is genuinely heterogeneous — a product catalog where every category has different attributes — and when you're moving fast and the cost of a lock-taking ALTER TABLE on a huge table is real. It's a liability everywhere else, because the enforcement doesn't disappear, it just moves to the place where it's weakest. And if I want the flexible case inside Postgres, JSONB gives me most of it with an escape hatch back to constraints."

"Your like counts sometimes go 43 → 41 → 43 on refresh. What's happening and how do you fix it?"

A model-recognition question in disguise. Name the model.

"That's a monotonic reads violation. Successive reads are landing on different replicas with different replication lag, so a fresher value is followed by a staler one — time appears to run backwards for that reader. Eventual consistency permits this; it just looks like a broken app."

"The fix is cheap: pin a given reader to a single replica — hash the user id or session id to a replica so their reads are consistently served from the same node. They may see stale data, but it never goes backwards. If the counter matters more than that, read it from the leader or from a Redis counter that's the authority for that value."

"When would you NOT use a cache in front of the database?"

Tests whether you add components reflexively. 06 goes deep; here's the storage-side answer.

"When the workload is write-heavy rather than read-heavy — a cache doesn't help writes and every write now also has to invalidate, which is a second correctness problem for no gain. When the data must be strongly consistent, like a balance or seat inventory — a cache is a second copy and a second source of staleness, and stale money is a bug. When the read distribution is uniform rather than skewed — a cache only pays if a small hot set serves most requests; hitting a huge keyspace uniformly gives you a low hit rate and you've added a network hop and an operational dependency for nothing."

"And there's an honest floor: at a few hundred QPS with a warm buffer cache, Postgres is the cache. Adding Redis there is complexity with no benefit, and I'd say so rather than draw the box because it looks impressive."

"You have 10 TB of data and 50k writes/second. Now what?"

The numbers are the point. Compute before you decide.

"50k writes/second is past what I'd ask one Postgres primary to do comfortably — it's not impossible with batching and a fast disk, but it's the wrong shape of problem to fight. Two questions decide it. First: is the write path append-only — events, messages, telemetry — with no cross-row invariants? If yes, this is an LSM workload and I'd use Cassandra or DynamoDB, partitioned on whatever key the reads use, and the 10 TB spreads across nodes as a side effect. Second: does it have invariants — money, inventory, uniqueness? Then I can't just move to an eventually-consistent store, and I'd shard Postgres by tenant or entity id, keeping every transaction inside a single shard so I never need a distributed transaction."

"In practice it's usually both: the transactional core stays relational and small, and the high-volume append-only stream goes to a store built for it. The mistake would be forcing one engine to do both jobs badly."

Then hand off to 04: "and how I pick the partition key is the next conversation — get that wrong and you've built a hot shard."

What to remember

The night before the interview, read only this.

  • Access patterns first, store second. Never name a database before you've named the queries and the numbers. The grammar of the answer is: "because the dominant access pattern is X and it needs Y, I'll use Z — and I'm accepting that this makes W harder." The last clause is the one you get paid for.
  • "NoSQL because it scales" is a rejection. The real trade is flexible queries vs horizontal write scale. Relational buys you questions you haven't thought of yet, and you pay with a sharding problem later. NoSQL buys you linear write scale, and you pay by having to know every question up front.
  • Postgres is the honest default and it scales further than candidates think. Introduce a second store only when you can name the pattern Postgres can't serve.
  • Schemaless is a lie. There's always a schema; the only question is whether it lives in the database or in your application code, unenforced.
  • B-tree vs LSM is the senior answer to "why Cassandra?" B-tree: find the page, update in place, random I/O. LSM: append to a commit log, insert into the memtable, flush immutable sorted SSTables, compact in the background — writes never seek. You pay in read amplification (bloom filters help), compaction I/O, and p99 spikes. It's not magic; it's the write path.
  • Indexes make reads fast and writes slow. Every index is a second copy of your data that every write must maintain. Indexing is a search-cost fix — it does nothing for a capacity problem, and you cannot index your way out of needing to shard.
  • Isolation levels: Read Committed (Postgres default) allows non-repeatable reads and phantoms. Repeatable Read (MySQL default) pins one snapshot. Only Serializable stops write skew — and in Postgres it aborts transactions, so you must retry.
  • Pessimistic locks under high contention, optimistic (conditional update / version column) under low. Best of all: a database constraint, which makes the invalid state unrepresentable — stronger than any if in your code.
  • Read replicas scale reads and introduce lag. Always say the second half. The bug is read-your-own-writes: post a comment, refresh, it's gone. Fix by sticky-routing that user's reads to the leader after a write. Prefer semi-synchronous replication: one replica acks, so a committed write exists on two machines.
  • CAP is a starting point, not a design. P isn't optional, so it reduces to: during a partition, stale data (AP) or errors (CP)? PACELC is the useful lens — else, in normal operation, latency vs consistency. That's the trade you make on every request, not once a year.
  • Consistency is per-access-pattern, not per-system. Strong for money and inventory; read-your-writes for a user's own actions; causal for threaded conversations; eventual for counts and feeds. Know the user-visible symptom of each: "my comment vanished" (read-your-writes), "the count went backwards" (monotonic reads), "the reply appears before the message" (causal).
  • Blobs go in S3, pointers go in the DB. Presigned URLs so the bytes never touch your API servers; CDN for delivery; an explicit status state machine plus a reconciliation sweep, because the DB write and the S3 write aren't in one transaction.
  • The one habit: after every storage decision, say what it costs. A design without a stated cost reads as a guess, even when it's right.
If you take one sentence from this page into the room, take this one: "I don't pick a database — I pick an access pattern, and the database follows." Everything else here is an elaboration of that.
