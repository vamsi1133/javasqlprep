27 — Trade-off Flashcards

The last page. A revision deck of "why X over Y" — the deciding factor, and the sentence to say out loud.

Why this matters in the interview

At 7 years in, you are not graded on knowing that two options exist — the interviewer assumes that. You are graded on picking one and defending it in a sentence. The weak answer lists both sides ("SQL is consistent, NoSQL scales") and stops, leaving the interviewer to do the choosing. The strong answer names the one variable that decides it ("do my access patterns fit a fixed schema with joins, or are they a single denormalized read by key?") and commits. Every card below is built the same way: the matchup, the deciding factor, and the say-it-out-loud line that lands the point. Reveal the answer, then cover it and try to reproduce the deciding factor from memory.

This is your night-before deck. It is deliberately dense and repetitive — the same theme ("what does this bound?", "what's the one number that flips it?", "name the race you're accepting") recurs across storage, consistency, caching, and messaging, because it is the same senior move wearing different clothes. Where a card touches a topic you can go deeper on, it links the tutorial in parentheses. Don't read this to learn the material for the first time — read the earlier pages for that. Read this to rehearse the decision until it's reflex.

Data & storage

SQL vs NoSQL — when do you pick each?

Deciding factor: your access patterns, not your data volume. Do you need ad-hoc queries, joins across entities, and multi-row transactions? SQL. Do you have one or two known read patterns, each satisfiable by a single lookup on a partition key? NoSQL. "It's big data" is not the reason — a sharded Postgres holds plenty, and DynamoDB is chosen for its predictable single-digit-ms key lookups and horizontal write scaling, not its capacity.

Say it: "I'll start with Postgres because I want transactions and my access patterns aren't frozen yet. I'd move a specific high-volume, single-access-pattern table — the feed, the session store, the event log — to a key-value or wide-column store when its write throughput or its need to scale horizontally outgrows one primary." The senior move is that it's a per-table decision, not a religion. (See 03.)

Normalized vs denormalized — which schema?

Deciding factor: read:write ratio and how expensive the join is at read time. Normalize to make writes cheap and correct (one fact, one place, no update anomalies); denormalize to make reads cheap (the data is pre-joined, one lookup returns the whole view). Normalized is the default because correctness is the default; denormalize when a specific read is hot and the join is killing you.

Say it: "I'll normalize the source of truth and denormalize into a read model when the read pattern demands it — accepting that I now own keeping the copy in sync, usually via an event or CDC. Denormalization trades write complexity and storage for read latency." This is the same trade-off as fan-out-on-write (10) and materialized views. (See 03.)

B-tree vs LSM-tree — which storage engine?

Deciding factor: is the workload read-heavy or write-heavy? B-trees (Postgres, MySQL/InnoDB) update in place — great reads, random-write I/O, so writes are the ceiling. LSM-trees (Cassandra, RocksDB, LevelDB) buffer writes in memory and flush sequentially — brilliant write throughput, but reads may touch several SSTables (mitigated by bloom filters, 06) and background compaction steals I/O.

Say it: "Write-heavy ingest — time series, event logs, a feed — I want an LSM engine for sequential writes. Read-heavy, transactional, range-scan work I want a B-tree. The one-liner is: B-tree optimizes reads and pays on writes; LSM optimizes writes and pays on reads and compaction." (See 03.)

Row store vs column store — OLTP or OLAP?

Deciding factor: do you read whole rows or whole columns? Row stores keep a record's fields together — perfect for "fetch this order" (OLTP). Column stores keep each column together — perfect for "sum revenue over 2 billion rows" (OLAP), because you scan only the columns you need and they compress viciously (adjacent values are similar).

Say it: "Transactional serving path → row store. Analytics, aggregations, dashboards → columnar (Redshift, ClickHouse, Parquet). Don't run analytics on your OLTP primary; ship it to a column store via CDC." (See 03, 18.)

Offset vs cursor (keyset) pagination — how do you page?

Deciding factor: does the underlying list change while users page, and how deep do they go? OFFSET 100000 LIMIT 20 makes the database scan and discard 100k rows (slow at depth) and skips or duplicates items when rows are inserted mid-scroll. Cursor pagination remembers the last seen key (WHERE (created_at, id) < (?, ?) ORDER BY ... LIMIT 20) — O(1) via the index, and stable under inserts.

Say it: "Cursor pagination for anything infinite-scroll or high-volume — it's index-seek fast at any depth and immune to shifting data. Offset only for small, bounded, jump-to-page-N admin tables where deep pages and concurrent inserts don't happen." (See 04, 10.)

Optimistic vs pessimistic locking — how do you handle concurrent writes?

Deciding factor: contention probability. Pessimistic (SELECT ... FOR UPDATE) grabs the lock up front — correct under heavy contention, but it serializes and can deadlock. Optimistic (a version column; the update says WHERE version = ? and fails if someone else moved it) assumes conflicts are rare, does no locking, and retries the loser. Cheap when writes rarely collide, wasteful when they always do.

Say it: "Optimistic (version check + retry) by default, because most rows aren't contended and I avoid holding locks. I switch to pessimistic for genuine hotspots — decrementing the last ticket, moving money on one account — where the conflict rate is high enough that retry storms cost more than just serializing." (See 05, 14.)

Consistency & replication

Strong vs eventual consistency — which do you promise?

Deciding factor: what's the cost of a user reading stale data for a few hundred milliseconds? Strong consistency (every read sees the latest write) costs latency and availability — a read must reach the leader or a quorum. Eventual consistency lets replicas lag, buying low latency and availability, at the price of a window where readers see the old value.

Say it: "I default to eventual and buy strong only where a stale read causes real harm. A like count can be seconds stale; an account balance at withdrawal cannot. The trick is to scope strong consistency to the few operations that need it rather than paying for it globally." The senior tell is read-your-own-writes: users forgive seeing someone else's stale post, never their own vanished edit — so route the writer's own reads to the leader. (See 05.)

CAP — CP vs AP under a partition?

Deciding factor: during a network partition, is it worse to return an error or to return possibly-wrong data? CAP only bites during a partition; then you choose. CP (refuse writes / errors on the minority side) keeps data correct but sacrifices availability — pick it for money, inventory, bookings. AP (keep serving, reconcile later) keeps you up but may serve or accept conflicting data — pick it for feeds, carts, presence, likes.

Say it: "CAP is a choice you only make during a partition, and it's per-operation, not per-system. Checkout is CP — I'd rather show an error than double-sell. The product catalogue is AP — serve slightly stale rather than go down. Also note PACELC: even with no partition, I'm trading latency for consistency on every request." (See 05.)

Synchronous vs asynchronous replication — how do replicas follow the leader?

Deciding factor: can you tolerate losing the last few acknowledged writes on failover (RPO)? Sync replication waits for a replica to confirm before acking the client — zero data loss on failover, but every write pays the slowest replica's latency and a slow replica stalls writes. Async acks immediately and ships to replicas after — fast writes, but a leader crash loses whatever hadn't replicated yet.

Say it: "Async by default for latency; semi-sync (wait for one of N replicas) where I need RPO near zero without paying full synchronous cost. Fully synchronous cross-region replication means every write eats a round-the-world round trip — I'd only accept that for a ledger." (See 05.)

Leader-follower vs multi-leader (vs leaderless) — how many can accept writes?

Deciding factor: do you need to accept writes in more than one region/place at once? Single-leader is simple and gives a clean ordering — all writes serialize through one node — but that node is a write bottleneck and a failover point. Multi-leader accepts writes in several places (multi-region, offline-capable apps) but hands you write conflicts — the hardest problem in the space, requiring conflict resolution (last-write-wins, CRDTs, app-level merge). Leaderless (Dynamo/Cassandra) uses quorums and read-repair.

Say it: "Single-leader unless I genuinely need local writes in multiple regions. Multi-leader's cost isn't the replication, it's conflict resolution — two people editing the same doc offline. If I can partition the data so each key is only written in one region, I dodge conflicts entirely, and that's usually the better move." (See 05.)

2PC vs saga — how do you keep a multi-service operation consistent?

Deciding factor: can you hold locks across services, and do the steps span databases you don't control? Two-phase commit gives atomicity but holds locks through a blocking coordinator — one slow/failed participant stalls everyone, and it doesn't scale or cross service boundaries well. A saga is a sequence of local transactions, each with a compensating action to undo it; no distributed locks, but you get eventual consistency and must design the rollbacks.

Say it: "I avoid 2PC across microservices — the blocking coordinator is a liability. I'd use a saga: local commit per service, compensating transactions to unwind, and idempotent steps. It's more code and it's eventually consistent, but it doesn't hold cross-service locks." Mention orchestration vs choreography for driving it (see Architecture). (See 05, 13.)

Quorum reads/writes — why R + W > N?

Deciding factor: how do you get strong-ish consistency from replicas with no leader? With N replicas, if you write to W and read from R such that R + W > N, the read set and write set must overlap in at least one node that has the latest value — so a read is guaranteed to see the most recent acknowledged write. Tune the split: W = N, R = 1 for fast reads / slow durable writes; W = 1, R = N for fast writes / slow reads; W = R = ⌈(N+1)/2⌉ for balance.

Say it: "Quorum is how leaderless systems fake strong consistency: pick R and W so R + W > N and the sets overlap. It's still not linearizable — concurrent writes need conflict resolution — but a reader can't miss the newest durable value." (See 05.)

Communication & APIs

REST vs gRPC vs GraphQL — which API style?

Deciding factor: who is the caller, and what's the shape of the data need? REST for public, cacheable, resource-shaped APIs over HTTP where broad compatibility matters. gRPC for internal service-to-service calls where you want low latency, binary Protobuf, streaming, and a typed contract. GraphQL when many clients need different slices of a rich, connected graph and you want to kill over-/under-fetching and N round trips.

Say it: "gRPC between my services for the typed contract and speed, REST at the public edge for cacheability and ubiquity, and GraphQL specifically when a mobile client would otherwise make eight REST calls to render one screen. GraphQL moves the cost to the server — resolver N+1 and caching — so it's not free." (See 02.)

SSE vs WebSockets — which for server-to-client push?

Deciding factor: is the traffic one-directional (server → client) or truly bidirectional? Server-Sent Events are one-way, ride plain HTTP, auto-reconnect for free, and are dead simple — perfect for a notification feed, live scores, a progress stream, LLM token streaming. WebSockets are full-duplex over a persistent upgraded connection — use them only when the client also sends a steady stream: chat, multiplayer, collaborative editing.

Say it: "If the client only receives, I'll use SSE — it's HTTP, it reconnects itself, and it's far less to operate. I reach for WebSockets when I need low-latency messages in both directions. Picking WebSockets for a one-way feed is over-engineering." (See 02, 11.)

Long polling vs WebSockets — how do you deliver "real-time"?

Deciding factor: message frequency and latency tolerance. Long polling (hold the request open until data or timeout, then reconnect) works everywhere, needs no special infra, but each message costs a full request cycle and it's clumsy at high frequency. WebSockets hold one connection open for instant push in both directions, at the cost of stateful connections you must manage, scale, and route (sticky sessions / a pub-sub fabric).

Say it: "Long polling is the pragmatic fallback — low-frequency updates, hostile proxies, or when I can't run a WebSocket tier. Once messages are frequent or latency-sensitive, WebSockets win, but now I own connection state, fan-out, and reconnection. The progression is polling → long polling → SSE → WebSockets, and I pick the least machinery that meets the latency bar." (See 02, 11.)

Synchronous vs asynchronous communication — call it or queue it?

Deciding factor: does this need to finish before I respond to the user? Synchronous request-response is simple, gives an immediate answer, and propagates errors directly — but it couples caller to callee's availability and latency (a slow dependency is now your slow response). Async (queue/event) decouples, absorbs spikes, and survives a down dependency — at the cost of eventual consistency, idempotency work, and a "processing…" UI state.

Say it: "I walk the write path asking, per step, 'must this happen before I return 200?' The critical path stays synchronous and fast; everything else — email, transcoding, indexing, notifications — goes on a queue. Async isn't free: I've bought eventual consistency and I now need idempotent consumers." (See 06.)

JWT vs session cookie — how do you keep users logged in?

Deciding factor: can you tolerate not being able to instantly revoke a credential? Sessions store state server-side (a session ID in a cookie, data in Redis) — trivially revocable, but every request hits the session store and you must share it across instances. JWTs are self-contained and signed — stateless, no lookup, great for cross-service auth — but you can't revoke one before it expires without rebuilding the stateful blocklist you were trying to avoid.

Say it: "Stateless JWTs with short expiry plus refresh tokens when I want services to verify auth without a central lookup. Server-side sessions when instant revocation matters — logout-everywhere, banned users, admin panels. The JWT's virtue (no server state) is exactly its flaw (no easy revocation), so I keep access-token TTL short."

Caching

Cache-aside vs write-through — how does the cache stay populated?

Deciding factor: is the data read right after it's written, and do you want the app or the cache to own the DB? Cache-aside (the default): app checks cache, on miss reads DB and populates, and on write deletes the key. Only data someone actually asked for gets cached, but the first read after a write is a miss. Write-through: every write goes through the cache, which synchronously writes the DB — cache is never stale and the just-written value is hot, but you pay two hops per write and cache things nobody reads.

Say it: "Cache-aside by default, and I delete the key on write rather than update it — delete is idempotent, so a concurrent-write race costs one extra DB read instead of pinning a stale value forever. I'd use write-through for config or catalogue data that's written once and read immediately, paired with a TTL." (See 06.)

Write-through vs write-behind — sync or async to the DB?

Deciding factor: can you lose recent writes if a cache node dies? Write-through writes the DB synchronously before acking — durable and consistent, but slow writes. Write-behind (write-back) acks from the cache immediately and flushes to the DB asynchronously in batches — the fastest writes there are, and it coalesces (1,000 counter increments become one DB write) — but a crash before the flush loses those writes.

Say it: "Write-behind only where loss is acceptable and I can coalesce: view counts, 'last seen', analytics counters. Never for money. The moment durability matters, I'm write-through or I write the DB first." (See 06.)

TTL vs explicit invalidation — how do you keep the cache fresh?

Deciding factor: how much staleness can this data tolerate, and can you find every writer? TTL is dead simple and self-healing — any missed invalidation, any out-of-band DBA write, corrects itself within the TTL — but you're explicitly serving stale data for up to N seconds. Explicit invalidation gives millisecond freshness but requires finding every code path that mutates the data, forever, and fan-out is brutal (which 40,000 cached feeds contained that deleted tweet?).

Say it: "I treat TTL as a correctness budget, not a performance knob — a 60-second TTL is me saying 60 seconds of staleness is fine. I add explicit invalidation only where it isn't, which is almost always read-your-own-writes. Versioned keys (user:42:v7) are a third option that sidesteps invalidation races entirely." (See 06.)

Local (in-process) vs distributed cache — where does the cache live?

Deciding factor: can the copies be allowed to disagree? A local cache (Caffeine, a map in the process) is nanoseconds fast with zero network — but every instance has its own copy, so they go stale independently and a user hitting refresh sees the old value from some pods and the new from others. A distributed cache (Redis) is one shared truth over ~0.5–1 ms of network — consistent and centrally evictable, but a network hop and an operational dependency.

Say it: "Local caches are the fastest thing I have and the most dangerous — I only use them for immutable data, or where bounded staleness is genuinely fine, or with a pub-sub invalidation broadcast. For anything that must agree across instances, it goes in Redis. Often I layer both: local L1 in front of shared L2." (See 06, 08.)

LRU vs LFU eviction — what do you throw out when full?

Deciding factor: is your hot set stable over time, or does a scan blow it away? LRU (least recently used) approximates "what's hot now" cheaply and adapts fast — but one batch job reading a million cold rows evicts your entire hot set. LFU (least frequently used) resists that scan (a single cold read can't beat a key read 10,000 times) but clings to yesterday's popular key long after it cooled, unless you age the counters.

Say it: "LRU is the sane default and what most systems ship. I'd consider LFU when I have a stable hot set threatened by periodic scans. In practice Redis's sampled-LRU or LFU-with-decay covers both — and random eviction is within a few percent of LRU with zero bookkeeping." (See 06.)

CDN push vs pull — how does content reach the edge?

Deciding factor: how big is the catalogue and how predictable is demand? Pull (origin-pull): the CDN fetches from your origin on the first request for an asset and caches it — zero pre-work, self-managing, but the first user in each region pays a cache-miss round trip to origin. Push: you upload assets to the CDN ahead of time — no cold-miss latency and origin never gets hit, but you manage distribution and store everything at the edge whether it's requested or not.

Say it: "Pull for the common case — a large catalogue with a long tail, where pre-pushing everything wastes edge storage. Push for a small set of critical, predictably-hot assets (a launch, a big video) where I can't afford the first-request miss. Pull is lazy and self-healing; push is eager and controlled." (See 02.)

Architecture

Monolith vs microservices — how do you slice the system?

Deciding factor: organizational scale and independent-deploy pressure, not "which is modern." A monolith is one deployable — simplest to build, test, and reason about; in-process calls, one database, transactions for free. It hurts when many teams contend over one codebase and one deploy pipeline. Microservices buy independent deploys, independent scaling, and team autonomy — at the cost of network calls, distributed transactions (sagas), eventual consistency, and a mountain of operational overhead (tracing, service discovery, versioning).

Say it: "I'd start with a well-structured monolith — a modular monolith — and extract a service only when a real force demands it: a component needs independent scaling, a team needs an independent deploy cadence, or a boundary needs isolation. Microservices are an organizational solution to a scaling-the-org problem, and you pay in distributed-systems complexity. Don't buy that bill before you have the problem." (See 01.)

Orchestration vs choreography — how do you drive a saga / workflow?

Deciding factor: how many steps, and do you need a single place to see and control the flow? Orchestration uses a central coordinator that tells each service what to do next — the flow is explicit, visible, and easy to change or monitor, but the orchestrator is a component you own and a potential bottleneck. Choreography has services react to each other's events with no central brain — loosely coupled and resilient, but the end-to-end flow is emergent and hard to follow, debug, or change.

Say it: "Choreography for two or three simple, stable steps — just publish events. Orchestration once the workflow has real branching, compensation, and needs to be observable — an order saga with payment, inventory, and shipping. I'd rather have one place that owns 'what happens next' than chase the logic across eight event handlers." (See 05, 13.)

Stateless vs stateful services — where does the state go?

Deciding factor: can any instance handle any request? Stateless services keep no per-client state in the process — any instance serves any request, so they scale horizontally trivially, restart freely, and need no sticky routing. Stateful services (a WebSocket tier, an in-memory game session) hold connection or session state, which enables low-latency local access but forces sticky sessions, complicates scaling and failover, and makes a lost node a lost session.

Say it: "I keep application services stateless and push state to a shared store (Redis, the DB) so I can scale by adding boxes and lose one without losing data. Where state must be local — persistent connections, real-time sessions — I isolate it in its own tier with sticky routing and plan for connection re-establishment on failover."

L4 vs L7 load balancing — which layer routes?

Deciding factor: do you need to route on request content? An L4 (transport) balancer forwards by IP/port without looking inside — extremely fast, protocol-agnostic, but it can't route by URL path, header, or cookie. An L7 (application) balancer terminates the connection and reads the HTTP request — so it can do path-based routing, sticky sessions, TLS termination, and header rewrites — at higher CPU cost and lower raw throughput.

Say it: "L7 at the edge for smart routing — send /api here, /images there, do sticky sessions and TLS termination. L4 where I just need to spread raw connections as fast as possible with no inspection. Often both: L4 in front, L7 behind." (See 02.)

API gateway vs service mesh — north-south or east-west?

Deciding factor: are you governing traffic into the system or between services? An API gateway sits at the edge (north-south): auth, rate limiting, routing, request shaping for external clients — one front door. A service mesh (sidecars, e.g. Envoy/Istio) handles service-to-service traffic (east-west): mTLS, retries, timeouts, circuit breaking, and observability, transparently, without app code changes.

Say it: "Gateway for everything crossing the trust boundary from clients; mesh for the cross-cutting resilience and security concerns between internal services so I'm not re-implementing retries and mTLS in every service. They're complementary, not alternatives — different traffic axes."

Async & messaging

Message queue vs log (SQS/RabbitMQ vs Kafka) — which broker?

Deciding factor: does more than one consumer care about the same message, and do you need replay? A queue is a to-do list — consuming destroys the message (ack = delete), competing consumers split the work, one job to one worker. A log is a tape — consuming just moves a per-group offset; messages stay for the retention window, N consumer groups each read the whole stream independently, and you can rewind to replay.

Say it: "For 'send this email' I use SQS/RabbitMQ — one task, one worker, delete on completion, retries and a DLQ for free, zero ops. I reach for Kafka when an event has multiple independent consumers, or when I need replay — order events feeding billing, search, and analytics. Kafka is a commitment; I want a reason for it, not a reflex." (See 06.)

At-least-once vs exactly-once delivery — what do you design for?

Deciding factor: there is no real exactly-once across a network boundary — so the question is how you tolerate duplicates. At-most-once (ack before processing) silently loses messages on a crash. At-least-once (ack after processing) may process twice on redelivery. "Exactly-once" holds only within Kafka; the moment the side effect leaves (charge a card, send an email), you can't atomically do it and record that you did.

Say it: "Exactly-once delivery is a myth at the boundary. What's real is at-least-once plus an idempotent consumer, which is observationally equivalent. My consumer writes a dedup key — the message ID or a natural key — in the same local transaction as the side effect; a duplicate hits a unique-constraint violation, I ack and move on." (See 06.)

Fan-out on write vs fan-out on read — how do you build the feed?

Deciding factor: the follower distribution — specifically, do you have celebrities? Fan-out on write (push) copies each new post into every follower's precomputed timeline — reads are a single fast lookup, but a celebrity's post triggers millions of writes and the write amplification is brutal. Fan-out on read (pull) assembles the timeline by querying who you follow at read time — cheap writes, but every read does a scatter-gather and is slow for users following thousands.

Fan-out on write (push)Fan-out on read (pull)
Write costHigh (one write per follower)Low (one write)
Read costLow (precomputed)High (gather at read)
Breaks onCelebrities (millions of writes)Users following many; hot reads

Say it: "Hybrid, and I'd say so explicitly: fan-out on write for the common case, so most timelines are precomputed and reads are cheap; but for celebrity accounts I fan-out on read and merge their recent posts in at query time. That kills the write-amplification tail while keeping normal reads fast." (See 10.)

Push vs pull consumers — who initiates delivery?

Deciding factor: does the consumer need to control its own pace (backpressure)? Push (broker sends as messages arrive) gives low latency but can overwhelm a slow consumer — the broker must handle flow control. Pull (consumer polls for the next batch when ready) lets the consumer set its own rate and batch efficiently — that's why Kafka is pull-based — at a small latency cost and some wasted empty polls.

Say it: "Pull when the consumer needs backpressure and batching — it can't be swamped because it asks for work when it's ready. Push when latency matters more than the consumer's ability to self-throttle. Kafka chose pull precisely so a slow consumer just lags rather than falling over." (See 06, 12.)

Backpressure — buffer vs shed load?

Deciding factor: is the overload a brief spike or sustained beyond capacity? Buffering (a queue) absorbs a temporary spike — depth and latency grow, but nothing fails, and consumers drain it. But an unbounded buffer under sustained overload just moves the failure: latency climbs until everything times out (bufferbloat). Load shedding drops or rejects work early (429s, drop low-priority) to protect the system that's genuinely past capacity.

Say it: "The ladder is: buffer the spike → scale consumers → throttle the producer → shed load. Fail fast beats fail slow — a fast 429 is recoverable, a request stuck 30 seconds behind a full buffer isn't. I shed by priority and drop work whose deadline has already passed." (See 06, 09.)

Scaling & partitioning

Horizontal vs vertical scaling — bigger box or more boxes?

Deciding factor: have you hit the biggest single box, and can the workload be split? Vertical (a bigger machine) is the simplest thing that works — no code changes, no distribution — but there's a hard ceiling, it's a single point of failure, and cost climbs non-linearly. Horizontal (more machines) is effectively unbounded and fault-tolerant, but only works if the workload shards cleanly, and it drags in load balancing, distributed state, and coordination.

Say it: "Scale up first — it's cheap in engineering time and buys a lot of runway. Scale out when I hit the ceiling, need fault tolerance, or the load is naturally partitionable. Stateless app tiers scale out trivially; the database is the hard part, which is why I delay sharding it as long as read replicas and a bigger primary hold." (See 04.)

Hash vs range partitioning — how do you split the keyspace?

Deciding factor: do you need range/scan queries, or just even load? Hash partitioning (shard on hash(key)) spreads load evenly and avoids hotspots — but destroys locality, so "all events between Monday and Friday" becomes a scatter-gather across every shard. Range partitioning keeps ordered keys together, so range scans hit one or few shards — but a monotonically increasing key (timestamp, auto-increment ID) sends all new writes to the last shard, a guaranteed hotspot.

Say it: "Hash for even distribution when I only look up by key — user by ID, session. Range when I need ordered scans — time series, alphabetical browse — accepting I must watch for a hot tail shard on monotonic keys, which I fix by prefixing the key with something high-cardinality." (See 04.)

Consistent hashing vs mod-N hashing — how do you assign keys to nodes?

Deciding factor: how often does the node count change? Plain hash(key) % N is simple and perfectly even — but when N changes (a node added or dies), almost every key remaps to a different node, so your entire cache is invalidated at once. Consistent hashing places nodes on a ring so adding or removing one only remaps the keys in that node's arc — roughly 1/N of keys move, not all of them. Virtual nodes smooth out the distribution.

Say it: "Consistent hashing any time membership changes at runtime — distributed caches, Dynamo-style stores — because mod N would remap everything on a single node failure and dump the whole load on the database. Plain mod N is fine only for a fixed, never-changing node set." (See 04, 08.)

Sharding vs replication — which scaling problem are you solving?

Deciding factor: are you out of write/storage capacity, or out of read capacity / availability? They solve different problems and you usually need both. Replication copies the same data to more nodes — scales reads, adds availability and durability — but every replica holds the full dataset, so it does nothing for write throughput or storage limits. Sharding splits different data across nodes — scales writes and storage — but adds routing, cross-shard queries, and rebalancing pain.

Say it: "Replicas first — they scale reads and give me failover, and most systems are read-heavy. I shard only when writes or dataset size outgrow one primary, because sharding is where cross-shard joins, distributed transactions, and hot-shard problems begin. In production you shard and replicate each shard." (See 04.)

Round-robin vs least-connections vs consistent-hash — which LB algorithm?

Deciding factor: are requests uniform, variable in cost, or do you need session affinity? Round-robin is simplest and fine when requests are cheap and uniform, but it ignores that one box may be stuck on slow requests. Least-connections routes to the least-busy server — better when request cost varies widely. Consistent-hash (by client IP or key) sends the same client to the same server — needed for sticky sessions or cache locality.

Say it: "Round-robin as the default; least-connections when request durations are uneven so I don't pile onto a saturated box; consistent-hash when I need affinity — a stateful WebSocket tier or maximizing local cache hits. And I want health checks under all of them so I stop routing to a dead node." (See 02.)

Frontend

CSR vs SSR (vs SSG) — where do you render?

Deciding factor: does this page need fast first paint and SEO, or is it an app behind a login? Client-side rendering ships a JS bundle that renders in the browser — great for rich, interactive, authenticated apps, but slow first paint and weak SEO (the crawler sees an empty shell). Server-side rendering returns finished HTML — fast first contentful paint and SEO-friendly — at the cost of server compute per request and a time-to-interactive gap while JS hydrates. SSG pre-renders at build time — fastest of all, for content that doesn't change per request.

Say it: "SSG for marketing and docs — render once, serve from a CDN. SSR for content that's dynamic but SEO- and first-paint-sensitive — a product page. CSR for the app behind auth where SEO is irrelevant and interactivity dominates. Modern frameworks blur this with per-route streaming SSR, so I choose per route, not per app." (See 19, 20.)

Client state vs server state — where does the data belong?

Deciding factor: is this data owned by the server (and merely cached in the client) or purely a UI concern? Server state (the user list, a post) is asynchronous, shared, can go stale, and needs caching, refetching, and invalidation — that's React Query / SWR territory, not useState. Client state (is this modal open, the current form input, the active tab) is synchronous, local, and owned entirely by the UI — plain component state or a small store.

Say it: "The classic mistake is dumping server data into Redux and hand-writing loading/error/caching. I keep server state in a data-fetching cache that owns staleness and refetch, and reserve client state tools for genuine UI state. Conflating the two is where most frontend state bugs live." (See 20.)

Optimistic vs pessimistic UI updates — when do you show the result?

Deciding factor: how likely is the write to fail, and how bad is a rollback? Optimistic UI updates the screen immediately, before the server confirms, then reconciles — it feels instant (a like, a message send), but you must handle rollback when the request fails and briefly show possibly-wrong state. Pessimistic waits for the server's OK before updating — always truthful, never a rollback, but the UI feels laggy on every action.

Say it: "Optimistic for high-frequency, low-stakes, rarely-failing actions — likes, toggles, sending a chat — because the perceived latency win is huge and the failure is cheap to undo. Pessimistic for money and destructive or rarely-repeated actions, where showing a success that then reverses is worse than a spinner." (See 20.)

Debounce vs throttle — how do you rate-limit an event?

Deciding factor: do you want the final value after activity stops, or a steady sample during it? Debounce waits until the event has stopped firing for X ms, then runs once — perfect for a search-as-you-type box (fire the query when the user pauses, not on every keystroke). Throttle runs at most once per X ms during continuous firing — perfect for scroll/resize/mousemove handlers where you want regular updates, not just the last one.

Say it: "Debounce when only the final state matters and I want to collapse a burst into one call — typeahead, autosave. Throttle when I need periodic updates throughout — infinite-scroll triggers, drag, resize. One word: debounce = 'wait until they're done', throttle = 'at most every N ms'." (See 21.)

Virtualization vs pagination — how do you render a huge list?

Deciding factor: is it a continuous feed the user scrolls, or a dataset they navigate? Virtualization (windowing) renders only the rows currently in view and recycles DOM nodes as you scroll — keeps a 10,000-row feed at 60fps with a tiny DOM, but complicates focus, find-in-page, and variable-height rows. Pagination loads a bounded page at a time — simpler, shareable/bookmarkable page URLs, bounded memory — but interrupts the flow with page controls.

Say it: "Virtualize a continuous scroll of many uniform rows — a chat log, a feed — so the DOM stays small regardless of list length. Paginate (or its infinite-scroll variant with cursor paging) when the data is naturally chunked or when I want addressable pages. Under the hood both need cursor pagination on the API, not offset." (See 20.)

What to remember

The meta-lesson of this whole deck, and the single habit that reads as senior: don't recite both sides — name the one variable that decides, then commit. A mid-level answer is "X is faster, Y is more consistent." A senior answer is "the deciding factor here is the read:write ratio / the conflict rate / whether I have celebrities / whether I can tolerate a stale read — and given ours, it's X." The interviewer is testing whether you can compress a trade-off to its pivot and defend a choice, not whether you can list features.

  • Every card has a pivot. SQL vs NoSQL → access patterns, not volume. Strong vs eventual → cost of a stale read. Fan-out write vs read → do you have celebrities. Cache-aside vs write-through → is it read-after-write. 2PC vs saga → can you hold cross-service locks. Consistent hashing vs mod-N → how often membership changes. Learn the pivot, and the answer falls out.
  • Almost everything defaults to the simple side, and you earn the complex side. Monolith before microservices. Single-leader before multi-leader. Replicas before shards. Scale up before out. Cache-aside before the exotic patterns. Sync before async. Say the default, then name the specific force that would make you flip.
  • Name the cost you're accepting, out loud. "Async, which buys me eventual consistency and forces idempotent consumers." "Write-behind, which can lose data — never for money." "Optimistic UI, so I must handle rollback." Naming the downside you're choosing is the strongest signal; pretending it isn't there is the weakest.
  • Most of these are per-operation, not per-system. Consistency, CAP, sync-vs-async, and caching are chosen per endpoint. Checkout is CP; the catalogue is AP. The same app is strongly consistent for balances and eventually consistent for like counts. Saying "it depends on the operation" and then scoping it is a senior move.
  • Recurring themes across all eight categories: jitter/randomness breaks synchronized load; the read:write ratio drives storage and caching; idempotency is how you survive at-least-once anything; the partition/ shard key simultaneously buys you ordering and exposes you to hot spots; and the cheapest work is the work you move off the critical path.
Close the loop in the room: "For each of these I'd state the deciding factor, make the call, and name the one cost I'm taking on — because at this level the interview isn't 'do you know the options', it's 'can you choose and defend one under real constraints'." That sentence, said once near the end, is worth more than another comparison table. You've reached the end of the course — now go practice saying these out loud, because talking is the skill being graded.
