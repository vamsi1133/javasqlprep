05 — Coordination, Consensus & Distributed Transactions

Getting independent machines to agree — quorums, leaders, locks, 2PC vs saga, and why "exactly once" is a sales pitch.

Why this matters in the interview

Every previous page in this phase made your system bigger: more replicas (03), more shards (04). This page is the bill for that. The moment your data lives on more than one machine, you have a new class of problem that does not exist on a single box: two nodes can hold different opinions about the truth, and there is no referee. A single Postgres instance gives you ACID for free — BEGIN … COMMIT, one lock manager, one clock, one answer. Spread the same workload across three services and two databases and every one of those guarantees has to be rebuilt by hand, badly, at the application layer. Coordination is the study of how to rebuild them, and how much of them you can afford to give up.

Calibrate hard here, because this topic is a trap in both directions. Interviewers will not ask you to implement Raft. If you spend eight minutes on the whiteboard drawing election terms and log-matching invariants, you have misread the room, burned the clock, and answered a question nobody asked — that reads as junior, not deep. Raft and Paxos are awareness level: know what consensus buys you, know the shape of it, and know the one sentence that closes the topic ("I'd use etcd or ZooKeeper rather than build this"). What does decide interviews — especially the enterprise and fintech loops you're targeting — is the boring, money-adjacent half: 2PC vs saga, the outbox pattern, idempotency keys, and why your Redis lock is not actually a lock. Those come up in almost every payments, orders, booking, or inventory question, and a 7-YOE candidate is expected to reach for them unprompted. Go deep there. Name the rest and move on.

1. Why coordination is hard

There is exactly one root cause, and everything else on this page is a consequence of it:

You send a request to another node and get no reply. You cannot tell whether the node is dead, the node is slow, the network dropped your request, or the network dropped its reply.

Sit with that. From the caller's point of view those four situations are byte-for-byte identical: silence. And the four demand opposite responses. If the node is dead, you should fail over and let someone else take its work. If the node is merely slow — a 4-second JVM stop-the-world GC pause, a noisy-neighbour VM, a saturated NIC — then failing over means two nodes are now doing the same job, and if that job is "deduct money from this account," you have just built a bug that costs real currency.

So a timeout is not a fact. A timeout is a guess. Choosing 5 seconds instead of 30 doesn't make the guess correct; it just trades false positives (declaring a healthy node dead) against slow failure detection. There is no timeout value that eliminates both. Say that sentence in an interview when someone asks "how do you detect that a node is down" and you have already out-answered most candidates — the wrong answer is "heartbeats," full stop; the right answer is "heartbeats, and then I have to accept that my failure detector will sometimes be wrong, so the design has to stay correct even when it is."

The theory, honestly, in one paragraph

Two results get name-dropped in this space and you should be able to name them back without pretending to more depth than you have. The Two Generals Problem proves that over a lossy channel, two parties can never become certain they've agreed — every acknowledgement needs an acknowledgement, forever. That's why no distributed commit is ever truly atomic across a network; you can only shrink the window of doubt. The FLP impossibility result (Fischer, Lynch, Paterson, 1985) proves that in a fully asynchronous network with no timing bounds, no algorithm can guarantee consensus if even one node may crash — because, again, you can't distinguish crashed from slow. Real systems escape FLP by cheating in a benign way: they assume partial synchrony (the network is usually well-behaved) and use timeouts, giving up guaranteed liveness (progress) in order to keep safety (never two truths). Raft can stall during a bad partition; it will never elect two leaders in the same term.

The sentence that shows you understand the whole field: "Consensus protocols always prioritise safety over liveness — if the network is bad enough, the system stops making progress rather than risk giving two different answers. My job as a designer is to decide which of those failure modes my product can tolerate." A checkout that stalls for 10 seconds is annoying. A checkout that double-charges is a Jira ticket with a legal team attached.
Awareness level. Say "FLP" or "two generals" once, in one clause, to show you know the ground you're standing on — then move to the practical content. Nobody has ever passed a system design loop by proving an impossibility result at the whiteboard.

2. Quorums — R + W > N

You have N replicas of a piece of data. If you write to one replica and read from another, you can read stale data — that's just replication lag (page 03). A quorum is the cheapest possible fix, and it's pure arithmetic, no protocol required.

  • N — the replication factor: how many copies of each key exist.
  • W — the write quorum: how many replicas must acknowledge a write before the client is told "OK".
  • R — the read quorum: how many replicas the coordinator must hear from before returning a read.

If R + W > N, the read set and the write set must overlap in at least one replica — so any read is guaranteed to see at least one copy of the latest acknowledged write.

That's the entire idea, and it's just the pigeonhole principle. You wrote to W replicas; you're reading from R replicas; if R + W exceeds the total N, those two sets cannot be disjoint. At least one node you read from was one of the nodes you wrote to. Attach a version number or timestamp to each copy, take the newest one you see, and the read is fresh.

Concrete numbers: N = 3, W = 2, R = 2

The default in Cassandra, DynamoDB, and roughly every Dynamo-style store. 2 + 2 = 4 > 3 ✓. 

N = 3 replicas: [ A ] [ B ] [ C ]

WRITE v7 with W=2  ──▶ A: v7 ✓   B: v7 ✓   C: still v6 (slow/down — write is
                                                        replicated later, async)
                       └─ 2 acks received → client told "OK"

READ with R=2      ──▶ ask A and C
                       A returns v7   C returns v6
                       └─ coordinator picks the highest version → v7  ✓ FRESH
                          (and repairs C in the background — "read repair")

Try to escape it and you can't: whichever 2 of the 3 you read, at least one of them is A or B. Now break the arithmetic — W = 1, R = 1 (1 + 1 = 2, not > 3). Write v7 lands on A only. Read hits C. You get v6. Stale. That is not a bug in the database; it's a configuration you chose, and it's sometimes exactly the right one.

Tunable consistency: what each dial buys and costs

This is the point of quorums in an interview. Cassandra lets you set R and W per query, which means consistency stops being a property of your database and becomes a property of each individual operation. That's a genuinely powerful thing to say out loud.

Setting (N=3)R + W > N?What it buysWhat it costsUse it for

Read that table as a single insight: W and R are a seesaw. Whatever you take off one side you pay for on the other. Pushing W up makes writes slower and less available but reads cheap; pushing R up does the mirror image. Quorum (W=R=2 at N=3) is the balanced middle, which is why it's the default — not because it's magic, but because it's the least-bad compromise when you don't know the workload.

The line that lands: "I'd run N=3, W=2, R=2 by default — that satisfies R+W > N so reads are consistent — but for the analytics write path I'd drop to W=1, because losing a click event is fine and I want that path to never block a user request." You just demonstrated that consistency is a per-workload decision, not a database feature. That is a 7-YOE answer.

What quorums do NOT give you

Be careful here, because this is where over-claiming gets punished. Quorums give you fresh reads. They do not give you transactions, and they do not give you consensus.

  • Concurrent writes still conflict. Two clients both write to a quorum at the same time with the same version. Both succeed. Now replicas disagree and someone must resolve it — last-write-wins (which silently drops data), vector clocks, or a CRDT. Quorum arithmetic is silent on this.
  • "Latest" needs a definable order. LWW relies on timestamps, and timestamps rely on clocks, and clocks on different machines drift. A write with a fast clock can beat a genuinely later write. Real fix: logical versions, not wall clocks.
  • Sloppy quorums make it worse. Dynamo-style systems under partition will accept a write on any N healthy nodes, not necessarily the "right" N (this is "hinted handoff"). Availability goes up; your R+W>N guarantee quietly evaporates, because the overlapping node you were counting on wasn't in the write set at all.
Do not say "we use quorum reads so it's strongly consistent." Quorum gives you read-your-writes on acknowledged writes; it does not give you linearizability in the presence of concurrent writers, failed writes that partially landed, or clock skew. If the interviewer needs true linearizability (leader election, a lock, a unique-constraint), you need consensus — the next two sections — or a single-writer database, not a quorum.

3. Leader election, leases & fencing tokens

Why you want a leader at all

Concurrency is only hard when two things write at once. So the oldest trick in distributed systems is to make sure only one thing ever writes: elect a leader, funnel all writes through it, and the conflict problem disappears by construction. The leader serialises operations into a single ordered log; the followers replay that log. No conflict resolution, no vector clocks, no last-write-wins data loss — because there is never a "last write" ambiguity in the first place.

You already know a system built exactly this way: Postgres primary + read replicas. One writer. Everything else follows. Kafka does it per partition (one leader broker per partition). MySQL, MongoDB, Elasticsearch shards, HDFS NameNode — same shape. When an interviewer asks "how do you avoid write conflicts across replicas," the first-order answer is "I don't allow concurrent writers — I elect a leader."

The cost is equally simple and you should name it in the same breath: the leader is a bottleneck and a single point of failure. Write throughput is capped by one machine, and when it dies you have a gap where nobody can write until a new leader is chosen. Which raises the actual hard question — how do you choose one, and how do you make sure you only ever have one?

Leases: a lock with an expiry

The naive approach — "the leader holds a flag in a database" — deadlocks the instant the leader dies while holding the flag. Nobody can take over; the flag never gets released. So real systems use a lease: leadership is granted for a bounded time (say 10 seconds) and the leader must keep renewing it (heartbeating) to stay leader. Stop renewing — because you crashed, or you were partitioned — and the lease expires on its own, and someone else can claim it. 

t=0s    Node A acquires lease  (expires t=10s)     A is leader ✓
t=3s    A renews               (expires t=13s)
t=6s    A renews               (expires t=16s)
t=9s    A ────────────────────── CRASHES ─────────────────
t=16s   lease expires — nobody holds it
t=16s   Node B acquires lease  (expires t=26s)     B is leader ✓

        Gap with no leader: ~7s. That gap is the price of the timeout.
        Shorter lease = faster failover, but more false expiries.

Note the trade-off baked into the lease duration — it is the timeout problem from section 1 wearing a different hat. A 30-second lease means 30 seconds of write unavailability after a crash. A 2-second lease means a leader with a 3-second GC pause gets fired for no reason. There is no correct number, only a choice.

Split-brain — and why it's a correctness bug, not a hiccup

Split-brain is when two nodes both believe they are the leader. It follows directly from section 1: A is paused, not dead; A's lease expires; B takes over; A wakes up still believing it's the leader, because from A's point of view no time passed at all. Now both A and B are writing. 

       A (leader, lease → t=10s)
       │
   t=4 ├── begins a 12-SECOND STOP-THE-WORLD GC PAUSE  ⏸
       │                     (A is frozen; it does not know it)
  t=10 │   ····· lease expires ·····
  t=11 │   B acquires the lease → B is now leader, starts writing
       │
  t=16 ├── A wakes up. Its last thought was "I am the leader."
       └── A writes to the storage system.        💥 TWO WRITERS

The obvious "fix" — have A check whether its lease is still valid before writing — does not work, and understanding why is the whole point. A can check the lease, see it's valid with 6 seconds left, and then get paused for 12 seconds between the check and the write. There is no way to make "check the lease" and "perform the write" atomic across two different machines. The check is always stale by the time the write lands. Any design that relies on the pauser noticing it was paused is broken, because a paused process by definition notices nothing.

Candidates almost always say "we use a lock with a TTL, so if the node dies the lock is released." That sentence is fine right up until the node isn't dead. GC pauses, VM live-migration, a stalled disk write, an overloaded scheduler, a laptop lid closing — the process freezes and resumes with no awareness of the gap. A TTL protects you against crashes; it actively creates the two-writer window against pauses. Do not stop your answer at "TTL."

Fencing tokens — the fix, and the thing that makes interviewers sit up

You cannot stop a node from being paused, and you cannot stop a paused node from writing. So stop trying. Instead, make the storage system reject the stale write.

Every time the lock/lease service grants leadership, it hands out a monotonically increasing number — the fencing token. Token 33, then 34, then 35; never reused, never decreasing. The leader must include its token with every write. The storage system remembers the highest token it has ever seen and rejects any write carrying a token lower than that. 

  Lock service          Client A            Client B         Storage
       │                    │                   │               │  (highest seen: 32)
       │◀── acquire ────────┤                   │               │
       ├─── token 33 ──────▶│                   │               │
       │                    │ ⏸ GC PAUSE        │               │
       │        lease expires                   │               │
       │◀────────── acquire ────────────────────┤               │
       ├─────────── token 34 ──────────────────▶│               │
       │                    │                   ├── write(tok=34) ──▶  ✓ 34 > 32
       │                    │                   │               │  (highest seen: 34)
       │                    │ ▶ wakes up        │               │
       │                    ├── write(tok=33) ──────────────────▶  ✗ REJECTED
       │                    │                   │               │     33 < 34

A's write is refused by the resource itself, without the resource needing to know anything about leases, pauses, or who is supposed to be leader. It just enforces one rule: tokens never go backwards. This turns a distributed-agreement problem into a local monotonicity check — which is why it actually works.

In Java, against a normal SQL database, the fencing check is a WHERE clause. That's it:

// The resource (a row) remembers the highest fencing token it has accepted.
// The conditional UPDATE makes "check token" and "write" ONE atomic operation
// inside the database — which is exactly what we could not do across machines.
@Transactional
public void writeWithFencing(String resourceId, long token, String payload) {
    int rows = jdbc.update("""
        UPDATE resource
           SET payload     = ?,
               fence_token = ?
         WHERE id = ?
           AND fence_token < ?          -- reject anything from a stale leader
        """, payload, token, resourceId, token);

    if (rows == 0) {
        // A newer leader has already written. We were paused. We are not the leader.
        // Correct behaviour: give up loudly. Do NOT retry — retrying is the bug.
        throw new FencedOutException("stale token " + token + " for " + resourceId);
    }
}

Where does the token come from? Anything that can hand out a strictly increasing sequence with consensus behind it: ZooKeeper's zxid or a sequential znode, etcd's revision on a key, a Postgres sequence, or Kafka's producer epoch. The number itself is trivial. What's expensive — and what a coordination service exists to sell you — is the guarantee that no two callers ever get the same number.

Say this and watch the interviewer's pen move: "A TTL-based lock is safe against crashes but not against pauses — a GC pause longer than the TTL gives you two live holders. If correctness depends on the lock, I need fencing: the lock service issues a monotonically increasing token, and the resource rejects any write with a lower token than one it's already seen. That way the stale writer is stopped at the resource, not at the lock." This is the single highest-value paragraph on this page. It's the exact thing that separates "has read a blog post about Redis locks" from "has debugged one at 3am."
If the resource can't enforce fencing — say it's a third-party payment API with no token concept — then no lock can make it safe, and you must stop pretending otherwise. Push the correctness down to something that can enforce it: an idempotency key at the provider (section 9), or a unique constraint in your own database. This escape hatch is the honest answer, and interviewers respect it far more than a more elaborate lock.

4. Raft & Paxos AWARENESS

Read this section once, understand the shape, and do not go deeper. I'm being explicit about the calibration because this is where prep material lies to you. You will find 40-minute videos on Raft log compaction and Multi-Paxos ballot numbers. That knowledge has approximately zero expected value in a 45-minute system design round, and negative value if it tempts you into a tangent. Nobody is going to ask a 7-YOE full-stack candidate to derive a consensus protocol. What they will do is check that you know what consensus is, that you know when your design needs it, and — critically — that you know not to build it yourself.

What consensus actually gives you

Consensus is the primitive that lets a group of N nodes agree on a single value even though some of them may crash, and — this is the part that matters — never disagree. Not "usually agree." Never disagree. Feed a consensus system a sequence of decisions and it gives every surviving node the same replicated log in the same order. Every distributed-coordination primitive you actually want is built on top of that one log:

  • Leader election — the log's first entry for a term says who the leader is; everybody agrees.
  • Distributed locks — "client X holds lock L" is just an entry in the log.
  • Fencing tokens — the log index is a monotonic counter, free of charge.
  • Config / service discovery — a small, strongly-consistent key-value store that is the log's state machine.
  • Atomic compare-and-swap on a key — the operation on which everything else is built.

The shape of Raft (this is enough)

Raft was designed to be understandable, so the summary is fair rather than a cartoon. Four ideas:

  1. Roles. Every node is a leader, a follower, or a candidate. There is at most one leader at a time.
  2. Terms. Time is divided into numbered terms — a logical clock. Each term has at most one leader. Term numbers only ever increase, and a node seeing a higher term immediately steps down. (Notice: that's a fencing token again. Same idea, different name.)
  3. Election. Followers expect heartbeats from the leader. If a follower's randomised election timeout fires without hearing one, it bumps the term, becomes a candidate, and asks the others to vote for it. A candidate that wins votes from a majority becomes leader. The randomised timeouts are what stop everyone from campaigning simultaneously forever.
  4. Log replication & majority commit. All client writes go to the leader. The leader appends the entry to its log and ships it to followers. Once a majority (including itself) has durably stored it, the entry is committed and can be applied and acknowledged to the client.
            client write
                 │
                 ▼
          ┌─────────────┐   AppendEntries   ┌───────────┐
          │   LEADER    │──────────────────▶│ FOLLOWER  │  ack ✓
          │  (term 7)   │──────────────────▶│ FOLLOWER  │  ack ✓
          └─────────────┘──────────────────▶│ FOLLOWER  │  (slow — no ack yet)
                 │                          └───────────┘
                 │   2 acks + itself = 3 of 5 = MAJORITY
                 └─▶ entry COMMITTED → apply to state machine → reply "OK"

  The slow follower catches up later. It does not block the commit.

"Majority" is why cluster sizes are odd. With 5 nodes, a majority is 3, so you survive 2 failures. With 3 nodes, a majority is 2, so you survive 1. Adding a 4th node to a 3-node cluster raises the majority to 3 — you still only survive 1 failure, but you now have one more machine that can break. That's the whole reason etcd and ZooKeeper clusters are 3 or 5 and never 4. And majorities are what make split-brain structurally impossible: two disjoint majorities can't exist in one set, so two leaders in the same term can't exist either. The minority side of a partition simply stops accepting writes — it sacrifices liveness to preserve safety, exactly as section 1 predicted.

Paxos, in the only depth you need

Paxos (Lamport, 1989) is the original, and it solves the same problem: get a majority to agree on one value, safely, despite failures. It's famously hard to follow and famously under-specified for real use, which is why practical systems use Multi-Paxos, ZAB (ZooKeeper's protocol), or Raft — Raft being explicitly a "make Paxos understandable" project. You need one sentence: "Paxos and Raft solve the same problem — majority-based consensus over a replicated log — and Raft is the one people actually implement because it's comprehensible. Google's Chubby uses Paxos; ZooKeeper uses ZAB; etcd and Consul use Raft." Done. Move on.

What to actually say in the interview

The expected answer, verbatim: "For leader election and distributed locks I'd use an existing consensus system — etcd or ZooKeeper — rather than build one. Implementing Raft correctly, including membership changes and log compaction, is a multi-year project and it's a solved problem. What matters for this design is where I need consensus and where I don't." That sentence is not a dodge. It is the senior answer, and offering to hand-roll Raft is the junior one. The follow-up — "so where do you need it here?" — is the real question, and it's the one worth your remaining minutes.

And the honest engineering point underneath: consensus is expensive. Every decision costs a network round trip to a majority, and every write is serialised through one leader. You would never run your product's data plane through Raft — you'd never put a million QPS of user writes through etcd. Consensus systems handle small, infrequent, critical metadata: who's the leader, what's the current shard map, who holds this lock. High volume goes to the databases and queues; the coordination service just tells them what the world looks like.

Don't put consensus in the hot path. A common wrong turn is "every request takes a distributed lock in ZooKeeper to be safe." You've now capped your entire system's throughput at the throughput of one Raft leader and added a round trip to every request. Ask instead: can the database enforce this with a unique constraint or a conditional update on the row you were already writing? Usually yes — and it's faster, simpler, and one fewer thing to run.

5. ZooKeeper, etcd & Consul

These are the products you point at when you say "I'd use an existing consensus system." All three are, at heart, a small, strongly-consistent, highly-available key-value store with a consensus protocol underneath and a few extra primitives on top (watches, ephemeral/TTL keys, atomic compare-and-swap). They are not databases. They hold megabytes, not terabytes — the whole dataset lives in memory on every node — and they answer a few thousand writes per second, not a million.

The lineage is worth 10 seconds of interview time: Google built Chubby (a Paxos-backed lock service) and wrote a famous paper about it; ZooKeeper is the open-source descendant of that idea, built at Yahoo for Hadoop; etcd is the modern, Raft-backed, HTTP/gRPC-native version that ended up as the brain of Kubernetes (every object in your cluster is a key in etcd); Consul is HashiCorp's take with service discovery and health checking as the headline feature.

What they're actually for

Use caseHow it worksReal example
Leader election Every candidate tries to create the same key with a compare-and-swap ("create only if absent"). Exactly one wins — consensus guarantees that. The key is ephemeral or has a TTL, so it vanishes when the winner's session dies, triggering a new election. Kafka controller election; HDFS active NameNode
Distributed locks & fencing Same mechanism as election, plus the store hands you a monotonic revision/zxid to use as a fencing token. Any "only one worker should run this job" cron
Service discovery Each instance registers itself (/services/payments/host-3 → 10.0.4.7:8080) with a TTL and heartbeats. Clients watch the prefix and get pushed an update the moment an instance appears or dies. Consul + Spring Cloud; Kubernetes' own endpoints
Dynamic config / feature flags Config lives in a key; services watch it and reconfigure live without redeploying. Rate-limit thresholds, kill switches
Cluster metadata / shard map "Which node owns which partition" is a single strongly-consistent document everyone reads. The directory-based sharding from page 04

Watches are the underrated feature. Without them, every service would poll ("is the config different yet?"), which is either slow or expensive. A watch is a subscription: the store pushes you the change. This is exactly the difference between polling an API in React and holding a WebSocket, and it's the same reason — push beats poll when changes are rare but latency matters.

When you'd add one, and when it's overkill

Add a coordination service when…It's overkill when…
You genuinely need exactly one of something running (one scheduler, one controller, one compactor) and two would be a correctness bug. You just need a lock and you already have Postgres — SELECT … FOR UPDATE or an advisory lock is free and it's actually linearizable, unlike your Redis lock.
You need service discovery in a dynamic fleet where instances come and go constantly. You're already on Kubernetes, which gives you Services and DNS out of the box — and which is itself backed by etcd. You have a coordination service; don't run a second one.
You need a shard map or cluster membership that every node must see identically. Your "config" changes twice a year. Put it in the repo and deploy it.
You need a fencing-token source with a real guarantee behind it. The uniqueness you need can be enforced by a unique constraint on a table you were writing to anyway. This is the boring right answer more often than candidates expect.
The judgment sentence: "ZooKeeper or etcd is another stateful cluster to operate, monitor, and back up — I'd only introduce one if I need coordination that my database genuinely can't express. Most 'we need a distributed lock' requirements turn out to be 'we need a unique constraint' or 'we need a conditional update', and those are cheaper, faster and harder to get wrong." Interviewers at enterprise shops love this, because they have all been burned by an under-maintained ZooKeeper ensemble.

6. Distributed locks — and why they're harder than they look

Ask a candidate "how do you stop two servers from processing the same order twice" and nine out of ten say "a distributed lock in Redis." It's the reflex answer, it's on every blog, and it is usually wrong. This section is about being the tenth candidate.

Start by separating the two reasons you might want a lock, because they have completely different answers:

  • Efficiency. "I'd rather not have two workers generate the same PDF — it's wasted CPU." If the lock occasionally fails and the PDF gets generated twice, you lose a few cents of compute. A Redis lock is fine here. Genuinely fine. Use it and move on.
  • Correctness. "Two workers must never both mark this seat as sold / both capture this payment / both write this file." If the lock fails, you have oversold a seat or double-charged a customer. A Redis lock is NOT fine here, and this is the distinction the interviewer is fishing for.

The naive Redis lock

Everyone's first implementation, and it has a bug on every line except the last one:

// SET key value NX PX 10000
//   NX      = only set if the key does NOT already exist  (this is the "lock")
//   PX 10000 = auto-expire after 10s, so a crashed holder doesn't deadlock everyone
//   value    = a random UUID identifying THIS holder — not optional, see below

String token = UUID.randomUUID().toString();
Boolean acquired = redis.set("lock:order:" + orderId, token,
                            SetArgs.Builder.nx().px(10_000));

if (!Boolean.TRUE.equals(acquired)) throw new LockBusyException();

try {
    processOrder(orderId);          // ← the "critical section" (allegedly)
} finally {
    // Release must be a compare-and-delete, NOT a plain DEL.
    // Plain DEL would let you delete a lock that ALREADY EXPIRED and was
    // re-acquired by somebody else — you'd be unlocking a stranger's lock.
    // Redis has no atomic compare-and-delete, so it takes a Lua script:
    redis.eval("if redis.call('get', KEYS[1]) == ARGV[1] " +
               "then return redis.call('del', KEYS[1]) else return 0 end",
               List.of("lock:order:" + orderId), token);
}

Even done correctly — with the UUID and the Lua compare-and-delete, which most implementations get wrong — this lock is not safe for correctness. Three independent reasons, and you should be able to list all three:

  1. GC pauses / process stalls. Section 3's whole story. You hold the lock, you freeze for 12 seconds, the 10-second TTL expires, someone else takes the lock, you wake up and write. Two holders. The lock told you nothing was wrong, because from your perspective nothing was.
  2. Clock skew. The TTL is enforced by Redis's own clock. If that clock jumps forward (NTP correction, VM snapshot restore, leap-second handling), the lock expires early — while its rightful holder is happily working. Any lock whose safety rests on wall-clock time is resting on sand.
  3. Failover loses the lock entirely. Redis replication is asynchronous. You acquire the lock on the primary; the primary dies before replicating that key; the replica is promoted and has never heard of your lock; a second client acquires it. Two holders again — and this one isn't even exotic, it's a routine failover.
  Client A ──SET NX──▶ Redis PRIMARY   (lock acquired ✓)
                            │
                            │  ✗ dies before replicating the key
                            ▼
                       Redis REPLICA  ──promoted──▶ new PRIMARY
                       (has no lock:order:42 key at all)

  Client B ──SET NX──▶ new PRIMARY     (lock acquired ✓ !!)

  A and B are both in the critical section. Nobody did anything wrong.

Redlock, and the honest note about it

Redis's author (Salvatore Sanfilippo, "antirez") proposed Redlock to fix reason 3: run 5 independent Redis masters, acquire the lock on a majority of them (3 of 5), and only consider yourself the holder if you got a majority and the acquisition took much less time than the TTL. It's a quorum, so a single node's failover can't hand the lock to two people.

Martin Kleppmann (the DDIA author) published a well-known critique arguing Redlock still isn't safe for correctness: it fixes the failover problem but does nothing about pauses and clock jumps, because it still fundamentally relies on timing assumptions, and it still issues no fencing token. Antirez replied. The exchange is one of the more famous arguments in the field, and it has never been settled to everyone's satisfaction.

Do not take a side; take the engineering position. Say: "Redlock is contested — Kleppmann's critique is that it still depends on timing assumptions and doesn't give you a fencing token, and I find that persuasive. But I'd frame it differently: if my correctness depends on the lock, I don't want to be in this argument at all. I want the resource itself to reject the stale writer, either through a fencing token or by pushing the invariant down into the database." That answer shows you know the literature and that you know arguing about it is not how you ship a correct payment system.

The conclusion that actually matters

Here is the decision rule. Learn it as a rule, because under interview pressure you want a rule and not a debate:

SituationReach forWhy
Lock is for efficiency (avoid duplicate work; duplicates are merely wasteful) Redis SET NX PX. One line. Ship it. The failure mode costs CPU, not money. Don't over-engineer.
Lock is for correctness and the resource is your own database Don't use a lock. Use a unique constraint, or a conditional/optimistic update: UPDATE … WHERE version = ? / WHERE status = 'AVAILABLE'. The DB already has a linearizable lock manager and a durable commit. Reuse it instead of rebuilding a worse one over the network.
Lock is for correctness and the resource is external (a file store, another service) Consensus-backed lock (etcd/ZooKeeper) + fencing token the resource enforces. Only monotonic tokens survive pauses. Nothing else does.
Correctness needed, but the resource cannot enforce fencing (third-party API) Idempotency keys (section 9). Make the duplicate call harmless instead of trying to prevent it. You can't prevent a duplicate you can't see. You can make it a no-op.

Notice that in three of four rows the answer is not a distributed lock. That's not an accident. The most senior move available on this topic is to eliminate the need for the lock, by pushing the invariant into a component that can enforce it atomically and durably. Postgres enforcing UNIQUE (event_id), or an UPDATE seats SET status='HELD' WHERE id=? AND status='AVAILABLE' that returns 0 rows when someone beat you to it — these are unglamorous, and they are correct, and correct is what you're being graded on.

7. Distributed transactions: 2PC vs saga

This is the core of the page and it is the highest-frequency distributed-systems question in enterprise and fintech loops. If you learn one thing here, learn this. "Place an order" touches the Order service, the Payment service, and the Inventory service — three services, three databases. On one Postgres you'd wrap all three writes in BEGIN … COMMIT and go home. Across three services you cannot, because there is no shared transaction. So what do you do when payment succeeds but inventory says "out of stock"?

There are exactly two families of answer, and an interviewer wants to hear you name both, pick one, and justify it.

7a. Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

2PC tries to preserve real atomicity across services. A coordinator drives all participants through two phases:

  1. Prepare (voting). The coordinator asks every participant: "can you commit this?" Each participant does all the work — validates, takes locks, writes to its own log — and then stops just short of committing, replying YES or NO. A YES is a promise: "I will be able to commit this even if I crash and restart." The participant is now holding locks and cannot release them.
  2. Commit (decision). If every vote was YES, the coordinator durably records COMMIT and tells everyone to commit. If any vote was NO (or timed out), it records ABORT and tells everyone to roll back. Participants have already promised, so they must obey.
  Coordinator          Order DB        Payment DB      Inventory DB
       │                  │                │                │
   1   ├─── prepare? ────▶│                │                │
       ├─── prepare? ──────────────────────▶│               │
       ├─── prepare? ───────────────────────────────────────▶│
       │◀── YES (locked) ─┤                │                │
       │◀── YES (locked) ──────────────────┤                │
       │◀── YES (locked) ───────────────────────────────────┤
       │
       │  ★ coordinator durably writes "COMMIT" to its own log ★
       │
   2   ├─── COMMIT ──────▶│  ✓             │                │
       ├─── COMMIT ────────────────────────▶│  ✓            │
       ├─── COMMIT ─────────────────────────────────────────▶│  ✓
       │
       └─▶ all-or-nothing achieved.  Locks held from prepare → commit.

The fatal flaw: coordinator death after prepare

Look at the star. Between "everyone voted YES" and "everyone got the decision," the coordinator crashes.

Every participant is now sitting in the prepared state, holding its locks, having promised to commit. It cannot commit — it doesn't know if the others voted YES. It cannot abort — it already promised, and if the coordinator wrote COMMIT before dying, aborting would break atomicity. It cannot ask its peers, because 2PC gives it no way to. So it does the only thing it can: it waits. Holding locks. Indefinitely.

This is why 2PC is called a blocking protocol, and it's the sentence you must say: "2PC's failure mode is that a coordinator crash after prepare leaves every participant blocked, holding locks, until the coordinator comes back — and meanwhile every other transaction that touches those rows queues up behind them." In a busy system that isn't a stalled order; it's a cascading outage, because the held locks take out unrelated traffic. (You can add a backup coordinator, or make the coordinator itself a Raft group — that's basically what Spanner does — but now you've built a consensus system to run your checkout.)

The other half of 2PC's cost is one candidates forget: it's synchronous and it holds locks across a network round trip. Your transaction's lock duration is now bounded by the slowest participant's network latency, not by your own database. Throughput collapses under contention, and one slow service degrades everything it touches. 2PC doesn't just fail badly — it performs badly even when it works.

When 2PC is still the right answer

Don't sneer at it. 2PC is still in production everywhere, and knowing when is a maturity signal:

  • Single organisation, few participants, all in one datacenter. Two databases and a message broker in the same rack, XA transactions via JTA — this works, it's boring, and it's correct.
  • Java/Spring's JtaTransactionManager with an XA-capable datasource is exactly this. You may well meet it at a bank.
  • When the business truly cannot tolerate a temporary inconsistency, even for milliseconds, and the volume is low enough that blocking is affordable. Some core-banking ledgers are genuinely in this bucket.
  • Inside a single database engine. Postgres does 2PC internally across its own shards; Spanner does 2PC over Paxos groups. The protocol is fine — it's the cross-service, cross-vendor, over-the-internet version that hurts.

What kills it is scale and heterogeneity: microservices with independent teams, third-party APIs (Stripe does not support XA), and any participant you don't control. That's most modern systems. Hence sagas.

7b. Sagas

A saga gives up on atomicity and buys back availability. Instead of one distributed transaction, you run a sequence of local transactions — each one commits immediately in its own service's database — and if a later step fails, you run compensating transactions to semantically undo the earlier ones.

No locks are held across services. Nothing blocks. The system is temporarily inconsistent — and you accept that, in exchange for never having a participant stuck waiting on a dead coordinator.

What "compensating transaction" really means

This is the part candidates hand-wave, and the part interviewers probe. A compensation is not a rollback. A rollback erases history as if nothing happened. A compensation is a new business action that counteracts the old one, and the original remains visible forever.

  • You cannot un-send an email. You send an apology email. The customer saw both.
  • You cannot un-charge a card. You issue a refund. The customer's statement shows a charge and a refund, and in some countries you've paid processing fees on both.
  • You cannot un-ship a package. You issue a return label.
  • You can't un-reserve inventory silently — you release the reservation, and in the intervening 200ms someone else may have failed to buy the item that was falsely held.

That last clause is the honest cost of sagas, and saying it out loud is a strong signal: a saga's intermediate states are visible to users and to other transactions. There is a window where the order exists but isn't paid, where money has left the account but no order exists yet. Your data model and your UI must tolerate that — which is why saga-based systems always have an explicit status field (PENDING, PAID, FAILED, COMPENSATING) rather than pretending a row springs into existence fully-formed.

A concrete order-placement saga

  STEP                        LOCAL TXN                 COMPENSATION (if a later step fails)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1. Create order             INSERT order              UPDATE order SET status='CANCELLED'
                              status = PENDING

  2. Reserve inventory        UPDATE stock              UPDATE stock
                              SET reserved += qty       SET reserved -= qty
                              WHERE available >= qty    ("release the hold")

  3. Charge payment           INSERT payment            INSERT refund
                              (call PSP w/ idem key)    (call PSP refund API)

  4. Confirm order            UPDATE order              — (final step: nothing to undo)
                              SET status='CONFIRMED'

  5. Notify customer          send email                send "we're sorry" email

  Failure at step 3 (card declined) ⇒ run compensations for 2 then 1, in REVERSE order:
      release the inventory hold, then cancel the order.  Nothing to compensate for 3
      because it never committed.

Two rules that fall out of that diagram, and both are worth saying:

  • Compensate in reverse order. Undo the most recent completed step first, exactly like unwinding a stack. Otherwise you can release inventory that a compensating step still depends on.
  • Order the steps so that the ones you can't compensate come last. Send the email last; ship the package last. If an irreversible step is in the middle, every failure after it leaves you with a mess a human has to clean up. Sequencing the saga to put the cheapest-to-compensate steps first and the impossible-to-compensate steps at the end is a real design decision, and it's the kind of thing a 3-YOE candidate never mentions.

The rule for which to reach for

  • Default to saga in any multi-service design. It's what real microservice systems do, it's what the interviewer expects to hear, and it's honest about the fact that you can't have distributed ACID for free.

8. The outbox pattern

This is the most immediately useful pattern on the page. It's small, it's cheap, you can implement it in an afternoon, and it fixes a bug that is sitting in production in a shocking number of systems right now.

The dual-write problem

A saga step almost always looks like this: change something in my database, then tell everyone else about it. Which in code becomes: 

@Transactional
public void placeOrder(Order order) {
    orderRepository.save(order);                              // write #1 → Postgres
    kafkaTemplate.send("orders", new OrderCreated(order));   // write #2 → Kafka
}
// ↑ This is broken. Two different systems, two different commits, no shared
//   transaction. @Transactional covers the DB. It does NOT cover Kafka.

You are writing to two systems that cannot commit together. There is no ordering of those two lines that is safe:

  • DB first, then Kafka: the DB commits, the process crashes (or Kafka is down) before the publish. The order exists. No event was ever emitted. Inventory is never reserved, no email is ever sent, the saga never starts. The order sits there forever, silently. This is the failure you'll actually see, and it's invisible until a customer complains.
  • Kafka first, then DB: the event is published, then the DB transaction rolls back. Now every downstream service is reacting to an order that does not exist. You've reserved stock and charged a card for a phantom.
  • "Just publish inside the @Transactional block": doesn't help. The send happens during the transaction, but the transaction can still roll back afterwards — and Kafka has already got the message. (Spring's TransactionalEventListener(AFTER_COMMIT) narrows the window but doesn't close it: the process can still die between the commit and the publish.)

You cannot fix this by reordering, retrying, or trying harder. It's structural. Two systems, no atomic commit across them. Naming it — "this is a dual-write problem" — is itself a scoring moment.

The fix: make it one write

If you can't atomically write to two systems, then write to one system twice. Insert the event into an outbox table in the same local transaction as your business data. Now the order and the intent-to-publish commit or roll back together, atomically, because they're in the same database. A separate relay process reads the outbox and publishes to Kafka afterwards. 

   ┌────────── ONE LOCAL POSTGRES TRANSACTION ──────────┐
   │                                                     │
   │   INSERT INTO orders  (...)                         │  ← atomic together.
   │   INSERT INTO outbox  (event payload)               │    both or neither.
   │                                                     │
   └────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────┘
                        │ COMMIT
                        ▼
                  ┌───────────┐
                  │  outbox   │  (a normal table in YOUR database)
                  │   table   │
                  └─────┬─────┘
                        │  relay: poll for unpublished rows
                        │         (or read the WAL via Debezium/CDC)
                        ▼
                  ┌───────────┐        ┌──────────────────┐
                  │  Kafka    │───────▶│ consumers (saga) │
                  └───────────┘        └──────────────────┘
                        │
              publish succeeded → mark the outbox row published
              publish failed     → the row is still there → RETRY.
                                   Nothing is lost. Worst case: a duplicate.

That last line is the whole point. The relay crashing after publishing but before marking the row means the event gets published twice. That's fine — it converts an unfixable problem (a lost event) into a trivially fixable one (a duplicate event), and duplicates are handled by idempotent consumers, which is the next section. At-least-once is achievable; at-most-once loses data; you always choose at-least-once and dedupe.

The table

CREATE TABLE outbox (
    id              BIGSERIAL    PRIMARY KEY,   -- also gives you ordering
    aggregate_type  TEXT         NOT NULL,      -- 'Order'   → becomes the topic
    aggregate_id    TEXT         NOT NULL,      -- '12345'   → becomes the partition key,
                                                --             so one order's events stay ordered
    event_type      TEXT         NOT NULL,      -- 'OrderCreated'
    payload         JSONB        NOT NULL,      -- the event body
    event_id        UUID         NOT NULL UNIQUE, -- the consumer's dedup key (section 9)
    created_at      TIMESTAMPTZ  NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
    published_at    TIMESTAMPTZ                  -- NULL = not yet published
);

-- The relay's query hits only unpublished rows, so this partial index stays
-- tiny even when the table has 100M historical rows in it.
CREATE INDEX idx_outbox_unpublished
    ON outbox (id) WHERE published_at IS NULL;

And the write side — note that there is now only one @Transactional boundary and it covers everything:

@Transactional                                  // ONE local transaction. Both rows or neither.
public Order placeOrder(PlaceOrderCommand cmd) {
    Order order = orderRepository.save(Order.pending(cmd));

    outboxRepository.save(new OutboxEvent(
        "Order",                              // aggregate_type
        order.getId().toString(),             // aggregate_id → Kafka partition key
        "OrderCreated",                       // event_type
        json.write(OrderCreated.from(order)),  // payload
        UUID.randomUUID()                     // event_id → consumer dedup key
    ));

    return order;   // commit. The event is now GUARANTEED to be published eventually.
}

FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED is the detail worth dropping: it's how you run several relay instances safely. Each one grabs a batch of rows nobody else has locked and skips the rest, so you get parallel relays with no coordination service, no lock, and no duplicate publishing from concurrent relays. That's a Postgres feature earning its keep — and a nice callback to the "let the database enforce it" theme.

Drop this into any design with a database and a queue, unprompted: "I'd use the outbox pattern here rather than writing to Postgres and publishing to Kafka in the same method — that's a dual write and it silently drops events when the publish fails after the commit. Instead the event goes into an outbox table in the same transaction, and a relay publishes it at-least-once. Consumers are idempotent, so the occasional duplicate is harmless." This single paragraph demonstrates: you know what a dual write is, you know why it's broken, you know the fix, and you know the fix's cost. Very few candidates volunteer it.
Don't forget to reap the table. An outbox on a busy service grows by millions of rows a day. Either delete rows once published (simplest) or partition by day and drop old partitions. An unbounded outbox table that nobody vacuums will eventually take down the very database it was protecting — a genuinely common production incident, and mentioning it shows you've operated something.

9. Idempotency & the exactly-once myth

Everything on this page has been quietly leading here. The outbox gives you at-least-once delivery. Retries give you at-least-once. A failed lock gives you a duplicate. A user double-clicking "Pay" gives you a duplicate. A client that times out and retries gives you a duplicate — and the original request may well have succeeded; the client just never saw the response. Duplicates are not an edge case in distributed systems. Duplicates are the weather.

So you stop trying to prevent them and instead make them harmless.

The definition, stated precisely

An operation is idempotent if performing it N times has the same effect as performing it once. You already live with this in HTTP: GET, PUT and DELETE are idempotent by specification; POST is not. SET balance = 100 is idempotent. balance = balance - 50 is emphatically not — run it twice and you've stolen fifty pounds. That distinction is the whole game, and most business operations are unfortunately in the second category.

Idempotency keys — how the second category gets fixed

The client generates a unique key for the intent and sends it with the request. Retries reuse the same key. The server records keys it has already processed, and on a repeat it does not redo the work — it returns the original response. This is exactly what Stripe's Idempotency-Key header does, and describing it correctly is a free win in any payments interview. 

  POST /payments
  Idempotency-Key: 7c9e6679-7425-40de-944b-e07fc1f90ae7     ← generated by the CLIENT,
  { "orderId": 12345, "amountCents": 4999 }                   stable across retries

  1st call  → key unseen  → charge the card → store (key → response) → 201 Created
  2nd call  → key SEEN    → do NOT charge   → replay stored response → 201 Created
             (identical body, identical payment id — the client can't tell,
              and more importantly the customer is charged exactly once)

Three details that separate a real implementation from a whiteboard sketch, and interviewers do probe them:

  • The key must come from the client, generated once per logical intent — not per HTTP attempt. A key the server generates is useless, because a retry would get a new one. In your React app: generate the UUID when the user opens the checkout form, not when the request fires.
  • Store the response, not just the key. If you only record "seen" and return 409 Conflict, the retrying client doesn't know whether it succeeded and has to go hunting. Replay the original response body and the retry becomes genuinely invisible.
  • Handle the concurrent case. Two copies of the same request arrive 5ms apart, both check "have I seen this key?", both see no, both charge the card. The check-then-act race is back. The fix is the same as always: don't check-then-act — let the database's unique constraint decide, atomically.

The implementation — the unique constraint does the work

CREATE TABLE idempotency_key (
    key           UUID        PRIMARY KEY,   -- ← THE lock. Not Redis. This.
    request_hash  TEXT        NOT NULL,      -- guards against key reuse with a different body
    status        TEXT        NOT NULL,      -- 'IN_PROGRESS' | 'COMPLETED'
    response_body JSONB,                     -- the stored reply, replayed on retry
    response_code INT,
    created_at    TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT now()  -- expire after ~24h
);

@Transactional
public PaymentResponse charge(UUID idemKey, ChargeRequest req) {

    // 1. Claim the key. The INSERT is the atomic check-and-act: the PRIMARY KEY
    //    constraint means exactly ONE concurrent caller can win this row. No race,
    //    no distributed lock, no Redlock argument. The database already solved it.
    int claimed = jdbc.update("""
        INSERT INTO idempotency_key (key, request_hash, status)
        VALUES (?, ?, 'IN_PROGRESS')
        ON CONFLICT (key) DO NOTHING
        """, idemKey, hash(req));

    if (claimed == 0) {
        // Somebody already claimed this key — a retry, or a concurrent duplicate.
        IdemRecord prior = idemRepo.findById(idemKey).orElseThrow();

        if (!prior.requestHash().equals(hash(req)))       // same key, DIFFERENT body:
            throw new IdempotencyKeyReuseException();      // a client bug. 422. Loudly.

        if (prior.status().equals("IN_PROGRESS"))          // original is still running:
            throw new RequestInFlightException();         // 409 + Retry-After. Do NOT
                                                             // run it a second time.
        return prior.responseBody();                      // COMPLETED → replay the reply ✓
    }

    // 2. We won the key. Do the actual work.
    PaymentResponse res = paymentGateway.capture(req);   // (pass idemKey downstream too!)

    // 3. Record the outcome IN THE SAME TRANSACTION as the business write, so the
    //    key and the payment can never disagree about whether this happened.
    paymentRepo.save(Payment.from(res));
    jdbc.update("UPDATE idempotency_key SET status='COMPLETED', response_body=?, " +
                "response_code=? WHERE key=?", json.write(res), 201, idemKey);
    return res;
}
Look at what's not in that code: no Redis, no ZooKeeper, no lock, no consensus. A primary key constraint and one transaction. That is the "boring right answer" this page keeps promising, and it's what a real payments service actually does. If you can write this on a whiteboard from memory in a fintech loop, you are ahead of most of the field.

The idempotent consumer

The same idea, applied to your Kafka consumers, closes the loop the outbox opened. The outbox publishes at-least-once, so a consumer will see duplicates. Give every event an event_id (you did — it's in the outbox schema) and have the consumer record processed IDs in the same transaction as its side effect: 

@Transactional
public void on(OrderCreated evt) {
    int inserted = jdbc.update(
        "INSERT INTO processed_event (event_id) VALUES (?) ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING",
        evt.eventId());

    if (inserted == 0) return;   // already processed this exact event — skip. Done.

    inventory.reserve(evt.orderId(), evt.items());   // the side effect, same txn
}
// The dedup row and the side effect commit together. If the consumer crashes
// after reserving but before acking to Kafka, the redelivery hits the dedup
// row and does nothing. THIS is what "effectively-once" actually means.

"Exactly-once delivery is a myth. Exactly-once processing is achievable."

Learn that sentence. Interviewers bait it deliberately — "so how would you guarantee exactly-once delivery?" — and the trap is to answer the question as asked.

Why delivery can't be exactly-once: straight back to section 1. The sender transmits a message and waits for an ack. The ack doesn't arrive. It cannot know whether the message was lost, or the message arrived and the ack was lost. It has two choices and only two: retry (risking a duplicate → at-least-once) or don't (risking a loss → at-most-once). There is no third option. Adding more acknowledgements doesn't help — that's Two Generals, and it's turtles all the way down. No protocol, no vendor, no amount of money escapes this.

Why processing can be exactly-once: because you control the receiver. Deliver at-least-once, and make the effect of processing idempotent — dedup by event ID, or write with a unique constraint, or use a conditional update. The message may arrive five times; the state changes once. The industry calls this effectively-once, and it is the only honest thing anyone means when they say "exactly-once."

Careful with Kafka's EOS claim in an interview — but be precise, not dismissive. Kafka does offer exactly-once semantics, and it's real: a producer epoch (a fencing token, again) that dedupes retried sends to a partition, plus transactions that atomically commit the produced messages and the consumer offsets together. But that only holds inside Kafka. The moment your consumer writes to Postgres or calls Stripe, you're outside the transaction and you're back to needing your own idempotency. Saying "Kafka EOS is real but it doesn't extend to my external side effects" is precisely the kind of nuance that separates you from someone who has only read the docs page.

Interview Q&A

"Why can't you just use a distributed transaction across microservices?"

You can — that's 2PC — but you almost never should, and the reason is its failure mode, not its complexity. 2PC has a prepare phase where every participant takes locks and promises to commit, and a commit phase where the coordinator tells them the decision. If the coordinator dies between those two phases, every participant is stuck holding locks indefinitely: it can't commit (it doesn't know the decision) and it can't abort (it already promised). It blocks, and everything queued behind those locks blocks with it. That turns a coordinator failure into a system-wide outage.

On top of that, it's synchronous — your transaction is as slow as the slowest participant — it doesn't scale with the number of services, and third parties won't join it (Stripe does not speak XA). So the standard answer is a saga: a chain of local transactions with compensating actions, which never blocks and never holds a cross-service lock, at the price of giving up isolation and accepting visible intermediate states.

And the senior addendum: "If two services need a distributed transaction on every single request, I'd first question whether they should be two services at all."

"Walk me through a saga for placing an order. What happens when the payment fails?"

Four local transactions, each committing in its own service, driven by an orchestrator:

  1. Order service: INSERT order (status=PENDING). Compensation: set status CANCELLED.
  2. Inventory service: reserve the stock (a conditional update: WHERE available >= qty). Compensation: release the reservation.
  3. Payment service: capture the card, passing an idempotency key. Compensation: refund.
  4. Order service: status = CONFIRMED. Final step — nothing to compensate.

Payment fails at step 3 ⇒ the orchestrator runs compensations in reverse order: release the inventory reservation, then cancel the order. Step 3 never committed, so there's nothing to undo there — but note that if the card was charged and we merely failed to hear about it, the compensation is a refund, not a rollback, and reconciliation against the PSP will catch it.

The two things to volunteer without being asked: (1) a compensation is a new business action, not a rollback — a refund is visible on the customer's statement forever; (2) the customer can observe the intermediate state, so the order status field is part of the contract, and the UI shows "processing" rather than pretending the order atomically appeared.

"You write to your database and publish to Kafka. What's wrong with that?"

It's a dual write — two systems, no atomic commit across them. If the DB commits and the process dies before the publish, the row exists and no event was ever emitted: the downstream saga never starts and nobody notices until a customer complains. Reverse the order and you can publish an event for a transaction that then rolls back — downstream services react to an order that doesn't exist. There is no ordering of those two writes that is safe.

The fix is the outbox pattern: insert the event into an outbox table in the same local transaction as the business data, so they commit or roll back together. A relay process (polling the table, or Debezium tailing the WAL) publishes rows to Kafka and marks them published. If the relay crashes after publishing but before marking, the event is published twice — which is fine, because consumers dedupe on event_id. You've converted an unfixable failure (a lost event) into a trivially fixable one (a duplicate).

"Is a Redis lock safe? How would you make a distributed lock correct?"

First I'd ask what the lock is for, because the answer differs:

  • Efficiency (avoid duplicate work; a duplicate just wastes CPU) — SET key val NX PX 10000 with a UUID value and a Lua compare-and-delete on release. Perfectly adequate. Ship it.
  • Correctness (a duplicate double-charges someone) — it is not safe, for three separate reasons: a GC pause longer than the TTL gives you two live holders; clock skew can expire the lock early; and Redis's async replication means a primary failover can hand the same lock to two clients.

Redlock (quorum across 5 independent Redis masters) fixes the failover case but not pauses or clocks — that's Kleppmann's critique, and I find it persuasive. The real fix is a fencing token: the lock service issues a monotonically increasing number with each grant, the holder attaches it to every write, and the resource rejects any write bearing a token lower than the highest it has seen. That stops the paused writer at the resource, which is the only place it can be stopped.

But my actual first answer: if the resource is my own database, don't use a lock at all. Use a unique constraint or a conditional update — UPDATE seats SET status='HELD' WHERE id=? AND status='AVAILABLE'. Zero rows updated means someone beat me. The database already has a correct, durable, linearizable lock manager; rebuilding a worse one over the network is the mistake.

"What is a fencing token and what exactly does it protect against?" ★

A strictly increasing number handed out by the lock service on every grant. The holder sends it with every write; the resource remembers the highest token it has accepted and rejects anything lower.

It protects against the paused-holder problem, which no TTL can fix. A node acquires a 10-second lease, then suffers a 12-second stop-the-world GC pause. The lease expires; another node legitimately acquires it and starts writing. The first node wakes up still believing it holds the lock — from its own perspective no time has passed — and writes. Two writers.

You can't fix this by "checking the lease before writing," because the pause can happen between the check and the write, and there's no way to make those two steps atomic across two machines. With fencing, the new holder got token 34 and has already written; the stale holder shows up with token 33 and the resource refuses it. The check becomes a local monotonicity comparison at the resource, which is atomic. In SQL that's literally UPDATE … SET fence_token = ? WHERE id = ? AND fence_token < ?.

"How do you guarantee exactly-once delivery?" (this is bait)

You don't — exactly-once delivery isn't achievable, and I'd push back on the framing. A sender that gets no acknowledgement cannot distinguish "my message was lost" from "the ack was lost." It has exactly two options: retry (at-least-once, risking duplicates) or don't (at-most-once, risking loss). More acknowledgements don't help — that's the Two Generals problem.

What is achievable is exactly-once processing, sometimes called effectively-once: at-least-once delivery plus an idempotent consumer. Give every message an event ID; the consumer inserts that ID into a processed_event table with a unique constraint in the same transaction as its side effect. A duplicate hits the constraint and becomes a no-op. The message may arrive five times; the state changes once.

And on Kafka's "exactly-once semantics": it's real, but it's scoped to Kafka — idempotent producers (a producer epoch, which is a fencing token) plus transactions that atomically commit produced messages and consumer offsets. The moment my consumer writes to Postgres or calls a payment provider, I'm outside that transaction and I need my own idempotency again.

"Explain R + W > N. What does N=3, W=2, R=2 give you, and when would you change it?"

With N replicas, if the write quorum W and the read quorum R sum to more than N, the read set and the write set must overlap in at least one node — pigeonhole. So any read is guaranteed to touch at least one replica holding the latest acknowledged write, and by taking the highest version among the replicas you read, you get a fresh value.

N=3, W=2, R=2 (4 > 3 ✓) is the balanced default: tolerates one node down on both the read and write paths, latency is that of the second-fastest replica. I'd change it per workload: W=1 for high-volume, low-value writes like click events (fast, never blocks a user request, accepts staleness); W=3, R=1 for read-heavy config data (writes become fragile — any node down blocks them — but reads are single-replica fast).

The caveat I'd volunteer: quorums give fresh reads, not transactions and not consensus. Concurrent writes still conflict and need LWW or vector clocks, and sloppy quorums with hinted handoff quietly break the overlap guarantee during a partition. If I need real linearizability — a lock, an election, a uniqueness constraint — a quorum isn't enough; I need consensus or a single-writer database.

"Would you implement Raft?"

No. "I'd use an existing consensus system — etcd, ZooKeeper, or Consul — rather than build one. Implementing Raft correctly, including membership changes, log compaction and snapshotting, is a multi-year effort and it's a solved problem." That's the whole answer, and it's not a dodge — it's the expected one.

What I would say about it: consensus gives a group of nodes an identical replicated log despite crashes, and every coordination primitive is built on that — leader election, locks, fencing tokens (the log index is a monotonic counter), and a strongly-consistent config store. Raft's shape is: one leader per numbered term, followers vote on randomised election timeouts, writes go to the leader and are committed once a majority has stored them. Majorities are why clusters are 3 or 5 and never 4, and why split-brain is structurally impossible — two disjoint majorities can't exist.

The design point that actually matters: consensus is expensive and belongs on the control plane, not the data plane. Every decision costs a round trip to a majority through one leader. You put cluster metadata and locks in etcd; you do not put a million QPS of user writes through it.

"Choreography or orchestration for your saga?"

Choreography: no coordinator — services emit events and others react. Loosely coupled, no single point of failure, and fine for two or three steps. The killer weakness is that the business process exists nowhere: to answer "what happens when an order is placed?" you read five codebases, and to debug a stuck order you trace events across all of them. You can also build accidental cycles nobody designed.

Orchestration: a coordinator service sends commands and tracks the saga's state in a database row. The flow is one readable state machine you can test, retry, and audit. It's a component to run, but it's effectively stateless — its state is in the DB, so it restarts and resumes.

My rule: two steps, choreography; anything with money or more than about three steps, orchestration. When someone asks "why is order 12345 stuck," choreography makes you read five services and orchestration makes you read one row. That's the whole argument.

"A user double-clicks 'Pay'. Two identical requests. What happens?"

Nothing bad, because the client attached an idempotency key — a UUID generated when the checkout form mounted, not when the button was clicked, so both requests carry the same key.

Server side, I don't do check-then-act (both requests would check "seen this key?", both see no, both charge). I let the database's unique constraint arbitrate: INSERT INTO idempotency_key (key, status) VALUES (?, 'IN_PROGRESS') ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING. Exactly one caller inserts a row. The winner charges the card and then stores the response body against the key, in the same transaction as the payment row. The loser sees 0 rows inserted and either replays the stored response (if COMPLETED) or gets a 409 with a Retry-After (if still IN_PROGRESS) — what it must never do is charge the card a second time.

I'd also pass the same idempotency key down to the payment provider, since Stripe and its peers implement exactly this mechanism — so even a retry inside my own service can't produce a second charge at the PSP.

"When would you actually reach for ZooKeeper or etcd?"

When I need exactly one of something and two would be a correctness bug — one scheduler, one controller, one compaction job — or when I need service discovery in a fleet where instances constantly come and go, or a cluster/shard map that every node must see identically, or a fencing-token source with a real guarantee behind it. They're small, strongly-consistent KV stores with consensus underneath, plus watches and ephemeral keys. Not databases: megabytes and a few thousand writes/sec.

When it's overkill: when Postgres can already do it. Advisory locks, SELECT … FOR UPDATE, a unique constraint, a conditional update — all linearizable, all free, all one fewer stateful cluster to operate and back up. And if I'm on Kubernetes I already have etcd and DNS-based service discovery; running a second coordination service on top would be silly.

The line I'd say: "Most 'we need a distributed lock' requirements turn out to be 'we need a unique constraint.'"

"Why can't you just use a longer timeout to tell if a node is dead?"

Because a timeout is a guess, not a fact — and lengthening it doesn't make the guess correct, it just moves you along a trade-off curve. A node that doesn't reply might be dead, might be slow, might have had your request dropped, or might have had its reply dropped; those four are indistinguishable from the caller's side. A short timeout gives fast failover and frequent false positives (you fire a healthy node, and now two nodes are doing its job). A long timeout gives fewer false positives and a longer outage window while everyone waits.

There's no value that eliminates both, which is FLP in practical clothing: with no bound on message delay you cannot reliably distinguish crashed from slow, so you can't guarantee consensus. Real systems assume partial synchrony, use timeouts anyway, and accept that the failure detector will sometimes be wrong.

The design consequence is the real answer: stop trying to make the detector perfect and make the system correct despite a wrong detector. That's what fencing tokens, majority quorums and idempotency are all for — they keep you safe when you've wrongly declared a live node dead.

What to remember

The one root cause. You cannot distinguish a slow node from a dead one. A timeout is a guess. Every pattern on this page exists to keep the system correct when that guess is wrong.
